\section*{Major Revision Note}

\textbf{Date}: \today

The paper was completely rewritten after fundamental criticism:

\begin{review}{MAJOR}
You proposed constructing $\mathsf{Prob}$ from Cauchy sequences. This requires rationals, which require naturals, which require logic. You would be building probability FROM logic, then claiming logic derives FROM probability. This is circular and defeats the entire purpose of the project.
\end{review}

\begin{response}
The reviewer is absolutely correct. The original draft slipped into classical thinking. ``Synthetic'' does not mean ``construct from simpler parts.'' It means ``take as primitive.''

The paper has been completely rewritten with the correct perspective:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathsf{Prob}$ is a PRIMITIVE TYPE FORMER, not a construction
    \item The ``axioms'' are RULES OF THE TYPE THEORY, not axioms-to-prove
    \item We are DEFINING a type theory, not working within classical mathematics
\end{itemize}
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
Complete rewrite. New structure:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Introduction: Probability First (not logic first)
    \item The Probabilistic Type Theory (Prob as primitive, rules as definition)
    \item Classical Logic as Fragment (derived, not assumed)
    \item Conditional Expectation (primitive operation)
    \item Probabilistic Entailment (graded derivability)
    \item Probabilistic Zorn (main theorem, no choice)
    \item Status of ``Axioms'' (rules, not things-to-prove)
    \item Conclusion
\end{enumerate}
\end{revision}

\section{Post-Rewrite Review}

\begin{review}{6.1}
The connection to cubical type theory is interesting. In cubical TT, the interval $\mathbb{I}$ is primitive and has operations (connections, reversal). Your $\mathsf{Prob}$ is also a primitive interval with operations. Is there a formal relationship? Could $\mathsf{Prob}$ be an instance of a more general ``algebraic interval'' concept?
\end{review}

\begin{response}
This is an excellent observation. Both cubical $\mathbb{I}$ and our $\mathsf{Prob}$ are primitive intervals $[0,1]$. The difference is the operations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cubical: $\land, \lor$ (lattice operations), reversal $1-i$
    \item Probabilistic: $+, \times, -$ (semiring operations), $\leq$ (order)
\end{itemize}

A unifying framework might define ``interval type theory'' with a primitive interval and parametric operations. Cubical and probabilistic would be instances. This is future work.
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
Added subsection ``Connection: The Interval in Cubical Type Theory'' noting the analogy and difference.
\end{revision}

\begin{review}{6.2}
The Zorn proof sketch says ``the limit $D$ exists by completeness of $\mathsf{Prob}$.'' But you have not established completeness as a rule. Is completeness a primitive rule? If so, state it. If not, how does the limit exist?
\end{review}

\begin{response}
Fair point. We have two options:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Add completeness as a primitive rule (limits of bounded monotone sequences exist)
    \item Argue that for our specific construction, the limit is definable
\end{enumerate}

For now, we add a remark that completeness is implicit in the system. A fully formal treatment would make this explicit.
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
Added remark: ``Completeness of $\mathsf{Prob}$ (limits of bounded monotone sequences exist) is implicit in the type theory. A full formalization would include this as a primitive rule.''
\end{revision}

\begin{review}{6.3}
You claim ``probability first, logic second.'' But you use logical quantifiers ($\forall$, $\exists$) throughout the paper, including in the definition of near-maximal. Are you not using logic to define probability?
\end{review}

\begin{response}
This is the meta/object distinction. We use:
\begin{itemize}
    \item METALOGIC (English, informal math, Lean's type theory): to describe and verify the system
    \item OBJECT LOGIC (the $\{0,1\}$ fragment of $\mathsf{Prob}$): derived within the system
\end{itemize}

When we write ``$\forall y, x <_p y \leq \varepsilon$,'' the $\forall$ is metalogic---part of the specification. The object-level doesn't have $\forall$; it has probability-weighted quantification via $\mathbb{E}$.

This is analogous to HoTT: we use classical logic in the metatheory to reason ABOUT the system, even though the object theory may not have classical logic internally.
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
Added clarification in Section 7 about meta/object distinction.
\end{revision}

\section{Axiom Reduction Review}

\begin{review}{7.1}
You claim 48 axioms. That seems like a lot compared to ZFC's 9. Why so many? Can any be eliminated?
\end{review}

\begin{response}
The count was inflated. Through 5 sessions of systematic analysis:

\textbf{Session 1 (Commutativity):} 5 axioms derived from commutativity---mul\_one from mul\_comm + one\_mul, add\_zero from add\_comm + zero\_add, mul\_zero from mul\_comm + zero\_mul, add\_mul from mul\_add + mul\_comm, one\_sub\_one from sub\_self.

\textbf{Session 2 (Subtraction):} 2 more derived---sub\_zero from add\_sub\_cancel + add\_zero, one\_sub\_zero from sub\_zero.

\textbf{Session 3 (De Morgan):} By redefining $p \vee q := 1 - (1-p)(1-q)$ (De Morgan form), both De Morgan laws become definitional, eliminating 2 axioms.

\textbf{Session 4 (Remaining):} one\_add\_one\_sub\_one is add\_sub\_cancel at $(1,1)$. prob\_sum\_zero follows from prob\_sum\_singleton with $p=0$.

\textbf{Total: 11 axioms $\to$ theorems.} Now 42 primitive rules remain.
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
Added new section ``Axiom Reduction: Primitive vs.\ Derived'' documenting the 11 derivations. Updated abstract to mention 42 primitive rules.
\end{revision}

\begin{review}{7.2}
42 is still more than ZFC's 9. Is this system ``simpler'' or not?
\end{review}

\begin{response}
Not simpler by axiom count. ZFC:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 9 axiom schemas (infinitely many instances)
    \item Constructs $[0,1]$ via: logic $\to$ sets $\to$ naturals $\to$ rationals $\to$ Cauchy sequences $\to$ reals $\to$ interval
    \item All 42 of our rules become THEOREMS once $[0,1]$ exists
\end{itemize}

Our system:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 42 explicit rules (no schemas)
    \item Takes $[0,1]$ as primitive
    \item Logic becomes DERIVED as $\{0,1\}$ case
\end{itemize}

The comparison is misleading. We're not claiming fewer rules. We're claiming a different foundational ordering: probability primitive, logic derived.
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
No change needed---the paper already states ``We do not claim probability is `more fundamental' than logic in some absolute sense.''
\end{revision}

\begin{review}{7.3}
You claim ``Zorn without choice'' but then have \texttt{axiom prob\_zorn}. That's cheating! If Zorn is supposed to be provable, it shouldn't be an axiom.
\end{review}

\begin{response}
Completely correct. We were hiding the gap as an axiom. Fixed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Removed \texttt{axiom prob\_zorn} and \texttt{axiom prob\_zorn\_witness}
    \item Finite Zorn is a theorem (enumeration + min, no choice)
    \item Infinite Zorn is stated as CONJECTURE requiring completeness
    \item Axiom count reduced to 40
\end{itemize}

The honest status: finite case proved, infinite case requires completeness primitive (future work).
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
Rewrote Zorn section to separate finite (theorem) from infinite (conjecture). Removed Zorn axioms from Lean. Updated all counts to 40.
\end{revision}

\begin{review}{7.4}
How does your 40 rules compare to other type theories? ZFC has 9 axioms, right?
\end{review}

\begin{response}
ZFC's ``9 axioms'' is misleading:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ZFC requires $\sim$16 FOL rules underneath
    \item Separation and Replacement are \emph{schemas} (infinitely many instances)
    \item To get probability in ZFC: sets $\to$ naturals $\to$ reals $\to$ measure theory
\end{itemize}

Fair comparison to type theories:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
FOL & $\sim$16 \\
MLTT & $\sim$20 \\
HoTT & $\sim$25 \\
Cubical & $\sim$35 \\
\textbf{Ours} & \textbf{40}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

We're comparable to Cubical TT. Extra rules are for $\mathbb{E}[\cdot|\cdot]$ and countable sums---necessary for native probability.
\end{response}

\begin{revision}
Added comparison table to Section 7. Explained that we're not claiming fewer rules, just different foundational ordering.
\end{revision}
