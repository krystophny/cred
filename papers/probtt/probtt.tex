\large Weights from a De Morgan Algebra}

\author{Probabilistic Foundations Project}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We define a type theory where judgments carry \emph{weights} from a De Morgan algebra. The algebra has multiplication, complement, zero, one, and order---with disjunction derived via De Morgan duality. When $\W = \{\zero, \one\}$, we recover Martin-L\"of Type Theory. When $\W = {[0,1]}$, we get probabilistic typing with conditioning via the chain rule (not division). The key insight: types and logic \emph{emerge} from weight structure. We add conditioning as a primitive, distinguishing this from purely algebraic approaches.
\end{abstract}

%=============================================================================
\section{The Weight Algebra}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Product De Morgan Structure}

We axiomatize a minimal structure: multiplication and complement. Disjunction is derived.

\begin{definition}[Weight Algebra]
$\W$ is a type with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Constants: $\one : \W$ (certainty), $\zero : \W$ (impossibility)
    \item Multiplication: $(\cdot) : \W \to \W \to \W$ (conjunction)
    \item Complement: $\negw : \W \to \W$ (negation)
    \item Order: $(\leq) : \W \to \W \to \Type$
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Multiplication Axioms]
\begin{align}
w \cdot \one &= w \tag{unit-right} \\
\one \cdot w &= w \tag{unit-left} \\
w \cdot \zero &= \zero \tag{annihilate-right} \\
\zero \cdot w &= \zero \tag{annihilate-left} \\
(u \cdot v) \cdot w &= u \cdot (v \cdot w) \tag{assoc} \\
u \cdot v &= v \cdot u \tag{comm}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Complement Axioms]
\begin{align}
\negw \zero &= \one \tag{neg-zero} \\
\negw \one &= \zero \tag{neg-one} \\
\negw \negw w &= w \tag{involution}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Order Axioms]
\begin{align}
w &\leq w \tag{refl} \\
u \leq v \land v \leq w &\Rightarrow u \leq w \tag{trans} \\
u \leq v \land v \leq u &\Rightarrow u = v \tag{antisym} \\
\zero &\leq w \tag{zero-least} \\
w &\leq \one \tag{one-greatest}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Derived Operations}

\begin{definition}[De Morgan Disjunction]
\[
w \vee v := \negw(\negw w \cdot \negw v)
\]
\end{definition}

When $\W = {[0,1]}$ with $\negw w = 1 - w$, we get:
\[
w \vee v = 1 - (1-w)(1-v) = w + v - wv
\]
This is the \textbf{probabilistic sum} (inclusion-exclusion for independent events).

\begin{theorem}[De Morgan Laws]
\begin{align}
\negw(w \cdot v) &= \negw w \vee \negw v \\
\negw(w \vee v) &= \negw w \cdot \negw v
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Direct calculation from definitions.
\end{proof}

\subsection{What We Have vs What We Don't}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l|l}
\textbf{Primitive} & \textbf{Derived} \\
\hline
$\cdot$ (multiplication/AND) & $\vee$ (De Morgan OR) \\
$\negw$ (complement/NOT) & $\to$ (implication, if needed) \\
$\zero, \one$ (constants) & \\
$\leq$ (order) &
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{NOT primitive}: addition $+$, subtraction $-$, division $/$.

\subsection{Instances}

\begin{example}[Boolean: $\W = \{\zero, \one\}$]
$\one \cdot \one = \one$, else $\zero$. $\negw \zero = \one$, $\negw \one = \zero$.

This is classical Boolean logic.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Probability: $\W = {[0,1]}$]
$w \cdot v = w \times v$ (ordinary multiplication). $\negw w = 1 - w$.

This is product fuzzy logic / probabilistic reasoning.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Three-valued: $\W = \{\zero, ?, \one\}$]
$? \cdot ? = ?$, $\negw ? = ?$.

Kleene's strong three-valued logic.
\end{example}

\section{Judgments}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Judgment Forms]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\Gamma \ctx$ \hfill (valid context)
    \item $\Gamma \vdash A \type$ \hfill (valid type)
    \item $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}$ \hfill (weighted typing)
    \item $\Gamma \vdash a \equiv b : A \at{w}$ \hfill (weighted equality)
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
$\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}$ means: term $a$ inhabits type $A$ with weight $w$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $w = \one$: $a$ definitely inhabits $A$
    \item $w = \zero$: $a$ does not inhabit $A$ (vacuously true)
    \item $\zero < w < \one$: $a$ partially/probably inhabits $A$
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\end{gather}

\section{Type Formers}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Functions (Dependent)}

\begin{gather}
\infer[\textsc{$\Pi$-Form}]
  {\Gamma \vdash (x : A) \to B \type}
  {\Gamma \vdash A \type \quad \Gamma, x : A \vdash B \type}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{$\Pi$-Intro}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \lambda x. b : (x : A) \to B \at{w}}
  {\Gamma, x : A \vdash b : B \at{w}}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{$\Pi$-Elim}]
  {\Gamma \vdash f\,a : B[a/x] \at{w \cdot v}}
  {\Gamma \vdash f : (x : A) \to B \at{w} \quad \Gamma \vdash a : A \at{v}}
\end{gather}

\textbf{Key rule}: weights multiply in elimination.

\subsection{Pairs (Dependent)}

\begin{gather}
\infer[\textsc{$\Sigma$-Form}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \Sigma(x : A). B \type}
  {\Gamma \vdash A \type \quad \Gamma, x : A \vdash B \type}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{$\Sigma$-Intro}]
  {\Gamma \vdash (a, b) : \Sigma(x : A). B \at{w \cdot v}}
  {\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w} \quad \Gamma \vdash b : B[a/x] \at{v}}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{$\Sigma$-Elim$_1$}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \pi_1\,t : A \at{w}}
  {\Gamma \vdash t : \Sigma(x : A). B \at{w}}
\qquad
\infer[\textsc{$\Sigma$-Elim$_2$}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \pi_2\,t : B[\pi_1 t / x] \at{w}}
  {\Gamma \vdash t : \Sigma(x : A). B \at{w}}
\end{gather}

Pair weight = product of component weights (joint probability).

\subsection{Sums (Coproducts)}

\begin{gather}
\infer[\textsc{$+$-Form}]
  {\Gamma \vdash A + B \type}
  {\Gamma \vdash A \type \quad \Gamma \vdash B \type}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{$+$-Intro$_L$}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathsf{inl}\,a : A + B \at{w}}
  {\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}}
\qquad
\infer[\textsc{$+$-Intro$_R$}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathsf{inr}\,b : A + B \at{w}}
  {\Gamma \vdash b : B \at{w}}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{$+$-Elim}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathsf{case}\,e\,\mathsf{of}\,\mathsf{inl}\,x \Rightarrow l \mid \mathsf{inr}\,y \Rightarrow r : C \at{w \cdot v}}
  {\Gamma \vdash e : A + B \at{w} \quad \Gamma, x : A \vdash l : C \at{v} \quad \Gamma, y : B \vdash r : C \at{v}}
\end{gather}

\textbf{Key rule}: weights multiply in sum elimination. The scrutinee weight $w$ combines with the branch weight $v$ to give joint weight $w \cdot v$.

\subsection{Empty and Unit}

\begin{gather}
\infer[\textsc{$\mathbf{0}$-Form}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathbf{0} \type}
  {\Gamma \ctx}
\qquad
\infer[\textsc{$\mathbf{0}$-Elim}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathsf{abort}\,e : A \at{w}}
  {\Gamma \vdash e : \mathbf{0} \at{w}}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{$\mathbf{1}$-Form}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathbf{1} \type}
  {\Gamma \ctx}
\qquad
\infer[\textsc{$\mathbf{1}$-Intro}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \star : \mathbf{1} \at{\one}}
  {\Gamma \ctx}
\end{gather}

\subsection{Identity}

\begin{gather}
\infer[\textsc{Id-Form}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathsf{Id}_A(a, b) \type}
  {\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{\one} \quad \Gamma \vdash b : A \at{\one}}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{Id-Intro}]
  {\Gamma \vdash \mathsf{refl} : \mathsf{Id}_A(a, a) \at{w}}
  {\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}}
\\[1em]
\infer[\textsc{Id-Elim}]
  {\Gamma \vdash J(M, d, p) : M[b, p] \at{w \cdot v}}
  {\Gamma \vdash p : \mathsf{Id}_A(a, b) \at{w} \quad \Gamma \vdash d : M[a, \mathsf{refl}] \at{v}}
\end{gather}

The J eliminator transports along an identity proof. Weights multiply: the proof weight $w$ combines with the base case weight $v$.

\section{Computation Rules}
%=============================================================================

The computation (beta) rules reduce eliminator-intro pairs. In each case, weight multiplication is preserved.

\subsection{$\Pi$-Computation}
\[
(\lambda x. b)\,a \equiv b[a/x] : B[a/x] \at{w \cdot v}
\]
when $\Gamma, x : A \vdash b : B \at{w}$ and $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{v}$.

\subsection{$\Sigma$-Computation}
\begin{align}
\pi_1(a, b) &\equiv a : A \at{w \cdot v} \\
\pi_2(a, b) &\equiv b : B[a/x] \at{w \cdot v}
\end{align}
when $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}$ and $\Gamma \vdash b : B[a/x] \at{v}$.

\subsection{$+$-Computation}
\begin{align}
\mathsf{case}\,(\mathsf{inl}\,a)\,l\,r &\equiv l[a/x] : C \at{w \cdot v} \\
\mathsf{case}\,(\mathsf{inr}\,b)\,l\,r &\equiv r[b/y] : C \at{w \cdot v}
\end{align}

\subsection{Id-Computation}
\[
J(M, d, \mathsf{refl}) \equiv d : M[a, \mathsf{refl}] \at{w \cdot v}
\]
when $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}$ and $\Gamma \vdash d : M[a, \mathsf{refl}] \at{v}$.

\section{Negation}
%=============================================================================

There are two approaches to negation:

\subsection{Type-Theoretic Negation}

\[
\neg A := A \to \mathbf{0}
\]

A proof of $\neg A$ is a function from $A$ to the empty type.

\subsection{Weight Negation}

For a term $a : A \at{w}$, we can consider $\negw w$ as the ``complementary weight.''

In $\W = {[0,1]}$: if $P(A) = w$, then $P(\neg A) = 1 - w = \negw w$.

\begin{remark}[Relationship]
Type-theoretic negation ($A \to \mathbf{0}$) corresponds to weight negation in the following sense: if $a : A \at{w}$ with $w > \zero$, then there is no $f : A \to \mathbf{0} \at{\one}$.
\end{remark}

\section{Conditioning (Not Implication)}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Why Not Material Implication}

Material implication $A \to B := \negw A \vee B$ has paradoxes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathsf{False} \to \mathsf{Anything} = \mathsf{True}$ (ex falso)
    \item $\mathsf{Anything} \to \mathsf{True} = \mathsf{True}$
\end{itemize}

We use \textbf{conditioning} instead of implication.

\subsection{Chain Rule}

The fundamental equation is:
\[
P(A, B) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B) = P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)
\]

This is \textbf{multiplicative}---no division needed!

\begin{gather}
\infer[\textsc{Chain}]
  {\Gamma \vdash (a, b) : A \times B \at{u \cdot v}}
  {\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{u} \quad \Gamma \vdash b \mid a : B \at{v}}
\end{gather}

The conditional $b \mid a$ is \textbf{primitive}. We don't compute it by division; we assert it exists when the chain rule holds.

\subsection{Conditioning vs Implication}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l|l|l}
& \textbf{Implication} $A \to B$ & \textbf{Conditioning} $A \mid B$ \\
\hline
Formula & $\negw A \vee B$ & $P(A,B) / P(B)$ \\
Computable from & $A$ and $B$ alone & Joint $P(A,B)$ \\
False antecedent & ``True'' (lies) & Indeterminate (honest) \\
In logic & Yes & No \\
In probability & No & Yes \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Graded Ex Falso}

What about $P(A \mid B)$ when $P(B) = 0$?

In classical logic: $\bot \to A$ is always true (ex falso quodlibet).

In ProbTT: when $b : B \at{\zero}$:
\[
(a, b) : A \times B \at{\zero \cdot w} = \at{\zero}
\]
The conditional $a \mid b$ can have \emph{any} weight---the joint is already zero.

\textbf{Key insight}: Ex falso is the $w = 0$ limit of a continuous phenomenon:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $w = \one$: Evidence fully constrains conclusions
    \item $w = \epsilon$: Evidence weakly constrains conclusions
    \item $w = \zero$: Evidence imposes no constraint (ex falso)
\end{itemize}

Classical logic has only the endpoints; ProbTT has the entire spectrum.

\section{MLTT as the $\{\zero, \one\}$ Case}
%=============================================================================

\begin{theorem}[MLTT Embedding]
When $\W = \{\zero, \one\}$, ProbTT reduces to MLTT:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{\one}$ becomes $\Gamma \vdash a : A$
    \item $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{\zero}$ is vacuously satisfied
    \item Weight operations: $\one \cdot \one = \one$, else $\zero$
    \item Complement: $\negw\zero = \one$, $\negw\one = \zero$
    \item De Morgan OR: $w \vee v = \negw(\negw w \cdot \negw v)$ gives Boolean OR
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By inspection. All weight operations compute to Boolean.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Side-by-Side Comparison}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l|l|l}
\textbf{Rule} & \textbf{MLTT} & \textbf{ProbTT} \\
\hline
Variable & $x : A \vdash x : A$ & $x : A \vdash x : A \at{\one}$ \\
Application & $f\,a : B[a/x]$ & $f\,a : B[a/x] \at{w \cdot v}$ \\
Pair & $(a, b) : \Sigma A\,B$ & $(a, b) : \Sigma A\,B \at{w \cdot v}$ \\
Projection & $\pi_1 t : A$ & $\pi_1 t : A \at{w}$ \\
Case & $\mathsf{case}\,e\,l\,r : C$ & $\mathsf{case}\,e\,l\,r : C \at{w \cdot v}$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Key observation}: In MLTT, all judgments are implicitly at weight $\one$. Since $\one \cdot \one = \one$, weight multiplication is invisible. ProbTT makes this structure explicit and generalizes it to arbitrary weights.

\section{Contrast with Cost-Based Graded Types}
%=============================================================================

Graded type systems like Granule and Gerty use grades to track \emph{resource usage}. ProbTT uses weights to track \emph{inhabitation certainty}. These are philosophically distinct:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l|l|l}
& \textbf{Cost-Based (Granule)} & \textbf{ProbTT} \\
\hline
Algebraic structure & Semiring $(0, 1, +, \cdot)$ & De Morgan $(\zero, \one, \cdot, \negw)$ \\
Addition & Primitive (branch costs add) & Derived via De Morgan \\
Philosophy & Decorate MLTT with grades & Logic \emph{emerges} from grades \\
Implication & Material implication exists & Implication \emph{rejected} \\
Interpretation & Costs accumulate & Weights condition/constrain \\
MLTT relationship & MLTT is axiomatic & MLTT is \emph{derivative} \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Key distinction}: Cost-based systems ask ``how much resource does this computation use?'' ProbTT asks ``how certain are we that this type is inhabited?''

In cost-based grading:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $f : A \to B @ 2$ means ``$f$ uses 2 units of resource''
    \item Costs add at branches: if one branch costs 3 and another costs 5, total cost is at most 5
    \item The semiring axiom $0 + x = x$ reflects ``zero cost contributes nothing''
\end{itemize}

In ProbTT:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $f : A \to B \at{0.9}$ means ``$f$ produces $B$ with 90\% certainty''
    \item Weights multiply in composition: chaining 90\% and 80\% gives 72\%
    \item The De Morgan axiom $\zero \cdot w = \zero$ reflects ``impossible antecedent gives impossible conclusion''
\end{itemize}

\textbf{One-liner}: Costs measure \emph{computation performed}; weights measure \emph{truth surviving composition}.

\section{Related Structures}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{The Weight Algebra}

Our weight algebra is a \textbf{De Morgan algebra}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multiplication: $w \cdot v$ (conjunction)
    \item Complement: $\negw w$ (negation)
    \item De Morgan dual: $w \vee v = \negw(\negw w \cdot \negw v)$ (disjunction)
\end{itemize}

When $\W = {[0,1]}$ with $\negw w = 1 - w$, multiplication is the product and disjunction becomes $w + v - wv$.

\subsection{What ProbTT Adds}

Beyond the algebra:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Type theory}: $\Pi$, $\Sigma$, $\mathsf{Id}$, universes
    \item \textbf{Weighted judgments}: $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}$
    \item \textbf{Conditioning}: The chain rule as primitive
    \item \textbf{MLTT as limit}: The $\{\zero, \one\}$ case
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Distinction}

Pure De Morgan algebras have no notion of \textbf{conditioning}. We add it via the chain rule:
\[
P(A, B) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B)
\]
This is what distinguishes ProbTT from purely algebraic approaches.

\section{Categorical Semantics}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Weighted Category]
A \emph{$\W$-weighted category} has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Objects (types)
    \item For each $w : \W$, morphisms $\mathrm{Hom}_w(A, B)$
    \item Identity: $\mathrm{id}_A \in \mathrm{Hom}_\one(A, A)$
    \item Composition: $f \in \mathrm{Hom}_w(A, B)$, $g \in \mathrm{Hom}_v(B, C)$ gives $g \circ f \in \mathrm{Hom}_{w \cdot v}(A, C)$
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

When $\W = {[0,1]}$ with copy $\Delta : A \to A \times A$ and delete $! : A \to \mathbf{1}$, this becomes a \textbf{Markov category}.

\section{Incompleteness in ProbTT}
%=============================================================================

G\"odel's incompleteness theorems are often misunderstood as depending on classical logic. They do not. They require:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Effective syntax (proofs encodable as data)
    \item Enough expressivity to represent natural numbers and primitive recursion
    \item A notion of provability the system can talk about
\end{enumerate}

G\"odel does \emph{not} require classical logic, excluded middle, or bivalence. Incompleteness holds for intuitionistic arithmetic, type-theoretic systems, and constructive logics. Simply weakening logic does not escape G\"odel.

\subsection{Where G\"odel Relies on Sharp Truth}

G\"odel relies on one sharp notion: a binary ``provable / not provable'' predicate. This is where ProbTT shifts the ground.

\subsection{The G\"odel Sentence in ProbTT}

In classical logic, G\"odel constructs a sentence $G$ such that:
\[
G \leftrightarrow \text{``$G$ is not provable''}
\]
This relies on crisp negation and a sharp provability predicate.

In ProbTT:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Negation is $\negw w = 1 - w$
    \item Provability has a spectrum
\end{itemize}

The G\"odel sentence becomes not ``This statement is unprovable'' but something closer to ``This statement does not have weight $\one$.'' That is not a contradiction---it is almost tautological.

The fixed point becomes:
\[
w(G) = 1 - w(\mathsf{Provable}(G))
\]
This equation has many solutions in $[0,1]$. Instead of paradox, we get a \textbf{continuous equilibrium}---a fixed-point weight representing stable uncertainty.

\subsection{What G\"odel Still Implies}

\textbf{G\"odel still applies} in this sense: you cannot have full internal soundness, absolute internal consistency proofs, or total self-justification at weight $\one$. There will always be statements whose maximal achievable weight is $< \one$. That is G\"odel's shadow.

\textbf{G\"odel no longer implies} contradiction, undecidable-but-true sharp statements, or meta-theory collapse. Instead, it implies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Weight degradation
    \item Upper bounds on certainty
    \item Honest partial self-knowledge
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Comparison}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l|l}
\textbf{Classical logic} & \textbf{ProbTT} \\
\hline
Paradox $\Rightarrow$ explosion & Paradox $\Rightarrow$ uncertainty \\
Incompleteness = gap & Incompleteness = attenuation \\
Truth is brittle & Truth is elastic \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

G\"odel says: ``You cannot have everything true.''

ProbTT replies: ``Fine. Then I will track how true things are.''

\subsection{Internal Theorems}

You still cannot prove ``ProbTT is consistent'' at weight $\one$ inside ProbTT. But you can prove:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ``ProbTT is consistent with weight $\geq w$''
    \item ``Assuming axioms $A$ at weight $\one$, system properties hold at weight $\geq v$''
    \item ``Self-reference strictly lowers confidence''
\end{itemize}

These are meaningful, nontrivial internal theorems---strictly more expressive than what MLTT or classical logic can state.

\textbf{Bottom line}: G\"odel still applies, but it applies \emph{continuously}, not catastrophically. Incompleteness manifests as bounded certainty, not contradiction. This is a feature, not a bug.

\section{Summary}
%=============================================================================

\textbf{Weight algebra}: $(\zero, \one, \cdot, \negw, \leq)$ --- Product De Morgan

\textbf{Derived}: $w \vee v = \negw(\negw w \cdot \negw v)$ --- De Morgan OR

\textbf{Judgments}: $\Gamma \vdash a : A \at{w}$

\textbf{Key rule}: Weights multiply in elimination

\textbf{Conditioning}: Chain rule $P(A,B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B)$, no division

\textbf{MLTT}: The $\W = \{\zero, \one\}$ case

\textbf{Probability}: The $\W = {[0,1]}$ case

\textbf{Novelty}: De Morgan algebra + type theory + conditioning

\section{Open Problems}

\begin{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
