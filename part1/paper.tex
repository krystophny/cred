\begin{abstract}
We study the product De Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$ --- product conjunction,
probabilistic-sum disjunction, standard complement --- equipped with
chain-rule conditioning in the sense of R\'enyi and Popper.
Our main result is a \emph{complete congruence classification}: for
equivalence relations on $[0,1]$ compatible with $\mathbb{R}$-multiplication
and negation, the partition $\{0\},(0,1),\{1\}$ is the \emph{only}
non-trivial finite congruence --- no quotient with two, four, or more
classes exists.
The unique three-element quotient is the Kleene lattice, distinguishing
the product De Morgan triplet from MV-algebras, which have infinitely
many congruences.
Whether this rigidity persists under the weaker hypothesis of
$[0,1]$-multiplication alone remains open.
As context, we give Lean-verified proofs of known supporting facts:
uniqueness of the min copula among symmetric idempotent copulas,
path dependence of conditioning at evidence zero, and the collapse
homomorphism to the Kleene lattice.
All proofs are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

A \emph{credence} is a real number in $[0,1]$ representing degree of
belief~\cite{ramsey1931,definetti1937,jaynes2003}.
Probability theory builds on credences by adding a sigma-algebra, a measure,
and sigma-additivity.
Conditional probability is derived via division:
$P(A \mid B) = P(A \land B)/P(B)$ when $P(B) > 0$; when $P(B) = 0$, the
ratio is undefined.
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} and Popper~\cite{popper1959} proposed an
alternative: treat conditioning as \emph{primitive}, governed by the chain
rule $P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B) = P(A \land B)$ without invoking division.

We apply the same idea without a measure space:
keep only values in $[0,1]$, the complement $\negc c = 1 - c$, and the
chain rule
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.
The conjunction is the product t-norm, the disjunction its De Morgan dual
(the probabilistic sum), and together with complement they form the
\emph{product De Morgan triplet}~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998}.
In t-norm fuzzy logic, each left-continuous t-norm determines a unique
implication via \emph{residuation}: $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \otimes c \leq b\}$.
Since $0 \otimes c = 0 \leq b$ for all $c$ and $b$, every residuated
implication forces $0 \to b = 1$ --- \emph{ex falso quodlibet} is built
in~\cite{hajek1998}.
The chain rule avoids this: $c \cdot 0 = 0$ is satisfied by \emph{any}
value of $c$, so conditioning on impossible evidence is unconstrained.
This echoes de Finetti's three-valued conditional~\cite{definetti1936},
where conditioning on a false antecedent yields \emph{void} rather than
vacuous truth.
\'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021} developed the algebraic
and proof-theoretic foundations of de Finetti's conditional in detail;
their ``connexive'' conditional satisfies $\neg(A \to \neg A)$, which
is incompatible with ex falso.
Chain-rule conditioning achieves a similar effect by a different mechanism:
rather than adding a connexive axiom, it simply leaves $\cred(A \mid 0)$
unconstrained.

The resulting structure --- the product De Morgan triplet with chain-rule
conditioning --- connects probability-style reasoning on $[0,1]$ to
logic-style reasoning on the Kleene lattice
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ via a surjective homomorphism (the
\emph{Kleene collapse}, Section~\ref{sec:collapse}).
On the quotient, it recovers the shared truth tables of K$_3$, LP,
and RM3 --- and the min/max operations of possibility theory
(Remark~\ref{rem:possibility}).
Chain-rule conditioning motivates studying the product De Morgan triplet
as a unit: it is the specific choice of companion operations
(complement, product) that makes conditioning at zero unconstrained
rather than forcing ex falso.
The main contribution is showing that the collapse to the Kleene lattice
is the \emph{unique} non-trivial finite quotient: a complete congruence
classification (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence-main}).
The proof itself uses only product and negation; conditioning provides
the \emph{reason to care} about this particular signature.

\textbf{Main result.}
The \emph{complete congruence classification}
(Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence-main}):
for equivalence relations on $[0,1]$ compatible with
$\mathbb{R}$-multiplication and negation, the partition
$\{0\},(0,1),\{1\}$ is the only non-trivial finite congruence.
No quotient with two, four, or more classes exists.
This distinguishes the product De Morgan triplet from MV-algebras,
which have infinitely many congruences.

\textbf{Supporting results} (known results, newly Lean-verified).

\section{The Product De Morgan Triplet with Conditioning}
\label{sec:algebra}
%=============================================================================

This section defines the concrete structure we study: the product
De Morgan triplet (product t-norm, probabilistic sum, standard
complement)~\cite{klement2000,esteva2000} on $[0,1]$, combined with
chain-rule conditioning.
This is not an axiomatic class of algebras: all properties follow
from the specific arithmetic of $[0,1]$.
The structure is well-known in fuzzy
logic~\cite{hajek1998,klement2000,esteva2001};
Goguen~\cite{goguen1969} used the product t-norm as the conjunction
in his logic of inexact concepts.
The idea of primitive conditioning has precedents in
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} and Popper~\cite{popper1959}, though those
frameworks operate on measure spaces rather than bare values
(see Remark~\ref{rem:primitive-conditioning}).
The chain rule $\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is the sole structural constraint beyond the triplet.

\subsection{Primitive Operations}

\begin{definition}[Credence]
\label{def:credence}
A \emph{credence} is a real number $c \in [0, 1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Negation]
\label{def:neg}
The \emph{negation} of $c$ is $\negc c = 1 - c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Product]
\label{def:conj}
The \emph{product} of $c_1$ and $c_2$ is $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

The product is the product t-norm of fuzzy logic~\cite{klement2000}.
We write $\conjc$ for the algebraic product on $[0,1]$.
On the Kleene quotient $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, conjunction becomes
$\min$; we write $\min$ explicitly in that context to distinguish the
two operations.

\begin{remark}[Product vs.\ Joint]
\label{rem:product-vs-joint}
The product $c_1 \conjc c_2$ is an algebraic operation on credence
values, not a claim about the joint credence
$\cred(A \land B)$ of two propositions.
The joint depends on the dependence structure and lies in the
Fr\'echet-Hoeffding interval
$[\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0),\; \min(\cred(A), \cred(B))]$:
independence gives $\cred(A) \cdot \cred(B)$;
maximum positive dependence gives $\min(\cred(A), \cred(B))$;
maximum negative dependence gives $\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0)$.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Disjunction]
\label{def:disj}
The \emph{disjunction} is defined via De Morgan duality:
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = \negc(\negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2)
  = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

The triple $(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ is the \emph{product De Morgan
triplet}~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998}.

\begin{remark}[Credence vs.\ Probability]
\label{rem:not-probability}
Every probability space satisfies the complement and chain-rule constraints.
Probability adds a sigma-algebra, a measure determining $P(A \land B)$,
and sigma-additivity.
The structure studied here drops all of these
(Section~\ref{sec:probability}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Algebraic Properties}

% Lean: neg_neg, neg_zero, neg_one, conj_comm, conj_assoc, conj_one, conj_zero,
%       disj_comm, disj_assoc, disj_zero, disj_one, de_morgan_conj, de_morgan_disj
\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Properties]
\label{thm:algebraic}
For all $c, c_1, c_2, c_3 \in [0,1]$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\negc\negc c = c$ \hfill (involution)
\item $\negc 0 = 1$, $\negc 1 = 0$ \hfill (boundary)
\item $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_2 \conjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \conjc c_2) \conjc c_3 = c_1 \conjc (c_2 \conjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \conjc 1 = c$, $c \conjc 0 = 0$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_2 \disjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \disjc c_2) \disjc c_3 = c_1 \disjc (c_2 \disjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \disjc 0 = c$, $c \disjc 1 = 1$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $\negc(c_1 \conjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \disjc \negc c_2$,
  $\negc(c_1 \disjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2$ \hfill (De Morgan)
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Arithmetic on $[0,1]$.
Lean: \texttt{neg\_neg}, \texttt{neg\_zero}, \texttt{neg\_one},
\texttt{conj\_comm}, \texttt{conj\_assoc}, \texttt{conj\_one},
\texttt{conj\_zero},
\texttt{disj\_comm}, \texttt{disj\_assoc}, \texttt{disj\_zero},
\texttt{disj\_one},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_conj}, \texttt{de\_morgan\_disj}.
\end{proof}

The structure is two dual commutative monoids connected by De Morgan
duality; it is not a De Morgan algebra since $\conjc$ is not
idempotent (Lemma~\ref{thm:idempotence}).

\subsection{Non-Idempotence and Non-Distributivity}

% Lean: conj_idempotent_iff
\begin{lemma}[Idempotence Characterization]
\label{thm:idempotence}
$c \conjc c = c$ if and only if $c \in \{0, 1\}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 = c$ iff $c(c-1) = 0$.
Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff}.
\end{proof}

% Lean: conj_disj_not_distrib
\begin{lemma}[Non-Distributivity]
\label{thm:nondistrib}
The product does not distribute over disjunction.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
At $c_1 = c_2 = c_3 = \tfrac{1}{2}$:
$\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc (\tfrac{1}{2} \disjc \tfrac{1}{2})
= \tfrac{3}{8}$, but
$(\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc \tfrac{1}{2}) \disjc
(\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc \tfrac{1}{2})
= \tfrac{7}{16}$.
Lean: \texttt{conj\_disj\_not\_distrib}.
\end{proof}

\section{The Uniqueness Theorem}
\label{sec:uniqueness}
%=============================================================================

That the min copula is the unique symmetric, idempotent copula on
$[0,1]$ is classical~\cite[Theorem~2.3.5]{nelsen2006}.
We give a self-contained proof, verified in Lean, and draw the
consequence for many-valued logic:
Grzegorzewski~\cite{grzegorzewski2013} showed that copula-derived
implications connect to fuzzy implications; the uniqueness forces the
Kleene conjunction as the only truth-functional joint compatible with
Bayesian conditioning and idempotence.

\subsection{Requirements}

We impose four requirements on a truth-functional joint $j(a,b)$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item \textbf{Truth-functional}: $j(a,b)$ depends on $a$ and $b$ alone.
\item \textbf{Bayes-consistent}:
  $\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(B \mid A) \cdot \cred(A)$.
\item \textbf{Non-trivial}: $\cred(A \mid B) \neq \cred(A)$ in general.
\item \textbf{Idempotent}: $j(a,a) = a$.
\end{enumerate}
Independence $j(a,b) = ab$ fails (iii) and (iv);
\L ukasiewicz $j(a,b) = \max(a+b-1,0)$ fails (iv).
Lean: \texttt{prod\_trivial\_conditioning},
\texttt{luk\_not\_idempotent}.

\subsection{Uniqueness}

% Lean: min_copula_unique, copula_idempotent_unique
\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of Truth-Functional Conjunction]
\label{thm:uniqueness-main}
\label{thm:min-copula-unique}
Let $j : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ be a symmetric, idempotent copula
(symmetry, idempotence, zero boundary, unit marginal, 2-increasing).
Then $j(a,b) = \min(a,b)$ for all $a,b \in [0,1]$.
Moreover, $\min$ is non-trivial: $\min(a,b)/b \neq a$ in general.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Upper bound: 2-increasing on $[0,a] \times [b,1]$ with unit marginal
gives $j(a,b) \leq a$; by symmetry $j(a,b) \leq b$.
Lower bound: assume $a \leq b$.
Apply 2-increasing to $[0,a] \times [a,b]$:
$j(a,b) - j(a,a) - j(0,b) + j(0,a) \geq 0$.
By idempotence and zero boundary: $j(a,b) \geq a = \min(a,b)$.

Lean: \texttt{copula\_idempotent\_unique},
\texttt{min\_copula\_unique}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Comonotonicity]
The minimum joint is the \emph{comonotonicity copula}: the extremal case
where all random variables are increasing functions of a single latent
variable~\cite{dhaene2002,nelsen2006}.
\end{remark}

\section{Conditioning via the Chain Rule}
\label{sec:conditioning}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Conditioning Structure]
\label{def:conditioning}
A \emph{conditioning structure} for
$(\text{joint}, \text{evidence}) \in [0,1]^2$
consists of $\text{condCred} \in [0,1]$ satisfying
$\text{condCred} \conjc \text{evidence} = \text{joint}$.
In propositional notation:
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.
Such a structure exists if and only if
$\text{joint} \leq \text{evidence}$
(Proposition~\ref{thm:existence}); when $\text{evidence} = 0$, the
constraint forces $\text{joint} = 0$ but leaves $\text{condCred}$
unconstrained.
\end{definition}

The joint is supplied as input, not computed from marginals.
H\'ajek, Godo, and Esteva~\cite{hajek1995} used the chain rule constraint
as a technical device within fuzzy logic; here it is the defining constraint.
Jeffrey~\cite{jeffrey1965} generalized Bayesian conditioning to uncertain
evidence via a mixture formula, also using the chain rule as the primitive
mechanism.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed consistent
plausible reasoning must be isomorphic to probability; chain-rule
conditioning relaxes Cox's requirement that the conditional be a function.

\textbf{Evidence = 0.}
The chain rule gives $c \conjc 0 = 0$ for all $c$: the conditional is
\emph{unconstrained}\label{thm:noexfalso}.
This is an elementary consequence of the definition --- any $c$ satisfies
the equation --- but the design choice it reflects is significant:
the chain rule does not force $\cred(A \mid 0) = 1$, so it is
\emph{consistent} with ex falso but does not \emph{entail} it.
(Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}.)

\begin{remark}[Comparison with Primitive-Conditioning Frameworks]
\label{rem:primitive-conditioning}
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} and Popper~\cite{popper1959} also take
conditioning as primitive, but in different settings.
R\'enyi's conditional probability spaces define $P(A \mid B)$ as a
function on pairs where $B$ ranges over a designated family of
``permissible'' conditioning events, excluding measure-zero sets from
the domain rather than leaving the conditional unconstrained.
Popper functions similarly make $P(A \mid B)$ primitive but impose
axioms that force $P(A \mid B) = 1$ when $B$ entails $A$, retaining
a form of logical consequence.
Dubois and Prade~\cite{dubois1988} define conditional possibility as
$\Pi(A \mid B) = \Pi(A \cap B)$ when $\Pi(A \cap B) < \Pi(B)$ and
$1$ otherwise, which also avoids division but constrains the
zero-evidence case.
Our structure differs from all three: it works on bare values in
$[0,1]$ without a measure space or event algebra, and it leaves the
zero-evidence case genuinely unconstrained --- no value is preferred,
excluded, or forced.
This is the strictest form of ``no ex falso'': not even a default
value is supplied.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Path Dependence at Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:path-dependence}

The following observation is elementary but clarifies why conditioning
at evidence zero cannot be resolved by a limiting argument.
Under the min-copula joint $\min(a, b)$, the conditional for $b > 0$ is
$\min(a,b)/b$.
As $b \to 0$, the limit is path-dependent.

% Lean: path_dep_fixed_a, path_dep_proportional, path_dep_square
\begin{proposition}[Path Dependence at Evidence Zero]
\label{thm:path-dependence}
The limit of $\min(a,b)/b$ as $b \to 0$ is path-dependent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item Fixed $a > 0$: limit is $1$.
\item $a(b) = \lambda b$, $\lambda \in (0,1]$: limit is $\lambda$.
\item $a(b) = b^\alpha$, $\alpha > 1$: limit is $0$.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved; ex falso requires an added convention.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~For small $b < a$, $\min(a,b)/b = 1$.
(ii)~$\min(\lambda b, b)/b = \lambda$.
(iii)~$b^{\alpha-1} \to 0$.
Lean: \texttt{path\_dep\_fixed\_a}, \texttt{path\_dep\_proportional},
\texttt{path\_dep\_square} (case $\alpha = 2$),
\texttt{path\_any\_positive\_value}.
(The general case (iii) for $\alpha > 1$ is a straightforward
generalization not formalized in Lean.)
\end{proof}

\subsection{Existence and Uniqueness}

\begin{proposition}[Existence and Uniqueness of Conditioning]
\label{thm:existence}
\label{thm:uniqueness}
For $\mathrm{evidence} > 0$: a conditioning structure exists iff
$\mathrm{joint} \leq \mathrm{evidence}$, and is then unique:
$\mathrm{condCred} = \mathrm{joint} / \mathrm{evidence}$.
Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_mk}, \texttt{conditioning\_unique}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Implication Comparison}

When evidence $a > 0$ and joint $= \min(a,b)$, the chain rule gives
the conditional $\min(b/a,\; 1)$ --- the product residuated implication
$a \to b$ of H\'ajek's Product Logic ($\Pi$-logic)~\cite{hajek1998}.
The product De Morgan triplet and $\Pi$-logic share the same t-norm
but differ in the status of implication:
$\Pi$-logic defines $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$
(residuation), which forces $0 \to b = 1$ for all $b$ (ex falso);
chain-rule conditioning leaves $\cred(A \mid 0)$ unconstrained.
Thus the product residuated implication appears as a special case of
chain-rule conditioning (when evidence is positive), not as the
primitive.

\subsection{Bayes Consistency}

\begin{definition}[Bayes Consistency]
\label{def:bayes}
A binary arrow $f$ is \emph{Bayes-consistent} when
$f(a, b) \cdot a = f(b, a) \cdot b$ for all $a, b$.
\end{definition}

The product residuated implication is Bayes-consistent
(Lean: \texttt{prod\_resid\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).
The G\"odel implication fails on $[0,1]$
(Lean: \texttt{godel\_not\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).
The Kleene material conditional fails even on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$

\subsection{Relationship to Probability}
\label{sec:probability}

Every probability space satisfies the chain rule and complement axioms.
Probability adds a sigma-algebra, a countably additive measure, and
sigma-additivity.
The product De Morgan triplet drops all of these.
At evidence zero, probability is undefined; chain-rule conditioning is
unconstrained.

\section{Collapse to the Kleene Lattice}
\label{sec:collapse}
%=============================================================================

The partition $\{0\}, (0,1), \{1\}$ is a congruence on the product De Morgan
triplet.
The induced quotient is the Kleene lattice.
Ferm\"uller~\cite{fermuller2014} reached the same conclusion from game
semantics.

Define the collapse:
\[
\text{collapse}(c) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } c = 0, \\
\tfrac{1}{2} & \text{if } 0 < c < 1, \\
1 & \text{if } c = 1.
\end{cases}
\]

% Lean: collapse_neg, collapse_conj, collapse_disj, collapse_surjective
\begin{theorem}[Collapse Homomorphism]
\label{thm:collapse}
The collapse is a surjective homomorphism from
$([0,1], \negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ to
$(\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}, \mathrm{neg}, \min, \max)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Case analysis.
Negation: $0 \mapsto 1$, $1 \mapsto 0$; if $0 < c < 1$ then
$0 < 1-c < 1$.
Conjunction: $0$ absorbs, $1$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Disjunction: $1$ absorbs, $0$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_disj}.
\end{proof}

The target is the Kleene lattice~\cite{kleene1952}, underlying
K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952}, LP~\cite{priest1979}, and
RM3~\cite{meyer1972}.

\begin{remark}[Connection to Possibility Theory (aside)]
\label{rem:possibility}
The Kleene quotient's operations --- $\mathrm{conj} = \min$,
$\mathrm{disj} = \max$ --- are exactly the operations of possibility
theory~\cite{dubois1988}.
A possibility measure satisfies
$\Pi(A \cup B) = \max(\Pi(A), \Pi(B))$; the dual necessity measure
satisfies $N(A) = 1 - \Pi(\bar{A})$, matching the Kleene negation
$\mathrm{neg}(c) = 1 - c$.
The collapse homomorphism thus maps the product De Morgan triplet
(probability-compatible) to the possibility/necessity structure
(logic-compatible), providing an algebraic bridge between probability
and possibility theory.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Impossibility Results}

% Lean: no_boolean_neg_retraction
\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to Classical Logic]
\label{prop:no-classical}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ has no solution
in $\{0, 1\}$.
Lean: \texttt{no\_boolean\_neg\_retraction}.
\end{proof}

% Lean: no_godel_collapse
\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to G\"odel Logic]
\label{prop:no-godel}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ preserves G\"odel
negation~\cite{godel1932} (which has no fixed point).
Lean: \texttt{no\_godel\_collapse}.
\end{proposition}

% Lean: no_luk_collapse
\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to \L ukasiewicz Logic]
\label{prop:no-luk}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ preserves both negation
and \L ukasiewicz conjunction~\cite{lukasiewicz1920}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Negation forces $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Pick $c = \sqrt{1/2}$; then $c \conjc c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
But $\mathrm{conj}_{\text{\L}}(v,v) \in \{0, 1\}$ for all
$v \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, so no preimage of $\tfrac{1}{2}$ exists.
Lean: \texttt{no\_luk\_collapse}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Uniqueness of the Kleene Quotient}

% Lean: three_element_quotient_unique
\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the Three-Element Quotient]
\label{thm:quotient-unique}
Any surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to S$ ($|S|=3$) preserving negation
and conjunction yields the Kleene lattice (up to relabeling).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be the unique negation fixed point of $S$.
The remaining elements $\varphi(0)$, $\varphi(1)$ form the 2-cycle.
The conjunction table is then forced: $\varphi(0)$ absorbs, $\varphi(1)$
is identity,
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) \conjc \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) =
\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ (since $\tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$).
This is Kleene $\min$.
Lean: \texttt{three\_element\_quotient\_unique}.
\end{proof}

% Lean: zero_equiv_forces_trivial
\begin{proposition}[Zero Equivalence Forces Collapse]
\label{thm:zero-collapse}
If a congruence identifies $0$ with some $\varepsilon > 0$, it identifies
all elements.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
From $0 \sim \varepsilon$: $1 \sim 1 - \varepsilon$ (negation);
$x \sim x(1 - \varepsilon)$ for all $x$ (multiply); iterate to get
$x \sim x(1-\varepsilon)^n \to 0$, then $x \sim 0$.
Lean: \texttt{zero\_equiv\_forces\_trivial}.
\end{proof}

\section{Complete Congruence Classification}
\label{sec:congruence}
%=============================================================================

The results of Section~\ref{sec:collapse} combine into a complete
classification.

\begin{theorem}[Complete Congruence Classification]
\label{thm:congruence-main}
Let $\sim$ be an equivalence relation on $\mathbb{R}$ that is compatible
with multiplication ($a \sim b$ and $c \sim d$ imply $ac \sim bd$) and
with negation ($a \sim b$ implies $1 - a \sim 1 - b$).
If $\sim$ is non-trivial when restricted to $[0,1]$, then the only
non-trivial finite quotient of $[0,1]$ is the three-element Kleene
lattice with partition $\{0\},(0,1),\{1\}$.
\end{theorem}

The hypothesis that $\sim$ is compatible with $\mathbb{R}$-multiplication
(not just multiplication on $[0,1]$) is essential for part~(4): the
scaling trick multiplies by $(a^i)^{-1} > 1$, which lies outside $[0,1]$.
This hypothesis is natural for two reasons.
First, the product t-norm on $[0,1]$ is the restriction of
$\mathbb{R}$-multiplication; a congruence that respects the ambient
field inherits the scaling and inversion structure that the proof
requires.
Second, $[0,1]$ is not closed under multiplicative inverses --- there is
no internal operation that ``undoes'' multiplication by a value in $(0,1)$
--- so restricting to $[0,1]$-multiplication alone removes the algebraic
leverage (cancellation, scaling) that distinguishes the product t-norm
from, say, the minimum t-norm.
The $\mathbb{R}$-compatibility hypothesis makes this leverage explicit.

Whether part~(4) holds under the weaker hypothesis of
$[0,1]$-multiplication alone is an open question.
A counterexample would require an equivalence relation on $[0,1]$
compatible with multiplication and negation that has four or more finite
classes but does not extend to a congruence on $(\mathbb{R}, \cdot)$.
We know of no such example, but the proof technique above does not
apply without inverses.
The Lean formalization uses $\mathbb{R}$-multiplication explicitly
(\texttt{CredCongruence.conj\_compat}).

\begin{proof}
We establish four facts.

\textbf{(1) $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ are singletons.}
If $0 \sim \varepsilon$ for $\varepsilon > 0$,
Proposition~\ref{thm:zero-collapse} collapses everything.
By negation duality, the same holds for $\{1\}$.

\textbf{(2) No 2-element quotient exists.}
Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}: no negation-preserving surjection
$[0,1] \to \{0,1\}$.

\textbf{(3) Any 3-element quotient is Kleene.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}.

\textbf{(4) No quotient with $n \geq 4$ classes exists.}
Pick $a = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
By the pigeonhole principle on $n$ classes, there exist $0 \leq i < j \leq n$
with $a^i \sim a^j$.
Write $a^j = a^i \cdot a^p$ where $p = j - i \geq 1$.
Multiplying both sides by $(a^i)^{-1}$ (using $\mathbb{R}$-multiplication
compatibility) yields $1 \sim a^p$.
Applying negation compatibility gives $0 \sim 1 - a^p$.
But $0 < a^p < 1$, so $1 - a^p > 0$, contradicting~(1).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Lean verification]
All four parts are fully Lean-verified in \texttt{Cred/Congruence.lean}:
\texttt{singleton\_zero}, \texttt{singleton\_one},
\texttt{no\_two\_element\_quotient},
\texttt{unique\_three\_element\_quotient},
\texttt{no\_four\_or\_more\_classes}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Comparison with MV-algebras and BL-algebras]
\label{rem:mv-comparison}
The congruence structure of $[0,1]$ varies sharply with the choice of
signature, as summarized below.

\smallskip
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Signature} & \textbf{Congruences on $[0,1]$} & \textbf{Reference} \\
\midrule
MV: $(\max(a{+}b{-}1,0),\; 1{-}(\cdot))$ & Infinitely many
  & \cite{cignoli2000,mundici2011} \\
BL (product): $({\cdot},\; {\to})$ & Simple (none)
  & \cite{hajek1998,agliano2003} \\
De Morgan: $({\cdot},\; 1{-}(\cdot))$ & Exactly one finite
  & Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence-main} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}
\smallskip

In MV-algebras~\cite{cignoli2000,mundici2011}, Chang's completeness
theorem gives a rich congruence lattice: every MV-algebra is a subdirect
product of totally ordered ones, and the standard MV-algebra on $[0,1]$
has infinitely many congruences (one for each rational).
In BL-algebras~\cite{hajek1998,agliano2003} (the algebraic semantics of
Basic Logic), the standard product algebra $[0,1]_\Pi$ is
\emph{simple}: it has no non-trivial BL-congruences at all.
The residuated implication $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$
is so constraining that no quotient survives.

Our signature --- $({\cdot},\, 1{-}(\cdot))$ --- sits between these
extremes.
Replacing residuation with complement removes enough structure to allow
one non-trivial finite congruence (the Kleene quotient) but not more.
The complement's involutivity ($\negc\negc c = c$) forces the
three-element structure via the negation fixed point at $\tfrac{1}{2}$;
the product's non-idempotence prevents any coarser finite partition
from being well-defined.
The rigidity is thus a property of the product t-norm paired with
involutive negation, modulated by which companion operation
(residuation or complement) is present.
This suggests a broader question: for a fixed continuous t-norm, how
does the congruence lattice of $(T, \negc)$ relate to the congruence
lattice of $(T, \to_T)$?
The product case gives one data point; the \L ukasiewicz case (where
MV-algebras and the De Morgan triplet coincide) gives another.
\end{remark}

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$, equipped with chain-rule
conditioning, has a rigid quotient structure.
The main result (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence-main}) shows that the
partition $\{0\},(0,1),\{1\}$ is the unique non-trivial finite
congruence (under $\mathbb{R}$-multiplication compatibility),
distinguishing the product De Morgan triplet from MV-algebras and
forcing the Kleene lattice as the sole finite quotient.
Whether this rigidity persists under the weaker hypothesis of
$[0,1]$-multiplication alone remains open.

The supporting results --- uniqueness of the min copula
(Theorem~\ref{thm:uniqueness-main}, classical~\cite{nelsen2006}),
path dependence (Proposition~\ref{thm:path-dependence}), and the
collapse homomorphism (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}) --- are known or
elementary; our contribution is the Lean verification and the
congruence classification.
Open problems: the congruence classification under $[0,1]$-only
multiplication; a general theory of how congruence lattices vary
across signatures sharing the same t-norm; and whether the
classification constrains the space of definable consequence relations.

\section{Preliminaries: T-Norms, Copulas, and Coherence}
\label{app:prelim}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[T-norm]
\label{def:tnorm}
A \emph{triangular norm} (t-norm) is a binary operation
$\otimes : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ that is commutative, associative,
monotone, and has identity~$1$~\cite{klement2000}.
The three basic continuous t-norms are:
G\"odel $\min(a, b)$,
product $a \cdot b$, and
\L ukasiewicz $\max(a + b - 1, 0)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Residuated Implication]
\label{def:resid}
Given a t-norm $\otimes$:
$a \to b = \sup\{c \in [0,1] : a \otimes c \leq b\}$.
For every t-norm, $0 \to b = 1$~\cite{hajek1998}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Copula]
\label{def:copula}
A \emph{copula} is $C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ with boundary conditions
($C(0,b) = C(a,0) = 0$, $C(1,b) = b$, $C(a,1) = a$) and the
2-increasing property~\cite{nelsen2006}.
Fr\'echet-Hoeffding bounds:
$\max(a + b - 1, 0) \leq C(a, b) \leq \min(a, b)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Coherence]
\label{def:dutchbook}
A credence assignment is \emph{coherent} if no combination of bets
guarantees a net loss~\cite{ramsey1931,definetti1937}:
$\cred(A) + \cred(\negc A) = 1$ and
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.
\end{definition}
