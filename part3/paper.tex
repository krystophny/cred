\begin{abstract}
We study the foundations layer of the credence algebra: values in
$[0,1]$ with negation $\negc c = 1 - c$, product conjunction
$a \conjc b = ab$, the De Morgan dual disjunction
$a \disjc b = a + b - ab$, and \emph{external} conditioning given by the
admissible set $\Cond(j,e) = \{c \in [0,1] : c\,e = j\}$ of the chain
rule.
Classical logic embeds exactly in the crisp fragment $\{0,1\}$;
the unique divergence is the empty antecedent, where conditioning is
underdetermined rather than vacuously true.
Self-reference is classified by solution sets: the liar and the Russell
scalar have the singleton $\{\tfrac12\}$, the truth-teller has all of
$[0,1]$ (the same set as zero-evidence conditioning), and Curry
equations acquire the solution $\sqrt b$ only after a product residuum
is added.
The Curry block theorem proves that no total operation on $[0,1]$
carries modus ponens, conditional proof, and contraction together;
this is why the language has no internal conditional.
Threshold consequence $\Gamma \models_t \varphi$ has sharp bounds:
explosion has a countermodel exactly for $0 < t \leq \tfrac12$, and
excluded middle holds exactly for $t \leq \tfrac34$.
A labelled sequent calculus with external conditioning side judgments
is sound for these relations and derives no ex falso.
On top sits an arithmetic $\Sigma_1$ layer: a standard model of
Robinson~$Q$ in which every axiom is certain, $\Sigma_1$ witness
completeness, and a full arithmetization chain (pairing, modulo,
G\"odel $\beta$, sequence access) ending in an explicit object formula
that represents a course-of-values recursive tree predicate.
The second-incompleteness boundary is structural: the internal L\"ob
arrow would need exactly the package the Curry block excludes.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib.
The wider setting, in which this opening commitment is seen as
irreducible across probability, logic, and foundations, is developed
in Part~4.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Russell's paradox asks for a set $R$ with
$R \in R \Leftrightarrow R \notin R$.
The classical contradiction comes from forcing the membership claim to
be true or false.
If instead the claim carries a credence $c \in [0,1]$ and negation is
the complement $\negc c = 1 - c$, the same self-reference is one scalar
equation,
\[
  c = 1 - c,
\]
with the unique solution $c = \tfrac12$ (\Cref{lem:neg-fixed}).
Nothing explodes: the equation has a solution, the solution is unique,
and no unrelated sentence inherits any value from it.

This calculation is the entry point to a larger separation, developed
in Parts~1 and~2 of this series~\cite{part1,part2} and pushed here into
foundations.
The value algebra is the product De Morgan
triplet~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998} on $[0,1]$:
\[
  \negc c = 1 - c, \qquad
  a \conjc b = ab, \qquad
  a \disjc b = \negc(\negc a \conjc \negc b) = a + b - ab.
\]
Conditioning is not a connective of the object language.
It is the admissible set of the chain rule,
\[
  \Cond(j, e) = \{c \in [0,1] : c\,e = j\},
\]
following the primitive-conditioning tradition of
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955}, Popper~\cite{popper1959}, and
de Finetti~\cite{definetti1936}.
For $e > 0$ and $j \leq e$ this set is the singleton $\{j/e\}$; for
$e = j = 0$ it is all of $[0,1]$; for $j > e$ it is empty
(\Cref{thm:cond-trichotomy}).
Impossible evidence never licenses an arbitrary conclusion, because the
equation $c \cdot 0 = 0$ constrains nothing.

This paper proves what remains of classical foundations once values,
consequence, and conditioning are kept apart.
The results form five groups.

\textbf{Crisp fragment} (\Cref{sec:crisp}).
The Boolean embedding $\mathsf{false} \mapsto 0$,
$\mathsf{true} \mapsto 1$ is a homomorphism, formula evaluation
commutes with it, and classical, certainty, and positivity consequence
coincide on crisp valuations
(\Cref{thm:crisp-eval,thm:crisp-embedding,thm:crisp-coincide}).
The single divergence is the empty antecedent: classical logic assigns
vacuous truth, the chain rule returns the whole interval
(\Cref{thm:material-divergence}).

\textbf{Solution sets} (\Cref{sec:solutions}).
A self-reference pattern is an operator $\Phi : [0,1] \to [0,1]$ and
its meaning is the fixed-point set $\Sol(\Phi)$.
The liar and the Russell scalar give $\{\tfrac12\}$, the truth-teller
gives $[0,1] = \Cond(0,0)$, and Curry equations are solvable
(by $\sqrt b$) only after an internal residuum is added
(\Cref{thm:liar-sol,thm:truth-teller,thm:russell,thm:curry-sqrt}).

\textbf{Graded comprehension} (\Cref{sec:comprehension}).
Graded predicates with pointwise operations and $\inf$/$\sup$
quantifiers satisfy De Morgan duality and recover classical
quantification on crisp predicates
(\Cref{thm:quant-duality,thm:crisp-quant}).

\textbf{The Curry block and the proof layer}
(\Cref{sec:curry,sec:proof}).
Modus ponens and conditional proof force any internal conditional to be
the product residuum on positive antecedents
(\Cref{thm:resid-unique}), and the residuum violates contraction
(\Cref{prop:no-contraction}); hence no total conditional carries all
three (\Cref{thm:curry-block}).
Threshold consequence has the sharp bounds $t \leq \tfrac12$
(explosion countermodel) and $t \leq \tfrac34$ (excluded middle), and a
labelled calculus with external conditioning judgments is sound and
non-explosive
(\Cref{thm:explosion-sharp,thm:em-sharp,thm:seq-sound,thm:no-ex-falso}).

\textbf{The arithmetic $\Sigma_1$ layer} (\Cref{sec:arith}).
A standard model of Robinson~$Q$ lives inside the graded semantics:
crisp equality, quantifiers as genuine $\inf$/$\sup$ over $\Nat$, every
axiom certain, and $\bot$ not provable at certainty
(\Cref{thm:axioms-one,thm:q-con}).
Quantifier-free formulas evaluate crisply, $\Sigma_1$ truths have
numeral witnesses (\Cref{thm:sigma1}), and the classical
arithmetization chain~\cite{godel1931,fflfoundation} is carried out in
full: pairing, modulo, G\"odel's $\beta$-function, single-code sequence
access, and an explicit object formula representing a course-of-values
recursive tree predicate
(\Cref{thm:pair-rep,thm:beta-rep,thm:tree-beta,thm:tree-rep}).
The boundary of the program is \Cref{sec:loeb}: the second
incompleteness theorem does not internalize, because the L\"ob arrow
would need the exact package the Curry block excludes
(\Cref{thm:no-loeb}).

All theorems are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib; each result
cites its Lean name once.
\Cref{app:lean} maps the development.
The claim is deliberately local: the framework resolves certain
self-reference equations and keeps classical proof on the crisp
fragment.
It does not solve G\"odel incompleteness, replace
Kripke~\cite{kripke1975} or fuzzy set theory~\cite{takeuti1984}, or
select a canonical posterior at zero evidence.

\section{The Value Algebra and External Conditioning}
\label{sec:algebra}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Credence]
\label{def:credence}
A \emph{credence} is a real number $c \in [0,1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Operations]
\label{def:operations}
For credences $a, b, c$:
\[
  \negc c = 1 - c, \qquad
  a \conjc b = a \cdot b, \qquad
  a \disjc b = \negc(\negc a \conjc \negc b) = a + b - ab.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Triplet identities]
\label{prop:triplet}
Negation is involutive, $\conjc$ and $\disjc$ are commutative and
associative with identities $1$ and $0$ and annihilators $0$ and $1$,
and the De Morgan laws hold:
$\negc(a \conjc b) = \negc a \disjc \negc b$ and
$\negc(a \disjc b) = \negc a \conjc \negc b$.
The product is idempotent only at the boundary: $c \conjc c = c$ iff
$c \in \{0,1\}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
All identities are polynomial identities on $[0,1]$; for idempotence,
$c^2 = c$ iff $c(c-1) = 0$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{neg\_neg} through \texttt{de\_morgan\_disj},
\texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff}.)
\end{proof}

The fixed-point structure of negation drives everything that follows.

\begin{lemma}[Unique negation fixed point]
\label{lem:neg-fixed}
For a credence $c$:
\[
  \negc c = c \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad c = \tfrac12 .
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
If $1 - c = c$ then $2c = 1$, so $c = \tfrac12$.
Conversely $1 - \tfrac12 = \tfrac12$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{liar\_fixed\_point},
\texttt{neg\_fixed\_point\_unique}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Admissible conditioning set]
\label{def:cond}
For credences $j$ (joint) and $e$ (evidence), the \emph{admissible set}
of conditional credences is the solution set of the chain rule
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \wedge B)$:
\[
  \Cond(j, e) = \{c \in [0,1] : c \conjc e = j\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The joint $j$ is supplied as data; it is not computed from marginals.
There is no formula constructor for $\condc{A}{B}$: conditioning is a
relation between values, external to the object language.

\begin{theorem}[Trichotomy of conditioning]
\label{thm:cond-trichotomy}
For all credences $j, e$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item if $e > 0$ and $j \leq e$, then $\Cond(j,e) = \{j/e\}$;
\item $\Cond(0,0) = [0,1]$;
\item $\Cond(j,e) \neq \varnothing$ iff $j \leq e$; in particular
  $\Cond(j,0) = \varnothing$ for $j > 0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
(i) For $e > 0$ the equation $ce = j$ has the unique real solution
$c = j/e$, and $0 \leq j/e \leq 1$ because $0 \leq j \leq e$.
(ii) $c \cdot 0 = 0$ holds for every $c$.
(iii) If $ce = j$ then $j = ce \leq 1 \cdot e = e$.
Conversely, for $e > 0$ take $c = j/e$; for $e = 0$ the bound forces
$j = 0$ and $c = 0$ works.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{cond\_singleton\_of\_pos},
\texttt{cond\_zero\_zero\_univ}, \texttt{cond\_nonempty\_iff}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[No ex falso by design]
\label{rem:no-ex-falso-design}
Residuated implications of t-norm logic force $0 \to b = 1$ for all
$b$~\cite{hajek1998}: ex falso is built into residuation.
Case (ii) of \Cref{thm:cond-trichotomy} replaces that convention by
underdetermination: at zero evidence every conditional value is
admissible and none is selected.
Read as imprecise probability, $\Cond(j,e)$ is a credal set of
posteriors.
\end{remark}

\section{The Crisp Fragment}
\label{sec:crisp}
%=============================================================================

Formulas are built from atoms by $\neg$, $\wedge$, $\vee$ and evaluated
by
\[
  \evalc{v}{\neg\varphi} = \negc \evalc{v}{\varphi}, \qquad
  \evalc{v}{\varphi \wedge \psi} =
    \evalc{v}{\varphi} \conjc \evalc{v}{\psi}, \qquad
  \evalc{v}{\varphi \vee \psi} =
    \evalc{v}{\varphi} \disjc \evalc{v}{\psi},
\]
for a valuation $v$ of atoms into credences.
A valuation (or predicate) is \emph{crisp} when its values lie in
$\{0,1\}$.

\begin{definition}[Boolean embedding]
\label{def:embed}
$\iota : \Bool \to [0,1]$ with $\iota(\mathsf{false}) = 0$ and
$\iota(\mathsf{true}) = 1$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Embedding is a homomorphism]
\label{prop:embed-hom}
For all $b, b_1, b_2 \in \Bool$:
$\negc \iota(b) = \iota(\lnot b)$,
$\iota(b_1) \conjc \iota(b_2) = \iota(b_1 \wedge b_2)$, and
$\iota(b_1) \disjc \iota(b_2) = \iota(b_1 \vee b_2)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Four cases each: e.g.\ $1 \conjc 1 = 1$, $1 \conjc 0 = 0 \conjc 0 = 0$,
and $a \disjc b = a + b - ab \in \{0,1\}$ matches Boolean disjunction
on $\{0,1\}$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{embed\_neg}, \texttt{embed\_conj},
\texttt{embed\_disj}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Evaluation commutes with the embedding]
\label{thm:crisp-eval}
For every Boolean valuation $v$ and formula $\varphi$,
\[
  \evalc{\iota \circ v}{\varphi} = \iota\bigl(\evalc{v}{\varphi}^{\Bool}\bigr),
\]
where $\evalc{v}{\cdot}^{\Bool}$ is classical two-valued evaluation.
In particular every crisp valuation gives every formula a crisp value.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Induction on $\varphi$; each step is one clause of
\Cref{prop:embed-hom}.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{crisp\_eval\_eq}, \texttt{evalCred\_crisp}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Crisp embedding of validity]
\label{thm:crisp-embedding}
A formula $\varphi$ is a classical tautology iff
$\evalc{w}{\varphi} = 1$ for every crisp valuation $w$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Every crisp valuation factors as $\iota \circ v$ for a Boolean $v$;
apply \Cref{thm:crisp-eval} in both directions and use that
$\iota(b) = 1$ iff $b = \mathsf{true}$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{crisp\_embedding},
\texttt{crisp\_exists\_bool}.)
\end{proof}

Three consequence relations restrict to the crisp fragment:
\emph{classical} consequence (Boolean valuations, truth in and out),
\emph{certainty} consequence ($\evalc{w}{p} = 1$ for all premises
forces $\evalc{w}{\varphi} = 1$), and \emph{positivity} consequence
($\evalc{w}{p} > 0$ for all premises forces
$\evalc{w}{\varphi} > 0$).

\begin{theorem}[Consequence coincidence on crisp valuations]
\label{thm:crisp-coincide}
On crisp valuations the three relations coincide:
positivity consequence holds iff certainty consequence holds iff
classical consequence holds.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
On a crisp value $c$, positivity and certainty are the same condition:
$0 < c$ iff $c = 1$.
This identifies crisp positivity with crisp certainty, and
\Cref{thm:crisp-eval} translates crisp certainty into classical
consequence.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{crisp\_positivity\_iff\_certainty},
\texttt{crisp\_certainty\_iff\_classical},
\texttt{crisp\_positivity\_iff\_classical}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Crisp proof patterns]
\label{prop:crisp-patterns}
For every crisp valuation $w$ and formula $\varphi$:
$\evalc{w}{\varphi \vee \neg\varphi} = 1$,
$\evalc{w}{\varphi \wedge \neg\varphi} = 0$, and
$\evalc{w}{\neg\neg\varphi} = \evalc{w}{\varphi}$ (the last on all
valuations).
In the interior, excluded middle degrades but stays bounded:
$(c \disjc \negc c) = 1 - c(1-c) \geq \tfrac34$ for all
$c \in [0,1]$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
For crisp $c$, $c \disjc \negc c = 1 - c(1-c) = 1$ and
$c \conjc \negc c = c(1-c) = 0$.
Double negation is involutivity.
For the bound, $c(1-c) \leq \tfrac14$ with maximum at $c = \tfrac12$,
since $\tfrac14 - c(1-c) = (c - \tfrac12)^2 \geq 0$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{crisp\_excluded\_middle\_formula},
\texttt{crisp\_non\_contradiction\_formula},
\texttt{double\_neg\_elim\_formula},
\texttt{certainty\_ge\_three\_quarters}.)
\end{proof}

\subsection{The divergence: conditioning at the empty antecedent}

On crisp values the material conditional, read off the (joint,
evidence) pair, is $j \vee \lnot e$.
Coherence means a true joint forces true evidence ($j \leq e$).

\begin{theorem}[Material divergence]
\label{thm:material-divergence}
Let $j, e \in \Bool$ be coherent.
Then
\[
  \Cond(\iota(j), \iota(e)) = \{\iota(j \vee \lnot e)\}
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad e = \mathsf{true}.
\]
At $e = \mathsf{false}$ (hence $j = \mathsf{false}$ by coherence) the
admissible set is all of $[0,1]$, while the material conditional picks
the single value $1$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
If $e = \mathsf{true}$ then $\Cond(\iota(j), 1) = \{\iota(j)\}$ by
\Cref{thm:cond-trichotomy}(i), and
$j \vee \lnot e = j$.
If $e = \mathsf{false}$ then coherence gives $j = \mathsf{false}$ and
$\Cond(0,0) = [0,1]$ by \Cref{thm:cond-trichotomy}(ii), which is not a
singleton since $0 \neq 1$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{material\_divergence},
\texttt{vacuous\_truth\_admissible}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Exact underdetermination]
\label{prop:underdet}
For $j, e \in \Bool$:
$\Cond(\iota(j), \iota(e)) = [0,1]$ iff
$j = e = \mathsf{false}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
If $e = \mathsf{true}$ the set is a singleton; if $e = \mathsf{false}$
and $j = \mathsf{true}$ it is empty
(\Cref{thm:cond-trichotomy}(iii)); the remaining case is
\Cref{thm:cond-trichotomy}(ii).
(Machine-checked, \texttt{cond\_underdetermined\_iff}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No Boolean retraction]
\label{prop:no-bool}
No map $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ satisfies
$\varphi(\negc c) = 1 - \varphi(c)$ for all $c$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
At $c = \tfrac12$, \Cref{lem:neg-fixed} gives
$\varphi(\tfrac12) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac12)$, which has no solution in
$\{0,1\}$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{no\_boolean\_neg\_retraction}.)
\end{proof}

\Cref{fig:tower} summarizes: classical logic sits at the bottom of a
tower whose top level $[0,1]$ supports the liar fixed point, and the
two levels are connected only through the three-valued Kleene quotient
of Part~1~\cite{part1,priest1979,belnap1977}.

\section{Self-Reference as Solution Sets}
\label{sec:solutions}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Solution set]
\label{def:solutions}
For an operator $\Phi : [0,1] \to [0,1]$, the \emph{solution set} of
the self-referential definition $c = \Phi(c)$ is
\[
  \Sol(\Phi) = \{c \in [0,1] : \Phi(c) = c\},
\]
the fixed-point set of $\Phi$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{solutions},
\texttt{solutions\_eq\_fixedPoints}.)
\end{definition}

A paradox, on this reading, is an equation; its pathology or health is
the shape of its solution set.

\begin{theorem}[Liar]
\label{thm:liar-sol}
$\Sol(\negc) = \{\tfrac12\}$, while the Boolean liar has no solution:
$\{b \in \Bool : \lnot b = b\} = \varnothing$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The first claim is \Cref{lem:neg-fixed}.
For the second, $\lnot\mathsf{true} = \mathsf{false}$ and
$\lnot\mathsf{false} = \mathsf{true}$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{solutions\_neg\_eq\_singleton},
\texttt{bool\_neg\_solutions\_empty}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Truth-teller and zero evidence]
\label{thm:truth-teller}
$\Sol(\mathrm{id}) = [0,1]$, and this is the same set as zero-evidence
conditioning:
\[
  \Sol(\mathrm{id}) = [0,1] = \Cond(0,0).
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Every $c$ satisfies $c = c$; the second equality is
\Cref{thm:cond-trichotomy}(ii).
(Machine-checked, \texttt{solutions\_id\_eq\_univ},
\texttt{truth\_teller\_same\_shape\_as\_zero\_evidence}.)
\end{proof}

The two full intervals have different sources: for the truth-teller the
defining equation is the identity and supplies no information; at zero
evidence the chain rule degenerates to $c \cdot 0 = 0$ and supplies no
constraint.
The common shape is underdetermination, not contradiction.

\begin{theorem}[Russell scalar]
\label{thm:russell}
The scalar Russell equation has the singleton solution set
\[
  \{c \in [0,1] : c = 1 - c\} = \{\tfrac12\}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
\Cref{lem:neg-fixed}.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{russell\_fixed\_point},
\texttt{russell\_scalar\_solutions}.)
\end{proof}

Curry's pattern differs from all three: it uses no negation, only a
conditional that can be diagonalized.
The algebra has no internal conditional, so the Curry equation cannot
even be written until one is added.
The canonical candidate is the product residuum.

\begin{definition}[Product residuum]
\label{def:prodresid}
\[
  a \Rightarrow b =
  \begin{cases}
    1, & a = 0,\\
    \min\!\bigl(1,\, b/a\bigr), & a > 0.
  \end{cases}
\]
(Machine-checked, \texttt{prodResid}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Curry solutions at positive conclusion]
\label{thm:curry-sqrt}
For $b > 0$,
\[
  \sqrt{b} \in \Sol\bigl(c \mapsto (c \Rightarrow b)\bigr),
\]
so the solution set is nonempty.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Set $c = \sqrt b > 0$.
Then $b/c = b/\sqrt b = \sqrt b$, and $\sqrt b \leq 1$ since
$b \leq 1$, so
$c \Rightarrow b = \min(1, \sqrt b) = \sqrt b = c$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{curry\_sqrt\_solution},
\texttt{curry\_solutions\_nonempty\_positive}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Curry at zero conclusion]
\label{thm:curry-zero}
$\Sol\bigl(c \mapsto (c \Rightarrow 0)\bigr) = \varnothing$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
If $c = 0$ then $c \Rightarrow 0 = 1 \neq 0$.
If $c > 0$ then $c \Rightarrow 0 = \min(1, 0/c) = 0 \neq c$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{curry\_zero\_solutions\_empty},
\texttt{curry\_no\_fixed\_point\_zero}.)
\end{proof}

\section{Graded Predicates and Comprehension}
\label{sec:comprehension}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Graded predicate]
\label{def:graded-pred}
A \emph{graded predicate} on a domain $\alpha$ is a function
$P : \alpha \to [0,1]$.
Operations are pointwise:
$(\negc P)(x) = 1 - P(x)$,
$(P \conjc Q)(x) = P(x) Q(x)$,
$(P \disjc Q)(x) = P(x) \disjc Q(x)$.
A predicate is \emph{crisp} when $P(x) \in \{0,1\}$ for all $x$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Quantifiers]
\label{def:quantifiers}
For nonempty $\alpha$:
\[
  \cred(\forall x.\, P(x)) = \inf_{x \in \alpha} P(x), \qquad
  \cred(\exists x.\, P(x)) = \sup_{x \in \alpha} P(x).
\]
Both lie in $[0,1]$ because the range of $P$ is bounded.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{credInf}, \texttt{credSup}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Pointwise De Morgan laws and instance bounds]
\label{prop:pred-demorgan}
$\negc(P \conjc Q) = \negc P \disjc \negc Q$ and
$\negc(P \disjc Q) = \negc P \conjc \negc Q$ pointwise; moreover
$\inf_x P(x) \leq P(a) \leq \sup_x P(x)$ for every $a$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Pointwise instances of \Cref{prop:triplet}; the bounds are the defining
properties of $\inf$ and $\sup$ on the bounded range.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{GradedPredicate.de\_morgan\_conj},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_disj}, \texttt{forall\_le}, \texttt{le\_exists}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Quantifier duality]
\label{thm:quant-duality}
For every graded predicate $P$ on nonempty $\alpha$,
\[
  1 - \inf_{x} P(x) = \sup_{x} \bigl(1 - P(x)\bigr),
\]
i.e.\ $\negc \forall x. P(x) = \exists x. \negc P(x)$ at the value
level.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Both ranges are bounded.
For $\leq$: for each $a$, $\inf_x P(x) \leq P(a)$, so
$1 - P(a) \leq 1 - \inf_x P(x)$; taking $\sup$ over $a$ gives
$\sup_x (1 - P(x)) \leq 1 - \inf_x P(x)$.
For $\geq$: for each $a$,
$1 - P(a) \leq \sup_x(1 - P(x))$, so
$P(a) \geq 1 - \sup_x(1 - P(x))$; taking $\inf$ gives
$\inf_x P(x) \geq 1 - \sup_x(1 - P(x))$, i.e.\
$1 - \inf_x P(x) \leq \sup_x(1 - P(x))$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{quantifier\_duality\_val}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Crisp quantifier recovery]
\label{thm:crisp-quant}
For a crisp predicate $P$ on nonempty $\alpha$:
\[
  \inf_x P(x) = 0 \iff \exists a,\ P(a) = 0,
  \qquad
  \sup_x P(x) = 1 \iff \exists a,\ P(a) = 1.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
If some $P(a) = 0$ the infimum is $0$ since all values are
nonnegative.
If no $P(a) = 0$ then crispness forces $P \equiv 1$ and the infimum is
$1$, not $0$.
The supremum claim is symmetric.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{crisp\_inf\_zero\_iff},
\texttt{crisp\_sup\_one\_iff}.)
\end{proof}

\subsection{Comprehension as a constraint}

A graded comprehension principle has the schematic form
\[
  \cred\bigl(x \in \{y : \varphi(y)\}\bigr) = \cred(\varphi(x)).
\]
For Russell's predicate $R(x) = \negc\, x(x)$, instantiating at $R$
itself yields the scalar equation $c = 1 - c$ of \Cref{thm:russell}.
Comprehension here is a constraint on values: a defining condition cuts
out the solution set of credences compatible with it, and an
unsatisfiable condition yields an empty set rather than a trivializing
theorem.

This places the framework next to, not in place of, several
established programs:
White's consistency proof for unrestricted comprehension in
infinite-valued \L ukasiewicz logic~\cite{white1979}, the
H\'ajek\,/\,Paris\,/\,Shepherdson analysis of fuzzy truth
predicates~\cite{hajek2000,hajek2000liar}, Takeuti and Titani's fuzzy
set theory~\cite{takeuti1984}, and four-valued ZF-style theories such
as BZFC~\cite{khomskii2024}.
What is proved here is the scalar and predicate-level interface
(\Cref{thm:quant-duality,thm:crisp-quant,thm:russell});
extensionality, equality, and substitution for a full graded set
theory are addressed at the first-order layer of \Cref{sec:arith} and
remain open beyond it.
Russell is the entrance exam; Curry, next, is the harder test.

\section{The Curry Block}
\label{sec:curry}
%=============================================================================

Curry's paradox needs an internal conditional with three structural
properties.
For a total operation $f : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$, write $f(a,b)$ for the
value of the conditional with antecedent value $a$ and consequent
value $b$.

\begin{definition}[MP, CP, contraction, residuation]
\label{def:mp-cp}
\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{MP}(f) &:\iff \forall a, b:\ f(a,b) \conjc a \leq b,\\
  \mathrm{CP}(f) &:\iff \forall a, b, c:\
    c \conjc a \leq b \implies c \leq f(a,b),\\
  \mathrm{Contr}(f) &:\iff \forall a, b:\
    f\bigl(a, f(a,b)\bigr) \leq f(a,b),\\
  \mathrm{Resid}(f) &:\iff \forall a, b, c:\
    \bigl(c \conjc a \leq b \iff c \leq f(a,b)\bigr).
\end{align*}
(Machine-checked, \texttt{MP}, \texttt{CP}, \texttt{Contraction},
\texttt{Residuation}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[MP and CP amount to residuation]
\label{lem:mp-cp-resid}
If $\mathrm{MP}(f)$ and $\mathrm{CP}(f)$, then $\mathrm{Resid}(f)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
CP is one direction.
For the other, $c \leq f(a,b)$ gives
$c \conjc a = ca \leq f(a,b)\,a = f(a,b) \conjc a \leq b$ by
monotonicity of multiplication and MP.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{mp\_cp\_residuation}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the residuated conditional]
\label{thm:resid-unique}
If $\mathrm{Resid}(f)$, then for all $a > 0$ and all $b$,
\[
  f(a,b) = \min\!\bigl(1,\, b/a\bigr) = (a \Rightarrow b).
\]
Hence MP and CP pin every internal conditional to the product residuum
on positive antecedents.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Upper bound: $f(a,b) \leq 1$, and taking $c = f(a,b)$ in residuation
gives $f(a,b)\,a \leq b$, so $f(a,b) \leq b/a$; together
$f(a,b) \leq \min(1, b/a)$.
Lower bound: $r = \min(1, b/a)$ is a credence and satisfies
$r a \leq b$ (if $b \leq a$ then $r = b/a$ and $ra = b$; otherwise
$r = 1$ and $ra = a < b$), so residuation gives $r \leq f(a,b)$.
The second equality with \Cref{def:prodresid} is definitional for
$a > 0$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{residuation\_unique},
\texttt{prodResid\_unique\_positive}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[The residuum fails contraction]
\label{prop:no-contraction}
At $a = \tfrac12$, $b = \tfrac14$:
\[
  a \Rightarrow b = \tfrac12,
  \qquad
  a \Rightarrow (a \Rightarrow b) = \tfrac12 \Rightarrow \tfrac12 = 1,
  \qquad
  1 \not\leq \tfrac12 .
\]
So $\mathrm{Contr}(\Rightarrow)$ fails.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\tfrac12 \Rightarrow \tfrac14 = \min(1, \tfrac{1/4}{1/2}) = \tfrac12$
and
$\tfrac12 \Rightarrow \tfrac12 = \min(1, 1) = 1$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{prod\_resid\_no\_contraction}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Curry block]
\label{thm:curry-block}
No total $f : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfies $\mathrm{MP}(f)$,
$\mathrm{CP}(f)$, and $\mathrm{Contr}(f)$ together.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Suppose $f$ satisfies all three.
By \Cref{lem:mp-cp-resid,thm:resid-unique},
$f(\tfrac12, \tfrac14) = \tfrac12$ and
$f(\tfrac12, \tfrac12) = 1$.
Contraction at $(a,b) = (\tfrac12, \tfrac14)$ then reads
\[
  1 = f\bigl(\tfrac12, f(\tfrac12,\tfrac14)\bigr)
    \leq f(\tfrac12, \tfrac14) = \tfrac12,
\]
a contradiction.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{curry\_block}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why the language has no arrow]
\label{rem:no-arrow}
\Cref{thm:curry-block} is the architectural reason the formula language
of \Cref{sec:crisp} and the first-order language of \Cref{sec:arith}
have no implication constructor.
Curry is not repaired after internalization; the internalization is
refused, and conditioning enters only as the external judgment
$c \in \Cond(j,e)$.
The diagnosis matches substructural logic: contraction is the unsafe
rule in the presence of self-reference~\cite{restall1993}; here the
obstruction is numerical and machine-checked for the product carrier.
The crisp fragment is unaffected: on $\{0,1\}$ the material conditional
$\max(\negc a, b)$ satisfies MP, CP, and contraction, so
\Cref{thm:curry-block} is a statement about the interior of $[0,1]$.
\end{remark}

\section{Threshold Consequence and the Labelled Calculus}
\label{sec:proof}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Threshold consequence}

\begin{definition}[Threshold consequence]
\label{def:threshold}
For a threshold $t \in [0,1]$, premises $\Gamma$, and a conclusion
$\varphi$:
\[
  \Gamma \models_t \varphi
  \quad:\iff\quad
  \forall v:\
  \bigl(\forall p \in \Gamma:\ \evalc{v}{p} \geq t\bigr)
  \implies \evalc{v}{\varphi} \geq t,
\]
where $v$ ranges over all credence valuations.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{thresholdConsequence}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Structural rules]
\label{prop:threshold-structural}
For every $t$: reflexivity ($\varphi \models_t \varphi$),
monotonicity ($\Gamma \models_t \varphi$ and
$\Gamma \subseteq \Delta$ imply $\Delta \models_t \varphi$), and cut
($\Gamma \models_t \varphi$ and
$\varphi, \Gamma \models_t \psi$ imply $\Gamma \models_t \psi$).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Immediate from \Cref{def:threshold}: a valuation satisfying the
premises of the conclusion relation satisfies the premises of the
hypotheses.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{threshold\_reflexivity},
\texttt{threshold\_monotonicity}, \texttt{threshold\_cut}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Endpoints]
\label{prop:threshold-endpoints}
$\Gamma \models_1 \varphi$ iff certainty consequence holds
(premise values $1$ force conclusion value $1$), and positivity
consequence holds iff for every valuation a positive threshold
witnesses the conclusion whenever positive thresholds witness all
premises.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
At $t = 1$, $\geq 1$ on $[0,1]$ is $= 1$.
For positivity, $0 < c$ iff some $t > 0$ has $t \leq c$ (take
$t = c$).
(Machine-checked, \texttt{threshold\_one\_iff\_certainty},
\texttt{formulaPositivity\_iff\_exists\_threshold}.)
\end{proof}

The two sharp bounds calibrate proof design.
Write $A$ for an atom and consider explosion
$\{A, \neg A\} \models_t B$ with $B$ an unrelated atom.

\begin{theorem}[Sharp explosion bound]
\label{thm:explosion-sharp}
For $t > 0$, there is a valuation $v$ with
$\evalc{v}{A} \geq t$, $\evalc{v}{\neg A} \geq t$, and
$\evalc{v}{B} < t$
\emph{iff} $t \leq \tfrac12$.
Hence explosion fails as a $\models_t$ rule exactly for
$0 < t \leq \tfrac12$, and holds vacuously for $t > \tfrac12$
(its premises are unsatisfiable).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
If such a $v$ exists, then $t \leq v(A)$ and $t \leq 1 - v(A)$, so
$2t \leq 1$.
Conversely, for $0 < t \leq \tfrac12$ take $v(A) = \tfrac12$,
$v(B) = 0$: then $v(A) = 1 - v(A) = \tfrac12 \geq t$ and
$0 < t \not\leq 0$.
For $t > \tfrac12$, $t \leq v(A)$ and $t \leq 1 - v(A)$ would give
$2t \leq 1$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{threshold\_explosion\_countermodel\_iff},
\texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}, \texttt{graded\_explosion\_vacuous}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Sharp excluded-middle bound]
\label{thm:em-sharp}
$t \leq c \disjc \negc c$ holds for all $c \in [0,1]$ \emph{iff}
$t \leq \tfrac34$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$c \disjc \negc c = c + (1-c) - c(1-c) = 1 - c(1-c)$, and
$c(1-c) = \tfrac14 - (c - \tfrac12)^2 \leq \tfrac14$ with equality at
$c = \tfrac12$.
So the minimum of $c \disjc \negc c$ over $[0,1]$ is $\tfrac34$,
attained at $\tfrac12$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{threshold\_excluded\_middle\_iff}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[A working window]
\label{cor:window}
The interval $(\tfrac12, \tfrac34]$ is nonempty, and every threshold in
it validates excluded middle while making the explosion premises
unsatisfiable.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{threshold\_window\_nonempty}.)
\end{corollary}

\subsection{The labelled calculus}

The proof layer makes the separation syntactic.
A \emph{label} is one of $\mathsf{pos}$, $\mathsf{cert}$, or
$\mathsf{thr}(t)$; a credence $c$ satisfies them when $c > 0$,
$c = 1$, or $c \geq t$ respectively.
A \emph{conditioning side judgment} is a triple
$J = (c; \varphi_j, \varphi_e)$ of a credence and two formulas; a
valuation $v$ satisfies $J$ when
$c \in \Cond\bigl(\evalc{v}{\varphi_j}, \evalc{v}{\varphi_e}\bigr)$,
i.e.\ $c \conjc \evalc{v}{\varphi_e} = \evalc{v}{\varphi_j}$.
A context $\Gamma$ is a list of labelled formulas plus a list of side
judgments.

\begin{definition}[Labelled derivations]
\label{def:derivation}
$\Gamma \vdash \ell : \varphi$ is generated by: hypothesis, weakening,
cut; threshold monotonicity
($\mathsf{thr}(s)$ to $\mathsf{thr}(t)$ for $t \leq s$),
$\mathsf{thr}(t)$ to $\mathsf{pos}$ for $t > 0$, $\mathsf{cert}$ to
$\mathsf{thr}(t)$; semantic import of any verified positivity,
certainty, or threshold consequence; conjunction elimination and
disjunction introduction at threshold labels; double-negation
introduction and elimination; and the \emph{chain-rule cut}
\[
  \frac{(c; \varphi_j, \varphi_e) \in \Gamma
        \qquad \ell_0 \leq c
        \qquad \Gamma \vdash \mathsf{thr}(t) : \varphi_e}
       {\Gamma \vdash \mathsf{thr}(\ell_0 \conjc t) : \varphi_j}.
\]
There is no rule introducing a conditional formula, because the
language has none.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{Derivation}, \texttt{CondJudgment}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Soundness]
\label{thm:seq-sound}
If $\Gamma \vdash \ell : \varphi$, then every valuation satisfying all
bounds and side judgments of $\Gamma$ gives $\evalc{v}{\varphi}$ a
value satisfying $\ell$.
Consequently a derivation from threshold-$t$ premises to a
threshold-$t$ conclusion certifies $\Gamma \models_t \varphi$, and
likewise for certainty and positivity.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Induction on the derivation.
The only rule beyond bookkeeping is the chain-rule cut: from
$c \conjc \evalc{v}{\varphi_e} = \evalc{v}{\varphi_j}$,
$\ell_0 \leq c$, and $t \leq \evalc{v}{\varphi_e}$, monotonicity of the
product gives
$\ell_0 \conjc t \leq c \conjc \evalc{v}{\varphi_e}
  = \evalc{v}{\varphi_j}$.
Conjunction elimination uses $a \conjc b \leq a, b$; disjunction
introduction uses $a, b \leq a \disjc b$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{derivation\_sound},
\texttt{derivation\_sound\_thresholdConsequence},
\texttt{derivation\_sound\_formulaCertainty},
\texttt{derivation\_sound\_formulaPositivity}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[No ex falso in the calculus]
\label{thm:no-ex-falso}
Let $\Gamma_0$ contain exactly the bounds $\mathsf{pos} : A$ and
$\mathsf{pos} : \neg A$ and no side judgments.
Then $\Gamma_0 \nvdash \mathsf{pos} : B$ for an atom $B$ distinct
from $A$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The valuation $v(A) = \tfrac12$, $v(B) = 0$ satisfies $\Gamma_0$:
$\tfrac12 > 0$ and $1 - \tfrac12 > 0$.
By \Cref{thm:seq-sound} a derivation would force $v(B) > 0$,
contradicting $v(B) = 0$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{labelled\_no\_ex\_falso}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Comparison]
Pavelka-style graded provability~\cite{pavelka1979i} and H\'ajek's
product logic~\cite{hajek1998} are the mature calculi for graded truth
with internal residuated implication.
The calculus above takes neither unchanged: by
\Cref{thm:curry-block} an internal residuated arrow with contraction
is not available, so conditioning stays a side judgment and the
chain-rule cut transports bounds instead of detaching implications.
Completeness is deliberately not claimed; a premature completeness
theorem risks smuggling a residuated implication into the rules.
\end{remark}

\section{The Arithmetic \texorpdfstring{$\Sigma_1$}{Sigma-1} Layer}
\label{sec:arith}
%=============================================================================

The foundations claim needs arithmetic, not just propositional logic.
This section equips the graded semantics with a standard model of
Robinson~$Q$ and carries out the classical arithmetization
chain~\cite{godel1931}, following the route of the
FormalizedFormalLogic development~\cite{fflfoundation} transported to
credence semantics.

\subsection{The standard model and Robinson Q}

The first-order language has function symbols $0, S, +, \cdot$,
equality as a formula constructor, the De Morgan connectives, and
quantifiers; it has \emph{no} implication or conditional constructor
(\Cref{rem:no-arrow}).
Material implication, where needed inside axioms, is the encoding
$\varphi \to \psi := \neg\varphi \vee \psi$, which on crisp values is
the classical conditional.

\begin{definition}[Standard model]
\label{def:natmodel}
The structure $N$ has domain $\Nat$ with the genuine $0$, successor,
addition, and multiplication; crisp equality
\[
  \evalc{N}{a = b} =
  \begin{cases} 1, & a = b,\\ 0, & a \neq b; \end{cases}
\]
and quantifiers as genuine infima and suprema over the whole domain:
\[
  \evalc{N}{\forall x.\,\varphi} = \inf_{n \in \Nat}
    \evalc{N}{\varphi[n]},
  \qquad
  \evalc{N}{\exists x.\,\varphi} = \sup_{n \in \Nat}
    \evalc{N}{\varphi[n]}.
\]
(Machine-checked, \texttt{natModel}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Robinson Q]
\label{def:robinson-q}
The axioms are the closed formulas
\begin{align*}
  &Q_1:\ \forall x.\ \neg(Sx = 0)
  &&Q_2:\ \forall x \forall y.\ Sx = Sy \to x = y\\
  &Q_3:\ \forall x.\ \bigl(x = 0 \vee \exists y.\ x = Sy\bigr)
  &&Q_4:\ \forall x.\ x + 0 = x\\
  &Q_5:\ \forall x \forall y.\ x + Sy = S(x + y)
  &&Q_6:\ \forall x.\ x \cdot 0 = 0\\
  &Q_7:\ \forall x \forall y.\ x \cdot Sy = x \cdot y + x
\end{align*}
with $\to$ the De Morgan encoding.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{arithQ.axioms}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[$N \models Q$ at certainty]
\label{thm:axioms-one}
Every axiom of \Cref{def:robinson-q} evaluates to $1$ in $N$, under
every assignment.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Equality is crisp, so each quantifier-free matrix is the indicator of
a true statement about $\Nat$: e.g.\ for $Q_2$ the matrix at $(x,y)$ is
$\negc[Sx = Sy] \disjc [x = y]$, which is $1$ because $S$ is injective;
for $Q_3$ at $x = k+1$ the existential body has witness $k$, and the
supremum dominates the instance.
Each matrix is therefore constantly $1$, and the infimum of a constant
family of ones is $1$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{axioms\_eval\_one}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Consistency of Q over the standard model]
\label{thm:q-con}
$\bot$ is not provable from the $Q$ axioms at certainty in the
foundation proof calculus.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The calculus is sound: a proof at threshold $1$ from premises
designated $1$ in a structure with crisp equality and the quantifier
laws yields $1 \leq \evalc{N}{\bot}$.
By \Cref{thm:axioms-one} the premises are designated in $N$, and
$\evalc{N}{\bot} = 0$, so $1 \leq 0$, a contradiction.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{arithQ\_con},
\texttt{FoundationProof.sound}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-vacuity]
The provability relation is not empty: $0 = 0$ is derivable by the
reflexivity rule, and universal instantiation of $Q_4$ derives
$0 + 0 = 0$; the concrete equation $2 + 1 = 3$ evaluates to $1$ in
$N$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{provableFromQ\_zero\_refl},
\texttt{provableFromQ\_addZero\_zero}, \texttt{natModel\_two\_add\_one}.)
\end{remark}

\subsection{Crisp evaluation and \texorpdfstring{$\Sigma_1$}{Sigma-1}
  completeness}

\begin{definition}[Definable order]
\label{def:leF}
The signature has no order symbol; define
\[
  (a \leq b) := \exists c.\ a + c = b,
  \qquad
  (a < b) := (Sa \leq b).
\]
(Machine-checked, \texttt{leF}, \texttt{ltF}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The definable order is the order, crisply]
\label{thm:leF}
For all terms $a, b$ and assignments,
$\evalc{N}{a \leq b} = 1$ iff
$\evalc{N}{a} \leq \evalc{N}{b}$ in $\Nat$, and the value is $0$
otherwise.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The body $a + c = b$ at witness $c$ is the crisp indicator of
$\evalc{N}{a} + c = \evalc{N}{b}$.
By \Cref{thm:crisp-quant} the supremum over $c$ is $1$ iff some witness
exists, i.e.\ iff $\evalc{N}{a} \leq \evalc{N}{b}$; otherwise the
family is constantly $0$ and the supremum is $0$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{leF\_eval\_one\_iff},
\texttt{leF\_eval\_eq}, \texttt{ltF\_eval\_one\_iff}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[$\Sigma_1$ formulas]
\label{def:sigma1}
A formula is \emph{quantifier-free} when it contains no quantifier.
A formula is $\Sigma_1$ when it is an existential block over a
quantifier-free matrix:
$\exists x_1 \cdots \exists x_k.\ \varphi$ with $\varphi$
quantifier-free.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{QuantifierFree}, \texttt{IsSigmaOne}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Quantifier-free formulas are crisp]
\label{prop:qf-crisp}
Every quantifier-free arithmetic formula evaluates to $0$ or $1$ in
$N$, under every assignment.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Induction.
Equality atoms are crisp by \Cref{def:natmodel}, and on $\{0,1\}$ the
operations $\negc$, $\conjc$, $\disjc$ stay in $\{0,1\}$
(\Cref{prop:embed-hom}).
(Machine-checked, \texttt{quantifierFree\_crisp}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[$\Sigma_1$ witness completeness]
\label{thm:sigma1}
Let $\varphi$ be quantifier-free.
Then
\[
  \evalc{N}{\exists x.\ \varphi} = 1
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \exists n \in \Nat:\ \evalc{N}{\varphi[n]} = 1 .
\]
In particular a true $\Sigma_1$ existential is witnessed by an actual
natural number, and a numeral witness certifies the existential.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By \Cref{prop:qf-crisp} the family
$n \mapsto \evalc{N}{\varphi[n]}$ is crisp, and by
\Cref{thm:crisp-quant} its supremum is $1$ iff some instance is $1$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{existsE\_crisp\_one\_iff},
\texttt{sigmaOne\_witness\_complete}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Numerals and representation]
\label{def:represents}
The numeral $\bar n$ is $S^n 0$; it evaluates to $n$.
An object formula $P$ with one free variable \emph{represents} a
predicate $R \subseteq \Nat$ in $N$ when
\[
  \forall n:\quad \evalc{N}{P[\bar n]} = 1
  \iff n \in R .
\]
In particular, a provability formula must represent the certificate
checker: the real-free checker $\mathsf{checkCodeNat}$ is a total
computable decision that accepts a code exactly when the encoded
certificate is valid.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{numeral}, \texttt{RepresentsChecker},
\texttt{provability\_representable}.)
\end{definition}

\subsection{The arithmetization chain}

The bricks below build a representing formula for a genuine recursive
predicate.
Mathlib's pairing function is
\[
  \pair(a,b) =
  \begin{cases}
    b^2 + a, & a < b,\\
    a^2 + a + b, & b \leq a,
  \end{cases}
\]
a bijection $\Nat^2 \to \Nat$ with inverse $\unpair$.

\begin{definition}[Pairing graph]
\label{def:pairgraph}
\[
  \mathrm{pairGraph}(x,y,z) :=
  \bigl(x < y \wedge z = y \cdot y + x\bigr)
  \vee
  \bigl(y \leq x \wedge z = x \cdot x + x + y\bigr),
\]
with the orders of \Cref{def:leF}.
The unpairing graph is the same formula read backward:
$\mathrm{unpairGraph}(z,x,y) := \mathrm{pairGraph}(x,y,z)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Pairing is represented]
\label{thm:pair-rep}
For all $a, b, c \in \Nat$:
\[
  \evalc{N}{\mathrm{pairGraph}(\bar a, \bar b, \bar c)} = 1
  \iff c = \pair(a,b),
\]
and
$\evalc{N}{\mathrm{unpairGraph}(\bar c, \bar a, \bar b)} = 1$ iff
$\unpair(c) = (a,b)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Each branch is a crisp conjunction of an order guard
(\Cref{thm:leF}) and an equality atom, and the disjunction of crisp
values is $1$ iff one disjunct is.
The two guards are exclusive and exhaustive, matching the case split in
the definition of $\pair$.
The unpairing claim follows from the bijection:
$c = \pair(a,b)$ iff $\unpair(c) = (a,b)$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{pairGraph\_represents},
\texttt{unpairGraph\_represents}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Modulo and $\beta$ graphs]
\label{def:betagraph}
\[
  \mathrm{modGraph}(a,m,r) :=
  \exists q.\ \bigl(a = q \cdot m + r \;\wedge\; r < m\bigr),
\]
and G\"odel's two-argument $\beta$-extraction
$\beta(a,d,i) = a \bmod \bigl(1 + (i+1)d\bigr)$ has the graph
\[
  \mathrm{betaGraph}(a,d,i,w) :=
  \mathrm{modGraph}\bigl(a,\ S((Si) \cdot d),\ w\bigr).
\]
The single-code form packs both arguments into one number,
$\beta(n, i) = \unpair(n)_1 \bmod \bigl((i+1) \cdot \unpair(n)_2 + 1\bigr)$,
with graph
\[
  \mathrm{betaN}(n,i,w) :=
  \exists a \exists d.\
  \bigl(\mathrm{unpairGraph}(n,a,d)
    \wedge \mathrm{betaGraph}(a,d,i,w)\bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[$\beta$-extraction is represented]
\label{thm:beta-rep}
For all $a, d, i, w, n, m, r \in \Nat$ with $m > 0$:
\begin{align*}
  \evalc{N}{\mathrm{modGraph}(\bar a, \bar m, \bar r)} = 1
    &\iff r = a \bmod m,\\
  \evalc{N}{\mathrm{betaGraph}(\bar a, \bar d, \bar i, \bar w)} = 1
    &\iff w = a \bmod \bigl(1 + (i+1)d\bigr),\\
  \evalc{N}{\mathrm{betaN}(\bar n, \bar i, \bar w)} = 1
    &\iff w = \beta(n, i).
\end{align*}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The matrix of $\mathrm{modGraph}$ is crisp, so \Cref{thm:sigma1}
reduces the existential to witness arithmetic: a quotient $q$ with
$a = qm + r$ and $r < m$ exists iff $r = a \bmod m$ (division with
remainder).
The $\mathrm{betaGraph}$ modulus term $S((Si) \cdot d)$ evaluates to
$(i+1)d + 1 \geq 1$, so no positivity side condition remains.
For $\mathrm{betaN}$, two applications of \Cref{thm:sigma1} peel the
binders; the witnesses are forced to be $\unpair(n)$ by
\Cref{thm:pair-rep}, and the remainder clause is the previous case.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{modGraph\_represents},
\texttt{betaGraph\_represents}, \texttt{betaNGraph\_represents}.)
\end{proof}

\subsection{A course-of-values predicate and its object formula}

The target predicate is genuine course-of-values recursion: the
well-formedness of a coded binary tree, the structural core of
proof-checking.

\begin{definition}[Tree codes]
\label{def:istree}
$\mathrm{isTree} : \Nat \to \Bool$ is defined by
\[
  \mathrm{isTree}(0) = \mathsf{true},
  \qquad
  \mathrm{isTree}(n+1) =
  \mathrm{isTree}\bigl(\unpair(n)_1\bigr) \wedge
  \mathrm{isTree}\bigl(\unpair(n)_2\bigr).
\]
The recursion terminates because $\unpair(n)_1, \unpair(n)_2 \leq n
< n + 1$.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{isTree}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Coherence of a $\beta$-table]
\label{def:cohere}
For $N, k \in \Nat$,
\[
  \mathrm{Coh}(N, k) :\iff
  \Bigl(\beta(N,k) = 1 \iff
    k = 0 \;\vee\;
    \bigl(0 < k \wedge \beta(N, \unpair(k-1)_1) = 1
      \wedge \beta(N, \unpair(k-1)_2) = 1\bigr)\Bigr).
\]
(Machine-checked, \texttt{CohereNat}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Course-of-values via $\beta$]
\label{thm:tree-beta}
For every $n \in \Nat$:
\[
  \mathrm{isTree}(n) = \mathsf{true}
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \exists N \in \Nat:\
  \beta(N, n) = 1 \;\wedge\;
  \forall k \leq n:\ \mathrm{Coh}(N,k).
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
($\Rightarrow$) Let $l = [\,\chi(0), \ldots, \chi(n)\,]$ with
$\chi(k) = 1$ if $\mathrm{isTree}(k)$ and $0$ otherwise, and let
$N = \mathrm{unbeta}(l)$ be a $\beta$-code of the list, so that
$\beta(N,k) = \chi(k)$ for $k \leq n$ (the G\"odel $\beta$-lemma,
via the Chinese remainder theorem).
Then $\beta(N,n) = 1$, and coherence at every $k \leq n$ is the
recursion equation of \Cref{def:istree} rewritten through $\chi$,
using that the children indices $\unpair(k-1)_j \leq k - 1 \leq n$
stay within the table.
($\Leftarrow$) Strong induction on $k \leq n$ with the claim
$\beta(N,k) = 1 \Rightarrow \mathrm{isTree}(k)$.
Coherence at $k$ either gives $k = 0$, where
$\mathrm{isTree}(0) = \mathsf{true}$, or gives $k = m + 1$ with both
children of $m$ marked; the children are strictly smaller and still
$\leq n$, so the induction hypothesis applies and the recursion
equation closes the case.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{isTree\_iff\_beta}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[The tree formula]
\label{def:treeformula}
With $\to$ and $\leftrightarrow$ the De Morgan encodings,
\[
  \mathrm{treeFormula}(x) :=
  \exists N.\ \Bigl(
    \mathrm{betaN}(N, x, \bar 1)
    \;\wedge\;
    \forall k.\ \bigl(k \leq x \to \mathrm{cohF}(N, k)\bigr)
  \Bigr),
\]
where $\mathrm{cohF}(N,k)$ is the object form of \Cref{def:cohere}:
\[
  \mathrm{betaN}(N, k, \bar 1)
  \;\leftrightarrow\;
  \Bigl(k = 0 \;\vee\;
    \exists a \exists b \exists p.\
    \bigl(k = Sp \wedge \mathrm{pairGraph}(a,b,p)
      \wedge \mathrm{betaN}(N,a,\bar 1)
      \wedge \mathrm{betaN}(N,b,\bar 1)\bigr)\Bigr).
\]
(Machine-checked, \texttt{treeFormula}.)
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The tree predicate is represented]
\label{thm:tree-rep}
For every $n \in \Nat$ and every assignment,
\[
  \evalc{N}{\mathrm{treeFormula}(\bar n)} = 1
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \mathrm{isTree}(n) = \mathsf{true}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Every brick of $\mathrm{treeFormula}$ evaluates crisply at every term
tuple: equality atoms and order guards by
\Cref{prop:qf-crisp,thm:leF}, the graphs by
\Cref{thm:pair-rep,thm:beta-rep}, the De Morgan implication and
biconditional because $\{0,1\}$ is closed under the connectives, the
bounded universal because an infimum of a crisp family is crisp.
On crisp values each connective is classical, so peeling the outer
existential (\Cref{thm:sigma1}), the conjunction, the guarded
universal, and the three inner existentials translates designation of
$\mathrm{treeFormula}(\bar n)$ into exactly the right-hand side of
\Cref{thm:tree-beta}; the node case matches \Cref{def:cohere} because
$k = p + 1$ with $p = \pair(a,b)$ forces
$(a,b) = \unpair(k-1)$.
\Cref{thm:tree-beta} closes the equivalence.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{treeFormula\_eval\_one\_iff},
\texttt{treeFormula\_represents}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Shape of the formula]
\label{rem:tree-shape}
$\mathrm{treeFormula}$ is an existential ($\beta$-code) over a matrix
whose only universal quantifier is bounded by $x$; in the classical
arithmetical hierarchy this is the $\Sigma_1$ shape of arithmetized
course-of-values recursion~\cite{godel1931}.
The biconditional and the bounded implication inside it are encoded
through $\negc$ and $\disjc$ alone, consistent with the absence of an
internal conditional (\Cref{rem:no-arrow}): on the crisp values where
the formula lives, the De Morgan encoding is the classical
conditional, and no contraction-bearing arrow is ever introduced.
\end{remark}

\section{The Second-Incompleteness Boundary}
\label{sec:loeb}
%=============================================================================

\Cref{sec:arith} delivers the semantic core of representability: a
standard model where $Q$ is certain ($\Cref{thm:axioms-one}$),
$\Sigma_1$ witness completeness ($\Cref{thm:sigma1}$), a named target
for a provability formula (\Cref{def:represents}), and a completed
arithmetization of a course-of-values recursive predicate
(\Cref{thm:tree-rep}).
The boundary of the program is equally precise.

\begin{theorem}[No internal L\"ob arrow]
\label{thm:no-loeb}
There is no total operation $f : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying
$\mathrm{MP}(f)$, $\mathrm{CP}(f)$, and $\mathrm{Contr}(f)$.
Consequently the L\"ob derivability argument, which requires an
internal implication with modus ponens, conditional proof, and
contraction, has no internal-arrow formulation in this algebra,
regardless of any representing provability formula.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The first sentence is \Cref{thm:curry-block}; the second reads off
which structural rules the standard proof of L\"ob's theorem uses on
the internal arrow.
(Machine-checked, \texttt{no\_internal\_loeb\_arrow}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Honest scope]
\label{rem:scope}
Three boundaries should not be blurred.
First, \Cref{thm:no-loeb} is an impossibility for an
\emph{internalized} second incompleteness argument; it does not refute
G\"odel's theorem, which concerns classical theories with a classical
conditional.
Second, zero-evidence conditioning ($\Cond(0,0) = [0,1]$) is
underdetermination by an equation; proof-theoretic undecidability is a
different phenomenon and does not assign any sentence the value
$\tfrac12$.
Third, the representability target of \Cref{def:represents} is named
and the mechanism for meeting it is proved on a genuine
course-of-values predicate (\Cref{thm:tree-rep}); instantiating the
same chain on the full certificate checker is mechanical but not yet
machine-checked, and nothing in this paper claims it.
\end{remark}

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The slogan ``paradox without explosion'' has a theorem-level reading.
Self-reference patterns are equations with solution sets:
$\{\tfrac12\}$ for liar and Russell, $[0,1]$ for the truth-teller and
for zero-evidence conditioning, $\varnothing$ or $\{\sqrt b\}$-type
sets for Curry once a residuum is added
(\Cref{thm:liar-sol,thm:truth-teller,thm:russell,thm:curry-sqrt,thm:curry-zero}).
Classical reasoning survives exactly where it should: the crisp
fragment is classical for evaluation and consequence
(\Cref{thm:crisp-eval,thm:crisp-coincide}), excluded middle holds for
thresholds up to $\tfrac34$, and explosion loses its countermodel only
when its premises become unsatisfiable
(\Cref{thm:explosion-sharp,thm:em-sharp}).
What disappears is one inference: from false or impossible evidence to
an arbitrary conclusion
(\Cref{thm:material-divergence,thm:no-ex-falso}).

The mechanism is a single design decision applied uniformly:
conditioning is the external relation $c \in \Cond(j,e)$, never an
internal arrow.
The Curry block (\Cref{thm:curry-block}) shows the decision is forced,
since MP, CP, and contraction have no common total carrier on
$[0,1]$; the labelled calculus (\Cref{thm:seq-sound}) shows the
decision is workable; and the arithmetic layer
(\Cref{thm:axioms-one,thm:sigma1,thm:tree-rep}) shows it reaches
genuine arithmetization, up to the structural boundary of
\Cref{thm:no-loeb}.

Two tasks remain open.
A graded set theory over this interface must settle extensionality,
equality, and substitution against the comprehension programs of
White, H\'ajek\,/\,Paris\,/\,Shepherdson, Takeuti and Titani, and
BZFC~\cite{white1979,hajek2000,takeuti1984,khomskii2024}, while
keeping conditioning external.
And the arithmetization chain of \Cref{fig:bricks} should be pushed
from the tree predicate to the full certificate checker, closing the
representability target of \Cref{def:represents}.
