\begin{abstract}
The credence algebra separates three operations that are often collapsed:
truth-value operations, consequence, and conditioning.
Its value algebra is the product De Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$:
$\negc c=1-c$, $a\conjc b=ab$, and
$a\disjc b=a+b-ab$.
Conditioning is the external admissibility relation
$\Cond(j,e)=\{c\in[0,1]:ce=j\}$.
This paper studies the foundations layer built from that separation.
Classical logic embeds exactly in the crisp fragment $\{0,1\}$, except that
empty antecedents give underdetermined conditioning rather than vacuous truth.
Self-reference is classified by solution sets: the liar and Russell equations
have the singleton solution $\{\tfrac12\}$, the truth-teller has all of
$[0,1]$, and Curry has positive solutions only after an internal product
residuum has been added.
The Curry block theorem proves that modus ponens, conditional proof, and
contraction have no common total carrier on $[0,1]$.
The result is a local foundations program: paradoxes become equations or
admissibility failures, while classical proof survives on the crisp fragment.
The wider setting, in which this opening commitment is seen as irreducible across probability, logic, and foundations, is developed in Part~4.
\end{abstract}

%=============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Russell's paradox asks for a set $R$ such that
\[
  R\in R \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R\notin R.
\]
The classical contradiction comes from forcing the left side to be either
true or false.
In the credence algebra, the same self-reference yields one scalar equation.
If $c$ is the credence of $R\in R$, then
\[
  c = 1-c,
\]
so $c=\tfrac12$.
Nothing explodes.
The equation has a solution, and the solution is unique.

This small calculation is not the whole theory.
It is the first test of a larger separation.
The algebra of values is
\[
  \negc c=1-c,\qquad
  a\conjc b=ab,\qquad
  a\disjc b=1-(1-a)(1-b)=a+b-ab .
\]
Conditioning is not a binary connective in the object language.
It is the admissibility relation
\[
  \Cond(j,e)=\{c\in[0,1]:c e=j\}.
\]
For $e>0$ this is the singleton $\{j/e\}$, provided $j\le e$.
For $e=0=j$ it is all of $[0,1]$.
For $e=0<j$ it is empty.
Thus false or impossible evidence never licenses arbitrary conclusions.
The chain rule imposes no conditional value at zero evidence.

Parts~1--2 proved the algebraic and semantic base.
Part~1 classified the product De Morgan triplet and its collapses.
Part~2 added valuations, LP/K3 bridges, update rules, and the
truth-functional no-go theorem for conditioning.
This paper asks what remains of classical foundations once those pieces are
kept apart.

The answer has four parts.
First, the crisp fragment $\{0,1\}$ recovers classical propositional reasoning.
The only divergence is conditioning on empty evidence.
Second, liar, truth-teller, Russell, and Curry patterns are best compared by
their solution sets.
Third, graded comprehension can state Russell-style self-membership without
making every sentence derivable.
Fourth, Curry's paradox is blocked at the architectural level: deriving it
requires a total internal conditional with structural rules that the algebra
does not supply.

This is a modest claim.
The paper does not replace Kripke fixed points, revision theory, fuzzy set
theory, paraconsistent set theory, or Pavelka proof theory.
It identifies the interface at which Cred meets those traditions.
The Lean development records the interface as theorem statements rather than
as metaphors.

\section{The Crisp Fragment}
\label{sec:crisp}
%=============================================================================

Classical values embed into credences by the map
\[
  \mathsf{false}\mapsto 0,\qquad \mathsf{true}\mapsto 1.
\]
On those two values the product De Morgan operations are Boolean:
\[
  1(1)=1,\quad 1(0)=0,\quad 0(0)=0,
\]
and
\[
  a\disjc b=a+b-ab
\]
is Boolean disjunction when $a,b\in\{0,1\}$.
Negation is Boolean complementation.

Lean names the embedding theorem \texttt{crisp\_eval\_eq}.
For every Boolean valuation $v$ and formula $\varphi$, evaluating
$\varphi$ classically and then embedding the result gives the same credence
as evaluating $\varphi$ directly in Cred under the embedded valuation.
The theorem \texttt{crisp\_embedding} states the corresponding validity
claim.

The consequence layer agrees on the same fragment.
Certainty consequence requires conclusion value $1$ whenever all premises
have value $1$.
Positivity consequence requires a positive conclusion whenever all premises
are positive.
On crisp valuations, positive means true.
Lean records this in three theorems:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \texttt{crisp\_certainty\_iff\_classical},
\item \texttt{crisp\_positivity\_iff\_certainty},
\item \texttt{crisp\_positivity\_iff\_classical}.
\end{itemize}

Conditioning is where the agreement stops.
With evidence $1$, the admissible conditional is forced:
\[
  \Cond(j,1)=\{j\}.
\]
With evidence $0$ and joint $0$, every conditional value is admissible:
\[
  \Cond(0,0)=[0,1].
\]
Material implication picks the value true for every false antecedent.
Cred makes no such selection.
Four Lean theorems isolate that boundary:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \texttt{material\_divergence},
\item \texttt{vacuous\_truth\_admissible},
\item \texttt{empty\_antecedent\_underdetermined},
\item \texttt{cond\_underdetermined\_iff}.
\end{itemize}

\begin{proposition}[Crisp conservativity with one boundary]
On the formulas built from atoms, negation, conjunction, and disjunction,
the crisp Cred fragment is classical.
For conditionals, the classical truth table and Cred agree at true evidence.
At false evidence with zero joint, Cred returns the whole interval
$[0,1]$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
This is the first reason to keep implication and conditioning apart.
Classical proof by cases, double-negation elimination, excluded middle on
crisp values, and non-contradiction on crisp values survive.
The empty antecedent receives no extra theorem.
\end{remark}

\section{Paradoxes as Solution Sets}
\label{sec:solutions}
%=============================================================================

A self-reference pattern determines an operator
\[
  \Phi:[0,1]\to[0,1].
\]
Its admissible values form the solution set
\[
  \Sol(\Phi)=\{c\in[0,1]:\Phi(c)=c\}.
\]
This is the common shape behind the elementary paradox cases.
Lean defines this set as \texttt{solutions} and proves
\texttt{solutions\_eq\_fixedPoints}.

\subsection{Liar}

The scalar liar pattern is negation self-reference:
\[
  c=\negc c=1-c.
\]
Its solution set is the singleton
\[
  \Sol(\negc)=\{\tfrac12\}.
\]
Lean records this as \texttt{solutions\_neg\_eq\_singleton} and
\texttt{liar\_solution}.
The Boolean version has no fixed point:
\[
  \{b\in\Bool: \neg b=b\}=\varnothing,
\]
proved as \texttt{bool\_neg\_solutions\_empty}.

The contrast is useful.
The graded interval turns the classical contradiction into a determined
middle value.
It does not infer arbitrary formulas from that value.

\subsection{Truth-Teller}

The truth-teller pattern is identity self-reference:
\[
  c=c.
\]
Every value solves it:
\[
  \Sol(\mathsf{id})=[0,1].
\]
Lean proves \texttt{solutions\_id\_eq\_univ} and
\texttt{truth\_teller\_any}.
The solution set has the same shape as zero-evidence conditioning:
\[
  \Sol(\mathsf{id})=[0,1]=\Cond(0,0).
\]
The theorem \texttt{truth\_teller\_same\_shape\_as\_zero\_evidence}
records the equality.

The interpretation differs in the two cases.
For the truth-teller, the equation supplies no information.
For zero evidence, the chain rule supplies no conditional value.
Both cases are underdetermined because the constraint is the identity.

\subsection{Russell}

For a graded predicate $P:\alpha\to[0,1]$, negation is pointwise:
\[
  (\neg P)(x)=1-P(x).
\]
The Lean module \texttt{Cred.Predicate} develops graded predicates,
pointwise De Morgan operations, infimum and supremum quantifiers, and the
crisp quantifier bridges.

Russell's predicate has the scalar form
\[
  c=1-c.
\]
The theorem \texttt{russell\_fixed\_point} proves that any credence satisfying
that equation is $\tfrac12$.
The theorem \texttt{russell\_scalar\_solutions} states the solution-set form:
\[
  \{c:c=1-c\}=\{\tfrac12\}.
\]

This is only the scalar core of graded set theory.
A full set theory needs extensionality, comprehension, equality, and
substitution.
The scalar theorem says that Russell's diagonal equation has a value.
It does not by itself give a complete foundation.

\subsection{Curry}

Curry self-reference has a different shape.
It does not use negation.
It uses a conditional that can be internalized and then contracted.

If one adds the product residuum
\[
  a\Rightarrow b =
  \begin{cases}
    1, & a=0,\\
    \min(1,b/a), & a>0,
  \end{cases}
\]
then positive Curry equations have square-root solutions.
For $b>0$,
\[
  c = (c\Rightarrow b)
\]
is solved by $c=\sqrt b$.
Lean proves the witness, the positive nonempty result, and the empty
zero-boundary result in \texttt{Cred/Fixpoint.lean}.

This boundary matters.
The product residuum is an added connective.
It is not Cred conditioning.
Cred's conditional relation remains external:
\[
  c\in\Cond(j,e).
\]
That relation cannot be placed into a formula and diagonalized.

\section{Graded Comprehension}
\label{sec:comprehension}
%=============================================================================

Graded predicates give the minimal language for a foundations application.
Membership is a value in $[0,1]$.
A comprehension principle has the schematic form
\[
  \cred(x\in\{y:\varphi(y)\})=\cred(\varphi(x)).
\]
For Russell's predicate this reduces to the fixed-point equation above.

This puts Cred near several established programs.
White proved consistency results for unrestricted comprehension in
infinite-valued {\L}ukasiewicz predicate logic~\cite{white1979}.
H\'ajek, Paris, and Shepherdson analyzed fuzzy truth predicates and showed
how graded truth interacts with conservativity and model existence
\cite{hajek2000,hajek2000liar}.
Takeuti and Titani developed fuzzy set theory with many-valued membership
\cite{takeuti1984}.
Paraconsistent and paracomplete ZF-style theories such as BZFC treat
inconsistent and incomplete membership through four-valued semantics
\cite{khomskii2024}.

Cred should be compared to these systems at the level of contracts.
The present paper proves a scalar and predicate-level interface:
pointwise graded predicates, De Morgan laws, quantifier duality, crisp
quantifier recovery, and Russell's fixed point.
It does not claim a ZF replacement or a bi-interpretability theorem.

The crisp fragment supplies the recovery constraint.
When a predicate is crisp, its infimum is zero exactly when some instance is
zero, and its supremum is one exactly when some instance is one.
Lean records these as \texttt{crisp\_inf\_zero\_iff} and
\texttt{crisp\_sup\_one\_iff}.
The theorem \texttt{quantifier\_duality\_val} gives the graded duality:
\[
  \inf_x P(x)=1-\sup_x(1-P(x)).
\]

These theorems are small, but they set the right burden of proof.
A future graded foundation must specify extensionality, equality, and
substitution in a way that preserves the crisp fragment and keeps
conditioning external.
Russell alone is the entrance exam.
Curry is the harder test.

\section{Curry Without an Internal Conditional}
\label{sec:curry}
%=============================================================================

Curry's paradox warns against an internal conditional with too much structural
strength.
The dangerous package contains three principles.
For a binary operation $f$ on credences:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
\mathrm{MP}: & a\conjc f(a,b)\le b,\\[0.3em]
\mathrm{CP}: & a\conjc c\le b\ \Rightarrow\ c\le f(a,b),\\[0.3em]
\mathrm{Contr}: & f(a,f(a,b))\le f(a,b).
\end{array}
\]
Modus ponens and conditional proof amount to residuation for product
conjunction.
They force the positive part of $f$ to be the product residuum:
\[
  f(a,b)=\min(1,b/a)\qquad(a>0).
\]
Lean proves the residuation step as:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \texttt{mp\_cp\_residuation},
\item \texttt{residuation\_unique},
\item \texttt{prodResid\_unique\_positive}.
\end{itemize}

The product residuum fails contraction.
At $a=\tfrac12$ and $b=\tfrac14$,
\[
  a\Rightarrow b=\tfrac12,\qquad
  a\Rightarrow(a\Rightarrow b)=1.
\]
Thus
\[
  a\Rightarrow(a\Rightarrow b)\le a\Rightarrow b
\]
fails.
Lean proves this counterexample in the Curry bridge module.
The theorem \texttt{curry\_block} packages it:
no total operation on $[0,1]$ satisfies MP, CP, and contraction together.

This is the formal reason the title can say ``without explosion.''
Cred does not repair Curry after internalizing the conditional.
It refuses the internalization.
Conditioning enters as the external judgment
\[
  c\in\Cond(j,e),
\]
and consequence is a metalinguistic relation.
There is no formula constructor $\varphi\Rightarrow\psi$ whose value can be
fed back into its own antecedent.

The result matches the diagnosis in relevance and substructural logic:
contraction is not harmless in the presence of self-reference.
Cred gives a numerical version of the same obstruction.
The obstruction is machine-checked for the product carrier used here.

\section{Conditioning and Proof}
\label{sec:proof}
%=============================================================================

The proof layer has two axes.
The first is consequence.
For each threshold $t\in[0,1]$, define
\[
  \Gamma\models_t\varphi
\]
to mean: every valuation that gives all formulas in $\Gamma$ value at least
$t$ also gives $\varphi$ value at least $t$.
Lean defines this as \texttt{thresholdConsequence}.
It proves reflexivity, monotonicity, and cut as
\texttt{threshold\_reflexivity},
\texttt{threshold\_monotonicity}, and \texttt{threshold\_cut}.

The endpoints recover the earlier consequence relations.
At $t=1$ one obtains certainty consequence
(\texttt{threshold\_one\_iff\_certainty}).
Positivity consequence is the union of positive thresholds
(\texttt{formulaPositivity\_iff\_exists\_threshold}).

Two sharp bounds matter for proof design.
Explosion has a countermodel exactly when $0<t\le\tfrac12$.
Excluded middle holds for all credence values exactly when $t\le\tfrac34$.
Lean records these as
\texttt{threshold\_explosion\_countermodel\_iff} and
\texttt{threshold\_excluded\_middle\_iff}.
The interval $(\tfrac12,\tfrac34]$ therefore validates excluded middle while
losing the simple explosion countermodel used below $\tfrac12$.

The second axis is conditioning.
The labelled calculus treats conditioning as a side judgment:
\[
  c\in\Cond(j,e).
\]
The chain-rule cut has the form
\[
  c\in\Cond(j,e),\quad e\ge t
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  j=ce.
\]
At positive evidence this can be read as the usual ratio rule.
At zero evidence it remains a constraint, not an implication theorem.

Pavelka-style proof theory gives a mature comparison point for graded
provability~\cite{pavelka1979i,pavelka1979ii,pavelka1979iii}.
H\'ajek's product logic gives a mature comparison point for residuated
truth-functional implication~\cite{hajek1998}.
Cred takes neither calculus unchanged.
The no-go theorem for truth-functional conditioning in Part~2 and the Curry
block above force the proof calculus to keep conditioning external.

Lean formalizes this interface in \texttt{Cred.Sequent}.
Labels mark positive, certain, or threshold judgments.
Contexts carry formula bounds and external conditioning side judgments.
The inductive derivation relation has structural rules, imports already
verified consequence relations, and contains a chain-rule cut:
from $c\in\Cond(j,e)$, a lower bound on $c$, and a lower bound on $e$, derive
the product lower bound on $j$.
The soundness theorems target threshold consequence, positivity consequence,
and certainty consequence.
The theorem \texttt{labelled\_no\_ex\_falso} gives the proof-layer witness
that $A$ and $\neg A$ do not derive an unrelated positive conclusion.

Completeness remains outside the present claim.
That is deliberate.
A premature completeness theorem risks hiding a residuated implication inside
the rules.

\section{Underdetermination, Incompleteness, and Updates}
\label{sec:underdetermination}
%=============================================================================

Three notions must stay separate.

First, zero-evidence conditioning is underdetermination by the chain rule:
\[
  \Cond(0,0)=[0,1].
\]
The evidence value is zero, so the equation $ce=0$ imposes no restriction on
$c$.
This is the same boundary that appears in primitive conditional probability,
conditional events, disintegration, and imprecise probability
\cite{renyi1955,popper1959,definetti1936,calabrese1987,walley1991}.

Second, undecidability is proof-theoretic.
A theory may prove neither $\varphi$ nor $\neg\varphi$.
That does not assign $\varphi$ the value $\tfrac12$.
The value $\tfrac12$ is forced only by equations such as $c=1-c$.
Chosen ignorance, independence, and unproved statements require extra
modelling assumptions.

Third, updates are dynamics.
Bayesian and Jeffrey update rules from Part~2 handle positive evidence.
At zero evidence, a Cred update semantics should return a set or interval of
admissible posteriors unless an additional selection principle is supplied.
This aligns more closely with imprecise probability than with a single
regular conditional probability chosen on a null set.

These distinctions keep the foundations claim honest.
The algebra resolves certain self-reference equations.
It does not solve G\"odel incompleteness, choose values for independent
statements, or define a canonical posterior under impossible evidence.

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The slogan ``paradox without explosion'' has a precise reading here.
Paradoxical patterns are not all treated alike.
Negation self-reference has the singleton solution $\{\tfrac12\}$.
Identity self-reference has the full interval.
Zero-evidence conditioning has the full interval for a different reason:
the chain rule imposes no value.
Curry requires an internal conditional plus structural rules, and those rules
have no common total carrier on $[0,1]$.

Classical reasoning survives where it should.
The crisp fragment recovers Boolean evaluation and classical consequence.
Proof by contradiction remains available as countercase elimination inside
that fragment.
What disappears is the move from false or impossible evidence to an arbitrary
conclusion.

This leaves one substantial next task.
A full graded-foundations comparison must still settle extensionality,
equality, substitution, and graded comprehension against White, Takeuti and
Titani, H\'ajek--Paris--Shepherdson, and BZFC.
That task must preserve the same boundary:
conditioning constrains values through the chain rule; it is not implication.

\section{Related set theories and value structures}
\label{sec:related}

This section positions Cred against three families of non-classical systems that
share its concern with contradiction, partiality, and graded or non-Boolean
membership. The aim is comparison of design, not a claim of superiority. We
separate throughout what Cred \emph{proves} (machine-checked Lean results, named
below) from what is a structural analogy with prior work.

\subsection{First Degree Entailment and the Belnap--Dunn four values}

First Degree Entailment, in the Belnap--Dunn reading \cite{belnap1977,dunn1976},
equips formulas with one of four values---$\mathbf{t}$ (true only), $\mathbf{f}$
(false only), $\mathbf{b}$ (both), $\mathbf{n}$ (neither)---tracking told-true and
told-false evidence as two independent bits. Collapsing the two paraconsistent
values $\mathbf{b},\mathbf{n}$ to a single middle value recovers the
three-valued logics of Kleene (strong K3) and Priest (LP)
\cite{priest1979,asenjo1966,priest2008}.

Cred sits below this picture as a quantitative refinement. The scalar fragment
takes values in $[0,1]$ with negation $\negc c = 1-c$, conjunction
$c_1 \conjc c_2$ (product), and the De Morgan dual $c_1 \disjc c_2$. The
collapse onto $\{0,\tfrac12,1\}$ sending $0 \mapsto 0$, $(0,1)\mapsto \tfrac12$,
$1\mapsto 1$ reproduces the Kleene tables for negation, conjunction, and
disjunction; this is verified in \texttt{Cred.ThreeVal} (\texttt{neg},
\texttt{conj}, \texttt{disj}, with the De Morgan laws \texttt{de\_morgan\_conj},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_disj} and the diagonal facts \texttt{conj\_half\_half},
\texttt{disj\_half\_half}). The scalar middle value $\tfrac12$ is a single point,
so it conflates ``both'' and ``neither'': one coordinate cannot record told-true
and told-false strength separately.

To separate them, Cred adds a two-coordinate value \texttt{BiCredence}, a pair
$\langle \mathrm{pos}, \mathrm{neg}\rangle$ of independent credences with no
constraint relating them. Negation swaps coordinates (\texttt{bneg}),
conjunction multiplies positive evidence and joins negative evidence
(\texttt{bconj}), and disjunction is the De Morgan dual (\texttt{bdisj}); negation
is involutive (\texttt{bneg\_bneg}). The four corners $\langle 0,0\rangle$,
$\langle 1,0\rangle$, $\langle 0,1\rangle$, $\langle 1,1\rangle$ are named
\texttt{neither}, \texttt{trueOnly}, \texttt{falseOnly}, \texttt{both}, and the
map \texttt{toFDE}, thresholding each coordinate at certainty, sends them to the
four FDE values exactly (\texttt{toFDE\_neither}, \texttt{toFDE\_trueOnly},
\texttt{toFDE\_falseOnly}, \texttt{toFDE\_both}). Negation acts on the corners as
in FDE: it swaps \texttt{trueOnly} and \texttt{falseOnly} and fixes \texttt{both}
and \texttt{neither} (\texttt{bneg\_trueOnly}, \texttt{bneg\_falseOnly},
\texttt{bneg\_both}, \texttt{bneg\_neither}). The positive projection
\texttt{projPos} is a homomorphism for conjunction and disjunction
(\texttt{projPos\_bconj}, \texttt{projPos\_bdisj}); it commutes with negation only
on the consistent fragment where $\mathrm{neg} = \negc\,\mathrm{pos}$
(\texttt{projPos\_bneg\_complementary}), which is exactly where the scalar value
and the FDE bilattice agree. So Cred refines FDE in two directions: the scalar
$[0,1]$ grades each FDE corner, while \texttt{BiCredence} keeps the two evidence
coordinates apart that the single $\tfrac12$ would merge.

\subsection{Paraconsistent set theory}

Paraconsistent and four-valued set theories build ZF-style universes over a
non-classical membership predicate, so that $a \in b$ may take a both-true-and-false
value without trivializing the theory; the algebra-valued constructions of
Tarafder and Chakraborty are representative \cite{tarafder2015,chakraborty2019}.
Cred's set layer, \texttt{CredSet}, is graded rather than four-valued: membership
is a credence \texttt{mem}\,$: U \to [0,1]$, with complement, intersection, and
union lifting the value operations pointwise (\texttt{compl}, \texttt{inter},
\texttt{union}) and \texttt{emptyset}, \texttt{univ} as the constant $0$ and $1$
sets.

The contrast is in where contradiction lives and how the classical theory is
recovered. A paraconsistent set theory locates inconsistency \emph{in a membership
value}---a designated ``both'' that is simultaneously a member and a non-member.
Cred has no such value: membership is a single number in $[0,1]$, and
contradiction is instead localized at the level of \emph{consequence}, by the
chain-rule conditioning that withholds explosion when evidence has credence $0$
(\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}; cf. \texttt{cred\_no\_ex\_falso}). Comprehension
is treated as constraint rather than as a value: a defining condition cuts out the
solution set of credences compatible with it, in the spirit of separation, without
forcing a membership degree where the condition is unsatisfiable. Classical set
theory is recovered exactly on the \emph{crisp} fragment, where every membership
value is $0$ or $1$ (\texttt{Crisp}). On crisp sets the graded operations restrict
to the Boolean ones and stay crisp (\texttt{compl\_crisp}, \texttt{inter\_crisp},
\texttt{union\_crisp}); the embedding \texttt{ofPred} of a predicate and its inverse
\texttt{toPred} form a bijection (\texttt{toPred\_ofPred}, \texttt{ofPred\_toPred},
\texttt{ofPred\_injective}, \texttt{ofPred\_surjective}) commuting with complement,
intersection, and union (\texttt{toPred\_compl}, \texttt{toPred\_inter},
\texttt{toPred\_union}); the pointwise subset relation becomes classical
implication of memberships (\texttt{crisp\_subset\_iff}); and graded membership
$\mathtt{mem}\,x = 1$ coincides with classical membership
(\texttt{mem\_iff\_classical}, \texttt{crisp\_mem\_iff\_toPred}). Cred thus
reproduces ordinary set theory on crisp data and confines non-classicality to
graded membership and to conditioning, rather than to an inconsistent membership
value.

\subsection{Twist-valued models and twist structures}

Twist structures present a paraconsistent algebra as pairs $\langle a, b\rangle$
drawn from an underlying lattice, with one coordinate tracking the positive part
and the other the negative; algebra-valued models of set theory have been built on
such structures by L\"owe, Tarafder, and collaborators \cite{loewe2015,tarafder2015}.
Cred's \texttt{BiCredence} is a twist-like construction over the credence algebra:
its two coordinates are the positive and negative credence, negation is the
coordinate swap (\texttt{bneg}), and conjunction and disjunction pair a product
meet on the positive axis with a De Morgan join on the negative axis
(\texttt{bconj}, \texttt{bdisj}). We state the relationship conservatively. Cred
does not claim to reproduce any particular twist-valued model of set theory, nor to
validate the same fixed set of axioms; what is verified is the algebra of the
two-coordinate value, its involutive negation, the homomorphic positive projection,
and the exact collapse to the four FDE values. The point of contact is structural:
the twist idea of carrying positive and negative information as separate
coordinates is exactly what \texttt{BiCredence} uses to recover the FDE distinctions
that the scalar value cannot express, and what a future graded set theory over
\texttt{BiCredence}-valued membership could build on.
