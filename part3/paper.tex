\begin{abstract}
Parts~1--2 established the credence algebra on $[0,1]$ with primitive
conditioning.
This paper develops three application areas:
(1)~graded set theory, where the negation fixed point resolves Russell's
paradox;
(2)~evidence-zero conditioning, positioned relative to disintegration,
imprecise probability, and conditional events;
(3)~self-reference, contrasted with Kripke and revision-theoretic fixed points.
We survey connections to Pavelka-style graded proof theory and outline open
directions including modal extensions and categorical perspectives.
\end{abstract}

%=============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Part~1~\cite{part1} proved two results about truth-functional conjunctions
on $[0,1]$.
First, $\min(a,b)$ is the unique conjunction satisfying Bayes consistency,
non-trivial conditioning, and idempotence---this forces the product
De~Morgan triplet (complement $1-c$, conjunction $\min$, disjunction $\max$).
Second, the conditional's limit as evidence approaches zero is
\emph{path-dependent}: ex falso is not forced by the algebra.
Part~2~\cite{part2} built the reasoning layer: valuations, consequence
relations, threshold semantics, and update rules on this foundation.

Part~3 develops three application areas enabled by these results, and surveys
two further directions.

\paragraph{Graded set theory.}
The negation fixed point $c = \negc c \Rightarrow c = \tfrac{1}{2}$,
proved in Part~1, gives a natural resolution of Russell's paradox:
the Russell set has credence $\tfrac{1}{2}$ of containing itself.
Section~\ref{sec:sets} develops graded comprehension and positions the
algebra within the Takeuti--Titani tradition of many-valued set
theory~\cite{takeuti1984}.

\paragraph{Evidence-zero conditioning.}
The chain rule $\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ is
well-formed at $\cred(B) = 0$ and forces only the joint to be zero; it
does not determine the conditional.
Section~\ref{sec:zero} places this underdetermination in the context of
primitive conditional probability (R\'enyi, Popper), conditional events
(de~Finetti, Calabrese), disintegration (Chang--Pollard), imprecise
probability (Walley), and probabilistic programming languages.

\paragraph{Self-reference.}
Section~\ref{sec:selfref} positions the Part~1 fixed point relative to
Kripke's monotone fixed-point construction~\cite{kripke1975} and
Gupta--Belnap revision theory~\cite{gupta1993}, clarifying that the
algebraic fixed point is a base case rather than a wholesale solution to
semantic paradox.

\paragraph{Further directions.}
Section~\ref{sec:proof} surveys connections to Pavelka-style graded proof
theory~\cite{pavelka1979i}, distinguishing semantic threshold consequence
(Part~2) from proof systems with provability degrees.
Section~\ref{sec:open} outlines open directions: modal extensions,
categorical perspectives, and computational aspects.

\paragraph{Notation.}
We write $\negc c$ for negation, $c_1 \conjc c_2$ for conjunction,
$c_1 \disjc c_2$ for disjunction, and $\condc{A}{B}$ for the conditional
credence of $A$ given $B$.

\section{Graded Set Theory}
\label{sec:sets}
%=============================================================================

The most immediate application of the $\tfrac{1}{2}$ fixed point is to set
theory.
Russell's paradox arises from the set $R = \{x : x \notin x\}$: asking
whether $R \in R$ leads to contradiction in classical logic.
In a graded setting, the paradox resolves naturally.

\subsection{Russell's Paradox Resolution}

The Russell predicate is $R(x) = \negc(x \in x)$.
Applied to itself: $R(R) = \negc(R \in R)$.
Let $c = \cred(R \in R)$.
Then $c = \negc c = 1 - c$, which gives $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
The Russell set has credence $\tfrac{1}{2}$ of containing itself.
This is neither full membership nor full non-membership---it is the unique
fixed point of negation.
The paradox is not \emph{solved} in the sense of being eliminated; it is
\emph{accommodated} by the algebra's fixed-point structure.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Graded Comprehension}

In classical set theory, comprehension says: for any property $\phi(x)$,
there exists a set $\{x : \phi(x)\}$.
Unrestricted comprehension leads to Russell's paradox.

In graded set theory, membership becomes graded:
$\cred(x \in A)$ takes values in $[0,1]$.
The comprehension axiom becomes:
\[
\cred(x \in \{y : \phi(y)\}) = \cred(\phi(x))
\]
This does not require restriction to ``safe'' properties---the graded setting
handles self-reference via fixed points rather than exclusion.

\subsection{The Takeuti--Titani Context}

There is a substantial tradition of ``many-valued mathematics'' in which
set-theoretic foundations are valued in $[0,1]$ or other
lattices~\cite{takeuti1984,zadeh1965}.
Takeuti and Titani develop a many-valued set theory based on fuzzy logic and
study standard set-theoretic issues---extensionality, comprehension, and the
interpretation of membership---in that setting.

The uniqueness result from Part~1 provides a canonical choice for graded
membership operations: the minimum conjunction $\min(a,b)$ is not merely one
option among many t-norms, but the unique truth-functional conjunction
compatible with probabilistic marginalization.
This narrows the design space for graded set theory to a single algebraically
forced choice of connectives, resolving an ambiguity that persists in the
general fuzzy-set-theory literature where the choice of t-norm is typically
a modelling decision~\cite{klement2000}.

\section{Evidence Zero and Null Conditioning}
\label{sec:zero}
%=============================================================================

The evidence-zero boundary is where the credence algebra's design choice---
leaving the conditional underdetermined when $\cred(B) = 0$---makes contact
with several established research programs.
The chain rule is well-formed at evidence zero and forces only the joint to be
zero; it does \emph{not} choose a specific conditional value.
This is closely related to the classical ambiguity of ratios $j/a$ as
$(j,a) \to (0,0)$: the limiting value depends on the relative rate at which
$j$ decays compared to $a$, and no single value is canonical.

\subsection{Primitive Conditional Probability}

R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} and Popper~\cite{popper1959} develop axiom systems
where conditional probability is primitive and the chain rule is central,
rather than defining conditional probability as a ratio.
Van~Fraassen develops and applies Popper-style approaches to the analysis of
probabilities of conditionals and to the connection between probability and
relevant logic~\cite{vanfraassen1976,vanfraassen1983}.

The credence algebra's conditioning is directly aligned with these
traditions: the chain rule $\condc{A}{B} \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is taken as a primitive constraint rather than derived from division.
The key difference is that the algebra makes underdetermination at evidence
zero an explicit first-class feature, whereas R\'enyi and Popper impose
additional structure (e.g., a family of conditional probability spaces) to
resolve it.

\subsection{Conditional Events}

De~Finetti treats conditional events as three-valued (true/false/void) in a
betting/coherence setting, providing an explicit semantics for the
antecedent-false case that avoids vacuous truth~\cite{definetti1936,definetti1937}.
Calabrese formalizes conditional events algebraically as Boolean fractions and
analyzes their operations~\cite{calabrese1987}.
The modern logic of indicative conditionals has revisited de~Finetti-style
tables and their proof theory; see, e.g., the De~Finettian logics DF/TT and
related systems studied by \'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021}.

More recently, the coherence-based literature develops conditional random
quantities and operations on conditionals, including conjunction and iterated
conditioning~\cite{gilio2014,gilio2013}.
Coletti and Scozzafava provide a broad synthesis of probabilistic logic in a
coherence setting and devote substantial attention to probability-zero events
and conditionalization at the boundary case~\cite{coletti2002}.

These works are especially relevant because they show how to reason coherently
when antecedents have probability zero \emph{without} forcing a single
truth-functional value, and because they explicitly separate:
(i)~the algebra of values, (ii)~the event structure, and (iii)~the rules
governing coherence and extension.

\subsection{Disintegration and Categorical Conditioning}

Standard measure theory uses regular conditional probabilities and
disintegration to define conditional probabilities with null evidence, with
uniqueness only up to almost-sure equivalence~\cite{chang1997}.
Category-theoretic approaches (e.g., Markov categories) treat conditioning
structurally rather than as division, making clear which parts come from
additional structure beyond the chain rule itself~\cite{fritz2020}.

The algebra's underdetermination at evidence zero corresponds precisely to
the non-uniqueness in disintegration: both recognize that the chain rule alone
does not select a canonical conditional when the evidence has measure zero.

\subsection{Imprecise Probability}

Imprecise probability develops a complementary view: at the boundary, one may
return \emph{sets} or \emph{intervals} of admissible conditionals rather than
a single number~\cite{walley1991,troffaes2014}.
The algebra's evidence-zero underdetermination sits naturally with this
perspective and suggests a set-valued update semantics as one viable
formalization.

\subsection{Probabilistic Programming}

Probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) make conditioning a primitive
operation.
In PPLs like Stan, PyMC, or Pyro, users write generative models and the
language handles conditioning automatically.
The chain rule $P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B) = P(A \land B)$ is the semantic
backbone.

The path-dependence result from Part~1 has direct implications for PPL
semantics at measure-zero observations:
continuous observations have probability zero, and standard conditioning uses
densities rather than probabilities.
The algebra's underdetermination at zero corresponds to the need for explicit
density specifications---one must choose a particular parameterization
(Lebesgue density, counting measure, etc.) to pin down the conditional.

Bayesian statistics sometimes uses improper priors (non-normalizable
measures).
The algebra's treatment of evidence-zero conditioning---underdetermined
rather than forced to any particular value---parallels this: there is no
canonical posterior when the prior is improper.

\section{Self-Reference in Context}
\label{sec:selfref}
%=============================================================================

The most visible self-reference result from Part~1 is the forced negation
fixed point: $c = \negc c$ implies $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
This is algebraic---it follows from the complement operation on $[0,1]$---and
should be positioned relative to the established fixed-point traditions
for truth and paradox.

\subsection{Kripke Fixed Points}

Kripke's fixed-point theory of truth uses monotone operators and fixed points
to give consistent semantics for truth predicates, building up a maximal
consistent extension from an initial ``ground'' model~\cite{kripke1975}.
The construction is proof-theoretic: it proceeds through transfinite stages,
and the choice of evaluation scheme (strong Kleene, weak Kleene, or
supervaluational) affects which sentences receive truth values at the fixed
point.

The credence algebra's fixed point is different in character: it is a single
algebraic equation ($c = 1 - c$) with a unique solution, rather than an
iterative construction.
The Kripke fixed point assigns truth-value gaps to liar-like sentences; the
credence algebra assigns the definite value $\tfrac{1}{2}$.

\subsection{Revision Theory}

Gupta--Belnap revision theory models truth as evolving under revisions:
a revision operator maps one extension of the truth predicate to
another, and the semantics is given by the long-run behavior of revision
sequences~\cite{gupta1993}.
This yields dynamical semantics for self-reference---truth values may
oscillate, stabilize, or exhibit more complex patterns.

The credence algebra's treatment of self-reference has no revision dynamics:
the fixed point is immediate.
This is appropriate for the algebraic base case (negation self-application)
but does not address the richer phenomena that revision theory
captures---iterated revision, mutual reference, and contingent
stabilization.

\subsection{Many-Valued Approaches}

Many-valued logics have their own tradition of analyzing paradoxes and
vagueness through graded truth values~\cite{bergmann2008}.
The credence algebra connects to this tradition through its product
De~Morgan structure and the collapse homomorphism to the Kleene lattice
established in Part~1.

\begin{remark}[Scope of the fixed point]
Part~3 should treat the Part~1 fixed point as the \emph{base case}---the
simplest instance of self-reference resolved by the algebraic structure.
It is not a wholesale solution to semantic paradox, and the relationship to
Kripke and revision-theoretic approaches is one of complementarity rather
than replacement.
\end{remark}

\section{Toward Graded Proof Theory}
\label{sec:proof}
%=============================================================================

Part~2 provides consequence relations (threshold semantics), but no proof
calculus with graded derivability.
There is an important distinction between two notions of ``graded proof'':

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),nosep]
\item A \emph{semantic threshold consequence relation}, as developed in
Part~2, where $A \models_\alpha B$ means that every valuation assigning
credence $\ge \alpha$ to $A$ also assigns credence $\ge \alpha$ to $B$.

\item A \emph{proof system with provability degrees}, in the sense of
Pavelka~\cite{pavelka1979i,pavelka1979ii,pavelka1979iii}, where theorems
carry graded provability values and completeness is formulated in terms
of these degrees.
\end{enumerate}

Part~2 addresses~(a).
The question for Part~3 is whether the credence algebra supports a natural
instance of~(b).

\subsection{Pavelka-Style Graded Provability}

Pavelka's 1979 series develops many-valued rules of inference, semantics via
enriched residuated lattices, and semantic completeness for many-valued
calculi~\cite{pavelka1979i,pavelka1979ii,pavelka1979iii}.
The key idea is that a proof calculus assigns a \emph{degree of provability}
$\|\varphi\|$ to each formula, and completeness requires
$\|\varphi\| = \|\varphi\|^*$ where the right-hand side is the semantic truth
degree.

H\'ajek's metamathematics of fuzzy logic organizes the ``big three''
continuous t-norm logics---\L ukasiewicz, G\"odel, and product---within the
BL-algebra framework~\cite{hajek1998}.
Product logic is a fully developed many-valued logic: it is $[0,1]$-valued,
its implication is the product residuum (Goguen
implication)~\cite{goguen1969}, and it validates a graded form of modus
ponens via the adjointness relationship $a \conjc f(a,b) \le b$.

The credence algebra's logic-style instantiation with joint
$j(a,b) = \min(a,b)$ agrees with product logic \emph{for positive evidence}:
for $a > 0$, the induced conditional $f(a,b) = \min(b/a, 1)$ is exactly the
product residuum.
The divergence is at the evidence-zero boundary: product logic sets
$f(0,b) = 1$ (ex falso / vacuous truth), while the credence algebra treats
evidence-zero conditioning as underdetermined by design.

\subsection{Proof-Theoretic Targets}

A graded proof theory for the credence algebra would need to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item specify a minimal proof calculus for a chosen consequence relation
(e.g., a threshold semantics on $[0,1]$ or a designated-values semantics
after collapse to the Kleene lattice);
\item prove soundness and, ideally, completeness with respect to the
Part~2 semantics;
\item clarify how cut elimination works in the graded setting.
\end{enumerate}

The algebraic semantics literature on residuated lattices and substructural
logics provides the natural framework for this
investigation~\cite{galatos2007,cintula2011}.

\section{Open Directions}
\label{sec:open}
%=============================================================================

Beyond the application areas developed above, several directions merit
investigation.

\subsection{Modal Extensions}

Modal logic (necessity, possibility) has natural graded analogues.
One can consider $\cred(\Box A)$ (credence that $A$ is necessary) and
$\cred(\Diamond A)$ (credence that $A$ is possible), with modal axioms
becoming constraints on how these credences relate.
The S5 axiom $\Box A \to A$, for instance, becomes the constraint
$\cred(\Box A) \le \cred(A)$.
The interaction between modality, conditioning, and the evidence-zero
boundary is unexplored.

\subsection{Categorical Perspectives}

The categorical treatment of probability via Markov
categories~\cite{fritz2020} provides structural perspectives on conditioning.
The uniqueness theorem from Part~1 may have categorical characterizations:
the minimum conjunction as a universal property, or the chain rule as a
natural transformation.
The connection between categorical conditioning (morphisms in a Markov
category) and algebraic conditioning (the chain rule on $[0,1]$) deserves
investigation.

\subsection{Computational Aspects}

Questions of complexity and tractability arise naturally: what is the
complexity of determining whether a credence assignment is consistent with a
set of constraints?
Can credence values be computed efficiently in specific fragments?
The collapse homomorphism to the Kleene lattice (Part~1) suggests that
finite approximations may be tractable even when the full $[0,1]$-valued
problem is not.

\subsection{Graded Type Theory}

The connection between graded set membership and type membership is
suggestive but underdeveloped.
Gradual typing~\cite{siek2006} uses a dynamic type to represent unknown type
information, and type consistency mediates between statically and dynamically
typed code.
Whether graded type membership ($\cred(e : T) \in [0,1]$) can be given a
rigorous semantics aligned with the credence algebra remains open.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The literature map developed in this paper suggests a clean synthesis:

\begin{quote}
The credence algebra isolates the chain rule as an algebraic constraint on
conditioning and combines it with the product De~Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$;
it is compatible with both (i)~truth-functional many-valued logics (when a
fixed joint is chosen) and (ii)~coherence/probability-style semantics (when
joints are supplied externally).
Its distinguishing choice is to leave the evidence-zero boundary
underdetermined by default, in line with conditional-event and
imprecise-probability viewpoints, rather than forcing ex falso via
residuation.
\end{quote}

This synthesis clarifies which parts of the framework are inherited
(t-norm logic, primitive conditional probability, conditional
events/coherence) and which parts are a deliberate stance (evidence-zero
underdetermination as a first-class feature rather than an exception to be
patched).

\paragraph{Roadmap targets.}
The concrete next steps for this program are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),nosep]
\item \textbf{A graded proof theory layer:} specify a minimal proof calculus
for a chosen consequence relation (e.g., a threshold semantics on $[0,1]$ or
a designated-values semantics after collapse) and prove soundness with
respect to the Part~2 semantics.
\item \textbf{A null-evidence update semantics:} formalize at least one
update mechanism that extends Part~2 updates to the evidence-zero boundary
(likely set-valued/interval-valued), and relate it to coherence and imprecise
probability.
\item \textbf{A self-reference interface:} state precisely what the Part~1
fixed-point result does and does not address, with explicit comparison to
Kripke and revision-theoretic fixed points.
\item \textbf{A graded-foundations worked example:} pick a concrete fragment
(e.g., graded predicates over a finite domain, or a tiny graded set fragment)
and work through extensionality/comprehension choices, using the many-valued
set theory literature to avoid reinventing known pitfalls.
\end{enumerate}
