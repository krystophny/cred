\begin{abstract}
Probability theory and many-valued logic share the same algebraic core
on $[0,1]$ --- product conjunction, probabilistic-sum disjunction,
standard complement --- but diverge on the conditional:
probability requires division (undefined at zero evidence);
product logic uses residuation (forcing ex falso at zero evidence).
We study \emph{chain-rule conditioning}, where the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$:
at positive evidence it reduces to Bayesian conditioning; at zero
evidence it leaves the conditional unconstrained.
The product De Morgan triplet admits a natural collapse to the
Kleene lattice $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, which we use to establish
a \emph{bridge theorem}: LP consequence on the Kleene lattice is
equivalent to positivity consequence on $[0,1]$, and K$_3$ consequence
is equivalent to certainty consequence, both at the level of
propositional formulas.
The bridge operates through the collapse homomorphism on the
connectives; chain-rule conditioning provides the complementary
mechanism for reasoning under uncertainty, connecting Bayesian update
to the same algebra without forcing ex falso.
A congruence classification separates
$[0,1]$-multiplication (Kleene quotient exists; four-or-more classes
remain open) from $\mathbb{R}$-multiplication (no non-trivial finite
quotient), and graded consequence exhibits a sharp no-explosion
threshold at $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib
(3018 lines across five modules, zero \texttt{sorry}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

You believe it will rain with credence $0.8$.
You see the street is wet.
How should your credence in rain change?
Both probability theory and many-valued logic answer this question
using values in $[0,1]$ --- but they answer it differently when the
evidence is impossible.

\emph{Probability theory}~\cite{ramsey1931,definetti1937,jaynes2003}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of belief.
Given $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$, a
probabilist computes the conditional via division:
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) = \cred(\text{rain} \land
\text{wet}) / \cred(\text{wet})$.
When $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0$ --- you have never seen a wet street ---
the ratio $0/0$ is undefined.

\emph{Many-valued logic}~\cite{hajek1998,gottwald2001,klement2000}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of truth.
Given a t-norm conjunction and the residuated
implication~\cite{galatos2007}
$a \to b = \sup\{c : a \otimes c \leq b\}$, the conditional at
evidence zero is forced: $0 \to b = 1$ for all $b$, since
$0 \otimes c = 0 \leq b$ for every $c$.
Impossible evidence proves everything --- ex falso quodlibet is built
in~\cite{hajek1998}.

Both traditions share a core algebraic structure when the t-norm is
the product: the \emph{product De Morgan triplet}
$(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ with $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$,
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$, and $\negc c = 1 - c$.
They diverge only in the status of the conditional.

This paper studies \emph{chain-rule conditioning}:
the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ without
invoking either division or residuation.
When evidence is positive, it reduces to division (recovering
Bayesian conditioning); when evidence is zero, the equation
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ is satisfied by every $c \in [0,1]$, leaving the
conditional unconstrained.
The chain rule is the sole additional axiom beyond the triplet:
we assume only $(\negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ on $[0,1]$ plus the
chain-rule equation.
The ``bridge'' in the title has two senses: the bridge theorem
(Section~\ref{sec:bridge}) connects three-valued logical consequence
to continuous $[0,1]$ consequence via the collapse homomorphism;
chain-rule conditioning connects Bayesian probabilistic reasoning to
the same algebraic framework.
Together they bridge probability and many-valued logic from two
complementary directions.

\textbf{Main results.}
We mark each result as \emph{known} (established in the literature,
newly Lean-verified) or \emph{new} (first appearing here, to our
knowledge).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Bridge theorem} [new]:
  LP consequence on the three-valued Kleene lattice is equivalent to
  positivity consequence on $[0,1]$; K$_3$ consequence is equivalent
  to certainty consequence, both at the level of propositional formulas
  (Theorems~\ref{thm:lp-formula-bridge}--\ref{thm:k3-formula-bridge}).
  Discrete logical reasoning and continuous $[0,1]$ reasoning are
  inter-simulable; the bridge transfers decidability (via a finite
  model property), paraconsistency, and paracompleteness
  between the two frameworks
  (Section~\ref{sec:bridge-consequences}).
\item \textbf{Algebraic fixed points}
  [known~\cite{klement2000,kripke1975}, verified here]:
  the values $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are exactly the
  conjunction-idempotent values together with the unique negation
  fixed point (Proposition~\ref{thm:equilibria}).
\item \textbf{Kleene collapse and congruence classification} [new]:
  the Kleene lattice is the unique non-trivial three-element quotient
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}); the congruence classification
  separates $[0,1]$- from $\Real$-multiplication
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}).
\item \textbf{Graded no-explosion} [new]:
  explosion fails for any threshold $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:graded-no-explosion});
  for $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$, explosion holds vacuously
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:graded-sharp}).
  The threshold $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is sharp.
\item \textbf{Collapse composition} [new]:
  composing an independent valuation with the collapse yields a
  Kleene valuation, and every Kleene valuation lifts to $[0,1]$
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-composition}, Lemma~\ref{lem:lifting}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Literature positioning.}
H\'ajek~\cite{hajek1998} and
Esteva--Godo~\cite{esteva2000,esteva2001} developed Product Logic
with the same t-norm but residuated implication (forcing ex falso);
the Handbook of Mathematical Fuzzy Logic~\cite{cintula2011} provides
a comprehensive treatment of the landscape.
Mundici~\cite{mundici2011} and
Flaminio--Montagna~\cite{flaminio2009} studied probability on
MV-algebras (\L ukasiewicz, not product).
Calabrese~\cite{calabrese1987} gave an algebraic synthesis of logic
and probability; Coletti and
Scozzafava~\cite{coletti2002} developed probabilistic logic in a
coherent setting; Gilio and
Sanfilippo~\cite{gilio2013,gilio2014} studied conditional events
and iterated conditioning.
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955}, Popper~\cite{popper1959}, and
de Finetti~\cite{definetti1936} proposed primitive conditioning in
measure-theoretic or Boolean settings;
\'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021} developed
de Finetti's conditional algebraically.
Adams~\cite{adams1975} developed a probabilistic logic of
conditionals; van Fraassen~\cite{vanfraassen1976,vanfraassen1983}
explored connections between probabilities and conditionals in
relevant logic.
Pavelka~\cite{pavelka1979i} developed graded inference for
many-valued logics with truth values on $[0,1]$;
B\v{e}hounek and Cintula~\cite{behounek2006} characterized fuzzy
logics via degree-preserving consequence on chains.
Suszko~\cite{suszko1977} argued that every Tarskian consequence
relation is logically bivalent (designated vs.\ undesignated);
Malinowski~\cite{malinowski1993} provided a systematic treatment;
Font, Jansana, and Pigozzi~\cite{font2003} surveyed the abstract
algebraic logic framework.
Avron~\cite{avron1991} gave a systematic classification and proof
theory for three-valued logics including LP and K$_3$.
Ferm\"uller~\cite{fermuller2014} reached the Kleene lattice from
game semantics; Gaines~\cite{gaines1978} unified fuzzy and
probabilistic uncertainty via population semantics.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed plausible
reasoning satisfying certain desiderata must be isomorphic to
probability (see also Paris~\cite{paris1994} for a mathematical
perspective on uncertain reasoning);
chain-rule conditioning drops the requirement that the
conditional be a function of the marginals alone.
Lewis's triviality result~\cite{lewis1976} shows that if one
treats $P(A \mid B)$ as the probability of a ``conditional
proposition'' $A \to B$ in the same algebra, then the probability
measure must be trivial.
Chain-rule conditioning avoids Lewis's conditions: the joint
$\cred(A \land B)$ is a separate parameter (not derived from the
conditional), and $\cred(A \mid B)$ is not identified with the
credence of any proposition.
Halpern~\cite{halpern2003} provides a detailed analysis of the
scope of Lewis's result.
The novelty of this work lies in the synthesis and formalization,
not in any single deep theorem: individual ingredients (chain-rule
conditioning, the Kleene collapse, degree-preserving consequence)
are known or elementary.
The contribution is to combine them into a single framework on
$[0,1]$ with machine-checked proofs, yielding the bridge theorem,
the congruence classification, and the connection between
Bayesian update and paraconsistent logical consequence.

\section{The Product De Morgan Triplet}
\label{sec:algebra}
%=============================================================================

This section defines the concrete structure we study: the product
De Morgan triplet~\cite{klement2000,esteva2000} on $[0,1]$.
This is not an axiomatic class of algebras: all properties follow from
the specific arithmetic of $[0,1]$.
Goguen~\cite{goguen1969} used the product t-norm as the conjunction in
his logic of inexact concepts.

\textbf{Why the product?}
Among the three basic continuous t-norms (product, G\"odel, \L
ukasiewicz; Appendix~\ref{app:prelim}), the product is the unique
one that: (i)~shares the conjunction of independent probability
($P(A \land B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$), (ii)~has non-trivial dynamics
under self-conjunction ($c^2 < c$ for $c \in (0,1)$), and (iii)~admits
exactly three algebraic fixed points $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
(Section~\ref{sec:equilibria}).
G\"odel's min is idempotent (no dynamics); \L ukasiewicz's
self-conjunction destroys the $\tfrac{1}{2}$ fixed point
(Table~\ref{tab:tnorm}).

\begin{definition}[Credence]
\label{def:credence}
A \emph{credence} is a real number $c \in [0, 1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Running Example: Rain and Wet Streets]
\label{ex:rain}
Let $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$.
Then $\cred(\text{no rain}) = 1 - 0.8 = 0.2$, and the product
conjunction gives $\cred(\text{rain}) \conjc \cred(\text{wet}) =
0.8 \times 0.9 = 0.72$.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Negation]
\label{def:neg}
$\negc c = 1 - c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Product Conjunction]
\label{def:conj}
$c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

The product is the product t-norm of fuzzy logic~\cite{klement2000}.
Note that $\conjc$ is not idempotent: $0.8^2 = 0.64 \neq 0.8$.
Repeating the same uncertain evidence \emph{decreases} the joint
credence.
Only $0$ and $1$ are conjunction-idempotent ($c^2 = c$).

\begin{definition}[Disjunction]
\label{def:disj}
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = \negc(\negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2)
  = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

In our running example, the De Morgan dual gives
$\negc(\negc 0.8 \conjc \negc 0.9) =
0.8 + 0.9 - 0.8 \times 0.9 = 0.98$.
This uses the product of the marginals, which equals the
joint $\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet})$ only under independence
(see Remark~\ref{rem:product-vs-joint}).

The triple $(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ is the \emph{product De Morgan
triplet}~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998}.

\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Properties; known~\cite{klement2000},
verified here]
\label{thm:algebraic}
For all $c, c_1, c_2, c_3 \in [0,1]$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\negc\negc c = c$ \hfill (involution)
\item $\negc 0 = 1$, $\negc 1 = 0$ \hfill (boundary)
\item $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_2 \conjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \conjc c_2) \conjc c_3 = c_1 \conjc (c_2 \conjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \conjc 1 = c$, $c \conjc 0 = 0$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_2 \disjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \disjc c_2) \disjc c_3 = c_1 \disjc (c_2 \disjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \disjc 0 = c$, $c \disjc 1 = 1$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $\negc(c_1 \conjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \disjc \negc c_2$,
  $\negc(c_1 \disjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2$ \hfill (De Morgan)
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{neg\_neg}, \texttt{conj\_comm}, \texttt{conj\_assoc},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_conj}, \texttt{de\_morgan\_disj}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Arithmetic on $[0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The structure is two dual commutative monoids connected by De Morgan
duality; it is not a De Morgan algebra since $\conjc$ is not idempotent
($c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0,1\}$;
Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff})
and $\conjc$ does not distribute over $\disjc$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_disj\_not\_distrib}).

\begin{remark}[Product vs.\ Joint]
\label{rem:product-vs-joint}
The product $c_1 \conjc c_2$ is an algebraic operation on credence
values, not a claim about the joint credence
$\cred(A \land B)$ of two propositions.
The joint depends on the dependence structure and lies in the
Fr\'echet-Hoeffding interval
$[\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0),\; \min(\cred(A), \cred(B))]$:
independence gives $\cred(A) \cdot \cred(B)$;
maximum positive dependence (comonotonicity) gives
$\min(\cred(A), \cred(B))$~\cite{nelsen2006}.
In our running example: $\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet})$ could be
anywhere in $[0.7,\, 0.8]$.
Under independence, it is $0.72$; under comonotonicity,
$\min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$.
\end{remark}

\textbf{Comparison with Product Logic.}
The product De Morgan triplet and H\'ajek's Product Logic
($\Pi$-logic)~\cite{hajek1998} share the same t-norm.
They differ in the companion operation: $\Pi$-logic uses
residuation $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$
(Appendix~\ref{app:prelim}), which
forces $0 \to b = 1$ (ex falso);
the triplet uses the involutive complement $\negc c = 1 - c$
and chain-rule conditioning, which leaves $\cred(A \mid 0)$
unconstrained.
On positive evidence with $\mathrm{joint} \leq \mathrm{evidence}$,
both yield $\mathrm{joint}/\mathrm{evidence}$: the residuated
implication gives $\min(\mathrm{joint}/\mathrm{evidence}, 1)$,
which equals $\mathrm{joint}/\mathrm{evidence}$ since
$\mathrm{joint} \leq \mathrm{evidence}$ ensures the ratio is
at most $1$; the chain rule gives the same by uniqueness
(Proposition~\ref{thm:existence}).

\textbf{Relationship to probability.}
Every probability space satisfies the chain rule and complement axioms.
Probability adds a sigma-algebra, a countably additive measure, and
sigma-additivity.
The product De Morgan triplet drops all of these: there is no sample
space, no sigma-algebra, no additivity beyond complement.
The triplet retains exactly the structure shared by both probability
and $[0,1]$-valued logic --- the greatest common substructure of the
two traditions.

\textbf{Conjunction selection.}
For logic-compatible valuations, idempotence
($\cred(A \land A) = \cred(A)$) is essential: repeating evidence
should not change it.
A \emph{copula} is a function $C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying:
$C(a, 0) = C(0, b) = 0$, $C(a, 1) = a$, $C(1, b) = b$, and the
\emph{2-increasing property} --- for all $a_1 \leq a_2$,
$b_1 \leq b_2$:
$C(a_2, b_2) - C(a_2, b_1) - C(a_1, b_2) + C(a_1, b_1) \geq 0$
(Appendix~\ref{app:prelim})~\cite{nelsen2006}.
Among truth-functional joints (copulas on $[0,1]$), the minimum is
the unique symmetric idempotent copula.

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of Truth-Functional Conjunction;
known~\cite{nelsen2006}, verified here]
\label{thm:min-copula-unique}
Let $C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ be a symmetric, idempotent copula.
Then $C(a,b) = \min(a,b)$ for all $a,b \in [0,1]$.
Lean: \texttt{copula\_idempotent\_unique},
\texttt{min\_copula\_unique}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Upper bound: the 2-increasing property on $[0,a] \times [b,1]$ with
unit marginal gives $C(a,b) \leq a$; by symmetry $C(a,b) \leq b$.
Lower bound: assume $a \leq b$.
Apply 2-increasing to $[0,a] \times [a,b]$:
$C(a,b) - C(a,a) - C(0,b) + C(0,a) \geq 0$.
By idempotence $C(a,a) = a$ and zero boundary $C(0,b) = C(0,a) = 0$,
so $C(a,b) \geq a = \min(a,b)$.
\end{proof}

The product fails idempotence ($c^2 \neq c$ for $c \in (0,1)$);
\L ukasiewicz fails similarly
($T_L(\tfrac{3}{4}, \tfrac{3}{4}) = \max(\tfrac{1}{2}, 0) =
\tfrac{1}{2} \neq \tfrac{3}{4}$;
Lean: \texttt{luk\_not\_idempotent}).
The minimum joint is the \emph{comonotonicity copula}: the
extremal case where all random variables are increasing functions of
a single latent variable~\cite{dhaene2002,nelsen2006}.
Independence ($c_1 \cdot c_2$) fails both idempotence and
non-trivial conditioning
(Lean: \texttt{prod\_trivial\_conditioning});
the RM3 joint $\max(1-a, b)$ fails Bayes consistency
(Lean: \texttt{rm3\_not\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).

\section{Chain-Rule Conditioning}
\label{sec:conditioning}
%=============================================================================

Suppose you want to condition on wet streets:
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$ should satisfy
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) \cdot \cred(\text{wet}) =
\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet})$.
When $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9 > 0$ and the joint is
$\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet}) = \min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$
(the min-copula joint; see Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique}),
this gives $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) = 0.8 / 0.9 = 8/9$.
But what if $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0$?
You have never seen a wet street.
What is $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$?

\begin{definition}[Conditioning Structure]
\label{def:conditioning}
A \emph{conditioning structure} for
$(\text{joint}, \text{evidence}) \in [0,1]^2$
consists of a value $\text{condCred} \in [0,1]$ satisfying
$\text{condCred} \conjc \text{evidence} = \text{joint}$.
In propositional notation we write
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$,
where $\condc{A}{B}$ abbreviates the condCred component of the
conditioning structure for joint $= \cred(A \land B)$ and
evidence $= \cred(B)$.
When evidence is positive, the conditioning structure is unique and
$\condc{A}{B}$ is a number (Proposition~\ref{thm:existence});
when evidence is zero, $\condc{A}{B}$ denotes any value in $[0,1]$
satisfying the chain rule.
\end{definition}

The joint $\cred(A \land B)$ is supplied as input, not computed from
marginals --- a deliberate departure from truth-functionality, since
the conditional depends on the dependence structure between $A$ and
$B$, not only on their individual credences.
A conditioning structure exists if and only if
$\text{joint} \leq \text{evidence}$ (when evidence $> 0$) or
$\text{joint} = 0$ (when evidence $= 0$); the constraint
$\text{joint} \leq \text{evidence}$ is thus a \emph{consequence}
of the conditioning structure existing with positive evidence, not
an independent hypothesis.
H\'ajek, Godo, and Esteva~\cite{hajek1995} used the chain rule
constraint as a technical device within fuzzy logic;
Fritz~\cite{fritz2020} developed a categorical approach to
conditioning as a primitive morphism;
Jeffrey~\cite{jeffrey1965} generalized Bayesian conditioning to
uncertain evidence via a mixture formula, also using the chain rule as
the primitive mechanism.
Calabrese~\cite{calabrese1987} and
Coletti--Scozzafava~\cite{coletti2002} developed algebraic and
coherence-based approaches to conditional probability as a primitive;
Gilio and Sanfilippo~\cite{gilio2013,gilio2014} extended this to
iterated conditioning and compounds of conditionals.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} showed plausible reasoning satisfying certain
desiderata must be isomorphic to probability; chain-rule conditioning
drops the requirement that the conditional be a function
$F(\cred(A), \cred(B))$ of the marginals alone.

\subsection{Three Answers to Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:three-answers}

The chain rule gives $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$: the conditional is
\emph{unconstrained}
(Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
Residuation gives $0 \to b = 1$ for all $b$: ex falso is
\emph{forced}.
Probability gives $0/0$: \emph{undefined}.

The three frameworks agree on every row except evidence $= 0$.
This single row is where probability, logic, and the chain rule
part ways.

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{r|ccc}
\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textbf{Kleene material}\textsuperscript{1}: $\max(\negc e, p)$} \\

$\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1 \\

\end{tabular}
\quad
\begin{tabular}{r|ccc}
\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textbf{Prod.\ resid.}\textsuperscript{2}: $\min(p/e, 1)$} \\
$e \!\downarrow\;\; p$ & 0 & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1 \\
\hline
0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 0 & 1 & 1 \\
1 & 0 & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1
\end{tabular}
\quad
\begin{tabular}{r|ccc}
\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textbf{Chain-rule with min-copula joint}} \\
$e \!\downarrow\;\; p$ & 0 & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1 \\
\hline
0 & $*$ & $*$ & $*$ \\
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 0 & 1 & 1 \\
1 & 0 & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}
\smallskip
The chain-rule table uses the min-copula joint $\min(e, p)$
(the unique symmetric idempotent copula;
Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique}).
The non-zero rows depend on the joint choice; only the zero-evidence
row ($*$) is invariant --- the unconstrained behavior at $e = 0$ is
a property of the chain rule itself, not of any particular joint.

{\footnotesize Rows = evidence $e$, columns = conclusion $p$.
$*$ = unconstrained (any value in $[0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule).
\textsuperscript{1}Shared by K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952},

\textsuperscript{2}Coincides with G\"odel on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$

\subsection{Existence and Uniqueness}
\label{sec:existence}

\begin{proposition}[Existence and Uniqueness of Conditioning;
cf.~\cite{renyi1955,popper1959}, formulation and verification here]
\label{thm:existence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item \textbf{Positive evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} > 0$, a conditioning structure exists iff
  $\mathrm{joint} \leq \mathrm{evidence}$, and is then unique:
  $\mathrm{condCred} = \mathrm{joint} / \mathrm{evidence}$.
\item \textbf{Zero evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} = 0$, the constraint
  $\mathrm{condCred} \cdot 0 = \mathrm{joint}$ forces
  $\mathrm{joint} = 0$ but leaves $\mathrm{condCred}$ entirely
  unconstrained: every $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_mk}, \texttt{conditioning\_unique},
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~If $0 < e$ and $j \leq e$, then $c = j/e \in [0,1]$ and
$c \cdot e = j$.
Uniqueness: if $c \cdot e = c' \cdot e$ with $e > 0$, then $c = c'$.
(ii)~$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c \in [0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The dichotomy is sharp: at positive evidence, the conditional is a
\emph{function} (division); at zero evidence, it is a \emph{relation}
(any value is consistent).

\subsection{Path Dependence at Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:path-dependence}

Why not just take a limit?
Under the min-copula joint $\min(a, b)$, the conditional for $b > 0$
is $\min(a,b)/b$.
As $b \to 0$, the limit depends on \emph{how} $a$ and $b$ approach
zero together.

\begin{example}[Rain as Wet Streets Vanish]
\label{ex:path}
What is $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$ as
$\cred(\text{wet}) \to 0$?
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ fixed, $\cred(\text{wet}) \to 0$:
  limit $= 1$.
  Strong belief overwhelms vanishing evidence.
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.5 \cdot \cred(\text{wet})$:
  limit $= 0.5$.
  Proportional decline preserves the ratio.
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = \cred(\text{wet})^2$:
  limit $= 0$.
  The proposition vanishes faster than the evidence.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved.
How evidence becomes impossible matters.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Path Dependence at Evidence Zero; verified here]
\label{thm:path-dependence}
The limit of $\min(a,b)/b$ as $b \to 0$ is path-dependent
(the Lean formalization verifies the algebraic identities at
each point; the topological limit statements are standard
elementary analysis, not formalized):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item Fixed $a > 0$: limit is $1$.
\item $a(b) = \lambda b$, $\lambda \in (0,1]$: limit is $\lambda$.
\item $a(b) = b^\alpha$, $\alpha > 1$: limit is $0$.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved; ex falso requires an added
convention.
Lean: \texttt{path\_dep\_fixed\_a}, \texttt{path\_dep\_proportional},
\texttt{path\_dep\_square}, \texttt{path\_any\_positive\_value}.
The Lean proofs verify the algebraic identities at each point
(e.g., $\min(a,b)/b = 1$ when $b < a$), not the topological limit
statements; the limits follow from these identities by elementary
analysis.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~For small $b < a$, $\min(a,b)/b = 1$.
(ii)~$\min(\lambda b, b)/b = \lambda$.
(iii)~$\min(b^\alpha, b)/b = b^{\alpha-1} \to 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/path_dependence}
\caption{Path dependence at evidence zero.
\textbf{Left:} $\min(a,b)/b$ near the origin; value depends on angle
$\theta$, not radius $r$.
\textbf{Right:} Limit as $r \to 0$: $1$ for $\theta \leq \pi/4$
(since $a \geq b$ gives $\min(a,b)/b = 1$),
$\cot\theta$ for $\theta > \pi/4$.
The singularity is essential, not removable.}
\label{fig:path-dependence}
\end{figure}

The singularity is essential: every value in $[0,1]$ is a valid limit
depending on the approach path, so no single convention (ex falso,
void, default~$1$) can be justified on continuity grounds.
The chain rule's response --- leave the conditional unconstrained ---
is the minimal commitment consistent with the data.

The set of all posteriors consistent with the chain rule at zero
evidence is $[0,1]$ --- the maximal imprecise
posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
An agent may narrow this set by adopting a prior over the zero-evidence
regime (de Finetti's void, Popper's convention, etc.), but the
chain rule itself imposes no such commitment.

\begin{remark}[Lewis Triviality and Imprecise Probability]
\label{rem:lewis}
Lewis~\cite{lewis1976} showed that identifying $P(A \mid B)$ with
the probability of a conditional proposition forces triviality.
Chain-rule conditioning avoids Lewis's conditions in two ways:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item The joint $\cred(A \land B)$ is a separate parameter, not
  derived from the conditional.
\item The conditional $\condc{A}{B}$ is not identified with
  the credence of any proposition in the algebra.
\end{enumerate}
See Halpern~\cite{halpern2003} for a detailed analysis of Lewis's
conditions.
At zero evidence, the set of chain-rule-consistent conditionals is
$[0,1]$ --- the vacuous lower prevision of
Walley~\cite{walley1991}, confirming algebraically that maximal
imprecision is the correct response to impossible
evidence~\cite{levi1980}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Update via Conditioning}
\label{sec:update}

Bayesian and Jeffrey update are direct consequences of chain-rule
conditioning.

\begin{definition}[Bayesian Update]
\label{def:bayesian-update}
Given a joint-parametrized valuation $v$ with evidence $B$ satisfying
$v(B) > 0$, the \emph{Bayesian posterior} is
$v'(A) = \cred(A \land B) / v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Chain-Rule Preservation and Uniqueness;
known~\cite{jaynes2003}, verified here]
\label{prop:bayesian-chain}
The posterior satisfies $v'(A) \conjc v(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ and
is the unique credence with this property.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule},
\texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$v'(A) = j/e$ by construction, so $v'(A) \cdot e = j$.
Uniqueness: if $c \cdot e = c' \cdot e$ with $e > 0$, then $c = c'$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Jeffrey Update]
\label{def:jeffrey-update}
For uncertain evidence with new credence $q$ for $B$:
$v'(A) = \cred(A \mid B) \cdot q + \cred(A \mid \negc B) \cdot (1 - q)$.
At $q = 1$ this reduces to Bayesian update; at $q = 0$ it conditions
on $\negc B$.
When $0 < v(B) < 1$, both conditionals are uniquely determined by
the chain rule (Proposition~\ref{thm:existence}), so the Jeffrey
posterior is uniquely determined by $v$, the joints, and $q$.
Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate}, \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_certain},
\texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_zero}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Zero-Evidence Underdetermination; new]
\label{prop:zero-underdetermined}
When $v(B) = 0$, any conditional $\cred(A \mid B) \in [0,1]$ satisfies
the chain rule, so neither Bayesian nor Jeffrey update produces a unique
posterior.
The set of consistent posteriors is $[0,1]$ --- the maximal imprecise
posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
Lean: \texttt{bayesian\_underdetermined\_at\_zero},
\texttt{jeffrey\_underdetermined\_at\_zero}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$.
\end{proof}

\section{Algebraic Fixed Points}
\label{sec:equilibria}
%=============================================================================

What happens when you conjoin a credence with itself?
$\cred(\text{rain}) \conjc \cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8^2 = 0.64 < 0.8$.
The product of two independent copies of the same uncertain credence
is strictly smaller than the original.
Only $0$ and $1$ satisfy $c^2 = c$; the negation fixed point
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ (Lemma~\ref{thm:liar}) is the third distinguished
value.
These are \emph{algebraic} facts about the product; we make no
dynamical claims about convergence of Bayesian posteriors (see
Remark~\ref{rem:attractor-caveat}).

\begin{proposition}[Self-Conjunction Decay;
known~\cite{klement2000}, verified here]
\label{prop:self-conj-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$: $c \conjc c = c^2 < c$.
Dually, for all $c \in (0,1)$: $c \disjc c > c$.
Lean: \texttt{self\_conj\_lt}, \texttt{self\_disj\_gt}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 < c \iff c(c - 1) < 0$, which holds since $0 < c < 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Iterated Self-Conjunction;
known~\cite{klement2000}, verified here]
\label{prop:pow-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$ and $n \in \Nat$:
$c^{n+1} < c^n$.
In particular, $c^n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$:
unrenewed evidence decays.
Lean: \texttt{pow\_strictly\_decreasing} (strict decrease).
The convergence follows because $\{c^n\}$ is strictly decreasing
and bounded below by $0$, so it converges; the only possible
limit is $0$ (since $L = cL$ forces $L = 0$).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^{n+1} = c^n \cdot c < c^n \cdot 1 = c^n$ since $0 < c < 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Unique Negation Fixed Point;
known~\cite{kripke1975}, verified here]
\label{thm:liar}
$\negc c = c$ if and only if $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Lean: \texttt{liar\_fixed\_point},
\texttt{neg\_fixed\_point\_unique}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Fixed Points;
known~\cite{klement2000,kripke1975}, verified here]
\label{thm:equilibria}
A credence $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies $c \conjc c = c$ or $\negc c = c$
if and only if $c \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.
These are the \emph{algebraic fixed points}: values invariant under
conjunction-idempotence or negation.
Lean: \texttt{equilibria\_characterization}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff}).
$1 - c = c$ iff $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lemma~\ref{thm:liar}).
\end{proof}

Every other credence $c \in (0,1) \setminus \{\tfrac{1}{2}\}$
is not a fixed point: $c^2 < c$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}) and $\negc c \neq c$.
Finite-valued logics take $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ as primitive;
the continuous framework \emph{derives} them as the algebraic
fixed points of the product De Morgan triplet.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{llcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{T-norm} & \textbf{Self-conj.}
  & \textbf{Non-idemp.?} & \textbf{$\negc$-fixed pt?}
  & \textbf{$T(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2})$}
  & \textbf{Exactly 3?} \\
\midrule
Product & $c^2 < c$ & Yes & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $\tfrac{1}{4}$ & Yes \\
G\"odel & $c$ & No & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & No$^*$ \\
\L ukasiewicz & $\max(2c{-}1,0)$ & Yes & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $0$ & No \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\caption{T-norm comparison.
All three t-norms share the negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$
(since $\negc c = 1 - c$ is independent of the t-norm).
Only the product has three algebraic fixed points: $\{0, 1\}$ from
idempotence plus $\tfrac{1}{2}$ from negation.
$^*$G\"odel has infinitely many idempotent values
($\min(c,c) = c$ for all $c$), so no finite fixed-point set.
\L ukasiewicz: $T_L(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 0 \neq
\tfrac{1}{2}$, so self-conjunction destroys the negation fixed point.}
\label{tab:tnorm}
\end{table}

\begin{remark}[Algebraic fixed points vs.\ dynamical convergence]
\label{rem:attractor-caveat}
Propositions~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}--\ref{prop:pow-decay}
establish that the sequence
$c, c^2, c^3, \ldots$ converges to $0$ for every $c \in (0,1)$.
This is a precise algebraic statement about the product t-norm,
not a claim about convergence of Bayesian posteriors.
A formal convergence theorem for iterated Bayesian update
toward $0$, $1$, or $\tfrac{1}{2}$ would require specifying a
stochastic model for the evidence sequence, which is beyond our scope.
\end{remark}

\section{The Kleene Collapse and Congruence Classification}
\label{sec:collapse}
%=============================================================================

The three algebraic fixed points from Section~\ref{sec:equilibria}
are not merely stable values --- they form a quotient algebra.
The partition $\{0\}, (0,1), \{1\}$ defines a congruence on the
product De Morgan triplet, and the quotient is the Kleene lattice.

Define the collapse:
\[
\text{collapse}(c) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } c = 0, \\
\tfrac{1}{2} & \text{if } 0 < c < 1, \\
1 & \text{if } c = 1.
\end{cases}
\]

\begin{theorem}[Collapse Homomorphism; elementary,
cf.~\cite{bergmann2008}; verified here]
\label{thm:collapse}
The collapse is a surjective homomorphism from
$([0,1], \negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ to
$(\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}, \mathrm{neg}, \min, \max)$.
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_disj}, \texttt{collapse\_surjective}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Case analysis.
Negation: $0 \mapsto 1$, $1 \mapsto 0$; if $0 < c < 1$ then
$0 < 1-c < 1$.
Conjunction: $0$ absorbs, $1$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Disjunction: $1$ absorbs, $0$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2 < 1$.
\end{proof}

The target is the Kleene lattice~\cite{kleene1952}, underlying
K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952}, LP~\cite{asenjo1966,priest1979}, and
RM3~\cite{anderson1975,meyer1972}.
Belnap~\cite{belnap1977} and Dunn~\cite{dunn1976} studied this
three-valued structure (and its four-valued extension) as a
``useful logic'' for reasoning with incomplete and inconsistent
information; Fitting~\cite{fitting1991,fitting2006} developed a
systematic bilattice framework in which the Kleene lattice
appears as one component of a product construction.

\begin{remark}[Connection to possibility theory]
\label{rem:possibility}
The Kleene quotient's $\min$/$\max$ operations are exactly the
conjunction/disjunction of possibility
theory~\cite{dubois1988}.
The collapse thus provides a bridge not only to many-valued logic but
also to possibility-theoretic reasoning.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Uniqueness and Impossibility}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to Classical Logic;
known~\cite{bergmann2008}, verified here]
\label{prop:no-classical}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation.
Lean: \texttt{no\_boolean\_neg\_retraction}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ has no solution
in $\{0, 1\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to G\"odel or \L ukasiewicz Logic;
elementary, verified here]
\label{prop:no-godel-luk}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ preserves G\"odel
negation (which has no fixed point), and no such $\varphi$
preserves both standard negation and \L ukasiewicz conjunction
(since $\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq
\tfrac{1}{2}$).
Lean: \texttt{no\_godel\_collapse}, \texttt{no\_luk\_collapse}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
G\"odel: $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be a fixed point of G\"odel
negation, which has none (G\"odel negation maps every non-zero value
to zero; Lean: \texttt{godel\_neg\_no\_fixed\_point}).
\L ukasiewicz: negation forces $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Pick $c$ with $c \conjc c = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (e.g., $c = \sqrt{1/2}$).
Then $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \text{\L{}uk}(\varphi(c), \varphi(c))$.
But $\text{\L{}uk}(v, v) \in \{0, 1\}$ for all
$v \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, contradicting
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the Three-Element Quotient; new]
\label{thm:quotient-unique}
Any surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to S$ ($|S|=3$) preserving negation
and conjunction yields the Kleene lattice (up to relabeling).
Lean: \texttt{three\_element\_quotient\_unique}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be the unique negation fixed point of $S$.
The remaining elements $\varphi(0)$, $\varphi(1)$ form the 2-cycle.
The conjunction table is forced: $\varphi(0)$ absorbs, $\varphi(1)$
is identity,
and $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc \tfrac{1}{2}) =
\varphi(\tfrac{1}{4}) = \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$
(since $\tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$).
This forces Kleene $\min$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Congruence Classification}
\label{sec:congruence}

The collapse results combine into a congruence classification at two
levels, depending on the scope of the multiplication compatibility
hypothesis.
A \emph{unit congruence} requires multiplication compatibility only for
$[0,1]$-valued inputs.
A \emph{real congruence} extends compatibility to all of $\Real$.

\begin{theorem}[Congruence Classification; new]
\label{thm:congruence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)},nosep]
\item \textbf{Singleton boundaries:}
  Under any non-trivial unit congruence, $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ are
  singleton classes.
\item \textbf{No two-element quotient:}
  The negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$ has no preimage in
  $\{0,1\}$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}).
\item \textbf{Uniqueness at three elements:}
  The Kleene lattice is the unique three-element quotient
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}), and the Kleene partition is
  a verified unit congruence (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}).
\item \textbf{$\Real$-rigidity:}
  Under $\Real$-multiplication, no non-trivial finite quotient
  exists with $n \geq 2$ classes.
\end{enumerate}
Whether four or more classes are possible under $[0,1]$-multiplication
is \textbf{open}.
Lean: \texttt{UnitCongruence.singleton\_zero},
\texttt{singleton\_one}, \texttt{kleeneCongruence},
\texttt{RealCongruence.no\_nontrivial\_finite\_quotient}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
See Appendix~\ref{app:congruence-proofs}.
\end{proof}

\section{The Bridge Theorem}
\label{sec:bridge}
%=============================================================================

The collapse from Section~\ref{sec:collapse} is a homomorphism of
connectives.
This section shows it induces an \emph{equivalence of consequence
relations}: three-valued logical consequence on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is the same as a natural consequence
relation on $[0,1]$, at the level of propositional formulas.

\subsection{Valuations}

The bridge theorem quantifies over all assignments
$v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ to propositional atoms, with compound
formulas evaluated recursively via the connectives.
We first define two structured valuation types used for the
collapse-composition result, then state the consequence relations
the bridge connects.

\begin{definition}[Independent Valuation]
\label{def:indep-valuation}
A \emph{complement-preserving} valuation satisfies
$v(\negc A) = \negc v(A)$.
It is \emph{independent} if additionally
$v(A \land B) = v(A) \conjc v(B)$ and
$v(A \lor B) = v(A) \disjc v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{CpValuation}, \texttt{IndepValuation}.
\end{definition}

Independent valuations treat all conjunctions as independent
(the product).
When propositions are correlated, a \emph{joint-parametrized}
valuation supplies $\cred(A \land B)$ separately
(Definition~\ref{def:conditioning}).

\textbf{Valuations vs.\ formula evaluation.}
The bridge theorem (below) uses neither type of valuation directly.
Instead, it quantifies over all assignments $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$
to \emph{propositional atoms} (variables), with the values of compound
formulas determined recursively by the connective truth tables
(Definition~\ref{def:consequence}, Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval}).
An assignment to atoms induces a unique evaluation of every formula;
the consequence relations are defined by this evaluation.
Independent valuations (Definition~\ref{def:indep-valuation}) are
used only for the collapse-composition result
(Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-composition}), showing that the
collapse preserves connective structure.

\begin{definition}[Kleene Valuation]
\label{def:kleene-valuation}
A complement-preserving valuation into
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is a \emph{Kleene valuation} if
$v(A \land B) = \min(v(A), v(B))$ and
$v(A \lor B) = \max(v(A), v(B))$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Collapse Composition; new]
\label{prop:collapse-composition}
Composing an independent valuation with the collapse homomorphism
yields a Kleene valuation:
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{collapse}(v(\negc A)) &= \mathrm{neg}(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \land B)) &= \min(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \lor B)) &= \max(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))).
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_val\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_val\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_val\_disj}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{lemma}[Lifting; new]
\label{lem:lifting}
Every Kleene valuation $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
lifts to a $[0,1]$-valued function $v$ such that
$\mathrm{collapse} \circ\, v = w$.
Specifically, map $0 \mapsto 0$, $\tfrac{1}{2} \mapsto \tfrac{1}{2}$,
$1 \mapsto 1$.
Lean: \texttt{liftThreeVal}, \texttt{lift\_collapse\_id}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Each of the three values has a canonical preimage in $[0,1]$.
The composition $\text{collapse} \circ \text{lift}$ is the identity
on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consequence Relations on $[0,1]$}

\begin{definition}[Consequence Relations]
\label{def:consequence}
For propositions drawn from a type $\alpha$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \emph{Positivity consequence:}
  $\Gamma \models^+ A$ iff for every $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$, if
  $v(p) > 0$ for all $p \in \Gamma$, then $v(A) > 0$.
\item \emph{Certainty consequence:}
  $\Gamma \models^1 A$ iff for every $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$, if
  $v(p) = 1$ for all $p \in \Gamma$, then $v(A) = 1$.
\item \emph{Three-valued consequence:}
  $\Gamma \models_D A$ (for designated set $D$) iff for every
  $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, if
  $w(p) \in D$ for all $p \in \Gamma$, then $w(A) \in D$.
\end{itemize}
Lean: \texttt{positivityConsequence},
\texttt{certaintyConsequence}, \texttt{threeValConsequence}.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[All assignments, not just homomorphisms]
\label{rem:graded-scope}
All consequence relations above quantify over \emph{all} assignments
$v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ (or $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$), not only
homomorphic valuations.
Restricting to homomorphisms would not change the equivalence:
lifted three-valued assignments land in $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
(closed under all connectives, hence automatically homomorphic),
and collapsed valuations are homomorphic because the collapse is a
homomorphism (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}).
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Structural Rules; verified here]
\label{prop:structural}
All consequence relations above satisfy the standard Tarskian
structural rules:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item \emph{Reflexivity:} $A \models A$.
\item \emph{Monotonicity:} if $\Gamma \models A$ and
  $\Gamma \subseteq \Delta$, then $\Delta \models A$.
\item \emph{Cut:} if $\Gamma \models A$ and $\Gamma, A \models B$,
  then $\Gamma \models B$.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{formulaLP\_reflexivity/monotonicity/cut},
\texttt{formulaK3\_reflexivity/monotonicity/cut},
\texttt{formulaPositivity\_reflexivity/monotonicity/cut},
\texttt{formulaCertainty\_reflexivity/monotonicity/cut}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
These hold because the consequence relations quantify over
all assignments --- adding premises restricts the set of
admissible valuations.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Bridge}

\begin{theorem}[LP Bridge Theorem; new]
\label{thm:lp-bridge}
LP consequence (designating $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is equivalent to positivity consequence
on $[0,1]$:
\[
\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{LP}} A
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \models^+ A.
\]
Lean: \texttt{lp\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$
Given $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ with $v(p) > 0$ for all $p \in \Gamma$,
define $w = \text{collapse} \circ\, v$.
Then $w(p)$ is LP-designated iff $v(p) > 0$
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_designated\_iff\_pos}), so all premises are
LP-designated.
By LP consequence, $w(A)$ is LP-designated, hence $v(A) > 0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$
Given $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ with all premises
LP-designated, define $v = \text{lift} \circ\, w$.
Each LP-designated value lifts to a positive credence
(Lean: \texttt{lift\_pos\_of\_designated\_lp}).
By positivity consequence, $v(A) > 0$.
Then $\text{collapse}(v(A))$ is LP-designated, and
$\text{collapse} \circ\, \text{lift} = \text{id}$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:lifting}) gives $w(A)$ LP-designated.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[K$_3$ Bridge Theorem; new]
\label{thm:k3-bridge}
K$_3$ consequence (designating $\{1\}$) on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is equivalent to certainty consequence
on $[0,1]$:
\[
\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{K}_3} A
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \models^1 A.
\]
Lean: \texttt{k3\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$
Given $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ with $v(p) = 1$ for all $p \in \Gamma$,
define $w = \text{collapse} \circ\, v$.
Then $w(p) = 1$ iff $v(p) = 1$
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_designated\_iff\_one}), so all premises are
K$_3$-designated.
By K$_3$ consequence, $w(A) = 1$, i.e.,
$\text{collapse}(v(A)) = 1$; the same characterization gives
$v(A) = 1$.

$(\Leftarrow)$
Given $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ with all premises
K$_3$-designated, define $v = \text{lift} \circ\, w$.
Each K$_3$-designated value lifts to credence $1$
(Lean: \texttt{lift\_one\_of\_designated\_k3}).
By certainty consequence, $v(A) = 1$.
Then $\text{collapse}(v(A)) = 1$, and
$\text{collapse} \circ\, \text{lift} = \text{id}$ gives $w(A) = 1$.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation.}
The proofs are short: each direction composes the collapse (or
lifting) with the designation characterization.
The value of the bridge theorems is conceptual rather than
technical --- they make precise the folklore intuition that
three-valued logical reasoning is faithfully encoded in continuous
$[0,1]$ reasoning, and vice versa.
An LP logician working with designated values
$\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$ is implicitly performing positivity reasoning
on the continuum; a K$_3$ logician working with designated
value $\{1\}$ is implicitly performing certainty reasoning.
The collapse does not merely project --- it \emph{reflects}:
if a consequence holds on $[0,1]$, it holds on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, and conversely.

\begin{example}[Bridge in Action: Rain and Wet Streets]
\label{ex:bridge-action}
\emph{Explosion failure.}
Set $\cred(\text{rain}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(maximum uncertainty) and $\cred(\text{snow}) = 0$.
In LP: $w(\text{rain}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $w(\text{snow}) = 0$ refutes
$\text{rain}, \negc\text{rain} \models_{\mathrm{LP}} \text{snow}$
--- both $w(\text{rain})$ and $\negc w(\text{rain}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$
are LP-designated, but $w(\text{snow}) = 0$ is not.
Via the bridge, the positivity witness $v(\text{rain}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$,
$v(\text{snow}) = 0$ satisfies $v(\text{rain}) > 0$ and
$\negc v(\text{rain}) > 0$ but $v(\text{snow}) = 0$, refuting
positivity explosion: contradictory evidence about rain does not
force belief in snow.
The witnesses correspond: $\text{collapse} \circ\, v = w$ and
$\text{lift} \circ\, w = v$.
\emph{Conjunction preservation.}
Set $\cred(\text{rain}) = 1$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 1$.
In K$_3$: $\min(1, 1) = 1$ is K$_3$-designated.
Via the bridge: $v(\text{rain}) \cdot v(\text{wet}) = 1$, so
certainty is preserved --- certain rain and certain wet streets
give certain conjunction.
\end{example}

\subsection{Extension to Formulas}

The assignment-level bridge (Theorems~\ref{thm:lp-bridge}--\ref{thm:k3-bridge})
treats propositions as opaque atoms: it says that if every
valuation making atoms designated also makes a target atom designated,
the same holds under the other consequence relation.
The formula-level bridge is not an independent result: it follows by
composing the assignment-level bridge with the commutativity of collapse
and evaluation (Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval} below).
Because $\mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v)
= \llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_{\mathrm{collapse}\circ v}$,
designated status transfers from atoms to compound formulas
built from negation, conjunction, and disjunction.
Define evaluation recursively using the product connectives:
given an assignment $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ to atoms,
$\llbracket \negc\varphi \rrbracket_v = \negc\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v$,
$\llbracket \varphi \conjc \psi \rrbracket_v =
\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v \conjc \llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v$
(product conjunction),
$\llbracket \varphi \disjc \psi \rrbracket_v =
\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v \disjc \llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v$;
analogously for three-valued evaluation $\llbracket\cdot\rrbracket_w$
using Kleene connectives.
Lean: \texttt{Formula}, \texttt{evalCred}, \texttt{evalThreeVal}.

\begin{proposition}[Collapse Commutes with Evaluation; new]
\label{prop:collapse-eval}
For any $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ and formula $\varphi$:
$\mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v)
  = \llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_{\mathrm{collapse} \circ\, v}$.
Conversely, for any $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$:
$\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_w
  = \mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_{\mathrm{lift} \circ\, w})$.
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_eval\_eq}, \texttt{lift\_eval\_eq}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Structural induction using
\texttt{collapse\_neg/conj/disj} (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse})
and $\mathrm{collapse} \circ\, \mathrm{lift} = \mathrm{id}$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:lifting}).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Formula-Level LP Bridge; new]
\label{thm:lp-formula-bridge}
For formulas $\Gamma$ and $\varphi$ over atoms $\alpha$:
LP formula consequence
$\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{LP}} \varphi$
is equivalent to formula positivity consequence
$\Gamma \models^+ \varphi$.
Lean: \texttt{lp\_formula\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$~Given $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ with
$\llbracket p \rrbracket_v > 0$ for all $p \in \Gamma$,
define $w = \mathrm{collapse} \circ\, v$.
By Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval},
$\llbracket p \rrbracket_w = \mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket p \rrbracket_v)$,
which is LP-designated by the positivity characterization.
LP consequence gives $\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_w$ LP-designated,
so $\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_v > 0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$~Given $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
with all premises LP-designated, lift to
$v = \mathrm{lift} \circ\, w$.
By Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval},
$\llbracket p \rrbracket_w = \mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket p \rrbracket_v)$,
so the designation characterization gives
$\llbracket p \rrbracket_v > 0$.
Positivity consequence gives
$\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_v > 0$, and
$\mathrm{collapse} \circ\, \mathrm{lift} = \mathrm{id}$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:lifting}) gives
$\llbracket \varphi \rrbracket_w$ LP-designated.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Formula-Level K$_3$ Bridge; new]
\label{thm:k3-formula-bridge}
For formulas $\Gamma$ and $\varphi$ over atoms $\alpha$:
K$_3$ formula consequence
$\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{K}_3} \varphi$
is equivalent to formula certainty consequence
$\Gamma \models^1 \varphi$.
Lean: \texttt{k3\_formula\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Identical to the LP case, replacing positivity with certainty
and using the characterization
$\mathrm{collapse}(c) = 1 \iff c = 1$.
\end{proof}

The key mechanism is the characterization of designated values via
the collapse:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $\text{collapse}(c)$ is LP-designated if and only if $c > 0$,
\item $\text{collapse}(c)$ is K$_3$-designated if and only if $c = 1$.
\end{itemize}
These are the two canonical ways to carve $[0,1]$ into
``designated'' and ``undesignated'': by positivity or by certainty.
Each corresponds to exactly one of the standard three-valued
consequence relations.

\subsection{Scope and Limitations}
\label{sec:bridge-scope}

\textbf{The bridge covers connectives, not conditioning.}
The bridge theorem applies to propositional formulas built from
atoms, negation, conjunction, and disjunction.
It does \emph{not} extend to a language with conditionals, and
this limitation is structural, not accidental.
The collapse is a homomorphism of the triplet
$(\negc, \conjc, \disjc)$, but conditioning is not a connective on
individual credences: $\condc{A}{B}$ depends on the joint
$\cred(A \land B)$, which is a separate parameter
(Definition~\ref{def:conditioning}).
There is no truth-functional three-valued conditional on the
Kleene lattice that corresponds to the chain rule.
The three conditioning tables of Section~\ref{sec:three-answers}
--- Kleene material, product residuation, chain-rule --- all differ
on the $e = 0$ row, and the chain rule's unconstrained entry has no
analogue in a truth-functional three-valued setting.
Extending the bridge to conditionals would require either
(i)~a non-truth-functional consequence relation on $\{0,\tfrac{1}{2},1\}$
that can express the joint as a parameter, or
(ii)~restricting to positive evidence, where the chain rule reduces
to division and the bridge would hold trivially.
Neither extension is pursued here; the bridge and conditioning are
complementary contributions.

\textbf{RM3 and the bridge.}
The bridge theorem covers LP (designating $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$)
and K$_3$ (designating $\{1\}$) but not RM3, which adds a
relevance constraint to the LP designated
set~\cite{anderson1975,meyer1972}.
RM3 consequence is strictly stronger than LP consequence (it
validates additional inferences via the material conditional
$\max(\negc e, p)$), and an RM3 bridge would require extending
the collapse to a language with implication --- blocked by the same
conditioning limitation above.

\textbf{Relationship to Suszko's thesis.}
Suszko~\cite{suszko1977} argued that every Tarskian consequence
relation is logically bivalent: the truth values reduce to
``designated'' and ``undesignated,'' regardless of the number of
algebraic truth values~\cite{malinowski1993}.
The bridge theorem can be seen as a concrete semantic realization
of the Suszko reduction for LP and K$_3$ over the product
De Morgan triplet: both consequence relations reduce to binary
distinctions (positive vs.\ zero, certain vs.\ uncertain) on
$[0,1]$, instantiating the designated/undesignated partition that
Suszko's thesis guarantees abstractly.
What the bridge adds beyond the abstract guarantee is the
specific mechanism: the collapse and lifting maps provide a
constructive witness translation between $[0,1]$ counterexamples
and $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ counterexamples, the formula-level
commutativity (Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval}) ensures this
works for compound formulas, and the designated sets are
characterized as exactly the two canonical partitions of
$[0,1]$ (positivity and certainty).

\textbf{Relationship to degree-preserving consequence.}
B\v{e}hounek and Cintula~\cite{behounek2006} characterized fuzzy
logics via \emph{degree-preserving consequence}:
$\Gamma \models_t \varphi$ holds iff every evaluation making all
premises $\geq t$ also makes the conclusion $\geq t$.
Certainty consequence coincides with degree-preserving consequence
at threshold $t = 1$.
Positivity consequence (which uses strict inequality $> 0$)
is the intersection $\bigcap_{t > 0} \models_t$: a consequence
holds under positivity iff it holds for all thresholds $t > 0$.
The bridge identifies these two extremes with K$_3$ and LP
respectively.

One might ask whether the bridge theorem is subsumed by the
degree-preserving framework.
It is not, for two reasons.
First, degree-preserving consequence is parametric in the threshold
$t$ and the algebra; it does not by itself identify which thresholds
(if any) correspond to standard named logics.
The bridge provides the specific identification: $0^+$ gives LP and
$1$ gives K$_3$, via the explicit collapse and lifting maps.
Second, the degree-preserving framework does not provide the
\emph{constructive witness translation}: the bridge theorem shows
that a $[0,1]$ counterexample can be explicitly converted to a
three-valued counterexample (by composing with the collapse) and
vice versa (by lifting), with the formula-level commutativity
(Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval}) ensuring this works for
compound formulas.
This witness translation is what yields the finite model property
(Corollary~\ref{thm:finite-model}) and the complexity transfer
(Section~\ref{sec:bridge-consequences}).

\subsection{Consequences of the Bridge}
\label{sec:bridge-consequences}

The bridge theorem is not merely an observation; it transfers
concrete results between the discrete and continuous settings.

\begin{corollary}[Finite Model Property; new]
\label{thm:finite-model}
For formulas $\Gamma$, $\varphi$ over atoms $\alpha$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item $\Gamma \not\models^+ \varphi$ if and only if there exists
  $v : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ with all premises
  positive and conclusion zero.
\item $\Gamma \not\models^1 \varphi$ if and only if there exists
  $v : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ with all premises $1$ and
  conclusion $\neq 1$.
\end{enumerate}
In particular, positivity and certainty consequence on $[0,1]$ are
decidable by exhaustive search over $3^{|\alpha|}$ assignments.
Non-consequence reduces to finding a counterexample in
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}^{|\alpha|}$ (verifiable in polynomial time),
placing consequence in coNP.
LP and K$_3$ consequence are known to be
coNP-complete~\cite{priest2008,avron1991}; the bridge
transfers this to positivity and certainty consequence on $[0,1]$.
Lean: direct corollary of \texttt{lp\_formula\_bridge},
\texttt{k3\_formula\_bridge}.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Immediate from
Theorems~\ref{thm:lp-formula-bridge}--\ref{thm:k3-formula-bridge}:
a $[0,1]$ counterexample exists iff an LP (resp.\ K$_3$)
counterexample exists.
Every LP/K$_3$ counterexample is an assignment into
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, which is finite.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Transfer of paraconsistency and paracompleteness.}
The bridge gives precise content to the following transfers
(each combining a bridge theorem with a Lean-verified discrete
result):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item LP is paraconsistent (explosion-free;
  Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion})
  $\iff$ positivity consequence on $[0,1]$ is explosion-free.
  The LP counterexample $w(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $w(B) = 0$ maps to
  the positivity counterexample $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $v(B) = 0$,
  and conversely.
\item K$_3$ is paracomplete (no tautologies;
  Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology})
  $\iff$ certainty consequence on $[0,1]$ has no tautologies.
  The K$_3$ counterexample $w(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ maps to
  $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (with $v(A) \neq 1$).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The bridge connects conditioning and consequence.}
The bridge and conditioning are largely independent mechanisms ---
the bridge operates on the connectives
$(\negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ and does not extend to conditionals
(Section~\ref{sec:bridge-scope}) --- but they are linked through
the negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
The chain rule at zero evidence is unconstrained because
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$ (Section~\ref{sec:three-answers}).
The same value $\tfrac{1}{2}$ that makes conditioning underdetermined
($\condc{A}{0} = \tfrac{1}{2}$ is consistent) is the witness that
defeats LP explosion: $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ satisfies $v(A) > 0$ and
$\negc v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} > 0$ but $v(B) = 0$.
Via the bridge, LP's paraconsistency is equivalent to the
underdetermination of conditioning at zero evidence: both are
manifestations of the algebraic fact that $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is
simultaneously the negation fixed point and an interior point of
$[0,1]$.
Formally: ex falso fails in LP iff
the positivity witness $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $v(B) = 0$ exists,
which is exactly the assignment that makes conditioning at
$\cred(B) = 0$ unconstrained.
The three-valued framework provides finitary decidability
(Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-model}); the continuous framework
provides the connection to credences, update, and the conditioning
apparatus of Sections~\ref{sec:conditioning}--\ref{sec:update}.

\section{Graded Consequence and No-Explosion}
\label{sec:consequence}
%=============================================================================

K$_3$ (designating $\{1\}$) is paracomplete: no tautologies
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology}).
LP (designating $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) is
paraconsistent~\cite{priest2008}: excluded
middle holds but explosion fails
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}).

\begin{proposition}[Graded No-Explosion; new]
\label{prop:graded-no-explosion}
For any threshold $0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, graded consequence on
$[0,1]$ is explosion-free: the valuation
$v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $v(B) = 0$ satisfies
$v(A) \geq t$ and $\negc v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$, so both
$A$ and $\negc A$ are above threshold, while $v(B) = 0 < t$ shows
the conclusion need not be.
Lean: \texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Sharp Threshold; new]
\label{prop:graded-sharp}
For $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$, graded explosion holds \emph{vacuously}:
no valuation can satisfy both $v(A) \geq t$ and
$\negc v(A) \geq t$, since these require $t \leq v(A)$ and
$t \leq 1 - v(A)$, giving $2t \leq 1$, contradicting
$t > \tfrac{1}{2}$.
The threshold $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is thus sharp:
paraconsistency holds for $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$ (the
constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ witness works) and fails for
$t > \tfrac{1}{2}$ (the premises are unsatisfiable).
At $t = 1$, graded consequence coincides with certainty
consequence, which the bridge (Theorem~\ref{thm:k3-formula-bridge})
identifies with K$_3$ consequence; hence paracomplete
(Lean: \texttt{graded\_at\_one\_is\_strict}).
Lean: \texttt{graded\_explosion\_vacuous}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
If $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $v(A) \geq t$, then
$\negc v(A) = 1 - v(A) \leq 1 - t < \tfrac{1}{2} < t$.
\end{proof}

The witness is the same in all explosion-free cases: the negation
fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$ makes both a proposition and its negation
designated without forcing arbitrary conclusions.

\begin{remark}[Graded excluded middle]
\label{rem:graded-em}
Excluded middle as a graded tautology
($\emptyset \models_t A \disjc \negc A$) holds for $t \leq
\tfrac{3}{4}$: the minimum of
$v(A) \disjc \negc v(A) = 1 - v(A)(1 - v(A))$ over $[0,1]$ is
$\tfrac{3}{4}$, attained at $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Under positivity consequence ($t > 0$), excluded middle holds
trivially since $1 - v(A)(1 - v(A)) > 0$ for all $v(A)$.
Lean: \texttt{certainty\_ge\_three\_quarters}.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$ admits a natural collapse
homomorphism to the Kleene lattice $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.
The main results of this paper fall into two groups.

\textbf{Properties of the triplet and its collapse} (independent of
conditioning):
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item The \textbf{bridge theorem} shows LP consequence is equivalent
  to positivity consequence on $[0,1]$, and K$_3$ consequence is
  equivalent to certainty consequence, at the level of propositional
  formulas.
  The bridge transfers decidability (finite model property),
  paraconsistency, and paracompleteness between the
  discrete and continuous frameworks.
\item The \textbf{algebraic fixed points} $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
  are exactly the conjunction-idempotent values together with the
  negation fixed point; they are derived from the algebra, not
  postulated.
\item The \textbf{Kleene collapse} to $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is the
  unique non-trivial three-element quotient.
\item The \textbf{congruence classification} shows the Kleene quotient
  exists under $[0,1]$-multiplication; no non-trivial finite quotient
  exists under $\Real$-multiplication.
\item The \textbf{graded no-explosion} shows paraconsistency for
  $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, with the same witness ($\tfrac{1}{2}$) in
  every case; for $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$ explosion holds vacuously
  (the premises are unsatisfiable).
  The threshold $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is sharp.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Properties depending on conditioning:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,resume]
\item \textbf{Chain-rule conditioning} at positive evidence reduces to
  Bayesian division; at zero evidence it is unconstrained.
  Bayesian and Jeffrey update preserve the chain rule
  (Section~\ref{sec:update}).
\end{enumerate}

The two groups are connected by the evidence-zero behavior.
The algebraic fact $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$ manifests as
underdetermined posteriors on the probabilistic side (connecting to
imprecise probability~\cite{walley1991}), and as paraconsistency on
the logical side (the negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$ witnesses
non-explosion).

The bridge and conditioning are complementary, not redundant.
The bridge theorem operates through the collapse homomorphism on the
connectives $(\negc, \conjc, \disjc)$; it does not extend to
conditionals (Section~\ref{sec:bridge-scope}), because the chain rule
depends on a joint parameter that has no truth-functional three-valued
analogue.
Conditioning provides the mechanism that makes the framework useful
for reasoning under uncertainty: it connects Bayesian update to the
same algebra without forcing ex falso, and its underdetermined
zero-evidence behavior is the probabilistic counterpart of
paraconsistency.
Together, the bridge and conditioning unify three roles of $[0,1]$:
as a space of truth degrees (fuzzy logic), as a space of credences
(probability), and as the domain of a consequence relation whose
discrete shadow is the Kleene lattice.
Graded predicates with inf/sup quantifiers extend the framework to
predicate logic; this development, including crisp agreement and
self-referential fixed points, is deferred to future work.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Four or more classes under $[0,1]$-multiplication.}
  The scaling trick (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}(4)) uses
  multiplicative inverses unavailable in $[0,1]$.
  A four-class quotient would need to partition $(0,1)$ into pieces
  closed under $[0,1]$-multiplication and compatible with complement.
  Strong constraints apply: for any $a \in (0,1)$, the sequence
  $a^n \to 0$, so the congruence class of $a$ must contain values
  arbitrarily close to $0$.
  Since $\{0\}$ is singleton (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}(1)),
  this means each class in $(0,1)$ is dense near $0$,
  precluding interval partitions.
  The question reduces to whether a non-interval partition of
  $(0,1)$, closed under $[0,1]$-multiplication and compatible with
  complement, can have more than one class.
\item \textbf{Proof theory.}
  Is there a sound and complete calculus for chain-rule conditioning
  on $[0,1]$?
  The analogous question for product logic (with residuation) is
  answered by H\'ajek's completeness theorem~\cite{hajek1998};
  replacing residuation with the chain rule changes the answer.
\item \textbf{Convergence under iterated update.}
  Under what stochastic model for the evidence sequence does
  iterated Bayesian update drive $\cred_n(A) \to 1$, $0$, or
  $\tfrac{1}{2}$?
\item \textbf{Congruence lattice of $(T, \negc)$ vs.\ $(T, \to_T)$.}
  For a fixed continuous t-norm $T$, how does the congruence lattice
  of the De Morgan reduct relate to the congruence lattice of the BL
  expansion?
\item \textbf{Four-valued extension.}
  K$_3$ and LP are both fragments of Belnap--Dunn four-valued logic
  (FDE)~\cite{belnap1977,dunn1976}.
  Does the bridge theorem extend to FDE, and what is the corresponding
  consequence relation on $[0,1]$?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{app:prelim}
%=============================================================================

This appendix collects standard definitions used in the paper.

\textbf{T-norms.}
A \emph{t-norm} is a binary operation
$T : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ that is commutative, associative,
monotone (in each argument), and satisfies $T(a, 1) = a$ for all
$a$~\cite{klement2000}.
The three basic continuous t-norms are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \emph{Product}: $T_P(a,b) = a \cdot b$.
\item \emph{G\"odel} (minimum): $T_G(a,b) = \min(a,b)$.
\item \emph{\L ukasiewicz}: $T_L(a,b) = \max(a + b - 1, 0)$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Copulas.}
A (bivariate) \emph{copula} is a function
$C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying~\cite{nelsen2006}:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item $C(a, 0) = C(0, b) = 0$ for all $a, b$ (grounded),
\item $C(a, 1) = a$ and $C(1, b) = b$ for all $a, b$
  (unit marginals),
\item $C(a_2, b_2) - C(a_2, b_1) - C(a_1, b_2) + C(a_1, b_1) \geq 0$
  for all $a_1 \leq a_2$, $b_1 \leq b_2$ (2-increasing).
\end{enumerate}
Every copula satisfies the Fr\'echet-Hoeffding bounds:
$\max(a + b - 1, 0) \leq C(a, b) \leq \min(a, b)$.
The upper bound $\min(a, b)$ is the \emph{comonotonicity copula};
the product $a \cdot b$ is the \emph{independence copula}.

\textbf{Coherence.}
De Finetti~\cite{definetti1937} introduced coherence as a criterion
for probability assignments: an assignment is coherent if no finite
combination of bets on it yields a sure loss.
Coletti and Scozzafava~\cite{coletti2002} extended coherence to
conditional probabilities, treating the conditional as a primitive
rather than derived via division.
The chain-rule constraint
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is a necessary condition for coherence~\cite{coletti2002,gilio2014}.

\textbf{Residuation.}
Given a (left-continuous) t-norm $T$, the \emph{residuum} is
$a \to_T b = \sup\{c : T(a,c) \leq b\}$~\cite{hajek1998,galatos2007}.
For the product t-norm: $a \to_P b = \min(b/a, 1)$ when $a > 0$, and
$0 \to_P b = 1$.
The last equation is ex falso: $0 \to b = 1$ regardless of $b$.
Residuation forces a truth-functional conditional; the chain rule
allows the conditional to depend on the joint.

\section{Congruence Classification Proofs}
\label{app:congruence-proofs}
%=============================================================================

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}]
(1)~Suppose $0 \sim \varepsilon$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$.
Negation compatibility gives $1 \sim 1 - \varepsilon$.
For any $x \in [0,1]$, multiplication compatibility yields
$x = x \cdot 1 \sim x(1 - \varepsilon)$.
By induction, $x \sim x(1 - \varepsilon)^n$ for all $n$.
Since $0 < 1 - \varepsilon < 1$, for any $x > 0$ there exists
$n$ with $x(1 - \varepsilon)^n \leq \varepsilon$.
Write $x(1 - \varepsilon)^n = \varepsilon \cdot q$ where
$q = x(1 - \varepsilon)^n / \varepsilon$; since
$0 \leq x(1-\varepsilon)^n \leq \varepsilon$, we have
$q \in [0,1]$.
From $0 \sim \varepsilon$, multiplication compatibility gives
$0 = 0 \cdot q \sim \varepsilon \cdot q = x(1 - \varepsilon)^n$.
By transitivity, $x \sim x(1-\varepsilon)^n \sim 0$
for every $x \in [0,1]$, so the quotient is trivial ---
contradiction.
For $\{1\}$: if $1 \sim x$ with $x < 1$, negation gives
$0 \sim 1 - x$ with $1 - x > 0$, reducing to the previous case.

(2)~A surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ must satisfy
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$, which has no
solution in $\{0,1\}$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}).

(3)~Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}.
The Kleene partition is a verified unit congruence
(Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}).

(4)~Set $a = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
With $n \geq 2$ classes, the $n + 1$ powers
$a^0, a^1, \ldots, a^n$ map into $n$ classes; by pigeonhole,
some $i < j \leq n$ satisfy $a^i \sim a^j$.
Write $a^j = a^i \cdot a^p$ where $p = j - i > 0$.
Under $\Real$-multiplication, multiply both sides of $a^i \sim a^j$
by $(a^i)^{-1}$ (which exists in $\Real$ but not in $[0,1]$,
illustrating why this step requires $\Real$-multiplication):
$1 = (a^i)^{-1} \cdot a^i \sim (a^i)^{-1} \cdot a^j = a^p$.
Since $0 < a^p < 1$, negation gives $0 \sim 1 - a^p$ with
$1 - a^p > 0$, contradicting singleton boundaries from~(1).
\end{proof}
