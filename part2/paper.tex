\begin{abstract}
We develop valuations, update rules, consequence relations, and graded
predicates on the product De Morgan triplet --- the concrete structure
on $[0,1]$ comprising product conjunction, probabilistic-sum disjunction,
and standard complement, equipped with chain-rule conditioning.
Part~I established the core algebra, uniqueness of the min copula,
and a complete congruence classification showing the Kleene lattice
as the unique non-trivial finite quotient.
Here we build on that foundation: independent, Kleene, and
joint-parametrized valuations; Bayesian and Jeffrey update with
zero-evidence underdetermination; K$_3$, LP, and graded consequence
relations; and graded predicates with inf/sup quantifiers and a
self-reference fixed point.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib.
\end{abstract}

%=============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

This paper is a companion to Part~I, which studied the product
De Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$ --- product conjunction ($c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$),
probabilistic-sum disjunction ($c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$),
and standard complement ($\negc c = 1 - c$) --- equipped with
chain-rule conditioning ($\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$).
Part~I established the algebraic properties, proved uniqueness of the
min copula among symmetric idempotent copulas, analyzed path dependence
at evidence zero, constructed the collapse homomorphism to the Kleene
lattice, and gave a complete congruence classification: the partition
$\{0\},(0,1),\{1\}$ is the unique non-trivial finite congruence.

Here we develop the applied theory on this structure.
Section~\ref{sec:valuations} defines complement-preserving, independent,
Kleene, and joint-parametrized valuations, and shows that composing any
valuation with the collapse yields a Kleene valuation.
Section~\ref{sec:update} develops Bayesian and Jeffrey update rules,
with zero-evidence underdetermination connecting to imprecise probability.
Section~\ref{sec:consequence} adds consequence relations (K$_3$, LP,
graded thresholds) via designated values.
Section~\ref{sec:predicates} extends to graded predicates with
quantifier duality and a self-reference fixed point.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib.

\section{Valuations}
\label{sec:valuations}
%=============================================================================

A \emph{valuation} assigns credences to propositions, bridging the
propositional and algebraic worlds.
The algebra of Part~I constrains how credences compose;
a valuation specifies which credences particular propositions receive.
Different choices of joint formula yield qualitatively different theories.

\begin{definition}[Complement-Preserving Valuation]
\label{def:cp-valuation}
A valuation $v : \mathrm{Prop} \to [0,1]$ is \emph{complement-preserving}
if $v(\sigma(A)) = \negc v(A)$ for a propositional negation $\sigma$.
Lean: \texttt{CpValuation}.
\end{definition}

Complement preservation is the minimal requirement: the algebraic
negation $\negc c = 1 - c$ matches propositional negation.

\begin{definition}[Independent Valuation]
\label{def:indep-valuation}
A complement-preserving valuation is \emph{independent} if
$v(A \land B) = v(A) \conjc v(B)$ and
$v(A \lor B) = v(A) \disjc v(B)$ for all $A, B$.
Lean: \texttt{IndepValuation}.
\end{definition}

Under independence, the conditional $\cred(A \mid B)$ satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot v(B) = v(A) \cdot v(B)$, so
$\cred(A \mid B) = v(A)$ whenever $v(B) > 0$:
evidence has no effect.
Independence also fails idempotence ($v(A \land A) = v(A)^2 \neq v(A)$
in general; Lean: \texttt{IndepValuation.conj\_not\_idempotent}).
These two failures motivate the alternatives.

\begin{definition}[Kleene Valuation]
\label{def:kleene-valuation}
A complement-preserving valuation is a \emph{Kleene valuation} if
$v(A \land B) = \min(v(A), v(B))$ and
$v(A \lor B) = \max(v(A), v(B))$.
\end{definition}

By the uniqueness theorem (Part~I, Theorem~3.1), the
minimum is the unique symmetric, idempotent copula.
A Kleene valuation is therefore the unique choice that is both
idempotent and Bayes-consistent --- the logic-compatible valuation.

\begin{definition}[Joint-Parametrized Valuation]
\label{def:joint-valuation}
A \emph{joint-parametrized valuation} consists of a complement-preserving
valuation $v$ together with an external joint function
$j : \mathrm{Prop}^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying the Fr\'echet bounds
$\max(v(A) + v(B) - 1,\, 0) \leq j(A,B) \leq \min(v(A), v(B))$.
Lean: \texttt{JointValuation}.
\end{definition}

The joint-parametrized valuation unifies the previous two:
setting $j(A,B) = v(A) \cdot v(B)$ recovers independence;
setting $j(A,B) = \min(v(A), v(B))$ recovers the Kleene valuation.
An arbitrary joint from empirical data gives full probability-style
reasoning.

\begin{proposition}[Collapse Composition]
\label{prop:collapse-composition}
Composing any product-copula valuation with the collapse homomorphism
(Part~I, Theorem~6.1) yields a Kleene valuation:
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{collapse}(v(\sigma(A))) &= \mathrm{neg}(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \land B)) &= \min(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \lor B)) &= \max(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))).
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_val\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_val\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_val\_disj}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each identity follows from the corresponding homomorphism property of
the collapse applied to the valuation's compatibility conditions.
\end{proof}

This is the mechanism connecting $[0,1]$ to K$_3$/LP/RM3:
regardless of the specific copula used on $[0,1]$, the collapsed
valuation always operates with Kleene min/max.
Independence gives trivial conditioning; min gives non-trivial logic;
an external joint gives full probability.

\section{Update Rules}
\label{sec:update}
%=============================================================================

The chain rule $\cred(A \mid B) \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
constrains how credences relate; update rules apply this constraint
dynamically, computing a posterior valuation from a prior and new
evidence.

\begin{definition}[Bayesian Update]
\label{def:bayesian-update}
Given a joint-parametrized valuation $v$ with evidence $B$ satisfying
$v(B) > 0$, the \emph{Bayesian posterior} is
$v'(A) = j(A,B) / v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Chain-Rule Preservation]
\label{prop:bayesian-chain}
The Bayesian posterior satisfies the chain rule:
$v'(A) \conjc v(B) = j(A,B)$.
The posterior is the unique credence with this property.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule}, \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By construction: $v'(A) = j(A,B)/v(B)$, so
$v'(A) \cdot v(B) = j(A,B)$.
Uniqueness follows from conditioning uniqueness (Part~I, Proposition~4.3).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Jeffrey Update]
\label{def:jeffrey-update}
For uncertain evidence with new credence $q$ for $B$, the
\emph{Jeffrey posterior}~\cite{jeffrey1965} is
\[
v'(A) = \cred(A \mid B) \cdot q + \cred(A \mid \negc B) \cdot (1 - q).
\]
At $q = 1$ this reduces to Bayesian update; at $q = 0$ it conditions
on $\negc B$.
Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate}, \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_certain},
\texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_zero}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Zero-Evidence Underdetermination]
\label{prop:zero-underdetermined}
When $v(B) = 0$, any conditional $\cred(A \mid B) \in [0,1]$ satisfies
the chain rule, so neither Bayesian nor Jeffrey update produces a unique
posterior.
Lean: \texttt{bayesian\_underdetermined\_at\_zero},
\texttt{jeffrey\_underdetermined\_at\_zero}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The chain rule gives $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$, so every $c$
is consistent.
\end{proof}

This underdetermination is the dynamic counterpart of the
static result (Part~I, Section~4):
conditioning on impossible evidence is unconstrained.
It connects to imprecise probability~\cite{walley1991,levi1980},
where sets of priors replace point estimates precisely when
the data underdetermine a unique posterior.

\section{Consequence Relations}
\label{sec:consequence}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet has no built-in consequence
relation~\cite{priest2008}.
This section shows what happens when one is added via designated values.

\subsection{Designated Values}

\begin{definition}[K$_3$, LP Designation]
\label{def:designated}
K$_3$ designates $\{1\}$; LP designates $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$.
Lean: \texttt{isDesignatedK3}, \texttt{isDesignatedLP}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Three-Valued Consequence]
\label{def:three-val-consequence}
$A_1, \ldots, A_n \models_D B$ iff every valuation $v$ with
$v(A_i) \in D$ for all $i$ satisfies $v(B) \in D$.
Lean: \texttt{threeValConsequence}.
\end{definition}

\subsection{K$_3$: Paracomplete}

K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952} designates only $1$ and has no tautologies.

\begin{proposition}[K$_3$ Has No Tautologies]
\label{prop:k3-no-taut}
$\not\models_{\mathrm{K}_3} A$ for any $A$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ valuation defeats every formula.
Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[K$_3$: Excluded Middle Fails]
\label{prop:k3-em}
$\mathrm{disj}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ is not
K$_3$-designated.
Lean: \texttt{k3\_excluded\_middle\_fails}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{LP: Paraconsistent}

LP~\cite{priest1979} designates $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$.

\begin{proposition}[LP: Excluded Middle Holds]
\label{prop:lp-em}
$\mathrm{disj}(c, \mathrm{neg}(c))$ is LP-designated for all $c$.
Lean: \texttt{lp\_excluded\_middle}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[LP: Explosion Fails]
\label{prop:lp-no-explosion}
Set $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $v(B) = 0$: both $v(A)$ and
$\mathrm{neg}(v(A))$ are LP-designated, but $v(B)$ is not.
Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Graded Consequence}

\begin{definition}[Graded Consequence]
\label{def:graded-consequence}
For threshold $t \in [0,1]$:
$A_1, \ldots, A_n \models_t B$ iff every $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ with
$v(A_i) \geq t$ for all $i$ satisfies $v(B) \geq t$.
Lean: \texttt{gradedConsequence}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[No Explosion]
\label{thm:no-explosion}
For $0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, graded consequence does not have
explosion: $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $v(B) = 0$ witnesses the failure.
Lean: \texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}.
\end{proposition}

Note that graded consequence on $[0,1]$ quantifies over all valuations
$v : \alpha \to [0,1]$; decidability and complexity of the resulting
entailment relation are not addressed here.

\section{Graded Predicates and Self-Reference}
\label{sec:predicates}
%=============================================================================

A graded predicate $P : X \to [0,1]$ generalizes fuzzy set
membership~\cite{zadeh1965}.
Pointwise operations
($(\negc P)(x) = 1 - P(x)$,
$(P \conjc Q)(x) = P(x) \cdot Q(x)$,
$(P \disjc Q)(x) = P(x) + Q(x) - P(x) \cdot Q(x)$)
satisfy all algebraic laws pointwise
(Lean: \texttt{GradedPredicate.neg\_neg},
\texttt{GradedPredicate.de\_morgan\_conj}, etc.).

\begin{definition}[Graded Predicate]
\label{def:graded-predicate}
A \emph{graded predicate} over domain $X$ is a function
$P : X \to [0,1]$.
Lean: \texttt{GradedPredicate}.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Quantifiers}

\begin{definition}[Quantifiers]
\label{def:quantifiers}
$\cred(\forall x.\; P(x)) = \inf_x P(x)$, \quad
$\cred(\exists x.\; P(x)) = \sup_x P(x)$.
Lean: \texttt{credInf}, \texttt{credSup}.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Quantifier Duality]
\label{thm:quantifier-duality}
$\negc\cred(\forall x.\; P(x)) = \cred(\exists x.\; \negc P(x))$.
Lean: \texttt{quantifier\_duality\_val}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[Classical Agreement]
\label{prop:classical-agreement}
For crisp predicates (range $\subseteq \{0, 1\}$):
$\inf_x P(x) = 0$ iff some $x$ has $P(x) = 0$;
$\sup_x P(x) = 1$ iff some $x$ has $P(x) = 1$.
Lean: \texttt{crisp\_inf\_zero\_iff}, \texttt{crisp\_sup\_one\_iff}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Self-Reference}

\begin{corollary}[Negation Fixed-Point Resolution]
\label{thm:russell}
If $c = \negc c$, then $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
(Immediate from Part~I, Lemma~5.1; Lean: \texttt{russell\_fixed\_point}.)
\end{corollary}

The corollary is an immediate consequence of the fixed-point lemma
(Part~I, Lemma~5.1), which is itself elementary.
Its interest is conditional: \emph{if} one works in an ambient
framework admitting unrestricted comprehension --- for instance,
paraconsistent set theory~\cite{brady2006,weber2010} --- then
Russell's predicate $R = \{x : x \notin x\}$ applied to itself gives
$R(R) = \negc R(R)$, and the corollary forces $R(R) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
We do not develop such a framework here.
The corollary merely shows that the product De Morgan triplet
\emph{assigns a consistent value} to the fixed-point equation, which
is a prerequisite for any graded paraconsistent set theory built on
this structure.

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet supports a natural suite of applied
constructions.
Valuations (complement-preserving, independent, Kleene,
joint-parametrized) assign credences to propositions under different
dependence assumptions; composing with the collapse homomorphism
always yields a Kleene valuation.
Bayesian and Jeffrey update propagate credences through the chain rule,
with zero-evidence underdetermination connecting to imprecise
probability~\cite{walley1991}.
On the Kleene quotient, consequence relations recover K$_3$
(paracomplete), LP (paraconsistent), and graded thresholds ---
all without explosion.
Graded predicates with inf/sup quantifiers satisfy classical duality,
and the negation fixed point at $\tfrac{1}{2}$ provides a consistent
resolution of self-referential equations, conditional on an ambient
framework.

Open problems: first-order extension with formal proof theory;
consequence relations compatible with unconstrained conditioning at
zero; connections to paraconsistent set theory; and the relationship
between the congruence classification (Part~I) and the space of
definable consequence relations.
