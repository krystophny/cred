\begin{abstract}
Probability theory and many-valued logic share the same algebraic core
on $[0,1]$ --- product conjunction, probabilistic-sum disjunction,
standard complement --- but diverge on the conditional:
probability requires division (undefined at zero evidence);
product logic uses residuation (forcing ex falso at zero evidence).
We study \emph{chain-rule conditioning}, where the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$:
at positive evidence it reduces to Bayesian conditioning; at zero
evidence it leaves the conditional unconstrained.
Our main result is a \emph{bridge theorem}: LP consequence on the
three-valued Kleene lattice is equivalent to positivity consequence on
$[0,1]$, and K$_3$ consequence is equivalent to certainty consequence.
Discrete three-valued reasoning and continuous $[0,1]$ reasoning are
thus two views of the same relation.
Supporting results include a congruence classification
(the Kleene lattice is the unique non-trivial three-element quotient
under $[0,1]$-multiplication; no non-trivial finite quotient exists
under $\Real$-multiplication) and graded consequence with no-explosion.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib
(approximately 3000 lines, zero \texttt{sorry}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

You believe it will rain with credence $0.8$.
You see the street is wet.
How should your credence in rain change?
Both probability theory and many-valued logic answer this question
using values in $[0,1]$ --- but they answer it differently when the
evidence is impossible.

\emph{Probability theory}~\cite{ramsey1931,definetti1937,jaynes2003}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of belief.
Given $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$, a
probabilist computes the conditional via division:
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) = \cred(\text{rain} \land
\text{wet}) / \cred(\text{wet})$.
When $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0$ --- you have never seen a wet street ---
the ratio $0/0$ is undefined.

\emph{Many-valued logic}~\cite{hajek1998,gottwald2001,klement2000}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of truth.
Given a t-norm conjunction and the residuated
implication~\cite{galatos2007}
$a \to b = \sup\{c : a \otimes c \leq b\}$, the conditional at
evidence zero is forced: $0 \to b = 1$ for all $b$, since
$0 \otimes c = 0 \leq b$ for every $c$.
Impossible evidence proves everything --- ex falso quodlibet is built
in~\cite{hajek1998}.

Both traditions share a core algebraic structure when the t-norm is
the product: the \emph{product De Morgan triplet}
$(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ with $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$,
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$, and $\negc c = 1 - c$.
They diverge only in the status of the conditional.

This paper studies \emph{chain-rule conditioning}:
the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ without
invoking either division or residuation.
When evidence is positive, it reduces to division (recovering
Bayesian conditioning); when evidence is zero, the equation
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ is satisfied by every $c \in [0,1]$, leaving the
conditional unconstrained.
The chain rule is the sole structural constraint beyond the triplet.

\textbf{Main results.}
We mark each result as \emph{known} (established in the literature,
newly Lean-verified) or \emph{new} (first appearing here, to our
knowledge).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]

  LP consequence on the three-valued Kleene lattice is equivalent to
  positivity consequence on $[0,1]$; K$_3$ consequence is equivalent

  (Theorems~\ref{thm:lp-bridge}--\ref{thm:k3-bridge}).

\item \textbf{Equilibria characterization} [new]:

\item \textbf{Kleene collapse and congruence classification} [new]:
  the Kleene lattice is the unique non-trivial three-element quotient

\item \textbf{Graded no-explosion} [new]:
  explosion fails for any threshold $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$

H\'ajek~\cite{hajek1998} and
Esteva--Godo~\cite{esteva2000,esteva2001} developed Product Logic

Mundici~\cite{mundici2011} and

and probability; Coletti and

coherent setting; Gilio and
Sanfilippo~\cite{gilio2013,gilio2014} studied conditional events
and iterated conditioning.
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955}, Popper~\cite{popper1959}, and
de Finetti~\cite{definetti1936} proposed primitive conditioning in
measure-theoretic or Boolean settings;
\'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021} developed
de Finetti's conditional algebraically.
Adams~\cite{adams1975} developed a probabilistic logic of
conditionals; van Fraassen~\cite{vanfraassen1976,vanfraassen1983}
explored connections between probabilities and conditionals in
relevant logic.
Pavelka~\cite{pavelka1979i} developed graded inference for
many-valued logics with truth values on $[0,1]$.
Ferm\"uller~\cite{fermuller2014} reached the Kleene lattice from
game semantics; Gaines~\cite{gaines1978} unified fuzzy and
probabilistic uncertainty via population semantics.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed plausible
reasoning satisfying certain desiderata must be isomorphic to
probability (see also Paris~\cite{paris1994} for a mathematical
perspective on uncertain reasoning);
chain-rule conditioning drops the requirement that the
conditional be a function of the marginals alone.
Lewis's triviality result~\cite{lewis1976} shows that if one
treats $P(A \mid B)$ as the probability of a ``conditional
proposition'' $A \to B$ in the same algebra, then the probability
measure must be trivial.
Chain-rule conditioning avoids Lewis's conditions: the joint
$\cred(A \land B)$ is a separate parameter (not derived from the
conditional), and $\cred(A \mid B)$ is not identified with the
credence of any proposition.
Halpern~\cite{halpern2003} provides a detailed analysis of the
scope of Lewis's result.
To our knowledge, no prior work combines chain-rule conditioning
(with evidence-zero analysis), the bridge theorem (connecting LP/K$_3$
consequence to $[0,1]$ consequence), the congruence classification,
and the unification of Bayesian update with paraconsistent logical
consequence into a single framework on $[0,1]$.

\section{The Product De Morgan Triplet}
\label{sec:algebra}
%=============================================================================

This section defines the concrete structure we study: the product
De Morgan triplet~\cite{klement2000,esteva2000} on $[0,1]$.
This is not an axiomatic class of algebras: all properties follow from
the specific arithmetic of $[0,1]$.
Goguen~\cite{goguen1969} used the product t-norm as the conjunction in
his logic of inexact concepts.

\begin{definition}[Credence]
\label{def:credence}
A \emph{credence} is a real number $c \in [0, 1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Running Example: Rain and Wet Streets]
\label{ex:rain}
Let $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$.
Then $\cred(\text{no rain}) = 1 - 0.8 = 0.2$, and the product
conjunction gives $\cred(\text{rain}) \conjc \cred(\text{wet}) =
0.8 \times 0.9 = 0.72$.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Negation]
\label{def:neg}
$\negc c = 1 - c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Product Conjunction]
\label{def:conj}
$c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

The product is the product t-norm of fuzzy logic~\cite{klement2000}.
Note that $\conjc$ is not idempotent: $0.8^2 = 0.64 \neq 0.8$.
Repeating the same uncertain evidence \emph{decreases} the joint
credence.
Only $0$ and $1$ satisfy $c^2 = c$.

\begin{definition}[Disjunction]
\label{def:disj}
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = \negc(\negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2)
  = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

In our running example,
$\cred(\text{rain} \lor \text{wet}) = 0.8 + 0.9 - 0.72 = 0.98$.

The triple $(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ is the \emph{product De Morgan
triplet}~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998}.

\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Properties; known~\cite{klement2000},
verified here]
\label{thm:algebraic}
For all $c, c_1, c_2, c_3 \in [0,1]$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\negc\negc c = c$ \hfill (involution)
\item $\negc 0 = 1$, $\negc 1 = 0$ \hfill (boundary)
\item $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_2 \conjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \conjc c_2) \conjc c_3 = c_1 \conjc (c_2 \conjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \conjc 1 = c$, $c \conjc 0 = 0$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_2 \disjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \disjc c_2) \disjc c_3 = c_1 \disjc (c_2 \disjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \disjc 0 = c$, $c \disjc 1 = 1$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $\negc(c_1 \conjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \disjc \negc c_2$,
  $\negc(c_1 \disjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2$ \hfill (De Morgan)
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{neg\_neg}, \texttt{conj\_comm}, \texttt{conj\_assoc},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_conj}, \texttt{de\_morgan\_disj}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Arithmetic on $[0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The structure is two dual commutative monoids connected by De Morgan
duality; it is not a De Morgan algebra since $\conjc$ is not idempotent
($c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0,1\}$;
Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff})
and $\conjc$ does not distribute over $\disjc$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_disj\_not\_distrib}).

\begin{remark}[Product vs.\ Joint]
\label{rem:product-vs-joint}
The product $c_1 \conjc c_2$ is an algebraic operation on credence
values, not a claim about the joint credence
$\cred(A \land B)$ of two propositions.
The joint depends on the dependence structure and lies in the
Fr\'echet-Hoeffding interval
$[\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0),\; \min(\cred(A), \cred(B))]$:
independence gives $\cred(A) \cdot \cred(B)$;
maximum positive dependence (comonotonicity) gives
$\min(\cred(A), \cred(B))$~\cite{nelsen2006}.
In our running example: $\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet})$ could be
anywhere in $[0.7,\, 0.8]$.
Under independence, it is $0.72$; under comonotonicity,
$\min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$.
\end{remark}

\textbf{Comparison with Product Logic.}
The product De Morgan triplet and H\'ajek's Product Logic
($\Pi$-logic)~\cite{hajek1998} share the same t-norm.
They differ in the companion operation: $\Pi$-logic uses
residuation $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$, which
forces $0 \to b = 1$ (ex falso);
the triplet uses the involutive complement $\negc c = 1 - c$
and chain-rule conditioning, which leaves $\cred(A \mid 0)$
unconstrained.
On positive evidence, the two coincide.

\textbf{Relationship to probability.}
Every probability space satisfies the chain rule and complement axioms.
Probability adds a sigma-algebra, a countably additive measure, and
sigma-additivity.
The product De Morgan triplet drops all of these: there is no sample
space, no sigma-algebra, no additivity beyond complement.
The triplet retains exactly the structure shared by both probability
and $[0,1]$-valued logic --- the greatest common factor of the two
traditions.

\textbf{Conjunction selection.}
For logic-compatible valuations, idempotence
($\cred(A \land A) = \cred(A)$) is essential: repeating evidence
should not change it.
A \emph{copula} is a function $C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying:
$C(a, 0) = C(0, b) = 0$, $C(a, 1) = a$, $C(1, b) = b$, and the
\emph{2-increasing property} --- for all $a_1 \leq a_2$,
$b_1 \leq b_2$:
$C(a_2, b_2) - C(a_2, b_1) - C(a_1, b_2) + C(a_1, b_1) \geq 0$
(Appendix~\ref{app:prelim})~\cite{nelsen2006}.
Among truth-functional joints (copulas on $[0,1]$), the minimum is
the unique symmetric idempotent copula.

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of Truth-Functional Conjunction;
known~\cite{nelsen2006}, verified here]
\label{thm:min-copula-unique}
Let $C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ be a symmetric, idempotent copula.
Then $C(a,b) = \min(a,b)$ for all $a,b \in [0,1]$.
Lean: \texttt{copula\_idempotent\_unique},
\texttt{min\_copula\_unique}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Upper bound: the 2-increasing property on $[0,a] \times [b,1]$ with
unit marginal gives $C(a,b) \leq a$; by symmetry $C(a,b) \leq b$.
Lower bound: assume $a \leq b$.
Apply 2-increasing to $[0,a] \times [a,b]$:
$C(a,b) - C(a,a) - C(0,b) + C(0,a) \geq 0$.
By idempotence $C(a,a) = a$ and zero boundary $C(0,b) = C(0,a) = 0$,
so $C(a,b) \geq a = \min(a,b)$.
\end{proof}

The product fails idempotence ($c^2 \neq c$ for $c \in (0,1)$);
\L ukasiewicz fails similarly
($\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$;
Lean: \texttt{luk\_not\_idempotent}).
The minimum joint is the \emph{comonotonicity copula}: the
extremal case where all random variables are increasing functions of
a single latent variable~\cite{dhaene2002,nelsen2006}.
Independence ($c_1 \cdot c_2$) fails both idempotence and
non-trivial conditioning
(Lean: \texttt{prod\_trivial\_conditioning});
the RM3 joint $\max(1-a, b)$ fails Bayes consistency
(Lean: \texttt{rm3\_not\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).

\section{Chain-Rule Conditioning}
\label{sec:conditioning}
%=============================================================================

Suppose you want to condition on wet streets:
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$ should satisfy
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) \cdot \cred(\text{wet}) =
\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet})$.
When $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9 > 0$, this gives
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) = \min(0.8, 0.9) / 0.9 = 8/9$.
But what if $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0$?
You have never seen a wet street.
What is $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$?

\begin{definition}[Conditioning Structure]
\label{def:conditioning}
A \emph{conditioning structure} for
$(\text{joint}, \text{evidence}) \in [0,1]^2$
with $\text{joint} \leq \text{evidence}$
consists of $\text{condCred} \in [0,1]$ satisfying
$\text{condCred} \conjc \text{evidence} = \text{joint}$.
In propositional notation:
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$,
where $\cred(A \land B) \leq \cred(B)$.
\end{definition}

The joint $\cred(A \land B)$ is supplied as input, not computed from
marginals; the constraint $\cred(A \land B) \leq \cred(B)$ is the
standard monotonicity requirement that the conjunction cannot exceed
either conjunct.
H\'ajek, Godo, and Esteva~\cite{hajek1995} used the chain rule
constraint as a technical device within fuzzy logic;
Fritz~\cite{fritz2020} developed a categorical approach to
conditioning as a primitive morphism;
Jeffrey~\cite{jeffrey1965} generalized Bayesian conditioning to
uncertain evidence via a mixture formula, also using the chain rule as
the primitive mechanism.
Calabrese~\cite{calabrese1987} and
Coletti--Scozzafava~\cite{coletti2002} developed algebraic and
coherence-based approaches to conditional probability as a primitive;
Gilio and Sanfilippo~\cite{gilio2013,gilio2014} extended this to
iterated conditioning and compounds of conditionals.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} showed plausible reasoning satisfying certain
desiderata must be isomorphic to probability; chain-rule conditioning
drops the requirement that the conditional be a function
$F(\cred(A), \cred(B))$ of the marginals alone.

\subsection{Three Answers to Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:three-answers}

The chain rule gives $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$: the conditional is
\emph{unconstrained}
(Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
Residuation gives $0 \to b = 1$ for all $b$: ex falso is
\emph{forced}.
Probability gives $0/0$: \emph{undefined}.

The three frameworks agree on every row except evidence $= 0$.
This single row is where probability, logic, and the chain rule
part ways.

\subsection{Existence and Uniqueness}
\label{sec:existence}

\begin{proposition}[Existence and Uniqueness of Conditioning;
known~\cite{renyi1955,popper1959}, verified here]
\label{thm:existence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item \textbf{Positive evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} > 0$, a conditioning structure exists iff
  $\mathrm{joint} \leq \mathrm{evidence}$, and is then unique:
  $\mathrm{condCred} = \mathrm{joint} / \mathrm{evidence}$.
\item \textbf{Zero evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} = 0$, the constraint
  $\mathrm{condCred} \cdot 0 = \mathrm{joint}$ forces
  $\mathrm{joint} = 0$ but leaves $\mathrm{condCred}$ entirely
  unconstrained: every $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_mk}, \texttt{conditioning\_unique},
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~If $0 < e$ and $j \leq e$, then $c = j/e \in [0,1]$ and
$c \cdot e = j$.
Uniqueness: if $c \cdot e = c' \cdot e$ with $e > 0$, then $c = c'$.
(ii)~$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c \in [0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The dichotomy is sharp: at positive evidence, the conditional is a
\emph{function} (division); at zero evidence, it is a \emph{relation}
(any value is consistent).

\subsection{Path Dependence at Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:path-dependence}

Why not just take a limit?
Under the min-copula joint $\min(a, b)$, the conditional for $b > 0$
is $\min(a,b)/b$.
As $b \to 0$, the limit depends on \emph{how} $a$ and $b$ approach
zero together.

\begin{example}[Rain as Wet Streets Vanish]
\label{ex:path}
What is $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$ as
$\cred(\text{wet}) \to 0$?
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ fixed, $\cred(\text{wet}) \to 0$:
  limit $= 1$.
  Strong belief overwhelms vanishing evidence.
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.5 \cdot \cred(\text{wet})$:
  limit $= 0.5$.
  Proportional decline preserves the ratio.
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = \cred(\text{wet})^2$:
  limit $= 0$.
  The proposition vanishes faster than the evidence.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved.
How evidence becomes impossible matters.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Path Dependence at Evidence Zero;
known~\cite{chang1997}, verified here]
\label{thm:path-dependence}
The limit of $\min(a,b)/b$ as $b \to 0$ is path-dependent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item Fixed $a > 0$: limit is $1$.
\item $a(b) = \lambda b$, $\lambda \in (0,1]$: limit is $\lambda$.
\item $a(b) = b^\alpha$, $\alpha > 1$: limit is $0$.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved; ex falso requires an added
convention.
Lean: \texttt{path\_dep\_fixed\_a}, \texttt{path\_dep\_proportional},
\texttt{path\_dep\_square}, \texttt{path\_any\_positive\_value}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~For small $b < a$, $\min(a,b)/b = 1$.
(ii)~$\min(\lambda b, b)/b = \lambda$.
(iii)~$\min(b^\alpha, b)/b = b^{\alpha-1} \to 0$.
\end{proof}

is the minimal commitment consistent with the data.

The set of all posteriors consistent with the chain rule at zero
evidence is $[0,1]$ --- the maximal imprecise
posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
An agent may narrow this set by adopting a prior over the zero-evidence
regime (de Finetti's void, Popper's convention, etc.), but the
chain rule itself imposes no such commitment.

\textbf{Lewis triviality.}
Lewis~\cite{lewis1976} showed that if one treats $P(A \mid B)$ as the
probability of a ``conditional proposition'' $A \to B$ in the same
algebra --- i.e., if $P(A \mid B) = P(A \to B)$ for some event
$A \to B$ --- then the probability measure must be trivial (supported
on at most two atoms).
Chain-rule conditioning avoids Lewis's conditions in two respects.
First, $\cred(A \mid B)$ is not identified with the credence of any
proposition; it is constrained only by the chain rule with the joint as
a \emph{separate parameter}.
Second, the joint $\cred(A \land B)$ is not derived from the
conditional; it is given data.
Lewis's proof requires both identifications, so the triviality
conclusion does not apply~\cite{halpern2003}.

\textbf{Connection to imprecise probability.}
When evidence has credence zero, the set of chain-rule-consistent
conditionals is $[0,1]$.
This is exactly the vacuous lower prevision of Walley~\cite{walley1991}:
the agent's state of belief about $\cred(A \mid B)$ is maximally
imprecise.
Levi~\cite{levi1980} argues that maximal imprecision at zero evidence
is the correct epistemic response.
Chain-rule conditioning reaches the same conclusion algebraically,
without assuming a framework of imprecise probabilities.

\section{Algebraic Equilibria}
\label{sec:equilibria}
%=============================================================================

What happens when you conjoin a credence with itself?
$\cred(\text{rain}) \conjc \cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8^2 = 0.64 < 0.8$.
Combining two independent copies of the same uncertain evidence
\emph{decreases} the joint credence.
Only $0$ and $1$ are stable: $0^2 = 0$, $1^2 = 1$.

\begin{proposition}[Self-Conjunction Decay;
known~\cite{klement2000}, verified here]
\label{prop:self-conj-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$: $c \conjc c = c^2 < c$.
Dually, $c \disjc c > c$.
Lean: \texttt{self\_conj\_lt}, \texttt{self\_disj\_gt}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 < c \iff c(c - 1) < 0$, which holds since $0 < c < 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Iterated Self-Conjunction;
known~\cite{klement2000}, verified here]
\label{prop:pow-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$ and $n \in \Nat$:
$c^{n+1} < c^n$.
In particular, $c^n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$:
unrenewed evidence decays.
Lean: \texttt{pow\_strictly\_decreasing}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^n \cdot c < c^n \cdot 1 = c^n$ since $0 < c < 1$.
Convergence: $c^n = e^{n \ln c} \to 0$ since $\ln c < 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Unique Negation Fixed Point;
known~\cite{kripke1975}, verified here]
\label{thm:liar}
$\negc c = c$ if and only if $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Lean: \texttt{liar\_fixed\_point},
\texttt{neg\_fixed\_point\_unique}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Equilibria Characterization; new]
\label{thm:equilibria}
A credence $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies $c \conjc c = c$ or $\negc c = c$
if and only if $c \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.
Equivalently, the set of conjunction-idempotent values $\{0, 1\}$
together with the unique negation fixed point $\{\tfrac{1}{2}\}$
exhausts the algebraic equilibria.
Lean: \texttt{equilibria\_characterization}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff}).
$1 - c = c$ iff $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lemma~\ref{thm:liar}).
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation.}
The three equilibria have distinct epistemic characters:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $0$ = \emph{refuted}: conjunction-idempotent at the lower
  boundary.
\item $1$ = \emph{confirmed}: conjunction-idempotent at the upper
  boundary.
\item $\tfrac{1}{2}$ = \emph{irreducibly uncertain}: the unique
  negation fixed point, satisfying
  $\negc\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{itemize}
Every other credence $c \in (0,1) \setminus \{\tfrac{1}{2}\}$
is not an equilibrium: $c^2 < c$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}), so iterated
self-conjunction drives it toward $0$; it is not a negation fixed
point either.
Finite-valued logics take the three values as primitive;
the continuous framework \emph{derives} them as the algebraic
equilibria of the product De Morgan triplet.

\begin{remark}[Algebraic equilibria vs.\ dynamical convergence]
\label{rem:attractor-caveat}
Propositions~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}--\ref{prop:pow-decay}
establish that iterated self-conjunction $c, c^2, c^4, \ldots$
converges to $0$ for every $c \in (0,1)$.
This is a precise algebraic statement about the product t-norm.
A formal convergence theorem for Bayesian update toward $0$, $1$,
or $\tfrac{1}{2}$ would require specifying a stochastic model for
the evidence sequence, which is beyond our scope.
\end{remark}

\section{The Kleene Collapse and Congruence Classification}
\label{sec:collapse}
%=============================================================================

The three equilibria from Section~\ref{sec:equilibria} are not just
fixed points --- they form a quotient algebra.
The partition $\{0\}, (0,1), \{1\}$ defines a congruence on the
product De Morgan triplet, and the quotient is the Kleene lattice.

Define the collapse:
\[
\text{collapse}(c) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } c = 0, \\
\tfrac{1}{2} & \text{if } 0 < c < 1, \\
1 & \text{if } c = 1.
\end{cases}
\]

\begin{theorem}[Collapse Homomorphism; folklore~\cite{bergmann2008},
verified here]
\label{thm:collapse}
The collapse is a surjective homomorphism from
$([0,1], \negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ to
$(\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}, \mathrm{neg}, \min, \max)$.
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_disj}, \texttt{collapse\_surjective}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Case analysis.
Negation: $0 \mapsto 1$, $1 \mapsto 0$; if $0 < c < 1$ then
$0 < 1-c < 1$.
Conjunction: $0$ absorbs, $1$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Disjunction: $1$ absorbs, $0$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2 < 1$.
\end{proof}

The target is the Kleene lattice~\cite{kleene1952}, underlying
K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952}, LP~\cite{priest1979}, and
RM3~\cite{anderson1975,meyer1972}.
Belnap~\cite{belnap1977} and Dunn~\cite{dunn1976} studied this
three-valued structure (and its four-valued extension) as a
``useful logic'' for reasoning with incomplete and inconsistent
information; Fitting~\cite{fitting1991,fitting2006} developed a
systematic bilattice framework in which the Kleene lattice
appears as one component of a product construction.

\begin{remark}[Connection to possibility theory]
\label{rem:possibility}
The Kleene quotient's $\min$/$\max$ operations are exactly the
conjunction/disjunction of possibility
theory~\cite{dubois1988}.
The collapse thus provides a bridge not only to many-valued logic but
also to possibility-theoretic reasoning.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Uniqueness and Impossibility}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to Classical Logic;
known~\cite{bergmann2008}, verified here]
\label{prop:no-classical}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation.
Lean: \texttt{no\_boolean\_neg\_retraction}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ has no solution
in $\{0, 1\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to G\"odel or \L ukasiewicz Logic;
new]
\label{prop:no-godel-luk}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ preserves G\"odel
negation (which has no fixed point), and no such $\varphi$
preserves both standard negation and \L ukasiewicz conjunction
(since $\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq
\tfrac{1}{2}$).
Lean: \texttt{no\_godel\_collapse}, \texttt{no\_luk\_collapse}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
G\"odel: $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be a fixed point of G\"odel
negation, which has none (G\"odel negation maps every non-zero value
to zero; Lean: \texttt{godel\_neg\_no\_fixed\_point}).
\L ukasiewicz: negation forces $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Pick $c$ with $c \conjc c = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (e.g., $c = \sqrt{1/2}$).
Then $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \text{\L{}uk}(\varphi(c), \varphi(c))$.
But $\text{\L{}uk}(v, v) \in \{0, 1\}$ for all
$v \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, contradicting
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the Three-Element Quotient; new]
\label{thm:quotient-unique}
Any surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to S$ ($|S|=3$) preserving negation
and conjunction yields the Kleene lattice (up to relabeling).
Lean: \texttt{three\_element\_quotient\_unique}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be the unique negation fixed point of $S$.
The remaining elements $\varphi(0)$, $\varphi(1)$ form the 2-cycle.
The conjunction table is forced: $\varphi(0)$ absorbs, $\varphi(1)$
is identity,
and $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc \tfrac{1}{2}) =
\varphi(\tfrac{1}{4}) = \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$
(since $\tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$).
This forces Kleene $\min$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Congruence Classification}
\label{sec:congruence}

The collapse results combine into a congruence classification at two
levels, depending on the scope of the multiplication compatibility
hypothesis.
A \emph{unit congruence} requires multiplication compatibility only for
$[0,1]$-valued inputs.
A \emph{real congruence} extends compatibility to all of $\Real$.

\begin{theorem}[Congruence Classification; new]
\label{thm:congruence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item \textbf{Singleton boundaries:}
  Under any non-trivial unit congruence, $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ are
  singleton classes.
\item \textbf{No two-element quotient:}
  No surjective homomorphism $[0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}).
\item \textbf{Uniqueness at three elements:}
  The Kleene lattice is the unique three-element quotient
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}), and the Kleene partition is
  itself a verified unit congruence (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}).
\item \textbf{$\Real$-rigidity:}
  Under $\Real$-multiplication, no non-trivial finite quotient exists
  with $n \geq 2$ classes.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{UnitCongruence.singleton\_zero},
\texttt{singleton\_one}, \texttt{kleeneCongruence},
\texttt{RealCongruence.no\_nontrivial\_finite\_quotient}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
(1)~Suppose $0 \sim \varepsilon$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$.
Negation compatibility gives $1 \sim 1 - \varepsilon$.
For any $x \in [0,1]$, multiplication compatibility yields
$x = x \cdot 1 \sim x(1 - \varepsilon)$.
By induction, $x \sim x(1 - \varepsilon)^n$ for all $n$.
Since $0 < 1 - \varepsilon < 1$, for any $x > 0$ there exists
$n$ with $x(1 - \varepsilon)^n \leq \varepsilon$.
Write $x(1 - \varepsilon)^n = \varepsilon \cdot q$ where
$q = x(1 - \varepsilon)^n / \varepsilon \in [0,1]$.
From $0 \sim \varepsilon$, multiplication compatibility gives
$0 = 0 \cdot q \sim \varepsilon \cdot q = x(1 - \varepsilon)^n$.
By transitivity, $x \sim x(1-\varepsilon)^n \sim 0$
for every $x \in [0,1]$, so the quotient is trivial ---
contradiction.
For $\{1\}$: if $1 \sim x$ with $x < 1$, negation gives
$0 \sim 1 - x$ with $1 - x > 0$, reducing to the previous case.

(2)~A surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ must satisfy
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$, which has no
solution in $\{0,1\}$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}).

(3)~Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}.
The Kleene partition is a verified unit congruence
(Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}).

(4)~Set $a = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
With $n \geq 2$ classes, pigeonhole on the $n + 1$ powers
$a^0, a^1, \ldots, a^n$ gives indices $i < j \leq n$ with
$a^i \sim a^j$.
Write $a^j = a^i \cdot a^p$ where $p = j - i > 0$.
Under $\Real$-multiplication, multiply both sides of $a^i \sim a^j$
by $(a^i)^{-1}$ (which exists in $\Real$):
$1 = (a^i)^{-1} \cdot a^i \sim (a^i)^{-1} \cdot a^j = a^p$.
Since $0 < a^p < 1$, negation gives $0 \sim 1 - a^p$ with
$1 - a^p > 0$, contradicting singleton boundaries from~(1).
\end{proof}

The Kleene lattice exists as a quotient under $[0,1]$-multiplication;
whether four or more classes are possible is \textbf{open}.
The gap between unit and real congruences isolates
$[0,1]$-cancellation as the key obstacle:
in $\Real$, $ax = bx$ with $x > 0$ implies $a = b$;
in $[0,1]$ the quotient $a/x$ may leave the interval.

\section{The Bridge Theorem}
\label{sec:bridge}
%=============================================================================

The collapse from Section~\ref{sec:collapse} is a homomorphism of
connectives.
This section shows it induces an \emph{equivalence of consequence
relations}: three-valued logical consequence on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is the same as a natural consequence
relation on $[0,1]$.

\subsection{Valuations}

\begin{definition}[Independent Valuation]
\label{def:indep-valuation}
A \emph{complement-preserving} valuation satisfies
$v(\negc A) = \negc v(A)$.
It is \emph{independent} if additionally
$v(A \land B) = v(A) \conjc v(B)$ and
$v(A \lor B) = v(A) \disjc v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{CpValuation}, \texttt{IndepValuation}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Kleene Valuation]
\label{def:kleene-valuation}
A complement-preserving valuation into
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is a \emph{Kleene valuation} if
$v(A \land B) = \min(v(A), v(B))$ and
$v(A \lor B) = \max(v(A), v(B))$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Collapse Composition; new]
\label{prop:collapse-composition}
Composing an independent valuation with the collapse homomorphism
yields a Kleene valuation:
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{collapse}(v(\negc A)) &= \mathrm{neg}(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \land B)) &= \min(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \lor B)) &= \max(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))).
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_val\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_val\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_val\_disj}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{lemma}[Lifting; new]
\label{lem:lifting}
Every Kleene valuation $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
lifts to a $[0,1]$-valued function $v$ such that
$\mathrm{collapse} \circ\, v = w$.
Specifically, map $0 \mapsto 0$, $\tfrac{1}{2} \mapsto \tfrac{1}{2}$,
$1 \mapsto 1$.
Lean: \texttt{liftThreeVal}, \texttt{lift\_collapse\_id}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Each of the three values has a canonical preimage in $[0,1]$.
The composition $\text{collapse} \circ \text{lift}$ is the identity
on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consequence Relations on $[0,1]$}

\begin{definition}[Consequence Relations]
\label{def:consequence}
For propositions drawn from a type $\alpha$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \emph{Positivity consequence:}
  $\Gamma \models^+ A$ iff for every $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$, if
  $v(p) > 0$ for all $p \in \Gamma$, then $v(A) > 0$.
\item \emph{Certainty consequence:}
  $\Gamma \models^1 A$ iff for every $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$, if
  $v(p) = 1$ for all $p \in \Gamma$, then $v(A) = 1$.
\item \emph{Three-valued consequence:}
  $\Gamma \models_D A$ (for designated set $D$) iff for every
  $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, if
  $w(p) \in D$ for all $p \in \Gamma$, then $w(A) \in D$.
\end{itemize}
Lean: \texttt{positivityConsequence},
\texttt{certaintyConsequence}, \texttt{threeValConsequence}.
\end{definition}

\subsection{The Bridge}

\begin{theorem}[LP Bridge Theorem; new]
\label{thm:lp-bridge}
LP consequence (designating $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is equivalent to positivity consequence
on $[0,1]$:
\[
\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{LP}} A
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \models^+ A.
\]
Lean: \texttt{lp\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$
Given $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ with $v(p) > 0$ for all $p \in \Gamma$,
define $w = \text{collapse} \circ\, v$.
Then $w(p)$ is LP-designated iff $v(p) > 0$
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_designated\_iff\_pos}), so all premises are
LP-designated.
By LP consequence, $w(A)$ is LP-designated, hence $v(A) > 0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$
Given $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ with all premises
LP-designated, define $v = \text{lift} \circ\, w$.
Each LP-designated value lifts to a positive credence
(Lean: \texttt{lift\_pos\_of\_designated\_lp}).
By positivity consequence, $v(A) > 0$.
Then $\text{collapse}(v(A))$ is LP-designated, and
$\text{collapse} \circ\, \text{lift} = \text{id}$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:lifting}) gives $w(A)$ LP-designated.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[K$_3$ Bridge Theorem; new]
\label{thm:k3-bridge}
K$_3$ consequence (designating $\{1\}$) on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is equivalent to certainty consequence
on $[0,1]$:
\[
\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{K}_3} A
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \models^1 A.
\]
Lean: \texttt{k3\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$
Given $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ with $v(p) = 1$ for all $p \in \Gamma$,
define $w = \text{collapse} \circ\, v$.
Then $w(p) = 1$ iff $v(p) = 1$
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_designated\_iff\_one}), so all premises are
K$_3$-designated.
By K$_3$ consequence, $w(A) = 1$, hence $v(A) = 1$.

$(\Leftarrow)$
Given $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ with all premises
K$_3$-designated, define $v = \text{lift} \circ\, w$.
Each K$_3$-designated value lifts to credence $1$
(Lean: \texttt{lift\_one\_of\_designated\_k3}).
By certainty consequence, $v(A) = 1$.
Then $\text{collapse}(v(A)) = 1$, and
$\text{collapse} \circ\, \text{lift} = \text{id}$ gives $w(A) = 1$.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation.}
The bridge theorems say that discrete three-valued logical reasoning
is faithfully encoded in continuous $[0,1]$ reasoning, and
vice versa.
An LP logician working with designated values
$\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$ is implicitly performing positivity reasoning
on the continuum; a K$_3$ logician working with designated
value $\{1\}$ is implicitly performing certainty reasoning.
The collapse does not merely project --- it \emph{reflects}:
if a consequence holds on $[0,1]$, it holds on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, and conversely.

\textbf{Scope.}
The consequence relations quantify over \emph{assignments} of values
to propositional variables, not over structured formulas with
connectives.
The collapse preserves connectives
(Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}), so for a logic with a fixed set of
connectives, the bridge extends to formulas built from those
connectives by induction.
But the bridge theorems themselves make no assumption about
formula structure: they hold for arbitrary assignments
$v : \alpha \to [0,1]$.

The key mechanism is the characterization of designated values via
the collapse:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $\text{collapse}(c)$ is LP-designated if and only if $c > 0$,
\item $\text{collapse}(c)$ is K$_3$-designated if and only if $c = 1$.
\end{itemize}
These are the two canonical ways to carve $[0,1]$ into
``designated'' and ``undesignated'': by positivity or by certainty.
Each corresponds to exactly one of the standard three-valued
consequence relations.

\subsection{Bayesian and Jeffrey Update}
\label{sec:update}

\begin{definition}[Bayesian Update]
\label{def:bayesian-update}
Given a joint-parametrized valuation $v$ with evidence $B$ satisfying
$v(B) > 0$, the \emph{Bayesian posterior} is
$v'(A) = \cred(A \land B) / v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Chain-Rule Preservation and Uniqueness;
known~\cite{jaynes2003}, verified here]
\label{prop:bayesian-chain}
The posterior satisfies $v'(A) \conjc v(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ and
is the unique credence with this property.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule},
\texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$v'(A) = j/e$ by construction, so $v'(A) \cdot e = j$.
Uniqueness: if $c \cdot e = c' \cdot e$ with $e > 0$, then $c = c'$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Jeffrey Update]
\label{def:jeffrey-update}
For uncertain evidence with new credence $q$ for $B$:
$v'(A) = \cred(A \mid B) \cdot q + \cred(A \mid \negc B) \cdot (1 - q)$.
At $q = 1$ this reduces to Bayesian update; at $q = 0$ it conditions
on $\negc B$.
Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate}, \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_certain},
\texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_zero}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Jeffrey Update: Typical Case]
\label{prop:jeffrey-typical}
When $0 < v(B) < 1$, both conditionals $\cred(A \mid B)$ and
$\cred(A \mid \negc B)$ are uniquely determined by the chain rule
(since $v(B) > 0$ and $v(\negc B) = 1 - v(B) > 0$).
The Jeffrey posterior is therefore uniquely determined by the prior
$v$, the joint credences $\cred(A \land B)$ and
$\cred(A \land \negc B)$, and the new evidence credence $q$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Both conditionals exist and are unique by
Proposition~\ref{thm:existence}(i), since both $v(B)$ and
$v(\negc B)$ are positive.
The Jeffrey formula is a convex combination of two uniquely
determined values.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Zero-Evidence Underdetermination; new]
\label{prop:zero-underdetermined}
When $v(B) = 0$, any conditional $\cred(A \mid B) \in [0,1]$ satisfies
the chain rule, so neither Bayesian nor Jeffrey update produces a unique
posterior.
The set of consistent posteriors is $[0,1]$ --- the maximal imprecise
posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
Lean: \texttt{bayesian\_underdetermined\_at\_zero},
\texttt{jeffrey\_underdetermined\_at\_zero}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consequence Relations}
\label{sec:consequence}

K$_3$ (designating $\{1\}$) is paracomplete: no tautologies
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology}).
LP (designating $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) is paraconsistent: excluded
middle holds but explosion fails
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}).

\begin{proposition}[Graded No-Explosion; new]
\label{prop:graded-no-explosion}
For any threshold $0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, graded consequence on
$[0,1]$ is explosion-free: the constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ valuation
gives $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$ and
$\negc v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$, so both $A$ and $\negc A$ are
designated, while $v(B) = 0 < t$ shows the conclusion
need not be designated.
Lean: \texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Graded consequence for $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$]
\label{rem:graded-large-t}
For $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$, the constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ witness fails:
$\tfrac{1}{2} < t$, so neither $A$ nor $\negc A$ is designated.
The explosion question for $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$ is thus not resolved by
this witness; it depends on which valuations are admitted by the logic.
At $t = 1$, graded consequence coincides with K$_3$ consequence
(Lean: \texttt{graded\_at\_one\_is\_strict}), which is paracomplete
rather than paraconsistent: explosion is vacuously avoided because
no valuation makes both $v(A) = 1$ and $\negc v(A) = 1$.
\end{remark}

The witness is the same in all explosion-free cases: the negation
fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$ makes both a proposition and its negation
designated without forcing arbitrary conclusions.
For $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, excluded middle holds:
$v(A) \disjc \negc v(A) \geq \tfrac{3}{4} \geq t$
(Lean: \texttt{certainty\_ge\_three\_quarters}).

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Designated} & \textbf{Tautologies?}
  & \textbf{Excl.\ middle?} & \textbf{Explosion?} \\
\midrule
Classical & $\{1\}$ (two-valued) & Yes & Yes & Yes \\
K$_3$ & $\{1\}$ & No & No & Vacuously$^\S$ \\
LP & $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$ & Yes$^\|$ & Yes$^\|$ & No \\
Graded ($t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$) & $\{c \geq t\}$
  & Yes$^\ddagger$ & Yes$^\ddagger$ & No \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}
\smallskip
\noindent
{\footnotesize
$^\|$Under homomorphic valuations.
$^\S$No valuation makes both $v(A)$ and $\negc(v(A))$
K$_3$-designated.
$^\ddagger$For $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$:
$v(A) \disjc \negc v(A) \geq \tfrac{3}{4} \geq t$, so
excluded middle and the tautology $A \disjc \negc A$ hold.}

\subsection{Graded Predicates}
\label{sec:predicates}

Graded predicates assign credences pointwise over a domain, extending
the framework from propositional to predicate logic.

\begin{definition}[Graded Predicate]
\label{def:graded-predicate}
A \emph{graded predicate} over a type $\alpha$ is a function
$P : \alpha \to [0,1]$.
Pointwise operations are defined by
$(\negc P)(x) = \negc P(x)$,
$(P \conjc Q)(x) = P(x) \conjc Q(x)$,
$(P \disjc Q)(x) = P(x) \disjc Q(x)$.
These inherit all algebraic properties of the product De Morgan
triplet (double negation, De Morgan laws, commutativity, etc.)
pointwise.
Lean: \texttt{GradedPredicate}, \texttt{GradedPredicate.neg},
\texttt{GradedPredicate.conj}, \texttt{GradedPredicate.disj}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Quantifiers]
\label{def:quantifiers}
For a graded predicate $P$ over a nonempty type $\alpha$:
\begin{align*}
\cred(\forall x.\, P(x)) &= \inf_{x \in \alpha} P(x), \\
\cred(\exists x.\, P(x)) &= \sup_{x \in \alpha} P(x).
\end{align*}
Since $P(x) \in [0,1]$ for all $x$, the infimum and supremum exist and
lie in $[0,1]$.
Lean: \texttt{credInf}, \texttt{credSup}.
\end{definition}

These quantifiers agree with Goguen's~\cite{goguen1969} and
Zadeh's~\cite{zadeh1965} definitions for fuzzy predicates.

\begin{proposition}[Quantifier Duality;
known~\cite{goguen1969,zadeh1965}, verified here]
\label{prop:quantifier-duality}
$\negc(\forall x.\, P(x)) = \exists x.\, \negc P(x)$
at the value level:
$1 - \inf_x P(x) = \sup_x (1 - P(x))$.
Lean: \texttt{quantifier\_duality\_val}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Standard real analysis: $\sup$ and $\inf$ are dual under affine
reflection $t \mapsto 1 - t$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Crisp Predicate]
\label{def:crisp}
A predicate $P$ is \emph{crisp} if $P(x) \in \{0, 1\}$ for all $x$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Crisp Agreement;
known~\cite{goguen1969}, verified here]
\label{prop:crisp-agreement}
For a crisp predicate $P$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item $\inf_x P(x) = 0$ iff $\exists x.\, P(x) = 0$,
\item $\sup_x P(x) = 1$ iff $\exists x.\, P(x) = 1$.
\end{enumerate}
In other words, graded quantifiers restricted to crisp predicates
recover classical $\forall$ and $\exists$.
Lean: \texttt{crisp\_inf\_zero\_iff}, \texttt{crisp\_sup\_one\_iff}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Russell Fixed Point; new]
\label{prop:russell}
If $c = \negc c$ (the abstract form of the Russell equation, where
a predicate's self-application yields its own negation), then
$c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
In the graded setting, the equation $c = \negc c$ has a unique
solution rather than producing a contradiction.
This does not resolve the full Russell paradox (which involves set
comprehension and quantification), but it shows that the
self-referential core of the paradox is absorbed by the algebra.
Lean: \texttt{russell\_fixed\_point}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Immediate from the uniqueness of the negation fixed point
(Lemma~\ref{thm:liar}).
\end{proof}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The chain rule
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is the shared content of probability theory and many-valued logic
on $[0,1]$.
At positive evidence, the two traditions agree: the conditional is
determined by division.
At evidence zero, they diverge --- probability is undefined,
residuation forces ex falso, and the chain rule leaves the
conditional unconstrained.
This single divergence at zero determines the entire qualitative
structure:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item The \textbf{bridge theorem} shows LP consequence is equivalent
  to positivity consequence on $[0,1]$, and K$_3$ consequence is
  equivalent to certainty consequence.
  Discrete logical reasoning is faithfully encoded in continuous
  credences.
\item The \textbf{equilibria} $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are exactly the
  conjunction-idempotent values together with the negation fixed
  point; they are derived from the algebra, not postulated.
\item The \textbf{Kleene collapse} to $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is the
  unique non-trivial three-element quotient.
\item The \textbf{congruence classification} shows the Kleene quotient
  exists under $[0,1]$-multiplication; no non-trivial finite quotient
  exists under $\Real$-multiplication.
\item The \textbf{graded no-explosion} shows paraconsistency for
  $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, with the same witness ($\tfrac{1}{2}$) in
  every case; for $t > \tfrac{1}{2}$ the witness fails.
\item \textbf{Graded predicates} with inf/sup quantifiers recover
  classical agreement on crisp predicates and resolve the abstract
  Russell equation $c = \negc c$ to $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{enumerate}

The same algebraic fact --- $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$ ---
manifests in two ways: as underdetermined posteriors on the
probabilistic side (connecting to imprecise
probability~\cite{walley1991}), and as paraconsistency on the
logical side (the negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$ witnesses
non-explosion).
The chain rule thus serves as a shared inference mechanism:
it is Bayes' rule when evidence is positive, and it prevents
explosion when evidence is zero.
The collapse projects Bayesian update to paraconsistent logical
consequence; the bridge theorem shows this projection is an
equivalence.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Four or more classes under $[0,1]$-multiplication.}
  The scaling trick fails without multiplicative inverses; the gap
  between unit and real congruences isolates $[0,1]$-cancellation
  as the precise obstacle.
\item \textbf{Proof theory.}
  Is there a sound and complete calculus for chain-rule conditioning
  on $[0,1]$?
  The analogous question for product logic (with residuation) is
  answered by H\'ajek's completeness theorem~\cite{hajek1998};
  replacing residuation with the chain rule changes the answer.
\item \textbf{Convergence under iterated update.}
  Under what stochastic model for the evidence sequence does
  iterated Bayesian update drive $\cred_n(A) \to 1$, $0$, or
  $\tfrac{1}{2}$?
\item \textbf{Congruence lattice of $(T, \negc)$ vs.\ $(T, \to_T)$.}
  For a fixed continuous t-norm $T$, how does the congruence lattice
  of the De Morgan reduct relate to the congruence lattice of the BL
  expansion?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{app:prelim}
%=============================================================================

This appendix collects standard definitions used in the paper.

\textbf{T-norms.}
A \emph{t-norm} is a binary operation
$T : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ that is commutative, associative,
monotone (in each argument), and satisfies $T(a, 1) = a$ for all
$a$~\cite{klement2000}.
The three basic continuous t-norms are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \emph{Product}: $T_P(a,b) = a \cdot b$.
\item \emph{G\"odel} (minimum): $T_G(a,b) = \min(a,b)$.
\item \emph{\L ukasiewicz}: $T_L(a,b) = \max(a + b - 1, 0)$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Copulas.}
A (bivariate) \emph{copula} is a function
$C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying~\cite{nelsen2006}:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item $C(a, 0) = C(0, b) = 0$ for all $a, b$ (grounded),
\item $C(a, 1) = a$ and $C(1, b) = b$ for all $a, b$
  (unit marginals),
\item $C(a_2, b_2) - C(a_2, b_1) - C(a_1, b_2) + C(a_1, b_1) \geq 0$
  for all $a_1 \leq a_2$, $b_1 \leq b_2$ (2-increasing).
\end{enumerate}
Every copula satisfies the Fr\'echet-Hoeffding bounds:
$\max(a + b - 1, 0) \leq C(a, b) \leq \min(a, b)$.
The upper bound $\min(a, b)$ is the \emph{comonotonicity copula};
the product $a \cdot b$ is the \emph{independence copula}.

\textbf{Coherence.}
De Finetti~\cite{definetti1937} introduced coherence as a criterion
for probability assignments: an assignment is coherent if no finite
combination of bets on it yields a sure loss.
Coletti and Scozzafava~\cite{coletti2002} extended coherence to
conditional probabilities, treating the conditional as a primitive
rather than derived via division.
The chain-rule constraint
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is a necessary condition for coherence~\cite{coletti2002,gilio2014}.

\textbf{Residuation.}
Given a (left-continuous) t-norm $T$, the \emph{residuum} is
$a \to_T b = \sup\{c : T(a,c) \leq b\}$~\cite{hajek1998,galatos2007}.
For the product t-norm: $a \to_P b = \min(b/a, 1)$ when $a > 0$, and
$0 \to_P b = 1$.
The last equation is ex falso: $0 \to b = 1$ regardless of $b$.
Residuation forces a truth-functional conditional; the chain rule
allows the conditional to depend on the joint.
