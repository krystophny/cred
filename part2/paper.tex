\begin{abstract}
Probability theory and many-valued logic both operate on $[0,1]$ and
share the same algebraic core --- product conjunction, probabilistic-sum
disjunction, standard complement --- yet they diverge on the conditional.
Probability derives it via division (undefined at zero evidence);
product logic derives it via residuation (forcing ex falso at zero
evidence).
We study \emph{chain-rule conditioning}, where the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
without invoking either division or residuation.
At positive evidence, the chain rule reduces to Bayesian conditioning;
at zero evidence, it leaves the conditional unconstrained --- neither
undefined nor explosive.
We show this single divergence at zero determines the entire qualitative
structure: the algebraic equilibria are exactly $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
(conjunction-idempotent values plus the unique negation fixed point),
the Kleene lattice is the unique non-trivial three-element quotient,
and the collapse from $[0,1]$ to $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ projects
Bayesian update to paraconsistent logical consequence.
The chain rule thus serves as a shared inference mechanism:
it is Bayes' rule when evidence is positive, and it prevents
explosion when evidence is zero.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

You believe it will rain with credence $0.8$.
You see the street is wet.
How should your credence in rain change?
Both probability theory and many-valued logic answer this question
using values in $[0,1]$ --- but they answer it differently when the
evidence is impossible.

\emph{Probability theory}~\cite{ramsey1931,definetti1937,jaynes2003}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of belief.
Given $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$, a
probabilist computes the conditional via division:
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) = \cred(\text{rain} \land
\text{wet}) / \cred(\text{wet})$.
When $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0$ --- you have never seen a wet street ---
the ratio $0/0$ is undefined.

\emph{Many-valued logic}~\cite{hajek1998,gottwald2001,klement2000}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of truth.
Given a t-norm conjunction and the residuated
implication~\cite{galatos2007}
$a \to b = \sup\{c : a \otimes c \leq b\}$, the conditional at
evidence zero is forced: $0 \to b = 1$ for all $b$, since
$0 \otimes c = 0 \leq b$ for every $c$.
Impossible evidence proves everything --- ex falso quodlibet is built
in~\cite{hajek1998}.

Both traditions share a core algebraic structure when the t-norm is
the product: the \emph{product De Morgan triplet}
$(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ with $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$,
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$, and $\negc c = 1 - c$.
They diverge only in the status of the conditional.

This paper studies \emph{chain-rule conditioning}:
the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ without
invoking either division or residuation.
When evidence is positive, it reduces to division (recovering
Bayesian conditioning); when evidence is zero, the equation
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ is satisfied by every $c \in [0,1]$, leaving the
conditional unconstrained.
The chain rule is the sole structural constraint beyond the triplet.

\textbf{Main results.}
We mark each result as \emph{known} (established in the literature,
newly Lean-verified) or \emph{new} (first appearing here, to our
knowledge).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Evidence-zero analysis} [new]:
  at zero evidence, chain-rule conditioning is unconstrained and
  path-dependent; no limiting convention resolves the singularity
  (Proposition~\ref{thm:path-dependence}).
  Every value in $[0,1]$ is a valid limit depending on the approach
  path.
\item \textbf{Equilibria characterization} [new]:
  the values $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are exactly the
  conjunction-idempotent values together with the unique negation
  fixed point (Theorem~\ref{thm:equilibria}).
  Self-conjunction is strictly decreasing on the interior.
\item \textbf{Kleene collapse uniqueness} [new]:
  the Kleene lattice is the unique non-trivial three-element quotient
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}); no collapse to Boolean,
  G\"odel, or \L ukasiewicz logic exists
  (Propositions~\ref{prop:no-classical}--\ref{prop:no-godel-luk}).
\item \textbf{Two-level congruence classification} [new]:
  under $[0,1]$-multiplication, boundary classes are singletons and
  the Kleene quotient exists;
  under $\Real$-multiplication, no non-trivial finite quotient exists
  at all (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}).
\item \textbf{Collapse composition} [new]:
  composing an independent valuation with the collapse yields a
  Kleene valuation (Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-composition}).
\item \textbf{Graded no-explosion} [new]:
  explosion fails for any threshold $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:graded-no-explosion}).
  The witness is always the negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Literature positioning.}
H\'ajek~\cite{hajek1998} and
Esteva--Godo~\cite{esteva2000,esteva2001} developed Product Logic
with the same t-norm but residuated implication (forcing ex falso).
Mundici~\cite{mundici2011} and
Flaminio--Montagna~\cite{flaminio2009} studied probability on
MV-algebras (\L ukasiewicz, not product).
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955}, Popper~\cite{popper1959}, and
de Finetti~\cite{definetti1936} proposed primitive conditioning in
measure-theoretic or Boolean settings;
\'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021} developed
de Finetti's conditional algebraically.
Ferm\"uller~\cite{fermuller2014} reached the Kleene lattice from
game semantics; Gaines~\cite{gaines1978} unified fuzzy and
probabilistic uncertainty via population semantics.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed plausible
reasoning satisfying certain desiderata must be isomorphic to
probability; chain-rule conditioning drops the requirement that the
conditional be a function of the marginals alone.
Lewis's triviality result~\cite{lewis1976} shows conditional
probability cannot equal the probability of a conditional event;
chain-rule conditioning sidesteps this because $\cred(A \mid B)$ is
constrained by the chain rule with the joint as a separate parameter,
so Lewis's conditions do not apply~\cite{halpern2003}.
To our knowledge, no prior work combines chain-rule conditioning
(with evidence-zero analysis), the Kleene collapse (with quotient
uniqueness and congruence classification), and the explicit
unification of Bayesian update with paraconsistent logical
consequence into a single framework on $[0,1]$.

\section{The Product De Morgan Triplet}
\label{sec:algebra}
%=============================================================================

This section defines the concrete structure we study: the product
De Morgan triplet~\cite{klement2000,esteva2000} on $[0,1]$.
This is not an axiomatic class of algebras: all properties follow from
the specific arithmetic of $[0,1]$.
Goguen~\cite{goguen1969} used the product t-norm as the conjunction in
his logic of inexact concepts.

\begin{definition}[Credence]
\label{def:credence}
A \emph{credence} is a real number $c \in [0, 1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Running Example: Rain and Wet Streets]
\label{ex:rain}
Let $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$.
Then $\cred(\text{no rain}) = 1 - 0.8 = 0.2$, and the product
conjunction gives $\cred(\text{rain}) \conjc \cred(\text{wet}) =
0.8 \times 0.9 = 0.72$.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Negation]
\label{def:neg}
$\negc c = 1 - c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Product Conjunction]
\label{def:conj}
$c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

The product is the product t-norm of fuzzy logic~\cite{klement2000}.
Note that $\conjc$ is not idempotent: $0.8^2 = 0.64 \neq 0.8$.
Repeating the same uncertain evidence \emph{decreases} the joint
credence.
Only $0$ and $1$ satisfy $c^2 = c$.

\begin{definition}[Disjunction]
\label{def:disj}
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = \negc(\negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2)
  = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

In our running example,
$\cred(\text{rain} \lor \text{wet}) = 0.8 + 0.9 - 0.72 = 0.98$.

The triple $(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ is the \emph{product De Morgan
triplet}~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998}.

\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Properties]
\label{thm:algebraic}
For all $c, c_1, c_2, c_3 \in [0,1]$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\negc\negc c = c$ \hfill (involution)
\item $\negc 0 = 1$, $\negc 1 = 0$ \hfill (boundary)
\item $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_2 \conjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \conjc c_2) \conjc c_3 = c_1 \conjc (c_2 \conjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \conjc 1 = c$, $c \conjc 0 = 0$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_2 \disjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \disjc c_2) \disjc c_3 = c_1 \disjc (c_2 \disjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \disjc 0 = c$, $c \disjc 1 = 1$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $\negc(c_1 \conjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \disjc \negc c_2$,
  $\negc(c_1 \disjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2$ \hfill (De Morgan)
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{neg\_neg}, \texttt{conj\_comm}, \texttt{conj\_assoc},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_conj}, \texttt{de\_morgan\_disj}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Arithmetic on $[0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The structure is two dual commutative monoids connected by De Morgan
duality; it is not a De Morgan algebra since $\conjc$ is not idempotent
($c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0,1\}$;
Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff})
and $\conjc$ does not distribute over $\disjc$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_disj\_not\_distrib}).

\begin{remark}[Product vs.\ Joint]
\label{rem:product-vs-joint}
The product $c_1 \conjc c_2$ is an algebraic operation on credence
values, not a claim about the joint credence
$\cred(A \land B)$ of two propositions.
The joint depends on the dependence structure and lies in the
Fr\'echet-Hoeffding interval
$[\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0),\; \min(\cred(A), \cred(B))]$:
independence gives $\cred(A) \cdot \cred(B)$;
maximum positive dependence (comonotonicity) gives
$\min(\cred(A), \cred(B))$~\cite{nelsen2006}.
In our running example: $\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet})$ could be
anywhere in $[0.7,\, 0.8]$.
Under independence, it is $0.72$; under comonotonicity,
$\min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$.
\end{remark}

\textbf{Comparison with Product Logic.}
The product De Morgan triplet and H\'ajek's Product Logic
($\Pi$-logic)~\cite{hajek1998} share the same t-norm.
They differ in the companion operation: $\Pi$-logic uses
residuation $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$, which
forces $0 \to b = 1$ (ex falso);
the triplet uses the involutive complement $\negc c = 1 - c$
and chain-rule conditioning, which leaves $\cred(A \mid 0)$
unconstrained.
On positive evidence, the two coincide.

\textbf{Relationship to probability.}
Every probability space satisfies the chain rule and complement axioms.
Probability adds a sigma-algebra, a countably additive measure, and
sigma-additivity.
The product De Morgan triplet drops all of these: there is no sample
space, no sigma-algebra, no additivity beyond complement.
The triplet retains exactly the structure shared by both probability
and $[0,1]$-valued logic --- the greatest common factor of the two
traditions.

\textbf{Conjunction selection.}
For logic-compatible valuations, idempotence
($\cred(A \land A) = \cred(A)$) is essential: repeating evidence
should not change it.
Among truth-functional joints (copulas on $[0,1]$), the minimum is
the unique symmetric idempotent copula
(Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique})~\cite{nelsen2006}.
The product fails idempotence ($c^2 \neq c$ for $c \in (0,1)$);
\L ukasiewicz fails similarly
($\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$).
The minimum joint is also the \emph{comonotonicity copula}: the
extremal case where all random variables are increasing functions of
a single latent variable~\cite{dhaene2002,nelsen2006}.
In our running example, the comonotonicity joint gives
$\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet}) = \min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$,
and then
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) = 0.8 / 0.9 = 8/9 \approx 0.89$.
Independence ($0.8 \times 0.9 = 0.72$) fails both idempotence and
non-trivial conditioning
(Lean: \texttt{prod\_trivial\_conditioning});
the RM3 joint fails Bayes-consistency: at $a = 0.3$, $c = 0.6$,
$\max(0.7, 0.6) \cdot 0.3 = 0.21 \neq
\max(0.4, 0.3) \cdot 0.6 = 0.24$
(Lean: \texttt{rm3\_not\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of Truth-Functional Conjunction;
known~\cite{nelsen2006}]
\label{thm:min-copula-unique}
Let $j : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ be a symmetric idempotent copula.
Then $j(a,b) = \min(a,b)$ for all $a,b \in [0,1]$.
Lean: \texttt{copula\_idempotent\_unique},
\texttt{min\_copula\_unique}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Upper bound: 2-increasing on $[0,a] \times [b,1]$ with unit marginal
gives $j(a,b) \leq a$; by symmetry $j(a,b) \leq b$.
Lower bound: assume $a \leq b$.
Apply 2-increasing to $[0,a] \times [a,b]$:
$j(a,b) - j(a,a) - j(0,b) + j(0,a) \geq 0$.
By idempotence and zero boundary: $j(a,b) \geq a = \min(a,b)$.
\end{proof}

\section{Chain-Rule Conditioning and Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:conditioning}
%=============================================================================

Suppose you want to condition on wet streets:
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$ should satisfy
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) \cdot \cred(\text{wet}) =
\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet})$.
When $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9 > 0$, this gives
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet}) = \min(0.8, 0.9) / 0.9 = 8/9$.
But what if $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0$?
You have never seen a wet street.
What is $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$?

\begin{definition}[Conditioning Structure]
\label{def:conditioning}
A \emph{conditioning structure} for
$(\text{joint}, \text{evidence}) \in [0,1]^2$
consists of $\text{condCred} \in [0,1]$ satisfying
$\text{condCred} \conjc \text{evidence} = \text{joint}$.
In propositional notation:
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.
\end{definition}

The joint is supplied as input, not computed from marginals.
H\'ajek, Godo, and Esteva~\cite{hajek1995} used the chain rule
constraint as a technical device within fuzzy logic;
Fritz~\cite{fritz2020} developed a categorical approach to
conditioning as a primitive morphism;
Jeffrey~\cite{jeffrey1965} generalized Bayesian conditioning to
uncertain evidence via a mixture formula, also using the chain rule as
the primitive mechanism.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} showed plausible reasoning satisfying certain
desiderata must be isomorphic to probability; chain-rule conditioning
drops the requirement that the conditional be a function
$F(\cred(A), \cred(B))$ of the marginals alone.

\subsection{Three Answers to Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:three-answers}

The chain rule gives $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$: the conditional is
\emph{unconstrained}
(Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
Residuation gives $0 \to b = 1$ for all $b$: ex falso is
\emph{forced}.
Probability gives $0/0$: \emph{undefined}.

The three frameworks agree on every row except evidence $= 0$.
This single row is where probability, logic, and the chain rule
part ways.

\subsection{Existence and Uniqueness}
\label{sec:existence}

\begin{proposition}[Existence and Uniqueness of Conditioning]
\label{thm:existence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item \textbf{Positive evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} > 0$, a conditioning structure exists iff
  $\mathrm{joint} \leq \mathrm{evidence}$, and is then unique:
  $\mathrm{condCred} = \mathrm{joint} / \mathrm{evidence}$.
\item \textbf{Zero evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} = 0$, the constraint
  $\mathrm{condCred} \cdot 0 = \mathrm{joint}$ forces
  $\mathrm{joint} = 0$ but leaves $\mathrm{condCred}$ entirely
  unconstrained: every $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_mk}, \texttt{conditioning\_unique},
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~If $0 < e$ and $j \leq e$, then $c = j/e \in [0,1]$ and
$c \cdot e = j$.
Uniqueness: if $c \cdot e = c' \cdot e$ with $e > 0$, then $c = c'$.
(ii)~$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c \in [0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The dichotomy is sharp: at positive evidence, the conditional is a
\emph{function} (division); at zero evidence, it is a \emph{relation}
(any value is consistent).

\subsection{Path Dependence at Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:path-dependence}

Why not just take a limit?
Under the min-copula joint $\min(a, b)$, the conditional for $b > 0$
is $\min(a,b)/b$.
As $b \to 0$, the limit depends on \emph{how} $a$ and $b$ approach
zero together.

\begin{example}[Rain as Wet Streets Vanish]
\label{ex:path}
What is $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})$ as
$\cred(\text{wet}) \to 0$?
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ fixed, $\cred(\text{wet}) \to 0$:
  limit $= 1$.
  Strong belief overwhelms vanishing evidence.
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.5 \cdot \cred(\text{wet})$:
  limit $= 0.5$.
  Proportional decline preserves the ratio.
\item $\cred(\text{rain}) = \cred(\text{wet})^2$:
  limit $= 0$.
  The proposition vanishes faster than the evidence.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved.
How evidence becomes impossible matters.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Path Dependence at Evidence Zero; new]
\label{thm:path-dependence}
The limit of $\min(a,b)/b$ as $b \to 0$ is path-dependent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item Fixed $a > 0$: limit is $1$.
\item $a(b) = \lambda b$, $\lambda \in (0,1]$: limit is $\lambda$.
\item $a(b) = b^\alpha$, $\alpha > 1$: limit is $0$.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved; ex falso requires an added
convention.
Lean: \texttt{path\_dep\_fixed\_a}, \texttt{path\_dep\_proportional},
\texttt{path\_dep\_square}, \texttt{path\_any\_positive\_value}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~For small $b < a$, $\min(a,b)/b = 1$.
(ii)~$\min(\lambda b, b)/b = \lambda$.
(iii)~$b^{\alpha-1} \to 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/path_dependence}
\caption{Path dependence at evidence zero.
\textbf{Left:} $\min(a,b)/b$ near the origin; value depends on angle
$\theta$, not radius $r$.
\textbf{Right:} Limit as $r \to 0$: $1$ for $\theta < \pi/4$,
$\cot\theta$ for $\theta > \pi/4$.
The singularity is essential, not removable.}
\label{fig:path-dependence}
\end{figure}

The singularity is essential: every value in $[0,1]$ is a valid limit
depending on the approach path, so no single convention (ex falso,
void, default~$1$) can be justified on continuity grounds.
The chain rule's response --- leave the conditional unconstrained ---
is the minimal commitment consistent with the data.

\subsection{Comparison with Primitive-Conditioning Frameworks}
\label{sec:frameworks}

\begin{center}
\small

The set of all posteriors consistent with the chain rule at zero
evidence is $[0,1]$ --- the maximal imprecise
posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
An agent may narrow this set by adopting a prior over the zero-evidence
regime (de Finetti's void, Popper's convention, etc.), but the
chain rule itself imposes no such commitment.

\section{Algebraic Equilibria}
\label{sec:equilibria}
%=============================================================================

What happens when you conjoin a credence with itself?
$\cred(\text{rain}) \conjc \cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8^2 = 0.64 < 0.8$.
Combining two independent copies of the same uncertain evidence
\emph{decreases} the joint credence.
Only $0$ and $1$ are stable: $0^2 = 0$, $1^2 = 1$.

\begin{proposition}[Self-Conjunction Decay; new]
\label{prop:self-conj-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$: $c \conjc c = c^2 < c$.
Dually, $c \disjc c > c$.
Lean: \texttt{self\_conj\_lt}, \texttt{self\_disj\_gt}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 < c \iff c(c - 1) < 0$, which holds since $0 < c < 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Iterated Self-Conjunction; new]
\label{prop:pow-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$ and $n \in \Nat$:
$c^{n+1} < c^n$.
In particular, $c^n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$:
unrenewed evidence decays.
Lean: \texttt{pow\_strictly\_decreasing}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^n \cdot c < c^n \cdot 1 = c^n$ since $0 < c < 1$.
Convergence: $c^n = e^{n \ln c} \to 0$ since $\ln c < 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Unique Negation Fixed Point]
\label{thm:liar}
$\negc c = c$ if and only if $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Lean: \texttt{liar\_fixed\_point},
\texttt{neg\_fixed\_point\_unique}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Equilibria Characterization; new]
\label{thm:equilibria}
A credence $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies $c \conjc c = c$ or $\negc c = c$
if and only if $c \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.
Equivalently, the set of conjunction-idempotent values $\{0, 1\}$
together with the unique negation fixed point $\{\tfrac{1}{2}\}$
exhausts the algebraic equilibria.
Lean: \texttt{equilibria\_characterization}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff}).
$1 - c = c$ iff $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lemma~\ref{thm:liar}).
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation.}
The three equilibria have distinct epistemic characters:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $0$ = \emph{refuted}: conjunction-idempotent at the lower
  boundary.
  Self-conjunction preserves it.
\item $1$ = \emph{confirmed}: conjunction-idempotent at the upper
  boundary.
  Self-conjunction preserves it.
\item $\tfrac{1}{2}$ = \emph{irreducibly uncertain}: the unique
  negation fixed point.
  Neither confirmable nor refutable by complement alone.
  It is $\negc\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}$: the credence of $A$
  equals the credence of $\negc A$.
\end{itemize}
Every other credence $c \in (0,1) \setminus \{\tfrac{1}{2}\}$
is not an equilibrium: $c^2 < c$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}), so iterated
self-conjunction drives it toward $0$; it is not a negation fixed
point either.
Finite-valued logics take the three values as primitive;
the continuous framework \emph{derives} them as the algebraic
equilibria of the product De Morgan triplet.

\begin{remark}[Algebraic equilibria vs.\ dynamical convergence]
\label{rem:attractor-caveat}
Propositions~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}--\ref{prop:pow-decay}
establish that iterated self-conjunction $c, c^2, c^4, \ldots$
converges to $0$ for every $c \in (0,1)$.
This is a precise algebraic statement about the product t-norm.
A formal convergence theorem for Bayesian update toward $0$, $1$,
or $\tfrac{1}{2}$ would require specifying a stochastic model for
the evidence sequence, which is beyond our scope.
\end{remark}

\section{The Kleene Collapse}
\label{sec:collapse}
%=============================================================================

The three equilibria from Section~\ref{sec:equilibria} are not just
fixed points --- they form a quotient algebra.
The partition $\{0\}, (0,1), \{1\}$ defines a congruence on the
product De Morgan triplet, and the quotient is the Kleene lattice.

Define the collapse:
\[
\text{collapse}(c) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } c = 0, \\
\tfrac{1}{2} & \text{if } 0 < c < 1, \\
1 & \text{if } c = 1.
\end{cases}
\]

\begin{theorem}[Collapse Homomorphism; folklore, verified here]
\label{thm:collapse}
The collapse is a surjective homomorphism from
$([0,1], \negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ to
$(\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}, \mathrm{neg}, \min, \max)$.
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_disj}, \texttt{collapse\_surjective}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Case analysis.
Negation: $0 \mapsto 1$, $1 \mapsto 0$; if $0 < c < 1$ then
$0 < 1-c < 1$.
Conjunction: $0$ absorbs, $1$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Disjunction: $1$ absorbs, $0$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2 < 1$.
\end{proof}

The target is the Kleene lattice~\cite{kleene1952}, underlying
K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952}, LP~\cite{priest1979}, and
RM3~\cite{anderson1975,meyer1972}.
The Kleene quotient's $\min$/$\max$ operations are exactly the
conjunction/disjunction of possibility
theory~\cite{dubois1988}.

\subsection{Uniqueness and Impossibility}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to Classical Logic]
\label{prop:no-classical}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation.
Lean: \texttt{no\_boolean\_neg\_retraction}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ has no solution
in $\{0, 1\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to G\"odel or \L ukasiewicz Logic]
\label{prop:no-godel-luk}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ preserves G\"odel
negation (which has no fixed point), and no such $\varphi$
preserves both negation and \L ukasiewicz conjunction
(since $\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq
\tfrac{1}{2}$).
Lean: \texttt{no\_godel\_collapse}, \texttt{no\_luk\_collapse}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the Three-Element Quotient; new]
\label{thm:quotient-unique}
Any surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to S$ ($|S|=3$) preserving negation
and conjunction yields the Kleene lattice (up to relabeling).
Lean: \texttt{three\_element\_quotient\_unique}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be the unique negation fixed point of $S$.
The remaining elements $\varphi(0)$, $\varphi(1)$ form the 2-cycle.
The conjunction table is forced: $\varphi(0)$ absorbs, $\varphi(1)$
is identity,
and $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc \tfrac{1}{2}) =
\varphi(\tfrac{1}{4}) = \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$
(since $\tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$).
This forces Kleene $\min$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Congruence Classification}
\label{sec:congruence}

The collapse results combine into a congruence classification at two
levels, depending on the scope of the multiplication compatibility
hypothesis.
A \emph{unit congruence} requires multiplication compatibility only for
$[0,1]$-valued inputs.
A \emph{real congruence} extends compatibility to all of $\Real$.

\begin{theorem}[Congruence Classification; new]
\label{thm:congruence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item \textbf{Singleton boundaries:}
  Under any non-trivial unit congruence, $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ are
  singleton classes.
\item \textbf{No two-element quotient:}
  No surjective homomorphism $[0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}).
\item \textbf{Uniqueness at three elements:}
  The Kleene lattice is the unique three-element quotient
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}), and the Kleene partition is
  itself a verified unit congruence (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}).
\item \textbf{$\Real$-rigidity:}
  Under $\Real$-multiplication, no non-trivial finite quotient exists
  with $n \geq 2$ classes.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{UnitCongruence.singleton\_zero},
\texttt{singleton\_one}, \texttt{kleene\_congruence},
\texttt{RealCongruence.no\_nontrivial\_finite\_quotient}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
(1)~Suppose $0 \sim \varepsilon$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$.
For any $x \in [0,1]$, the sequence
$x, x(1-\varepsilon), x(1-\varepsilon)^2, \ldots$ converges to $0$.
By multiplication compatibility at each step and transitivity,
$x \sim 0$ for every $x$: trivial.
The argument for $\{1\}$ is dual (apply negation).
(2)~Immediate from the negation fixed point at $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
(3)~Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}.
(4)~Given $n \geq 2$ classes, pigeonhole on the powers
$a, a^2, \ldots, a^{n+1}$ gives $a^i \sim a^j$ with $i < j$.
Under $\Real$-multiplication, multiply by $(a^i)^{-1}$
to get $1 \sim a^{j-i}$, contradicting singleton boundaries from (1).
\end{proof}

The Kleene lattice exists as a quotient under $[0,1]$-multiplication;
whether four or more classes are possible is \textbf{open}.
The gap between unit and real congruences isolates
$[0,1]$-cancellation as the key obstacle:
in $\Real$, $ax = bx$ with $x > 0$ implies $a = b$;
in $[0,1]$ the quotient $a/x$ may leave the interval.

\section{Valuations, Update, and Consequence}
\label{sec:applications}
%=============================================================================

The chain rule supports two modes of inference on the same $[0,1]$
structure:
\emph{Bayesian update} (revise a single valuation in light of
evidence) and \emph{logical consequence} (ask which conclusions follow
from premises across all valuations).
Both are governed by the chain rule; they differ in quantifier
structure.
Bayesian update applies the chain rule \emph{pointwise} to a
particular valuation; logical consequence quantifies \emph{universally}
over all valuations.
This section develops both modes and shows how the collapse
(Section~\ref{sec:collapse}) connects them:
Bayesian reasoning on $[0,1]$ \emph{projects to} paraconsistent
logical reasoning on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.

\subsection{Valuations and Collapse Composition}

\begin{definition}[Independent Valuation]
\label{def:indep-valuation}
A \emph{complement-preserving} valuation satisfies
$v(\negc A) = \negc v(A)$.
It is \emph{independent} if additionally
$v(A \land B) = v(A) \conjc v(B)$ and
$v(A \lor B) = v(A) \disjc v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{CpValuation}, \texttt{IndepValuation}.
\end{definition}

Under independence, conditioning on $B$ with $v(B) > 0$ gives
$\cred(A \mid B) = v(A)$: evidence has no effect
(Lean: \texttt{prod\_trivial\_conditioning}).

\begin{definition}[Kleene Valuation]
\label{def:kleene-valuation}
A complement-preserving valuation is a \emph{Kleene valuation} if
$v(A \land B) = \min(v(A), v(B))$ and
$v(A \lor B) = \max(v(A), v(B))$.
\end{definition}

By the uniqueness theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique}), the
minimum is the unique symmetric, idempotent copula.
A Kleene valuation is the unique choice that is both
idempotent and Bayes-consistent --- the logic-compatible valuation.

\begin{example}[Running Example: Collapse]
\label{ex:collapse-val}
With $v(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $v(\text{wet}) = 0.9$, both collapse
to $\tfrac{1}{2}$ (the interior class).
Under independence:
$v(\text{rain} \land \text{wet}) = 0.72$, which also collapses to
$\tfrac{1}{2}$.
On the quotient: $\min(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
The collapse preserves the algebraic structure.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Collapse Composition; new]
\label{prop:collapse-composition}
Composing an independent valuation with the collapse homomorphism
yields a Kleene valuation:
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{collapse}(v(\negc A)) &= \mathrm{neg}(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \land B)) &= \min(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \lor B)) &= \max(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))).
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_val\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_val\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_val\_disj}.
\end{proposition}

A probabilist working with joint-parametrized valuations can always
project to a three-valued logic by composing with the collapse.
A logician working with K$_3$ or LP valuations can always lift to
$[0,1]$ by choosing any preimage of the three-valued assignment.

\subsection{Bayesian and Jeffrey Update}

\begin{definition}[Bayesian Update]
\label{def:bayesian-update}
Given a joint-parametrized valuation $v$ with evidence $B$ satisfying
$v(B) > 0$, the \emph{Bayesian posterior} is
$v'(A) = j(A,B) / v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Chain-Rule Preservation]
\label{prop:bayesian-chain}
$v'(A) \conjc v(B) = j(A,B)$.
The posterior is the unique credence with this property.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule},
\texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{definition}[Jeffrey Update]
\label{def:jeffrey-update}
For uncertain evidence with new credence $q$ for $B$:
$v'(A) = \cred(A \mid B) \cdot q + \cred(A \mid \negc B) \cdot (1 - q)$.
At $q = 1$ this reduces to Bayesian update; at $q = 0$ it conditions
on $\negc B$.
Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate}, \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_certain},
\texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_zero}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Zero-Evidence Underdetermination; new]
\label{prop:zero-underdetermined}
When $v(B) = 0$, any conditional $\cred(A \mid B) \in [0,1]$ satisfies
the chain rule, so neither Bayesian nor Jeffrey update produces a unique
posterior.
The set of consistent posteriors is $[0,1]$ --- the maximal imprecise
posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
Lean: \texttt{bayesian\_underdetermined\_at\_zero},
\texttt{jeffrey\_underdetermined\_at\_zero}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consequence}

K$_3$ (designating $\{1\}$) is paracomplete: no tautologies
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology}).
LP (designating $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) is paraconsistent: excluded
middle holds but explosion fails
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}).

\begin{proposition}[Graded No-Explosion; new]
\label{prop:graded-no-explosion}
For any threshold $0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, graded consequence on
$[0,1]$ is explosion-free: the constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ valuation
gives $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$ and
$\negc v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$, so both $A$ and $\negc A$ are
designated, while an arbitrary $v(B) = 0 < t$ shows the conclusion
need not be designated.
Lean: \texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}.
\end{proposition}

The witness is the same in all three cases: the negation fixed point
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ makes both a proposition and its negation designated
without forcing arbitrary conclusions.

\subsection{Two Modes of Inference}
\label{sec:two-modes}

The chain rule governs two modes of inference on the same $[0,1]$
structure.
We can now make the relationship precise.

\textbf{Mode 1: Bayesian update} (probabilistic inference).
Fix a valuation $v$, observe evidence $B$ with $v(B) > 0$, compute
the posterior $v'(A) = j(A,B)/v(B)$.
The chain rule determines the posterior \emph{uniquely} at positive
evidence.
At evidence zero, it yields a set of posteriors ($[0,1]$).
This is the chain rule used as an \emph{update rule}: revise one
valuation in light of data.

\textbf{Mode 2: Logical consequence} (many-valued inference).
Fix a threshold $t$, quantify over all valuations $v$:
$\Gamma \models_t B$ iff for every $v$, if $v(A) \geq t$ for all
$A \in \Gamma$, then $v(B) \geq t$.
This is the chain rule used as a \emph{constraint on conditionals}:
the evidence-zero behavior (unconstrained) prevents explosion, because
the constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ valuation satisfies both $v(A) \geq t$
and $v(\negc A) \geq t$ without forcing $v(B) \geq t$ for
arbitrary $B$.

\textbf{The collapse as bridge.}
The collapse (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}) connects these two modes.
Given a Bayesian reasoner working on $[0,1]$ with an independent
valuation $v$:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item Composing $v$ with the collapse yields a Kleene valuation
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-composition}).
\item On the quotient $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, Bayesian conditioning
  becomes the Kleene material conditional on all positive-evidence
  rows.
\item The logical consequence relation on the quotient (K$_3$ or LP)
  is the ``qualitative shadow'' of the continuous $[0,1]$ reasoning.
\end{enumerate}
The projection is information-losing: many $[0,1]$ valuations
collapse to the same Kleene valuation.
But it is structure-preserving: the algebraic operations, and
therefore the consequence relations, are faithfully projected.

The reason both modes work is the same mechanism:
the evidence-zero behavior of the chain rule.
At positive evidence, the chain rule reduces to division --- Bayesian
conditioning works and logical conditionals are determined.
At zero evidence, the chain rule is unconstrained rather than
explosive --- Bayesian posteriors are underdetermined (connecting to
imprecise probability) and logical consequence is paraconsistent
(the negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$ witnesses non-explosion).
The same algebraic fact ($c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$) manifests as
underdetermined posteriors on the probabilistic side and as
paraconsistency on the logical side.

The graded threshold $t \in (0, \tfrac{1}{2}]$ provides a continuous
interpolation.
At $t = 1$, consequence is K$_3$-strict (paracomplete, no tautologies).
At $t = \tfrac{1}{2}$, consequence is LP-equivalent (paraconsistent,
tautologies hold, explosion fails).
For intermediate values, consequence is paraconsistent with tautologies.
The threshold parameterizes a family of consequence relations, all
explosion-free, all governed by the chain rule.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The chain rule
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is the shared content of probability theory and many-valued logic
on $[0,1]$.
At positive evidence, the two traditions agree: the conditional is
determined by division.
At evidence zero, they diverge --- probability is undefined,
residuation forces ex falso, and the chain rule leaves the
conditional unconstrained.
This single divergence at zero determines the entire qualitative
structure:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item The \emph{evidence-zero analysis} shows chain-rule conditioning
  is unconstrained and path-dependent; no limiting convention
  resolves the singularity.
\item The \emph{equilibria} $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are exactly the
  conjunction-idempotent values together with the negation fixed
  point; they are derived from the algebra, not postulated.
\item The \emph{Kleene collapse} to $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is the
  unique non-trivial three-element quotient.
\item The \emph{congruence classification} shows the Kleene quotient
  exists under $[0,1]$-multiplication; no non-trivial finite quotient
  exists under $\Real$-multiplication.
\item The \emph{collapse composition} projects probabilistic valuations
  to logical ones.
\item The \emph{graded no-explosion} shows paraconsistency for
  $t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, with the same witness ($\tfrac{1}{2}$) in
  every case.
\end{enumerate}

The chain rule serves as a shared inference mechanism:
it is Bayes' rule when evidence is positive, and it prevents
explosion when evidence is zero.
The collapse projects Bayesian update to paraconsistent logical
consequence, connecting the two traditions through a single
algebraic structure.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Four or more classes under $[0,1]$-multiplication.}
  The scaling trick fails without multiplicative inverses; the gap
  between unit and real congruences isolates $[0,1]$-cancellation
  as the precise obstacle.
\item \textbf{Proof theory.}
  Is there a sound and complete calculus for chain-rule conditioning
  on $[0,1]$?
  The analogous question for product logic (with residuation) is
  answered by H\'ajek's completeness theorem~\cite{hajek1998};
  replacing residuation with the chain rule changes the answer.
\item \textbf{Convergence under iterated update.}
  Under what stochastic model for the evidence sequence does
  iterated Bayesian update drive $\cred_n(A) \to 1$, $0$, or
  $\tfrac{1}{2}$?
\item \textbf{Congruence lattice of $(T, \negc)$ vs.\ $(T, \to_T)$.}
  For a fixed continuous t-norm $T$, how does the congruence lattice
  of the De Morgan reduct relate to the congruence lattice of the BL
  expansion?
\end{enumerate}

(with Mathlib), totaling approximately 2900 lines with zero
\texttt{sorry}:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \texttt{Cred/Basic.lean}: core algebra, conditioning, fixed
  points, collapse, comparisons (1804 lines).
\item \texttt{Cred/Congruence.lean}: two-level congruence
  classification --- \texttt{UnitCongruence}, \texttt{RealCongruence},
  Kleene witness (396 lines).
\item \texttt{Cred/Valuation.lean}: valuations, collapse composition.
\item \texttt{Cred/Update.lean}: Bayesian/Jeffrey update.
\item \texttt{Cred/Consequence.lean}: K$_3$/LP designation,
  no-explosion.
\item \texttt{Cred/Predicate.lean}: graded predicates, quantifier
  duality, Russell fixed point.
\end{itemize}
