\begin{abstract}
Probability theory and many-valued logic both operate on $[0,1]$ and
share the product De Morgan triplet --- product conjunction, probabilistic-sum
disjunction, standard complement --- yet they diverge on the conditional:
probability derives it via division (undefined at zero), while product
logic derives it via residuation (forcing ex falso at zero).
We study \emph{chain-rule conditioning}, where the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
without invoking either division or residuation.
Among truth-functional joints (copulas), the minimum is the unique
symmetric idempotent copula (a known result we apply here to select
the conjunction); the product t-norm then provides the algebraic
operations, while the chain rule governs inference.
We develop Bayesian and Jeffrey update as graded inference on $[0,1]$
and prove that the \emph{equilibria} $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ ---
the conjunction-idempotent values together with the unique negation
fixed point --- are exactly the values invariant under the basic
algebraic operations: a formal characterization with Lean-verified
proof.
The Kleene lattice emerges as the unique non-trivial three-element
algebraic quotient (new) and as the qualitative summary of these
equilibria.
At evidence zero, chain-rule conditioning is unconstrained rather than
explosive: path dependence shows that no limiting convention can
resolve the conditional, connecting to imprecise probability.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

Two mathematical traditions assign values in $[0,1]$ to propositions.
\emph{Probability theory}~\cite{ramsey1931,definetti1937,jaynes2003}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of belief, adds a sigma-algebra and
countable additivity, and derives the conditional via division:
$P(A \mid B) = P(A \land B)/P(B)$ when $P(B) > 0$; when $P(B) = 0$,
the conditional is undefined.
\emph{Many-valued logic}~\cite{hajek1998,gottwald2001,klement2000}
interprets $[0,1]$ as degree of truth, supplies a t-norm conjunction,
an involutive negation, and derives the conditional via
\emph{residuation}: $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \otimes c \leq b\}$.
Since $0 \otimes c = 0 \leq b$ for all $c$ and $b$, every residuated
implication forces $0 \to b = 1$ --- ex falso quodlibet is built
in~\cite{hajek1998}.

Both traditions share a core algebraic structure when the t-norm is
the product: the \emph{product De Morgan triplet}
$(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ with $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$,
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$, and $\negc c = 1 - c$.
They diverge only in the status of the conditional.

This paper shows that \emph{chain-rule conditioning} ---
where the conditional satisfies
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ without
invoking either division or residuation --- bridges the two traditions.
The chain rule is the sole structural constraint beyond the triplet.
When evidence is positive, it reduces to division (recovering
Bayesian conditioning); when evidence is zero, it leaves the
conditional unconstrained (neither undefined nor explosive).
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} and Popper~\cite{popper1959} proposed
primitive conditioning in measure-theoretic settings;
de Finetti~\cite{definetti1936} treated conditioning on a false
antecedent as \emph{void} rather than vacuously true;
\'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021} developed the algebraic
and proof-theoretic foundations of de Finetti's conditional in detail.
Chain-rule conditioning achieves a similar effect by a different
mechanism: rather than adding a connexive axiom, it simply leaves
$\cred(A \mid 0)$ unconstrained.

\textbf{Literature positioning.}
The relationship between probability and many-valued logic on $[0,1]$
has been explored from several directions, none of which yields the
bridge we construct here.

\emph{Fuzzy logic with product t-norm.}
H\'ajek~\cite{hajek1998} and Esteva--Godo~\cite{esteva2000,esteva2001}
developed Product Logic ($\Pi$-logic) with the residuated implication
$a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$.
They share our t-norm but impose ex falso via residuation; the chain
rule avoids this.

\emph{Probability over \L ukasiewicz logic.}
Mundici~\cite{mundici2011} and
Flaminio--Montagna~\cite{flaminio2009} studied states (probability
functions) on MV-algebras.
Their base logic is \L ukasiewicz, not the product, and the
relationship to conditioning is not developed.

\emph{Primitive conditioning in probability.}
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} axiomatized conditional probability as
primitive (restricting to positive-measure conditioning events);
Popper~\cite{popper1959} axiomatized it on a Boolean algebra (forcing
$P(A \mid B) = 1$ when $B$ entails $A$);
Coletti--Scozzafava~\cite{coletti2002} developed coherent conditional
probability without assuming countable additivity.
All of these operate in measure-theoretic or Boolean-algebraic
settings, not on bare $[0,1]$ values with a many-valued structure.

\emph{De Finetti's three-valued conditional.}
\'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021} developed the algebraic
and proof-theoretic foundations of de Finetti's
conditional~\cite{definetti1936}, showing it yields a connexive logic
where $\neg(A \to \neg A)$ holds.
Gilio and Sanfilippo~\cite{gilio2013,gilio2014} developed iterated
conditioning for conditional events using de Finetti's approach.
Our chain-rule conditioning achieves a similar no-explosion effect by
a different mechanism: rather than adding a connexive axiom, it
simply leaves $\cred(A \mid 0)$ unconstrained.

\emph{Game semantics and population semantics.}
Ferm\"uller~\cite{fermuller2014} reached the Kleene lattice from
Hintikka-style game semantics for fuzzy logics; we reach it
algebraically via the collapse homomorphism.
Gaines~\cite{gaines1978} gave an early unification of fuzzy and
probabilistic uncertainty via population semantics, but without copula
theory, update theory, or the algebraic bridge we construct here.

\emph{Copula theory.}
Grzegorzewski~\cite{grzegorzewski2013} connected copula-derived
operations to fuzzy implications, showing that different copulas yield
different fuzzy implication operators.
We apply the known copula-uniqueness result (the minimum is the
unique symmetric idempotent copula~\cite{nelsen2006}) to select the
conjunction; the novelty is the application to many-valued logic,
not the copula theorem itself.

\emph{Algebraic synthesis.}
Calabrese~\cite{calabrese1987} proposed a conditional event algebra
that treats conditional events as primitive objects within a Boolean
framework, reducing to probability when embedded in a measure space.
Our approach differs in two respects: we work within a many-valued
(non-Boolean) framework on bare $[0,1]$ values, and our chain-rule
conditioning operates on credences rather than on events equipped
with an algebra.
The goals are related --- algebraic unification of logic and
probability --- but the starting points and resulting structures differ.

\emph{Cox's theorem.}
Cox~\cite{cox1946,cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed that
plausible reasoning satisfying certain desiderata (associativity of
conjunction, a functional relationship between the plausibility of a
conditional and that of its components, and twice-differentiability)
must be isomorphic to probability.
Chain-rule conditioning drops one specific desideratum: that
$\cred(A \mid B)$ be a function $F(\cred(A), \cred(B))$ of the
marginals alone.
Instead, the joint $\cred(A \land B)$ is an independent parameter,
and the conditional at zero evidence is unconstrained rather than
determined by Cox's functional equation.
The other Cox desiderata (complement law, associativity) are retained.

To our knowledge, no prior work combines copula uniqueness (applied
to select a conjunction), chain-rule conditioning (with evidence-zero
analysis), and the Kleene collapse (with quotient uniqueness and
congruence classification) into a single framework on $[0,1]$.
The individual ingredients are not all new (we mark each result
accordingly); the combination and its consequences are.

\textbf{Main results.}
We mark each result as \emph{known} (established in the literature)
or \emph{new} (first appearing here, to our knowledge).
(1)~\emph{Known (applied here):}
Among truth-functional joints (copulas), the minimum is the unique
symmetric idempotent copula
(Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique})~\cite{nelsen2006}.
The application to many-valued logic --- selecting the conjunction
for a $[0,1]$-valued framework --- is new.
(2)~\emph{New:}
At evidence zero, chain-rule conditioning is unconstrained and
path-dependent (Proposition~\ref{thm:path-dependence}); no limiting
convention resolves the singularity.
(3)~\emph{New:}
The Kleene lattice $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is the unique
non-trivial three-element algebraic quotient
(Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}); no collapse to Boolean,
G\"odel, or \L ukasiewicz logic exists
(Propositions~\ref{prop:no-classical}--\ref{prop:no-godel-luk}).
(4)~\emph{New:}
The equilibria $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are exactly the
conjunction-idempotent values together with the unique negation fixed
point (Theorem~\ref{thm:equilibria}); self-conjunction is strictly
decreasing on the interior (Proposition~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}).
This connects the algebraic quotient to the dynamics of
$[0,1]$-valued credence (Section~\ref{sec:attractors}).

\textbf{Argument outline.}
We proceed in three stages.
First (Sections~\ref{sec:algebra}--\ref{sec:forced}), we establish
the algebraic setting: the product De Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$,
with copula uniqueness selecting $\min$ as the conjunction that
supports both idempotence and Bayesian conditioning.
Second (Sections~\ref{sec:conditioning}--\ref{sec:collapse}), we
develop the two key structural results: chain-rule conditioning (with
its evidence-zero analysis and path dependence) and the Kleene
collapse (with its uniqueness and impossibility theorems).
Third (Sections~\ref{sec:valuations}--\ref{sec:update}), we build
the applied theory: valuations with collapse composition, Bayesian
and Jeffrey update, convergence toward the three attractors, and the
argument that this dynamics is unavailable in finite-valued logics.
The paper is self-contained: all results are either proved in full,
proof-sketched with the key idea, or stated as ``it can be shown''
with a Lean reference.

\section{The Product De Morgan Triplet}
\label{sec:algebra}
%=============================================================================

This section defines the concrete structure we study: the product De
Morgan triplet~\cite{klement2000,esteva2000} on $[0,1]$, combined
with chain-rule conditioning.
This is not an axiomatic class of algebras: all properties follow from
the specific arithmetic of $[0,1]$.
Goguen~\cite{goguen1969} used the product t-norm as the conjunction in
his logic of inexact concepts.

\subsection{Primitive Operations}

\begin{definition}[Credence]
\label{def:credence}
A \emph{credence} is a real number $c \in [0, 1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Negation]
\label{def:neg}
$\negc c = 1 - c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Product Conjunction]
\label{def:conj}
$c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

The product is the product t-norm of fuzzy logic~\cite{klement2000}.
We write $\conjc$ for the algebraic product on $[0,1]$.

\begin{definition}[Disjunction]
\label{def:disj}
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = \negc(\negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2)
  = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 \cdot c_2$.
\end{definition}

The triple $(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ is the \emph{product De Morgan
triplet}~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998}.

\begin{remark}[Product vs.\ Joint]
\label{rem:product-vs-joint}
The product $c_1 \conjc c_2$ is an algebraic operation on credence
values, not a claim about the joint credence
$\cred(A \land B)$ of two propositions.
The joint depends on the dependence structure and lies in the
Fr\'echet-Hoeffding interval
$[\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0),\; \min(\cred(A), \cred(B))]$:
independence gives $\cred(A) \cdot \cred(B)$;
maximum positive dependence gives $\min(\cred(A), \cred(B))$;
maximum negative dependence gives
$\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0)$~\cite{nelsen2006}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Algebraic Properties}

\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Properties]
\label{thm:algebraic}
For all $c, c_1, c_2, c_3 \in [0,1]$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\negc\negc c = c$ \hfill (involution)
\item $\negc 0 = 1$, $\negc 1 = 0$ \hfill (boundary)
\item $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_2 \conjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \conjc c_2) \conjc c_3 = c_1 \conjc (c_2 \conjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \conjc 1 = c$, $c \conjc 0 = 0$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_2 \disjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \disjc c_2) \disjc c_3 = c_1 \disjc (c_2 \disjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \disjc 0 = c$, $c \disjc 1 = 1$ \hfill (identity, annihilator)
\item $\negc(c_1 \conjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \disjc \negc c_2$,
  $\negc(c_1 \disjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2$ \hfill (De Morgan)
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{neg\_neg}, \texttt{conj\_comm}, \texttt{conj\_assoc},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_conj}, \texttt{de\_morgan\_disj}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Arithmetic on $[0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The structure is two dual commutative monoids connected by De Morgan
duality; it is not a De Morgan algebra since $\conjc$ is not idempotent
($c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0,1\}$;
Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff})
and $\conjc$ does not distribute over $\disjc$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_disj\_not\_distrib}).

\subsection{Comparison with Product Logic}

The product De Morgan triplet and H\'ajek's Product Logic
($\Pi$-logic)~\cite{hajek1998} share the same t-norm.
They differ in the companion operation: $\Pi$-logic uses
residuation $a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$, which
forces $0 \to b = 1$ (ex falso);
the triplet uses the involutive complement $\negc c = 1 - c$
and chain-rule conditioning, which leaves $\cred(A \mid 0)$
unconstrained.
On positive evidence, the two coincide: the residuated implication
$\min(b/a, 1)$ equals the chain-rule conditional $b/a$ when
$b \leq a$ and $a > 0$.

\subsection{Relationship to Probability}
\label{sec:probability}

Every probability space satisfies the chain rule and complement axioms.
Probability adds a sigma-algebra, a countably additive measure, and
sigma-additivity.
The product De Morgan triplet drops all of these: there is no sample
space, no sigma-algebra, no additivity beyond complement.
At evidence zero, probability is undefined; chain-rule conditioning is
unconstrained.
The triplet retains exactly the structure that is shared by all
probability spaces (complement, product, chain rule) while discarding
everything specific to measure theory --- making it the ``greatest
common factor'' of probability and $[0,1]$-valued logic.

\section{Selecting the Conjunction}
\label{sec:forced}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Copula Uniqueness}
\label{sec:copula}

We ask: which truth-functional binary operations on $[0,1]$ can serve
as a conjunction that supports both logical idempotence
($\cred(A \land A) = \cred(A)$) and Bayesian conditioning?
We do not claim that this ``forces'' the product t-norm as the
algebraic conjunction $\conjc$; the product is chosen as the natural
multiplication on $[0,1]$.
What we show is that idempotence and Bayes-consistency together select
$\min$ as the unique truth-functional \emph{joint}, which is then the
conjunction for logic-compatible valuations
(Definition~\ref{def:kleene-valuation}).
Four requirements:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
\item \textbf{Truth-functional}: $j(a,b)$ depends on $a$ and $b$ alone.
\item \textbf{Bayes-consistent}:
  $\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(B \mid A) \cdot \cred(A)$.
\item \textbf{Non-trivial}: $\cred(A \mid B) \neq \cred(A)$ in general.
\item \textbf{Idempotent}: $j(a,a) = a$.
\end{enumerate}
Independence $j(a,b) = ab$ fails (iii) and (iv):
conditioning is trivial ($\cred(A \mid B) = \cred(A)$ when $\cred(B)
> 0$; Lean: \texttt{prod\_trivial\_conditioning}),
and $\cred(A \land A) = \cred(A)^2 \neq \cred(A)$ in general.
\L ukasiewicz $j(a,b) = \max(a+b-1,0)$ fails (iv):
$\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lean: \texttt{luk\_not\_idempotent}).

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of Truth-Functional Conjunction;
known~\cite{nelsen2006}]
\label{thm:min-copula-unique}
Let $j : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ be a symmetric idempotent copula
(symmetric, idempotent, zero boundary, unit marginal, 2-increasing).
Then $j(a,b) = \min(a,b)$ for all $a,b \in [0,1]$.
Moreover, $\min$ is non-trivial: $\min(a,b)/b \neq a$ in general.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Upper bound: 2-increasing on $[0,a] \times [b,1]$ with unit marginal
gives $j(a,b) \leq a$; by symmetry $j(a,b) \leq b$.
Lower bound: assume $a \leq b$.
Apply 2-increasing to $[0,a] \times [a,b]$:
$j(a,b) - j(a,a) - j(0,b) + j(0,a) \geq 0$.
By idempotence and zero boundary: $j(a,b) \geq a = \min(a,b)$.
Lean: \texttt{copula\_idempotent\_unique},
\texttt{min\_copula\_unique}.
\end{proof}

The minimum joint is the \emph{comonotonicity copula}: the extremal
case where all random variables are increasing functions of a single
latent variable~\cite{dhaene2002,nelsen2006}.
The uniqueness result is well known in copula
theory~\cite{nelsen2006,dhaene2002}; the application to selecting a
conjunction for $[0,1]$-valued logic is, to our knowledge, new.

\textbf{Comparison of alternatives.}
The product copula ($ab$) satisfies Bayes-consistency and
truth-functionality, but fails idempotence ($a^2 \neq a$) and gives
trivial conditioning ($\cred(A \mid B) = \cred(A)$ when
$\cred(B) > 0$: evidence has no effect).
The \L ukasiewicz copula ($\max(a+b-1,0)$) is Bayes-consistent but
again fails idempotence.
The RM3 joint $\max(\negc a, c) \cdot a$ fails Bayes-consistency:
at $a = 0.3$, $c = 0.6$,
$\max(0.7, 0.6) \cdot 0.3 = 0.21 \neq
\max(0.4, 0.3) \cdot 0.6 = 0.24$
(Lean: \texttt{rm3\_not\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).
Only an external (non-truth-functional) joint can simultaneously
satisfy all four requirements --- this is the full probability
setting, where joint credences are empirical data rather than
computed from marginals.

\subsection{Bayes Consistency}
\label{sec:bayes}

\begin{definition}[Bayes Consistency]
\label{def:bayes}
A binary operation $f$ on $[0,1]$ is \emph{Bayes-consistent} when
$f(a, b) \cdot a = f(b, a) \cdot b$ for all $a, b$.
\end{definition}

The product residuated implication is Bayes-consistent
(Lean: \texttt{prod\_resid\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).
The G\"odel implication fails on $[0,1]$
(Lean: \texttt{godel\_not\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).
The Kleene material conditional fails even on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{rm3\_not\_bayes\_consistent\_real}).

\section{Conditioning and Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:conditioning}
%=============================================================================

\begin{definition}[Conditioning Structure]
\label{def:conditioning}
A \emph{conditioning structure} for
$(\text{joint}, \text{evidence}) \in [0,1]^2$
consists of $\text{condCred} \in [0,1]$ satisfying
$\text{condCred} \conjc \text{evidence} = \text{joint}$.
In propositional notation:
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.
\end{definition}

The joint is supplied as input, not computed from marginals.
H\'ajek, Godo, and Esteva~\cite{hajek1995} used the chain rule
constraint as a technical device within fuzzy logic; here it is the
defining constraint.
Jeffrey~\cite{jeffrey1965} generalized Bayesian conditioning to
uncertain evidence via a mixture formula, also using the chain rule as
the primitive mechanism.
Cox~\cite{cox1946,cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed that
plausible reasoning satisfying certain desiderata must be isomorphic
to probability; chain-rule conditioning drops the requirement that the
conditional be a function of the marginals (see
Section~\ref{sec:intro}).

\subsection{Chain Rule vs.\ Residuation}
\label{sec:chain-vs-resid}

Write $e$ for the evidence credence and $p$ for the conclusion
credence.
When $e > 0$ and the joint is $\min(e, p)$ (the comonotonicity
copula), the chain rule gives the conditional
$\min(p/e,\; 1)$ --- the product residuated implication
$e \to p$ of H\'ajek's Product Logic~\cite{hajek1998}.
The two definitions agree on all positive-evidence rows; they differ
only at evidence~$= 0$.

$*$ = unconstrained (any value in $[0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule).
\textsuperscript{1}Shared by K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952},
LP~\cite{priest1979}, RM3~\cite{anderson1975}.
\textsuperscript{2}Coincides with G\"odel on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{godel\_impl\_eq\_prod\_resid}).}

\medskip
The critical row is $e = 0$.
The chain rule gives $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$: the conditional is
\emph{unconstrained} (Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
Residuation gives $0 \to p = 1$: ex falso is forced.
The Kleene material conditional also gives $1$ at $e = 0$, but for a
different reason (it is defined as $\max(\negc e, p)$, so
$\max(1, p) = 1$).

\begin{remark}[Comparison with Primitive-Conditioning Frameworks]
\label{rem:primitive-conditioning}
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} excludes measure-zero events from the
conditioning domain.
Popper~\cite{popper1959} forces
$P(A \mid B) = 1$ when $B$ entails $A$.
de Finetti~\cite{definetti1936} treats conditioning on a false
antecedent as \emph{void} rather than vacuously true.
Dubois and Prade~\cite{dubois1988} define conditional possibility
with a default of~$1$ at zero evidence.
Chain-rule conditioning differs: it works on bare values in $[0,1]$
without a measure space, and leaves the zero-evidence case genuinely
unconstrained.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Path Dependence at Evidence Zero}
\label{sec:path-dependence}

Under the min-copula joint $\min(a, b)$, the conditional for $b > 0$ is
$\min(a,b)/b$.
As $b \to 0$, the limit is path-dependent.

\begin{proposition}[Path Dependence at Evidence Zero; new]
\label{thm:path-dependence}
The limit of $\min(a,b)/b$ as $b \to 0$ is path-dependent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item Fixed $a > 0$: limit is $1$.
\item $a(b) = \lambda b$, $\lambda \in (0,1]$: limit is $\lambda$.
\item $a(b) = b^\alpha$, $\alpha > 1$: limit is $0$.
\end{enumerate}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved; ex falso requires an added
convention.
Lean: \texttt{path\_dep\_fixed\_a}, \texttt{path\_dep\_proportional},
\texttt{path\_dep\_square}, \texttt{path\_any\_positive\_value}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~For small $b < a$, $\min(a,b)/b = 1$.
(ii)~$\min(\lambda b, b)/b = \lambda$.
(iii)~$b^{\alpha-1} \to 0$.
\end{proof}

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/path_dependence}
\caption{Path dependence at evidence zero.
\textbf{Left:} $\min(a,b)/b$ near the origin; value depends on angle
$\theta$, not radius $r$.
\textbf{Right:} Limit as $r \to 0$: $1$ for $\theta < \pi/4$,
$\cot\theta$ for $\theta > \pi/4$.}
\label{fig:path-dependence}
\end{figure}

The proposition shows that conditioning at evidence zero is not merely
``undefined'' in the sense of a removable singularity: the singularity
is essential.
Every value in $[0,1]$ is a valid limit depending on the approach
path, so no single convention (ex falso, void, default~$1$) can be
justified on continuity grounds.
The chain rule's response --- leave the conditional unconstrained ---
is the minimal commitment consistent with the data.

The philosophical significance of path dependence is this:
\emph{how} evidence becomes impossible matters.
If a proposition $A$ with fixed credence $a > 0$ is conditioned on
evidence $B$ whose credence decreases to $0$, the conditional
$\cred(A \mid B) \to 1$ (case~(i)): strong propositions become
certain relative to vanishing evidence.
If $A$'s credence decreases proportionally with $B$'s (case~(ii)),
the conditional stabilizes at $\lambda$: the \emph{correlation}
between $A$ and $B$ is preserved.
If $A$'s credence vanishes faster than $B$'s (case~(iii)), the
conditional goes to $0$: the proposition becomes incredible relative
to the evidence.
These are genuinely different epistemic situations that a single
convention cannot distinguish.

\subsection{Existence and Uniqueness}

\begin{proposition}[Existence and Uniqueness of Conditioning]
\label{thm:existence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item \textbf{Positive evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} > 0$, a conditioning structure exists iff
  $\mathrm{joint} \leq \mathrm{evidence}$, and is then unique:
  $\mathrm{condCred} = \mathrm{joint} / \mathrm{evidence}$.
\item \textbf{Zero evidence:}
  When $\mathrm{evidence} = 0$, the constraint
  $\mathrm{condCred} \cdot 0 = \mathrm{joint}$ forces
  $\mathrm{joint} = 0$ but leaves $\mathrm{condCred}$ entirely
  unconstrained: every $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_mk}, \texttt{conditioning\_unique},
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(i)~If $0 < e$ and $j \leq e$, then $c = j/e \in [0,1]$ and
$c \cdot e = j$.
Uniqueness: if $c \cdot e = c' \cdot e$ with $e > 0$, then $c = c'$.
(ii)~$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c \in [0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The dichotomy is sharp: at positive evidence, the conditional is a
\emph{function} of the joint and evidence (division); at zero
evidence, it is a \emph{relation} (any value is consistent).
The chain rule is thus strictly weaker than division as a definition
of conditioning: it agrees with division where division is defined,
and is permissive where division is not.

\section{The Kleene Collapse}

\subsection{Collapse Homomorphism}

\begin{lemma}[Unique Negation Fixed Point]
\label{thm:liar}
$\negc c = c$ if and only if $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Lean: \texttt{liar\_fixed\_point},
\texttt{neg\_fixed\_point\_unique}.
\end{lemma}

The partition $\{0\}, (0,1), \{1\}$ is a congruence on the product
De Morgan triplet.
Define the collapse:
\[
\text{collapse}(c) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } c = 0, \\
\tfrac{1}{2} & \text{if } 0 < c < 1, \\
1 & \text{if } c = 1.
\end{cases}
\]

\begin{theorem}[Collapse Homomorphism; folklore, verified here]
\label{thm:collapse}
The collapse is a surjective homomorphism from
$([0,1], \negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ to
$(\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}, \mathrm{neg}, \min, \max)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Case analysis.
Negation: $0 \mapsto 1$, $1 \mapsto 0$; if $0 < c < 1$ then
$0 < 1-c < 1$.
Conjunction: $0$ absorbs, $1$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Disjunction: $1$ absorbs, $0$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ then
$0 < c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_disj}, \texttt{collapse\_surjective}.
\end{proof}

The target is the Kleene lattice~\cite{kleene1952}, underlying
K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952}, LP~\cite{priest1979}, and
RM3~\cite{anderson1975,meyer1972}.
The Kleene quotient's $\min$/$\max$ operations are exactly the
conjunction/disjunction of possibility
theory~\cite{dubois1988}.

\subsection{Uniqueness and Impossibility}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to Classical Logic]
\label{prop:no-classical}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation.
Lean: \texttt{no\_boolean\_neg\_retraction}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to G\"odel or \L ukasiewicz Logic]
\label{prop:no-godel-luk}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ preserves G\"odel
negation (which has no fixed point), and no such $\varphi$
preserves both negation and \L ukasiewicz conjunction
(since $\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq
\tfrac{1}{2}$).
Lean: \texttt{no\_godel\_collapse}, \texttt{no\_luk\_collapse}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the Three-Element Quotient; new]
\label{thm:quotient-unique}
Any surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to S$ ($|S|=3$) preserving negation
and conjunction yields the Kleene lattice (up to relabeling).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be the unique negation fixed point of $S$.
The remaining elements $\varphi(0)$, $\varphi(1)$ form the 2-cycle.
The conjunction table is forced: $\varphi(0)$ absorbs, $\varphi(1)$
is identity,
and $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc \tfrac{1}{2}) =
\varphi(\tfrac{1}{4}) = \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$
(since $\tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$).
This forces Kleene $\min$.
Lean: \texttt{three\_element\_quotient\_unique}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Congruence Landscape}
\label{sec:congruence-landscape}

The collapse results combine into a congruence classification at two
levels, depending on the scope of the multiplication compatibility
hypothesis.

A \emph{unit congruence} requires multiplication compatibility only for
$[0,1]$-valued inputs.
A \emph{real congruence} extends compatibility to all of $\Real$.

\begin{theorem}[Congruence Classification; new]
\label{thm:congruence}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item \textbf{Singleton boundaries:}
  Under any non-trivial unit congruence, $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ are
  singleton classes.
\item \textbf{No two-element quotient:}
  No surjective homomorphism $[0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves negation
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}).
\item \textbf{Uniqueness at three elements:}
  The Kleene lattice is the unique three-element quotient
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}), and the Kleene partition is
  itself a verified unit congruence (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}).
\item \textbf{$\Real$-rigidity:}
  Under $\Real$-multiplication, no non-trivial finite quotient exists
  with $n \geq 2$ classes.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{UnitCongruence.singleton\_zero},
\texttt{singleton\_one}, \texttt{kleene\_congruence},
\texttt{RealCongruence.no\_nontrivial\_finite\_quotient}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
(1)~Suppose $0 \sim \varepsilon$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$.
Pick any $x \in [0,1]$.
Since $0 \sim \varepsilon$, multiplication compatibility gives
$0 \cdot (1-\varepsilon) \sim \varepsilon (1-\varepsilon)$, so
$0 \sim \varepsilon(1-\varepsilon)$.
Now use the key observation: for any $x \in [0,1]$, the sequence
$x, x(1-\varepsilon), x(1-\varepsilon)^2, \ldots$ converges to $0$.
By multiplication compatibility at each step,
$x(1-\varepsilon)^n \sim x(1-\varepsilon)^{n+1}$ (since
$0 \sim \varepsilon$ implies $x(1-\varepsilon)^n \cdot 0 \sim
x(1-\varepsilon)^n \cdot \varepsilon$, and the left side is $0$).
By transitivity, $x \sim 0$ for every $x$: the congruence is trivial.
The argument for $\{1\}$ is dual (apply negation).

(2)~Immediate from the negation fixed point at $\tfrac{1}{2}$.

(3)~Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}.

(4)~Given $n \geq 2$ classes and $a \in (0,1)$, pigeonhole on the
sequence $a, a^2, a^3, \ldots$ gives $a^i \sim a^j$ with $i < j$.
Under $\Real$-multiplication, multiply both sides by $(a^i)^{-1}$
to get $1 \sim a^{j-i}$, contradicting singleton boundaries from (1).
\end{proof}

The two levels of the classification arise from a tension between
algebra and topology.
In $\Real$, multiplicative inverses are available: from $a^i \sim
a^j$ one can multiply by $(a^i)^{-1}$ to deduce $1 \sim a^{j-i}$,
which contradicts singleton boundaries.
In $[0,1]$, the inverse $1/a^i > 1$ leaves the interval, so the
scaling trick is blocked.
This is not merely a proof technique failure: the Kleene partition
\emph{is} a unit congruence (Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}), so the
$[0,1]$ setting genuinely admits non-trivial quotients.
Whether it admits quotients with four or more classes remains
\textbf{open}: the gap between unit and real congruences isolates
$[0,1]$-cancellation as the key obstacle (Section~\ref{sec:open}).

\subsection{Valuation Types}

A \emph{valuation} assigns credences to propositions.
The algebra constrains how credences compose; a valuation specifies
which credences particular propositions receive.

\begin{definition}[Complement-Preserving Valuation]
\label{def:cp-valuation}
$v : \mathrm{Prop} \to [0,1]$ is \emph{complement-preserving}
if $v(\negc A) = \negc v(A)$.
Lean: \texttt{CpValuation}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Independent Valuation]
\label{def:indep-valuation}
A complement-preserving valuation is \emph{independent} if
$v(A \land B) = v(A) \conjc v(B)$ and
$v(A \lor B) = v(A) \disjc v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{IndepValuation}.
\end{definition}

Under independence, $\cred(A \mid B) = v(A)$ whenever $v(B) > 0$:
evidence has no effect.
Independence also fails idempotence ($v(A \land A) = v(A)^2 \neq v(A)$
in general; Lean: \texttt{IndepValuation.conj\_not\_idempotent}).

\begin{definition}[Kleene Valuation]
\label{def:kleene-valuation}
A complement-preserving valuation is a \emph{Kleene valuation} if
$v(A \land B) = \min(v(A), v(B))$ and
$v(A \lor B) = \max(v(A), v(B))$.
\end{definition}

By the uniqueness theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique}), the
minimum is the unique symmetric, idempotent copula.
A Kleene valuation is therefore the unique choice that is both
idempotent and Bayes-consistent --- the logic-compatible valuation.

\begin{definition}[Joint-Parametrized Valuation]
\label{def:joint-valuation}
A \emph{joint-parametrized valuation} consists of a complement-preserving
valuation $v$ together with an external joint function
$j : \mathrm{Prop}^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying the Fr\'echet bounds.
Lean: \texttt{JointValuation}.
\end{definition}

The joint-parametrized valuation unifies the previous two:
setting $j(A,B) = v(A) \cdot v(B)$ recovers independence;
setting $j(A,B) = \min(v(A), v(B))$ recovers the Kleene valuation.
An arbitrary joint from empirical data gives full probability-style
reasoning.

\subsection{Collapse Composition}

\begin{proposition}[Collapse Composition; new]
\label{prop:collapse-composition}
Composing any product-copula valuation with the collapse homomorphism
(Theorem~\ref{thm:collapse}) yields a Kleene valuation:
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{collapse}(v(\negc A)) &= \mathrm{neg}(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \land B)) &= \min(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))), \\
\mathrm{collapse}(v(A \lor B)) &= \max(\mathrm{collapse}(v(A)),\, \mathrm{collapse}(v(B))).
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_val\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_val\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_val\_disj}.
\end{proposition}

This is the mechanism connecting $[0,1]$ to K$_3$/LP/RM3:
regardless of the specific copula, the collapsed valuation always
operates with Kleene min/max.
It is the ``forgetful functor'' from quantitative (probability) to
qualitative (logic): every $[0,1]$-valued assessment has a unique
Kleene shadow.

The collapse composition theorem is the algebraic core of the
intersection thesis.
A probabilist working with joint-parametrized valuations (arbitrary
copulas, Fr\'echet bounds, external joint data) can always project
to a three-valued logic by composing with the collapse.
A logician working with K$_3$ or LP valuations can always lift to
$[0,1]$ by choosing any preimage of the three-valued assignment.
The collapse is the canonical such projection: surjective,
structure-preserving, and unique at three elements
(Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}).

\subsection{Graded Predicates}

A graded predicate $P : X \to [0,1]$ generalizes fuzzy set
membership~\cite{zadeh1965}.
Pointwise operations satisfy all algebraic laws.
Quantifiers: $\cred(\forall x.\; P(x)) = \inf_x P(x)$,
$\cred(\exists x.\; P(x)) = \sup_x P(x)$.

\begin{theorem}[Quantifier Duality]
\label{thm:quantifier-duality}
$\negc\cred(\forall x.\; P(x)) = \cred(\exists x.\; \negc P(x))$.
Lean: \texttt{quantifier\_duality\_val}.
\end{theorem}

For crisp predicates (range $\subseteq \{0, 1\}$), the quantifiers
agree with classical logic
(Lean: \texttt{crisp\_inf\_zero\_iff}, \texttt{crisp\_sup\_one\_iff}).
Self-reference: if $c = \negc c$, then $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lean: \texttt{russell\_fixed\_point}).
In a paraconsistent set-theoretic framework~\cite{brady2006,weber2010},
Russell's predicate applied to itself would receive credence
$\tfrac{1}{2}$; we do not develop such a framework here.

\subsection{Consequence Relations}

The product De Morgan triplet has no built-in consequence
relation~\cite{priest2008}.
On the Kleene quotient, designated values yield standard systems.
K$_3$ (designating $\{1\}$) is paracomplete: no tautologies
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology}).
LP (designating $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) is paraconsistent: excluded
middle holds but explosion fails
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}).
Graded consequence on $[0,1]$ (threshold $t$: all premises $\geq t$
implies conclusion $\geq t$) is explosion-free for
$0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$: the constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ valuation
gives $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$ and
$\negc v(A) = 1 - \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$, so both
a proposition and its negation are designated, while an arbitrary
$v(B) = 0 < t$ shows the conclusion need not be designated
(Lean: \texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}).
The witness is the same in all three cases: the negation fixed point
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ makes both a proposition and its negation
``designated'' without forcing arbitrary conclusions.

\subsection{Bayesian and Jeffrey Update}

\begin{definition}[Bayesian Update]
\label{def:bayesian-update}
Given a joint-parametrized valuation $v$ with evidence $B$ satisfying
$v(B) > 0$, the \emph{Bayesian posterior} is
$v'(A) = j(A,B) / v(B)$.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Chain-Rule Preservation]
\label{prop:bayesian-chain}
$v'(A) \conjc v(B) = j(A,B)$.
The posterior is the unique credence with this property.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule},
\texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{definition}[Jeffrey Update]
\label{def:jeffrey-update}
For uncertain evidence with new credence $q$ for $B$:
\[
v'(A) = \cred(A \mid B) \cdot q + \cred(A \mid \negc B) \cdot (1 - q).
\]
At $q = 1$ this reduces to Bayesian update; at $q = 0$ it conditions
on $\negc B$.
Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate}, \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_certain},
\texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_zero}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Zero-Evidence Underdetermination]
\label{prop:zero-underdetermined}
When $v(B) = 0$, any conditional $\cred(A \mid B) \in [0,1]$ satisfies
the chain rule, so neither Bayesian nor Jeffrey update produces a unique
posterior.
Lean: \texttt{bayesian\_underdetermined\_at\_zero},
\texttt{jeffrey\_underdetermined\_at\_zero}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$.
\end{proof}

This underdetermination connects to imprecise
probability~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}, where sets of priors replace
point estimates precisely when the data underdetermine a unique
posterior.
Rather than a defect, the underdetermination is a feature: impossible
evidence does not provide information, and the formalism reflects this.

Operationally: when $v(B) = 0$, the agent cannot update on $B$ in any
determinate way.
The chain rule records this honestly: every posterior in $[0,1]$ is
consistent with the chain rule when evidence is zero.
This yields a \emph{set} of posteriors $\{c : c \cdot 0 = 0\} =
[0,1]$, which is exactly the maximal imprecise posterior.
An agent may narrow this set by adopting a prior over the
``evidence-zero'' regime (de Finetti's void, Popper's convention,
etc.), but the chain rule itself imposes no such commitment.

\begin{remark}[Iterated Update]
\label{rem:iterated}
When evidence is positive, Bayesian update can be iterated:
given evidence $B_1, B_2, \ldots$ with $v(B_i) > 0$, each step
produces a unique posterior, and the chain rule is preserved at each
step by construction (Proposition~\ref{prop:bayesian-chain}).
Lean: \texttt{update\_preserves\_chain\_rule}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Equilibria: $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$}
\label{sec:attractors}

We now establish formally that $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are the
algebraic equilibria of the product De Morgan triplet, connecting the
algebraic quotient (Section~\ref{sec:collapse}) to the dynamics of
$[0,1]$-valued credence.

\begin{definition}[Spread Function]
\label{def:spread}
The \emph{spread} (Bernoulli variance) of a credence $c$ is
$s(c) = c \conjc \negc c = c(1-c)$.
Lean: \texttt{spread}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Spread Characterization; known]
\label{prop:spread}
\hfill
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item $s(c) = 0$ iff $c \in \{0, 1\}$.
\item $s(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{4}$
  (the maximum of $s$ on $[0,1]$).
\item $0 < s(c) \leq \tfrac{1}{4}$ for all $c \in (0,1)$.
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{spread\_eq\_zero\_iff}, \texttt{spread\_half},
\texttt{spread\_le\_quarter}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{definition}[Certainty Function]
\label{def:certainty}
The \emph{certainty} of $c$ is $c \disjc \negc c = 1 - c(1-c)$.
Lean: \texttt{certainty}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Certainty Bounds; known]
\label{prop:certainty}
$c \disjc \negc c \geq \tfrac{3}{4}$ for all $c \in [0,1]$, with
equality at $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$, and
$c \disjc \negc c = 1$ iff $c \in \{0, 1\}$.
Lean: \texttt{certainty\_ge\_three\_quarters},
\texttt{certainty\_eq\_one\_iff}.
\end{proposition}

The following three results make the equilibrium structure precise.

\begin{proposition}[Self-Conjunction Decay; new]
\label{prop:self-conj-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$: $c \conjc c = c^2 < c$.
Dually, $c \disjc c > c$.
Lean: \texttt{self\_conj\_lt}, \texttt{self\_disj\_gt}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 < c \iff c(c - 1) < 0$, which holds since $0 < c < 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Iterated Self-Conjunction; new]
\label{prop:pow-decay}
For all $c \in (0,1)$ and $n \in \Nat$:
$c^{n+1} < c^n$.
In particular, $c^n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.
Lean: \texttt{pow\_strictly\_decreasing}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$c^n \cdot c < c^n \cdot 1 = c^n$ since $0 < c < 1$ and $c^n > 0$.
Convergence: $c^n = e^{n \ln c} \to 0$ since $\ln c < 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Equilibria Characterization; new]
\label{thm:equilibria}
A credence $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies $c \conjc c = c$ or $\negc c = c$
if and only if $c \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$.
Equivalently, the set of conjunction-idempotent values $\{0, 1\}$
together with the unique negation fixed point $\{\tfrac{1}{2}\}$
exhausts the algebraic equilibria.
Lean: \texttt{equilibria\_characterization}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff}).
$1 - c = c$ iff $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lemma~\ref{thm:liar}).
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:equilibria} identifies $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
as the only credences invariant under the basic algebraic operations.
These are exactly the three values of the Kleene quotient
(Section~\ref{sec:collapse}).
The correspondence provides a dynamical reading of the Kleene lattice:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $0$ = \emph{refuted}: conjunction-idempotent at the lower
  boundary.
\item $1$ = \emph{confirmed}: conjunction-idempotent at the upper
  boundary.
\item $\tfrac{1}{2}$ = \emph{irreducibly uncertain}: the unique
  negation fixed point, neither confirmable nor refutable by
  complement alone.
\end{itemize}
Every other credence $c \in (0,1) \setminus \{\tfrac{1}{2}\}$
is \emph{not} an equilibrium: $c^2 < c$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}), so iterated
self-conjunction drives it toward $0$; it is not a negation fixed
point either.
Finite-valued logics take the three values as primitive;
the continuous framework derives them as the algebraic equilibria
of the product De Morgan triplet.

\begin{remark}[What ``attractor'' means here]
\label{rem:attractor-caveat}
Propositions~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}--\ref{prop:pow-decay}
establish that iterated self-conjunction $c, c^2, c^4, \ldots$
converges to $0$ for every $c \in (0,1)$.
This is a precise algebraic statement about the product t-norm.
The epistemological claim --- that \emph{Bayesian update under
consistent evidence} drives credence toward $0$ or $1$ --- is a
standard result in probability theory (Doob's martingale convergence;
merging of opinions~\cite{walley1991}).
The convergence to $\tfrac{1}{2}$ under balanced or contradictory
evidence is informal: we do not prove a convergence theorem for
$\tfrac{1}{2}$, but observe that the negation fixed point is the
natural resting place for credences that are equidistant from both
boundaries.
A formal treatment would require specifying a stochastic model for
the evidence sequence, which is beyond the scope of this paper
(see Section~\ref{sec:open}).
\end{remark}

\textbf{Non-idempotence as evidence decay.}
Proposition~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay} has a probabilistic
interpretation.
Under the independence assumption,
$v(A \land B) = v(A) \cdot v(B) = c^2$ when $v(A) = v(B) = c$.
Since $c^2 < c$ for $c \in (0,1)$, combining two independent copies
of the same uncertain evidence \emph{decreases} the joint credence.
The product t-norm creates dynamics (the system evolves under
iterated conjunction); the min t-norm freezes them
($\min(c,c) = c$, no change).
Starting from $c_0 \in (0,1)$, the sequence
$c_0, c_0^2, c_0^4, \ldots, c_0^{2^n}$ converges to $0$:
unrenewed evidence decays.
A credence must be sustained by fresh evidence to persist above zero.

\subsection{Comparison with Alternative T-Norms}
\label{sec:not-finite}

The equilibria characterization (Theorem~\ref{thm:equilibria})
depends on two properties of the product t-norm: non-idempotence
($c^2 \neq c$ for interior $c$) and the existence of a negation
fixed point ($\negc\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}$).
The three continuous t-norms differ in which of these they provide.

\emph{G\"odel} (min t-norm): $\min(c,c) = c$ for all $c$, so every
value is conjunction-idempotent.
There are no dynamics: iterated self-conjunction is the identity.
(G\"odel negation $\negc_G c = \begin{cases} 1 & c=0 \\ 0 &
c>0\end{cases}$ has no fixed point at all.)

\emph{\L ukasiewicz} ($\max(a+b-1, 0)$): self-conjunction gives
$\max(2c-1, 0)$, which is non-idempotent for $c \in (0,1)$.
But $\max(2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0$: the negation fixed
point maps to $0$ under self-conjunction.
The \L ukasiewicz t-norm destroys the middle equilibrium; this is
why no \L ukasiewicz collapse exists
(Proposition~\ref{prop:no-godel-luk}).

\emph{Product} ($c \cdot c = c^2$): non-idempotent for $c \in (0,1)$,
and $(\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = \tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$: the negation fixed
point maps to the interior, preserving its collapse class.
Only the product has both non-trivial dynamics and three equilibria.

\small
\begin{tabular}{llccc}
\toprule
\textbf{T-norm} & \textbf{Self-conj.}
  & \textbf{Non-idempotent?} & \textbf{$\negc$-fixed pt?}
  & \textbf{Three equil.?} \\
\midrule
Product ($c \cdot c$) & $c^2 < c$ & Yes & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & Yes \\
G\"odel ($\min(c,c)$) & $c$ & No & None & No \\
\L ukasiewicz & $\max(2c-1,0)$ & Yes & $\tfrac{1}{2} \mapsto 0$
  & No \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\end{center}

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Summary of Results}

We collect the main contributions, distinguishing known results
(applied in a new context) from new ones.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Copula uniqueness} (Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique};
  known~\cite{nelsen2006}, applied here to many-valued logic):
  $\min$ is the unique symmetric idempotent copula.
  This selects the conjunction for logic-compatible valuations.
\item \textbf{Evidence-zero analysis}
  (Proposition~\ref{thm:path-dependence}; new):
  chain-rule conditioning at zero evidence is unconstrained and
  path-dependent; no limiting convention resolves the singularity.
  Connection to imprecise probability~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
\item \textbf{Kleene collapse uniqueness}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}; new):
  the Kleene lattice is the unique non-trivial three-element quotient.
  No collapse to Boolean, G\"odel, or \L ukasiewicz logic exists.
\item \textbf{Congruence classification}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}; new):
  under $[0,1]$-multiplication, $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ are singletons
  in any non-trivial congruence; under $\Real$-multiplication, no
  non-trivial finite quotient exists.
\item \textbf{Equilibria characterization}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:equilibria}; new):
  $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are exactly the conjunction-idempotent
  values together with the negation fixed point.
  Self-conjunction is strictly decreasing on the interior
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}).
\item \textbf{Collapse composition}
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-composition}; new):
  composing any copula valuation with the collapse yields a Kleene
  valuation.
\end{enumerate}

The chain rule $\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is the shared content of probability theory (where it defines the
conditional for positive evidence) and many-valued logic (where it
constrains the conditioning operation).
At evidence zero, probability declares the expression undefined,
residuation declares it $1$, and the chain rule declares it
unconstrained.
This single difference at zero has cascading consequences:
no ex falso (Section~\ref{sec:conditioning}), underdetermined
posteriors connecting to imprecise probability
(Section~\ref{sec:update}), and the Kleene lattice as the qualitative
shadow of $[0,1]$-valued credence (Section~\ref{sec:collapse}).

\subsection{Open Problems}
\label{sec:open}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Four or more classes under $[0,1]$-multiplication.}
  The scaling trick (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}(4)) fails without
  multiplicative inverses; the gap between unit and real congruences
  isolates $[0,1]$-cancellation as the precise obstacle.
  A resolution would determine whether the Kleene lattice is the
  \emph{only} non-trivial finite quotient of the product De Morgan
  triplet.

\item \textbf{Proof theory.}
  Is there a sound and complete calculus for chain-rule conditioning
  on $[0,1]$?
  The analogous question for product logic (with residuation) is
  answered by H\'ajek's completeness theorem~\cite{hajek1998};
  replacing residuation with the chain rule changes the answer.

\item \textbf{Convergence under iterated update.}
  Propositions~\ref{prop:self-conj-decay}--\ref{prop:pow-decay}
  establish algebraic decay under iterated self-conjunction.
  Under what stochastic model for the evidence sequence does
  iterated Bayesian update drive $\cred_n(A) \to 1$, $0$, or
  $\tfrac{1}{2}$?
  The first two cases connect to Doob's martingale convergence and
  merging-of-opinions results; the third ($\tfrac{1}{2}$ as the limit
  under balanced evidence) requires a formal treatment beyond the
  algebraic characterization given here.

\item \textbf{Paraconsistent set theory.}
  The $\tfrac{1}{2}$ fixed point resolves Russell's paradox; what
  mathematics can be developed in a $[0,1]$-valued set theory with
  chain-rule conditioning?

\item \textbf{Congruence lattice of $(T, \negc)$ vs.\ $(T, \to_T)$.}
  For a fixed continuous t-norm $T$, how does the congruence lattice
  of the De Morgan reduct $(T, \negc)$ relate to the congruence
  lattice of the BL expansion $(T, \to_T)$?
  The product case gives one data point (enlargement under
  $[0,1]$-multiplication); the \L ukasiewicz case gives the
  opposite.

\item \textbf{Decidability.}
  Is graded consequence on $[0,1]$ decidable?
  The problem quantifies over all valuations $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$;
  decidability depends on the logical language.
  In propositional product logic (with residuation), H\'ajek's
  completeness theorem~\cite{hajek1998} settles this;
  replacing residuation with the chain rule changes the question.

\item \textbf{Relationship to Adams' probability logic.}
  Adams~\cite{adams1975} studied probabilistic validity: $A \models
  B$ iff $P(B) \geq P(A)$ for all probability measures $P$.
  Does chain-rule conditioning on $[0,1]$ (without additivity)
  yield a weaker or incomparable notion of validity?
  The connection to van Fraassen's~\cite{vanfraassen1976,
  vanfraassen1983} approach to conditional probability in
  non-classical settings is unexplored.
\end{enumerate}
