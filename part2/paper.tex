\begin{abstract}
Part~1 established \emph{Cred} as a constraint algebra on $[0,1]$: values,
complement, product conjunction, De~Morgan disjunction, and the chain rule
for conditioning.
This paper adds three layers---\emph{valuations} (assigning credences to
propositions), \emph{consequence relations} (determining which inferences
preserve credence), and \emph{update rules} (revising credences given new
evidence)---and extends to first order via \emph{graded predicates} with
inf/sup quantifiers.
Different choices at each layer recover different systems: probability,
product fuzzy logic, K$_3$, LP, RM3, and imprecise probability.
We prove that explosion (the classical principle that a contradiction
entails every proposition) fails non-vacuously for LP and for graded
consequence at thresholds $0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$
(K$_3$ has explosion only vacuously, a standard result), and that the
Russell predicate yields credence~$\tfrac{1}{2}$ rather than a paradox.
The main definitions and theorems are machine-checked in Lean~4.
\end{abstract}

%=============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

An algebra of credence values specifies how values relate to one
another---how negation, conjunction, and conditioning interact on $[0,1]$.
To reason with credences, three further capabilities are needed.
First, an \emph{interpretation}: a way to assign credence values to
propositions.
In many-valued logic this is called a \emph{valuation}---a function from
propositions to truth values~\cite{priest2008}; here the truth values are
the full interval $[0,1]$.
Second, a \emph{consequence relation}: a specification of which inferences
are valid---when does accepting the premises (at some credence level)
force acceptance of the conclusion~\cite{priest2008}?
In classical logic, consequence has \emph{explosion} (ex falso quodlibet):
from a proposition and its negation, any conclusion follows.
Third, an \emph{update rule}: a prescription for revising all credences
when new evidence arrives, generalizing Bayesian
conditionalization~\cite{jeffrey1965}.

Part~1~\cite{part1} provides the algebra: values in $[0,1]$, complement
$\negc c = 1 - c$, product conjunction
$c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$, De~Morgan disjunction
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = \negc(\negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2)$, and the chain rule
$\condc{A}{B} \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.
This paper adds the three layers above:

\textbf{Layer} & \textbf{Mechanism} & \textbf{Section} \\
\midrule
Interpretation & Valuations (independent and joint-parametrized)
  & Section~\ref{sec:valuations} \\
Consequence & Designated-value (K$_3$, LP, RM3) and graded
  & Section~\ref{sec:consequence} \\
Update & Bayesian and Jeffrey conditionalization
  & Section~\ref{sec:update} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}

\noindent
Each layer admits multiple instantiations, and different combinations
recover different known systems.
Independent valuations recover product fuzzy logic~\cite{hajek1998};
joint-parametrized valuations recover probability-like behavior.
Designated-value consequence yields K$_3$, LP, or RM3 depending on which
truth values are accepted~\cite{priest2008}; graded consequence
parametrizes validity by a threshold.
Bayesian and Jeffrey update both derive from the chain rule.

The paper then extends to first order via graded predicates
(Section~\ref{sec:predicates})---functions from a domain to
$[0,1]$, generalizing fuzzy set membership~\cite{zadeh1965}---with
inf/sup quantifiers satisfying duality, and shows that the
self-referential equation $c = \negc c$ (as would arise from
unrestricted comprehension) yields a fixed point ($\tfrac{1}{2}$)
rather than a paradox.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item Cred valuations with complement preservation, including independent
valuations (homomorphisms for product fuzzy logic) and joint-parametrized
valuations (where the joint is external data, as in probability), with the
idempotence problem as a diagnostic for independence
(Section~\ref{sec:valuations}).
\item Consequence relations in two families: designated-value consequence
(K$_3$, LP, RM3) via the three-valued collapse, and graded consequence
parametrized by a threshold on $[0,1]$.
Explosion fails non-vacuously for LP and graded consequence at
$0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$; K$_3$ has explosion only vacuously
(Section~\ref{sec:consequence}).
\item Bayesian and Jeffrey conditionalization as update rules on Cred
valuations, with chain-rule preservation and zero-evidence
underdetermination connecting to imprecise
probability~\cite{walley1991}
(Section~\ref{sec:update}).
\item Graded predicates with inf/sup quantifiers, quantifier duality,
classical agreement for crisp predicates, and unrestricted comprehension
yielding fixed points rather than paradoxes
(Section~\ref{sec:predicates}).
\item Lean~4 formalization of the above (Section~\ref{sec:lean}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Notation.}
We use the notation of Part~1: $\negc$ for complement ($1-c$),
$\conjc$ for product conjunction ($c_1 \cdot c_2$),
$\disjc$ for De~Morgan disjunction, and
$\condc{A}{B}$ for the conditional credence of $A$ given~$B$.
New notation: $\models_D$ and $\models_t$ for semantic consequence under
designation~$D$ or threshold~$t$.

\section{Valuations}
\label{sec:valuations}
%=============================================================================

A \emph{valuation} assigns credences to propositions---it is the bridge
between the propositional world and the algebraic world.
In many-valued logic~\cite{priest2008}, a valuation maps propositions
to a set of truth values; here the truth values are the full
interval $[0,1]$.

\begin{definition}[Complement-Preserving Valuation]
\label{def:cp-valuation}
Given a type $\alpha$ of propositions with a propositional negation
$\sigma : \alpha \to \alpha$, a \emph{complement-preserving valuation}
is a function $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ satisfying
\[
v(\sigma(A)) = \negc v(A) = 1 - v(A)
\]
for all $A \in \alpha$.
Lean: \texttt{CpValuation}.
Note that $\sigma$ is not required to be involutive
($\sigma(\sigma(A)) = A$); involutivity is an additional hypothesis
where needed.
\end{definition}

Complement preservation is the minimal constraint connecting the
propositional negation to the algebraic complement.
Beyond this, two natural choices arise for how $v$ handles conjunction.

\subsection{Independent Valuations}

\begin{definition}[Independent Valuation]
\label{def:indep-valuation}
An \emph{independent valuation} is a complement-preserving valuation
equipped with propositional conjunction $\wedge$ and disjunction $\vee$
such that
\[
v(A \wedge B) = v(A) \conjc v(B), \qquad
v(A \vee B) = v(A) \disjc v(B).
\]
Lean: \texttt{IndepValuation}.
\end{definition}

An independent valuation is a homomorphism from the propositional algebra
to the Cred algebra for all three connectives
(Lean: \texttt{indep\_is\_conj\_homomorphism}).
This is the natural choice for product fuzzy logic, where conjunction
\emph{is} the product t-norm.

\begin{example}[Rain and Wet Street]
\label{ex:rain}
Let $R$ = ``it rained'' and $W$ = ``the street is wet,'' with
$v(R) = 0.7$ and $v(W) = 0.9$.
An independent valuation gives
$v(R \wedge W) = 0.7 \times 0.9 = 0.63$---the product treats $R$ and $W$
as independent evidence.
But rain causes wet streets, so in practice one expects
$v(R \wedge W) > 0.63$.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Idempotence Problem]
\label{prop:idempotence}
Under an independent valuation, $v(A \wedge A) = v(A)^2 \neq v(A)$
whenever $v(A) \notin \{0, 1\}$.
In particular, if $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, then
$v(A \wedge A) = \tfrac{1}{4} \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
From Definition~\ref{def:indep-valuation}:
$v(A \wedge A) = v(A) \conjc v(A) = v(A)^2$.
Part~1's idempotence characterization (Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff})
shows $c \conjc c = c$ iff $c \in \{0, 1\}$.
Lean: \texttt{IndepValuation.conj\_not\_idempotent}.
\end{proof}

This is not a defect but a diagnostic: an independent valuation treats
$A$ and $A$ as independent evidence, so $A \wedge A$ is ``weaker''
than~$A$.
Real propositions are not independent of themselves---this is the
motivation for supplying joints externally.

\subsection{Joint-Parametrized Valuations}

\begin{definition}[Joint-Parametrized Valuation]
\label{def:joint-valuation}
A \emph{joint-parametrized valuation} is a complement-preserving
valuation equipped with a joint function
$j : \alpha \times \alpha \to [0,1]$ satisfying
$j(A, B) \leq v(A)$ and $j(A, B) \leq v(B)$ (marginal bounds).
Lean: \texttt{JointValuation}.
Symmetry ($j(A,B) = j(B,A)$) is not required; when modelling a
commutative conjunction, one may additionally impose symmetry.
\end{definition}

The joint function is external data---not computed from $v(A)$ and
$v(B)$ alone.
This is how probability works: the joint $P(A \land B)$ is determined
by the measure, not by the marginals.
Cred allows the same approach without requiring a measure.

\begin{example}[Rain and Wet Street, Continued]
\label{ex:rain-joint}
Continuing Example~\ref{ex:rain}: with $v(R) = 0.7$, $v(W) = 0.9$,
one might supply $j(W, R) = 0.65$ from data or domain knowledge.
This gives $v(W \mid R) = j(W,R) / v(R) = 0.65 / 0.7 \approx 0.93$:
knowing it rained raises the credence of a wet street from $0.9$
to $0.93$.
The Fr\'echet bounds require
$\max(0.7 + 0.9 - 1, 0) = 0.6 \leq j(W,R) \leq \min(0.7, 0.9) = 0.7$,
and $0.65$ is within this interval.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Fr\'echet Bounds on Valuations]
\label{prop:frechet-valuations}
For any joint-parametrized valuation:
\[
j(A, B) \leq \min(v(A), v(B)).
\]
If additionally $1 - v(A) - v(B) + j(A, B) \geq 0$ (complement
non-negativity), then
\[
\max(v(A) + v(B) - 1, 0) \leq j(A, B).
\]
Lean: \texttt{joint\_frechet\_upper}, \texttt{joint\_frechet\_lower}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Direct application of Part~1's Fr\'echet bounds
(\texttt{frechet\_upper}, \texttt{frechet\_lower}) to the valuation's
joint function.
\end{proof}

Complement non-negativity is not part of the \texttt{JointValuation}
definition; it must be supplied as an additional hypothesis, as in
Part~1.

When $v(B) > 0$, the chain rule has a unique solution; we write
$v(A \mid B) = j(A, B) / v(B)$ for the conditional credence
(Lean: \texttt{JointValuation.conditioning}, via Part~1's
\texttt{conditioning\_mk} for existence and
\texttt{conditioning\_unique} for uniqueness).

\subsection{Collapse Composition}

Composing a Cred valuation with Part~1's collapse homomorphism yields a
three-valued (Kleene) valuation.

\begin{proposition}[Collapse Preserves Connectives]
\label{prop:collapse-val}
Let $v$ be an independent valuation and $\kappa = \mathrm{collapse} \circ v$.
Then $\kappa$ preserves negation, conjunction, and disjunction:
\begin{align*}
\kappa(\sigma(A)) &= \mathrm{neg}(\kappa(A)), \\
\kappa(A \wedge B) &= \mathrm{conj}(\kappa(A), \kappa(B)), \\
\kappa(A \vee B) &= \mathrm{disj}(\kappa(A), \kappa(B)).
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_val\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_val\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_val\_disj}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each equation follows by rewriting with the valuation's compatibility
axiom and then applying Part~1's collapse homomorphism theorem
(\texttt{collapse\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_conj}, \texttt{collapse\_disj}).
\end{proof}

This is the mechanism by which Cred connects to K$_3$, LP, and RM3:
the collapse maps any Cred valuation to a Kleene valuation, and
the choice of designated values then determines the consequence relation.

The constant valuation $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ for all $A$, with

Its collapse to three values gives the constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$
three-valued valuation used in the K$_3$ no-tautology proof.
Lean: \texttt{CpValuation.constant\_half}.

\section{Consequence Relations}
\label{sec:consequence}
%=============================================================================

A \emph{consequence relation}~\cite{priest2008} specifies which inferences
are valid: premises $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ \emph{entail} conclusion $B$ when
every valuation that accepts the premises also accepts $B$.
In classical logic, consequence has \emph{explosion} (ex falso quodlibet):
a contradiction entails every proposition.
This section defines consequence relations for Cred and shows that
explosion fails non-vacuously for LP and graded consequence, while
K$_3$ has explosion only vacuously: for each three-valued
value~$v$, at least one of $v$ and $\mathrm{neg}(v)$ is not in
$\{1\}$ ($\mathrm{neg}(0) = 1$ but $0 \notin \{1\}$;
$\mathrm{neg}(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2} \notin \{1\}$;
$\mathrm{neg}(1) = 0 \notin \{1\}$), so the premise of explosion
is never satisfied.

\subsection{Designated Values}

The Kleene lattice $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ admits three standard
consequence relations, differing only in which values are
\emph{designated} (accepted as ``holding'').

\begin{definition}[K$_3$, LP Designation]
\label{def:designated}
K$_3$ designates $\{1\}$ (only certainty counts).
LP designates $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$ (certainty and the
intermediate value both count).
RM3 uses the same designation as LP ($\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) but
adds a relevance condition: its material conditional $A \to B$
receives value~$0$ when $A$ and $B$ share no propositional
variable~\cite{priest2008}.
Lean: \texttt{isDesignatedK3}, \texttt{isDesignatedLP}.
(RM3 designation is not formalized; the relevance condition is
syntactic.)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Three-Valued Consequence]
\label{def:three-val-consequence}
Premises $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ entail conclusion $B$ under designation
$D$ when every three-valued valuation $v$ satisfying
$v(A_i) \in D$ for all $i$ also satisfies $v(B) \in D$:
\[
A_1, \ldots, A_n \models_D B \iff
\forall v.\;(\forall i.\; D(v(A_i))) \Rightarrow D(v(B)).
\]
Lean: \texttt{threeValConsequence}.
As in the Lean formalization, $v$ ranges over all functions
$\alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, not just homomorphic
(truth-table-respecting) valuations.
\end{definition}

\subsection{K$_3$: Paracomplete}

A logic is \emph{paracomplete} if it lacks some classically valid
tautologies~\cite{priest2008}.
K$_3$ (Kleene's strong three-valued logic~\cite{kleene1952}) designates
only~$1$ and is maximally paracomplete: it has no tautologies at all.

\begin{proposition}[K$_3$ Has No Tautologies]
\label{prop:k3-no-taut}
Under K$_3$ consequence, no formula is a tautology:
$\not\models_{\mathrm{K}_3} A$ for any $A$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ valuation assigns every formula the value
$\tfrac{1}{2}$, which is not K$_3$-designated.
Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[K$_3$: Excluded Middle Fails]
\label{prop:k3-em}
There exists a value $v$ such that $v \disjc \mathrm{neg}(v)$ is not
K$_3$-designated.
It follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:k3-no-taut} that excluded middle
is not a K$_3$ tautology.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
At $v = \tfrac{1}{2}$: $\tfrac{1}{2} \disjc \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}$,
which is not in $\{1\}$.
Lean: \texttt{k3\_excluded\_middle\_fails}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{LP: Paraconsistent}

A logic is \emph{paraconsistent} if a contradiction does not entail
every proposition---i.e., explosion fails~\cite{priest2008}.
LP (the Logic of Paradox) designates $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$: both
certainty and the intermediate value count as ``holding.''

\begin{proposition}[LP: Excluded Middle Holds]
\label{prop:lp-em}
For every three-valued value $v$, $v \disjc \mathrm{neg}(v)$ is
LP-designated.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Case analysis: $0 \disjc 1 = 1$; $\tfrac{1}{2} \disjc \tfrac{1}{2} =
\tfrac{1}{2}$; $1 \disjc 0 = 1$.
All are in $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$.
Lean: \texttt{lp\_excluded\_middle}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[LP: Explosion Fails]
\label{prop:lp-no-explosion}
There exists a three-valued function $v$ and propositions $A$, $B$
such that both $v(A)$ and $\mathrm{neg}(v(A))$ are LP-designated but
$v(B)$ is not.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Set $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $v(B) = 0$.
Then $\mathrm{neg}(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, so both $v(A)$ and
$\mathrm{neg}(v(A))$ are LP-designated, but $v(B) = 0$ is not.
Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Graded Consequence}

On the full $[0,1]$, we can define consequence without collapsing to
three values.

\begin{definition}[Graded Consequence]
\label{def:graded-consequence}
For a threshold $t \in [0,1]$, \emph{graded consequence} holds when
every valuation making all premises $\geq t$ also makes the conclusion
$\geq t$:
\[
A_1, \ldots, A_n \models_t B \iff
\forall v.\; (\forall i.\; v(A_i) \geq t) \Rightarrow v(B) \geq t.
\]
Lean: \texttt{gradedConsequence}.
Here $v$ ranges over all functions $\alpha \to [0,1]$, not just
complement-preserving valuations.
This makes the consequence relation weaker (more functions must be
checked) but the no-explosion existential easier (the witness need
not be complement-preserving); see Theorem~\ref{thm:no-explosion}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Graded at $t=1$ Is Strict]
\label{prop:graded-strict}
Graded consequence at $t = 1$ holds iff every valuation making all
premises equal to~$1$ makes the conclusion equal to~$1$.
Lean: \texttt{graded\_at\_one\_is\_strict}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{No-Explosion Theorem}

The key structural result: Cred's unconstrained conditioning at zero
forces explosion to fail.

\begin{example}[Explosion Fails]
\label{ex:no-explosion}
In classical logic, if both ``it rained'' and ``it did not rain'' hold,
then every proposition follows---including ``the moon is made of cheese.''
In Cred at threshold $t = 0.3$: set $v(R) = 0.5$, so
$\negc v(R) = 0.5$; both exceed $0.3$.
But $v(\text{moon-cheese}) = 0$, which is below $0.3$.
The contradiction in $R$ does not force acceptance of an unrelated
proposition.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}[No Explosion]
\label{thm:no-explosion}
For any threshold $0 < t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$, graded consequence at~$t$
does not have explosion: there exist propositions $A$, $B$ and a
function $v$ with $v(A) \geq t$, $1 - v(A) \geq t$, and $v(B) < t$.
The condition is on the algebraic complement $1 - v(A)$, which is
strictly weaker than requiring $v(\sigma(A)) \geq t$ for a
complement-preserving valuation; since any complement-preserving
witness is also a valid unrestricted witness, the result implies
a~fortiori that explosion fails for complement-preserving valuations
as well.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Set $v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $v(B) = 0$.
Then $1 - v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$,
so both $v(A)$ and $1 - v(A)$ equal~$\tfrac{1}{2} \geq t$.
But $v(B) = 0 < t$.
Lean: \texttt{no\_explosion\_at\_half}, \texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Algebraic Basis]
The algebraic ingredient is Part~1's
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}: when evidence has credence~$0$, the
chain rule $c \cdot 0 = 0$ is satisfied by any $c \in [0,1]$
(re-exported as \texttt{unconstrained\_blocks\_explosion}).
The consequence-level Lean proofs (\texttt{lp\_no\_explosion},
\texttt{graded\_no\_explosion}) construct direct witness valuations
without invoking the conditioning machinery; the connection to
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any} is conceptual, not proof-level.
Informally, classical consequence (two-valued designation on
$\{0,1\}$) has explosion; it arises from a setting where conditioning
at zero is resolved by the measure, not left unconstrained.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Comparison}

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Designated} & \textbf{Tautologies?}
  & \textbf{Excl.\ middle?} & \textbf{Explosion?} \\
\midrule
Classical$^\|$ & $\{1\}$ (two-valued) & Yes & Yes & Yes \\
K$_3$ & $\{1\}$ & No & No & Holds vacuously$^\S$ \\
LP$^\|$ & $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$ & Yes & Yes & No \\
RM3$^{\dagger\|}$ & $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$+relev. & Yes & Yes & No \\
Graded ($t \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$) & $\{c \geq t\}$ & $^\ddagger$ & $^\ddagger$ & No \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}

\smallskip
\noindent
$^\|$\,Tautology and excluded-middle entries for Classical, LP, and
RM3 hold under the standard restriction to homomorphic
(truth-table-respecting) valuations.
Definition~\ref{def:three-val-consequence} quantifies over all
functions $\alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$; under that definition,
no formula is a tautology in any of these systems (the constant-$0$
function defeats every formula).
The K$_3$ no-tautology result and the LP/graded no-explosion
results are proved under the all-functions definition.\\
$^\S$\,K$_3$ has explosion vacuously: no valuation makes both $v(A)$
and $\mathrm{neg}(v(A))$ K$_3$-designated, so the premise of explosion
is never fulfilled.\\
$^\dagger$\,RM3 properties are well-known~\cite{priest2008}; they are not
formalized in Lean.\\
$^\ddagger$\,Tautologies and excluded middle for graded consequence depend
on the threshold and are not formalized.
At $t = 0$, trivially every formula is a tautology (all credences
are $\geq 0$ by definition); at $t > 0$, the constant-$0$ valuation
assigns every formula value~$0 < t$, so no formula is a tautology.
At the algebraic level, excluded middle holds whenever
$t \leq \tfrac{3}{4}$ for complement-preserving valuations
(Part~1, \texttt{certainty\_ge\_three\_quarters}; not connected to
graded consequence at the Lean level): expanding the De~Morgan
definition, $v(A) \disjc \negc v(A) = 1 - v(A)(1 - v(A)) \geq
\tfrac{3}{4}$ for all $v(A) \in [0,1]$, since
$v(A)(1 - v(A)) \leq \tfrac{1}{4}$.
This does not extend to unrestricted graded consequence, where $v$
need not be complement-preserving.

\section{Update Rules}
\label{sec:update}
%=============================================================================

The chain rule $\condc{A}{B} \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$ constrains
how three credences relate at a single moment.
An \emph{update rule} applies this constraint dynamically, prescribing how
an entire valuation changes when new evidence arrives.
In probability, Bayesian conditionalization computes the posterior by
dividing the joint by the evidence; Jeffrey
conditionalization~\cite{jeffrey1965} generalizes this to uncertain
evidence via a mixture formula.
Both are instances of chain-rule-based update.

\subsection{Bayesian Conditionalization}

\begin{definition}[Bayesian Update]
\label{def:bayesian-update}
Given a joint-parametrized valuation $v$ and evidence $B$ with
$v(B) > 0$, the \emph{Bayesian posterior} is
\[
v'(A) = \frac{j(A, B)}{v(B)}
\]
where $j(A, B)$ is the joint credence.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate} (single proposition),
\texttt{bayesianUpdateValuation} (returns a function
$\alpha \to \mathrm{Credence}$, not necessarily complement-preserving).
\end{definition}

The Bayesian update is the conditioning structure from Part~1 applied
propositionwise: $v'(A) \cdot v(B) = j(A, B)$ at every~$A$.
The posterior is well-defined ($j(A,B)/v(B) \in [0,1]$) because
$j(A,B) \leq v(B)$ by the marginal bound on the second argument.
Note that $v'$ need not be a complement-preserving valuation.

\begin{example}[Rain and Wet Street: Update]
\label{ex:rain-update}
From Example~\ref{ex:rain-joint}: $v(R) = 0.7$, $v(W) = 0.9$,
$j(W, R) = 0.65$.
You observe that it rained ($B = R$).
The Bayesian posterior for $W$ is
$v'(W) = j(W,R) / v(R) = 0.65 / 0.7 \approx 0.93$:
your credence in a wet street rises from $0.9$ to $0.93$.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Chain-Rule Preservation]
\label{prop:chain-rule-preservation}
The Bayesian posterior satisfies the chain rule by construction:
$v'(A) \cdot v(B) = j(A, B)$.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule},
\texttt{bayesianUpdateValuation\_chain\_rule}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Immediate from the definition via \texttt{conditioning\_mk}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Uniqueness]
\label{prop:bayesian-unique}
The Bayesian update is the unique credence satisfying the chain rule
with the given evidence and joint.
Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique} (via Part~1's
\texttt{conditioning\_unique}).
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Jeffrey Conditionalization}

Jeffrey~\cite{jeffrey1965} generalized Bayesian conditionalization to
uncertain evidence.
Instead of learning $B$ with certainty, one learns that $B$ has a new
credence $q$:
\[
v'(A) = v(A \mid B) \cdot q + v(A \mid \negc B) \cdot (1 - q).
\]
This is a weighted mixture of the conditional-on-$B$ and
conditional-on-$\negc B$ valuations.
Determining $v(A \mid B)$ and $v(A \mid \negc B)$ from a joint-parametrized
valuation requires $v(B) > 0$ and $v(\negc B) > 0$, respectively.

\begin{definition}[Jeffrey Update]
\label{def:jeffrey-update}
Given conditional credences $c_B = v(A \mid B)$ and
$c_{\negc B} = v(A \mid \negc B)$ in $[0,1]$, and a new evidence
credence $q \in [0,1]$, the Jeffrey update is
\[
v'(A) = c_B \cdot q + c_{\negc B} \cdot (1 - q).
\]
Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Jeffrey Bounds]
\label{prop:jeffrey-bounds}
The Jeffrey update satisfies $0 \leq v'(A) \leq 1$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Since $c_B, c_{\negc B} \in [0,1]$ and $q, (1-q) \geq 0$ with
$q + (1-q) = 1$, the expression $c_B \cdot q + c_{\negc B} \cdot (1-q)$
is a convex combination and thus in $[0,1]$.
Lean: verified by the type constraints of \texttt{jeffreyUpdate}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Jeffrey at Extreme Evidence]
\label{prop:jeffrey-bayesian}
At $q = 1$, the Jeffrey update returns~$c_B$.
When $c_B = v(A \mid B)$, this is the Bayesian posterior.
At $q = 0$, it returns~$c_{\negc B}$.
Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_certain}, \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_zero}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Zero-Evidence Underdetermination}

When $v(B) = 0$, the chain rule $\condc{A}{B} \cdot 0 = 0$ is satisfied
by any conditional (Part~1, \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
The conditional $v(A \mid B)$ is therefore underdetermined, and since both
Bayesian and Jeffrey update take this conditional as input, neither
produces a unique posterior at zero evidence.

\begin{proposition}[Underdetermination]
\label{prop:underdetermination}
For any credence $c$, there exists a conditioning structure with
evidence~$0$ and conditional~$c$.
That is, the conditional input to Bayesian and Jeffrey update is
unconstrained when evidence is zero.
Lean: \texttt{bayesian\_underdetermined\_at\_zero},
\texttt{jeffrey\_underdetermined\_at\_zero} (both re-export
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
\end{proposition}

This connects Cred to \emph{imprecise probability}~\cite{walley1991}:
when conditioning at zero is underdetermined, the honest response
is to report the full interval $[0,1]$ as the set of compatible
posteriors, rather than selecting a single value.
Levi~\cite{levi1980} argued that such indeterminacy is a genuine
epistemic state, not a failure of the formalism.

\subsection{Comparison}

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Update rule} & \textbf{Evidence type} &
  \textbf{Requirements beyond Cred} & \textbf{Zero evidence?} \\
\midrule
Bayesian & $B$ certain & Full joints, $v(B) > 0$ & Underdetermined \\
Jeffrey & $B$ has credence $q$ & Full joints, $v(B) > 0$, $v(\negc B) > 0^\star$ & Underdetermined \\
Disintegration & $B$ certain & Measure space & Resolved \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}

\smallskip
\noindent
$^\star$\,$v(\negc B) > 0$ is needed to determine the conditional input
$v(A \mid \negc B)$, not for the Jeffrey formula itself.

\smallskip
\noindent
The chain rule constrains what updates are coherent; it does not select one.
Each update rule requires additional structure beyond the algebra.

\section{Graded Predicates and Quantifiers}
\label{sec:predicates}
%=============================================================================

The extension from propositional to first-order requires predicates
varying over a domain and quantifiers aggregating over that domain.
A graded predicate---a function $P : X \to [0,1]$---generalizes
Zadeh's fuzzy set membership~\cite{zadeh1965}: $P(x)$ is the degree
to which $x$ has property~$P$.

\subsection{Graded Predicates}

\begin{definition}[Graded Predicate]
\label{def:graded-predicate}
A \emph{graded predicate} over a domain $X$ is a function
$P : X \to [0,1]$.
The value $P(x)$ is the degree to which $x$ satisfies~$P$.
Lean: \texttt{GradedPredicate}.
\end{definition}

Crisp predicates (range $\subseteq \{0, 1\}$) are the special case
corresponding to classical sets.

The Cred operations extend pointwise (expanding the De~Morgan
definition for disjunction):
\begin{align*}
(\negc P)(x) &= 1 - P(x) &\text{(complement)} \\
(P \conjc Q)(x) &= P(x) \cdot Q(x) &\text{(conjunction)} \\
(P \disjc Q)(x) &= P(x) + Q(x) - P(x) \cdot Q(x) &\text{(disjunction)}
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{GradedPredicate.neg}, \texttt{GradedPredicate.conj},
\texttt{GradedPredicate.disj}.

All Part~1 algebraic laws hold pointwise:

\begin{proposition}[Pointwise Laws]
\label{prop:pointwise}
For graded predicates $P$, $Q$, $R$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item $\negc\negc P = P$ (double negation),
\item $P \conjc Q = Q \conjc P$ and $P \disjc Q = Q \disjc P$ (commutativity),
\item $(P \conjc Q) \conjc R = P \conjc (Q \conjc R)$ (associativity),
\item $\negc(P \conjc Q) = \negc P \disjc \negc Q$ and
      $\negc(P \disjc Q) = \negc P \conjc \negc Q$ (De~Morgan).
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{GradedPredicate.neg\_neg}, \texttt{GradedPredicate.conj\_comm},
\texttt{GradedPredicate.conj\_assoc}, \texttt{GradedPredicate.disj\_comm},
\texttt{GradedPredicate.disj\_assoc},
\texttt{GradedPredicate.de\_morgan\_conj},
\texttt{GradedPredicate.de\_morgan\_disj}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each law follows from the corresponding Part~1 law applied at every point.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Quantifiers}

\begin{definition}[Quantifiers]
\label{def:quantifiers}
For a graded predicate $P : X \to [0,1]$ over a nonempty domain~$X$:
\begin{align*}
\cred(\forall x.\; P(x)) &= \inf_{x \in X} P(x), \\
\cred(\exists x.\; P(x)) &= \sup_{x \in X} P(x).
\end{align*}
Lean: \texttt{credInf} / \texttt{forall'}, \texttt{credSup} / \texttt{exists'}.
\end{definition}

The infimum and supremum are well-defined because credence values are
bounded in $[0,1]$.
The universal quantifier is bounded above by any instance
(Lean: \texttt{forall\_le}); the existential quantifier is bounded below
by any instance (Lean: \texttt{le\_exists}).

\begin{theorem}[Quantifier Duality]
\label{thm:quantifier-duality}
\[
1 - \inf_x P(x) = \sup_x (1 - P(x)).
\]
Informally: $\negc(\forall x.\; P(x)) = \exists x.\; \negc P(x)$.
Lean: \texttt{quantifier\_duality\_val} (formalizes the real-valued
identity; the credence-level reading follows by the definitions of
\texttt{credInf} and \texttt{credSup}).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
For the $\geq$ direction: for each $x$, $\inf P \leq P(x)$, so
$1 - P(x) \leq 1 - \inf P$.
Taking the supremum gives $\sup(1 - P) \leq 1 - \inf P$.
For the $\leq$ direction: for each $x$, $1 - P(x) \leq \sup(1 - P)$,
so $P(x) \geq 1 - \sup(1 - P)$ for all $x$,
hence $\inf P \geq 1 - \sup(1 - P)$, i.e., $1 - \inf P \leq \sup(1 - P)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Classical Agreement}

\begin{proposition}[Classical Agreement]
\label{prop:classical-agreement}
For crisp predicates (range $\subseteq \{0, 1\}$):
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item $\inf_x P(x) = 0$ iff some $x$ has $P(x) = 0$
  (Lean: \texttt{crisp\_inf\_zero\_iff}),
\item $\sup_x P(x) = 1$ iff some $x$ has $P(x) = 1$
  (Lean: \texttt{crisp\_sup\_one\_iff}).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
For (i): if $P(x) = 0$ for some $x$, then $\inf P \leq P(x) = 0$,
and $\inf P \geq 0$ since all credences are non-negative.
Conversely, if $\inf P = 0$ but $P(x) \neq 0$ for all $x$, then
$P(x) = 1$ for all $x$ (by crispness); since the domain is nonempty,
$\inf P = 1$, contradiction.
Symmetric for (ii).
\end{proof}

These are the ``boundary witness'' cases of classical agreement.
The complementary cases ($\inf P = 1$ iff $P(x) = 1$ for all $x$;
$\sup P = 0$ iff $P(x) = 0$ for all $x$) hold by similar arguments
but are not formalized.
Together, they show that for crisp predicates, $\inf = \forall$ and
$\sup = \exists$ in the classical sense.

\subsection{Unrestricted Comprehension}

\begin{theorem}[Negation Fixed-Point Resolution]
\label{thm:russell}
If a credence $c$ satisfies $c = \negc c$, then $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The equation $c = 1 - c$ has a unique solution at $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Part~1, \texttt{neg\_fixed\_point\_unique}).
Lean: \texttt{russell\_fixed\_point}.
\end{proof}

In particular, any self-referential predicate $R$ satisfying
$R(R) = \negc R(R)$ would have $R(R) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Russell's predicate $R = \{x : x \notin x\}$ applied to itself gives
$R(R) = \negc R(R) = \tfrac{1}{2}$: the set half-contains itself.
The algebraic core---$c = \negc c$ implies $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$---is
formalized in Lean; the self-application mechanism (unrestricted
comprehension producing the equation $R(R) = \negc R(R)$) requires
set-theoretic infrastructure not formalized here.
This connects Cred to paraconsistent set theory~\cite{brady2006,weber2010},
where comprehension is retained and contradictions are tolerated.
In Cred, the ``contradiction'' has a precise numerical value:
$\tfrac{1}{2}$, the unique negation fixed point.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=============================================================================

\textbf{Product Fuzzy Logic and Valuations.}
Goguen~\cite{goguen1969} introduced the product t-norm as a conjunction
for inexact concepts.
H\'ajek~\cite{hajek1998} developed product logic ($\Pi$) with the product
t-norm and residuated implication; a product fuzzy valuation is a
homomorphism from the propositional algebra to this t-norm algebra.
Klement, Mesiar, and Pap~\cite{klement2000} gave a comprehensive treatment
of triangular norms; the product t-norm, probabilistic sum, and standard
complement form the product De Morgan triplet.
Esteva et al.~\cite{esteva2000} showed that extending product logic with
an involutive negation yields exactly this triplet.
Independent Cred valuations (Section~\ref{sec:valuations}) are product fuzzy
valuations; joint-parametrized valuations generalize them by supplying the
joint as external data rather than deriving it from the t-norm.

\textbf{Primitive Conditioning and Update.}
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955} axiomatized conditional probability as primitive,
with the chain rule as a defining constraint rather than a ratio definition.
Popper~\cite{popper1959} independently argued that conditional probability
is more fundamental than absolute probability.
The update rules in Section~\ref{sec:update} apply the R\'enyi-Popper
chain-rule constraint dynamically: Bayesian update solves the chain rule for
the posterior, Jeffrey update mixes two such solutions for uncertain evidence.

\textbf{Conditional Events and Consequence.}
De Finetti~\cite{definetti1936} introduced the three-valued conditional:
true when $A \land B$, false when $\lnot A \land B$, void when $\lnot B$.
\'Egr\'e, Rossi, and Sprenger~\cite{egre2021} showed that de Finetti's
conditional combined with Kleene's strong connectives produces logics in
which explosion fails at the semantic level---via the truth table itself,
not just the consequence relation.
The no-explosion theorems in Section~\ref{sec:consequence} are the graded
counterpart: explosion fails for every consequence relation compatible with
unconstrained conditioning at zero.

\textbf{Three-Valued Logics and the Collapse.}
Kleene~\cite{kleene1952} introduced strong three-valued truth tables for
$\{\neg, \land, \lor\}$---the algebra underlying K$_3$, LP, and RM3.
Composing a Cred valuation with the collapse homomorphism yields a Kleene
valuation; the choice of designated values then selects K$_3$, LP, or RM3
consequence (Section~\ref{sec:consequence}).

\textbf{Adams' Probability Logic.}
Adams~\cite{adams1965,adams1998} showed that conditional probability
propagates uncertainty through valid classical inferences: if all premises
have probability $\geq 1 - \varepsilon$, then the conclusion has
probability $\geq 1 - n\varepsilon$ (for $n$ premises).
Whether analogous bounds hold for Cred valuations without additivity
is an open problem (see Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\textbf{Jeffrey Conditionalization.}
Jeffrey~\cite{jeffrey1965,jeffrey1983} generalized Bayesian updating to
uncertain evidence via a mixture formula.
Section~\ref{sec:update} implements Jeffrey update on Cred valuations:
the mixture $v'(A) = v(A|B) \cdot q + v(A|\negc B) \cdot (1-q)$ is
well-defined when both $v(B) > 0$ and $v(\negc B) > 0$.

\textbf{Imprecise Probability.}
Walley~\cite{walley1991} developed statistical reasoning with sets of
probability measures rather than a single one.
Levi~\cite{levi1980} argued that indeterminate credences are genuine
epistemic states.
When conditioning at zero, the set of compatible posteriors is the full
interval $[0,1]$---exactly an imprecise probability interval---so
zero-evidence underdetermination in the update layer connects directly to
imprecise probability.

\textbf{Paraconsistent Set Theory and Graded Predicates.}
Brady~\cite{brady2006} and Weber~\cite{weber2010} developed set theories
with unrestricted comprehension, tolerating the Russell paradox via
paraconsistency.
Graded predicates (Section~\ref{sec:predicates}) offer a complementary
approach: the Russell predicate has a determinate numerical value
($\tfrac{1}{2}$) rather than a contradiction.

\textbf{Relevant Logic.}
Anderson and Belnap~\cite{anderson1975} developed relevant logics that
reject ex falso via syntactic relevance conditions.
The collapse composition (Section~\ref{sec:valuations}) provides a
semantic route to the same logics: Cred valuations, collapsed to three
values, yield K$_3$/LP/RM3 valuations depending on designated values.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

Part~2 adds three layers to Cred's algebra, yielding a system that can
interpret propositions, determine valid inferences, and revise beliefs.
These layers instantiate known frameworks---product fuzzy
valuations~\cite{hajek1998,esteva2000}, K$_3$/LP
consequence~\cite{kleene1952}, Bayesian/Jeffrey
update~\cite{jeffrey1965}---within the Cred algebraic substrate
established in Part~1.

\emph{Valuations} connect the propositional world to the algebraic world.
Independent valuations make the product t-norm do double duty as both
algebraic and semantic conjunction, at the cost of idempotence failure.
Joint-parametrized valuations supply the joint externally, recovering
probability-like behavior without requiring a measure.
Collapse composition maps any Cred valuation to a Kleene valuation.

\emph{Consequence relations} come in two families: designated-value
consequence (K$_3$, LP, RM3) via the collapse, and graded consequence
parametrized by a threshold.
Explosion fails non-vacuously for LP and graded consequence;
K$_3$ has explosion only vacuously (no contradiction is
K$_3$-designatable).
Unconstrained conditioning at zero is the algebraic property that
distinguishes Cred from systems with ex falso.

\emph{Update rules} apply the chain rule dynamically.
Bayesian and Jeffrey conditionalization are both available when evidence
has positive credence; at zero evidence, both are underdetermined,
connecting Cred to imprecise probability.

\emph{Graded predicates} extend to first order with inf/sup quantifiers
satisfying duality and agreeing with classical quantifiers on crisp
predicates.
Unrestricted comprehension yields fixed points ($\tfrac{1}{2}$) rather
than paradoxes.

\medskip
\textbf{What Part~2 provides and what it does not.}
Part~2 provides interpretation, consequence, and update.
It does not provide a proof theory (deductive calculus with rules of
inference) or self-hosting (building mathematics on Cred foundations).
These remain for Part~3.

\medskip
\textbf{Future work.}
Proof theory for Cred, Adams-type uncertainty bounds without additivity,
graded mathematics, self-hosting/metareasoning, and connections to
probabilistic programming.
