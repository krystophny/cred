\begin{abstract}
Probability theory and many-valued logic share the same core algebra
on $[0,1]$: the product De Morgan triplet (product conjunction,
probabilistic-sum disjunction, standard complement).
They diverge on the conditional.
Probability divides and is undefined at zero; product residuation
forces ex falso at zero; chain-rule conditioning
($\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$)
leaves the conditional unconstrained at zero.
This paper develops chain-rule conditioning as a bridge
between the two traditions.
We show that the triplet collapses to the Kleene lattice
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ via a unique three-element quotient,
and establish a formula-level bridge: LP consequence on the
lattice is equivalent to positivity consequence on $[0,1]$,
and K$_3$ consequence to certainty consequence.
The bridge does \emph{not} extend to conditioning: no
truth-functional function on the lattice can reproduce the
min-copula conditional --- a constructive Lewis triviality result
witnessed by two explicit pairs.
We characterize the boundary conditions under which the bridge
does hold, show that Bayesian update inherits it, and prove
a zero-evidence trichotomy tying underdetermination, paraconsistency,
and bridge failure to the single algebraic fact $c \cdot 0 = 0$.
All results are machine-checked in Lean~4 with Mathlib
(3581 lines across seven modules, zero \texttt{sorry}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

You believe it will rain with credence $0.8$.
You see the street is wet with credence $0.9$.
What is your conditional credence in rain given wet streets?

A probabilist computes $\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})
= \cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet}) / \cred(\text{wet})$.
Under maximal positive dependence (where the joint is
$\min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$), this gives $0.8 / 0.9 = 8/9$.
The computation requires division, and at $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0$
the conditional is undefined.

A many-valued logician working in H\'ajek's Product
Logic~\cite{hajek1998} uses the residuated implication:
$a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$.
When $a > 0$ and the joint is the minimum, this yields the same
$\min(b/a,\, 1)$; but at $a = 0$, the supremum is $1$
--- ex falso quodlibet is built into every residuated
implication~\cite{hajek1998,esteva2001,cintula2011}.

Both traditions share the same core algebra on $[0,1]$: the
\emph{product De Morgan triplet}~\cite{klement2000}.
Product conjunction ($c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$),
probabilistic-sum disjunction
($c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$), and standard complement
($\negc c = 1 - c$) are common to both.
They differ \emph{only} in the conditional: probability uses division
(undefined at zero), Product Logic uses residuation (ex falso at zero).

Chain-rule conditioning offers a third answer.
The chain rule
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is the defining constraint.
When $\cred(B) > 0$, the conditional is uniquely determined:
$\condc{A}{B} = \cred(A \land B) / \cred(B)$ --- the same
as Bayesian conditioning.
When $\cred(B) = 0$, the equation $c \cdot 0 = 0$ holds for
\emph{every} $c \in [0,1]$: the conditional is unconstrained.
Impossible evidence provides no constraint.
This echoes R\'enyi's~\cite{renyi1955} and
Popper's~\cite{popper1959} primitive-conditioning approach to
probability, and de Finetti's~\cite{definetti1936} three-valued
conditional (where conditioning on a false antecedent is \emph{void}
rather than vacuously true).
Chain-rule conditioning achieves a similar effect without adding
a connexive axiom~\cite{egre2021}: it simply leaves
$\cred(A \mid 0)$ unconstrained.

The resulting structure sits at a crossroads.
On one side, it connects to Bayesian probability: at positive
evidence, chain-rule conditioning \emph{is} Bayes's rule.
On the other, it collapses to the Kleene
lattice~\cite{kleene1952} $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ via a surjective
homomorphism, recovering the shared truth tables of
K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952},
LP~\cite{asenjo1966,priest1979},
and RM3~\cite{anderson1975,meyer1972}.
The ``bridge'' has two senses: the collapse homomorphism connects
three-valued logic to $[0,1]$; chain-rule conditioning connects
Bayesian reasoning to the same algebra.
This paper studies both senses and their interaction.

\textbf{Main results.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Formula-level bridge} (Section~\ref{sec:bridge};
  new, machine-verified):
  the collapse commutes with propositional evaluation;
  LP consequence on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is equivalent to
  positivity consequence on $[0,1]$, and K$_3$ to certainty
  consequence.
\item \textbf{Algebraic fixed points and Kleene collapse}
  (Section~\ref{sec:triplet}; known~\cite{kleene1952,hajek1998,gottwald2001},
  newly verified):
  the product De Morgan triplet collapses to the Kleene lattice;
  \textbf{uniqueness of the three-element quotient} (new).
\item \textbf{Congruence classification}
  (Section~\ref{sec:congruence}, Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}; new):
  under $[0,1]$-multiplication, the Kleene lattice is the unique
  nontrivial three-element quotient and boundary classes are
  singletons; under $\Real$-multiplication, no nontrivial
  finite quotient exists at all.
\item \textbf{Conditional bridge impossibility}
  (Section~\ref{sec:cond-bridge}; new):
  no truth-functional function on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
  can reproduce the min-copula conditional under collapse
  --- a constructive Lewis triviality, witnessed by two
  explicit pairs.
\item \textbf{Update bridge and boundary characterization}
  (Section~\ref{sec:update}; new):
  the conditional bridge holds at boundaries
  (evidence certain, conclusion at a boundary, or conclusion at
  least as credible as evidence);
  Bayesian update inherits the bridge under these conditions.
\item \textbf{Zero-evidence trichotomy}
  (Section~\ref{sec:update}; new):
  the identity $c \cdot 0 = 0$ simultaneously forces
  underdetermined posteriors, LP explosion failure, and
  conditional bridge failure.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Relationship to Product Logic.}
The closest relative of the product De Morgan triplet in the
fuzzy-logic literature is H\'ajek's Product Logic
($\Pi$-logic)~\cite{hajek1998}, further developed by
Esteva and Godo~\cite{esteva2000,esteva2001} and surveyed
in~\cite{cintula2011}.
Product Logic uses the same t-norm but a different companion
operation: residuation ($a \to b = \sup\{c : a \cdot c \leq b\}$)
instead of the involutive complement.
Residuation links conjunction and implication so tightly that the
product algebra on $[0,1]$ is \emph{simple} (no nontrivial
congruences)~\cite{agliano2003}.
Replacing residuation with the involutive complement loosens this
constraint: the Kleene partition survives precisely because the
De Morgan reduct no longer forces $0 \to b = 1$.
The two systems agree on all positive-evidence conditioning;
they differ only at evidence zero.

The individual components --- the product t-norm, the Kleene
lattice, primitive conditioning, many-valued consequence --- are
all well studied in the literature; the novelty lies in their
synthesis and formalization.
Section~\ref{sec:related} surveys related work in detail.

\section{The Product De Morgan Triplet}
\label{sec:triplet}
%=============================================================================

This section defines the concrete structure: the product De Morgan
triplet on $[0,1]$~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998,esteva2000}.
This is not an axiomatic class of algebras: all properties follow
from arithmetic on $[0,1]$.
Goguen~\cite{goguen1969} used the product t-norm as the conjunction
in his logic of inexact concepts;
the triple $(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ is standard in fuzzy
logic~\cite{bergmann2008,cintula2011}.

\subsection{Primitive Operations}

\begin{definition}[Credence]
\label{def:credence}
A \emph{credence} is a value $c \in [0,1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Running Example: Rain and Wet Streets]
\label{ex:rain}
Throughout this paper:
$\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$ and $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Negation, Conjunction, Disjunction]
\label{def:operations}
The \emph{negation} is $\negc c = 1 - c$.
The \emph{conjunction} is $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$
(the product t-norm).
The \emph{disjunction} is the De Morgan dual:
$c_1 \disjc c_2 = \negc(\negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2)
  = c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$
(the probabilistic sum).
The triple $(\conjc, \disjc, \negc)$ is the
\emph{product De Morgan triplet}~\cite{klement2000,hajek1998}.
\end{definition}

In the running example:
$\negc \cred(\text{rain}) = 0.2$,
$\cred(\text{rain}) \conjc \cred(\text{wet})
= 0.8 \cdot 0.9 = 0.72$,
$\cred(\text{rain}) \disjc \cred(\text{wet})
= 0.8 + 0.9 - 0.72 = 0.98$.

\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Properties]
\label{prop:algebraic}
For all $c, c_1, c_2, c_3 \in [0,1]$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),nosep]
\item $\negc\negc c = c$ \hfill (involution)
\item $\negc 0 = 1$, $\negc 1 = 0$ \hfill (boundary)
\item $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_2 \conjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \conjc c_2) \conjc c_3 = c_1 \conjc (c_2 \conjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \conjc 1 = c$, $c \conjc 0 = 0$ \hfill (identity,
  annihilator)
\item $c_1 \disjc c_2 = c_2 \disjc c_1$,
  $(c_1 \disjc c_2) \disjc c_3 = c_1 \disjc (c_2 \disjc c_3)$
  \hfill (comm., assoc.)
\item $c \disjc 0 = c$, $c \disjc 1 = 1$ \hfill (identity,
  annihilator)
\item $\negc(c_1 \conjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \disjc \negc c_2$,
  $\negc(c_1 \disjc c_2) = \negc c_1 \conjc \negc c_2$
  \hfill (De Morgan)
\end{enumerate}
Lean: \texttt{neg\_neg}, \texttt{conj\_comm},
\texttt{conj\_assoc}, \texttt{conj\_one}, \texttt{conj\_zero},
\texttt{disj\_comm}, \texttt{disj\_assoc}, \texttt{disj\_zero},
\texttt{disj\_one}, \texttt{de\_morgan\_conj},
\texttt{de\_morgan\_disj}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Arithmetic on $[0,1]$.
\end{proof}

The structure consists of two dual commutative monoids connected by
De Morgan duality.
It is not a De Morgan algebra because $\conjc$ is not idempotent
($c^2 = c$ iff $c \in \{0,1\}$;
Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff})
and $\conjc$ does not distribute over $\disjc$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_disj\_not\_distrib}).

\subsection{Why the Product?}

The three basic continuous t-norms are G\"odel ($\min$), product
($\cdot$), and \L ukasiewicz ($\max(a+b-1, 0)$).
Why prefer the product?

\emph{G\"odel} ($\min$) is idempotent: $\min(c,c) = c$.
This makes it the weakest t-norm and gives it no ``dynamics'' ---
repeated conjunction has no effect.
As a model of belief combination, this is unrealistically static:
combining two independent pieces of evidence should compound their
uncertainty.

\emph{\L ukasiewicz} ($\max(a+b-1, 0)$) destroys the
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ fixed point of negation:
$\max(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} - 1, 0) = 0 \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lean: \texttt{luk\_not\_idempotent}).
In a system where $\tfrac{1}{2}$ represents maximal uncertainty
and equals its own negation, the conjunction of maximal uncertainty
with itself should remain uncertain, not collapse to impossibility.

\emph{Product} ($\cdot$) occupies the middle ground.
It is not idempotent ($c^2 \neq c$ for $c \in (0,1)$), so
conjunction has dynamic content.
It preserves the interior:
$0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$ implies $0 < c_1 c_2 < 1$,
so uncertain credences remain uncertain after conjunction.
And it is the only basic continuous t-norm whose De Morgan
dual ($c_1 + c_2 - c_1 c_2$) is the probabilistic sum ---
the standard formula for the probability of a disjunction
under independence~\cite{klement2000}.

\begin{remark}[Product vs.\ Joint]
\label{rem:product-vs-joint}
The product $c_1 \conjc c_2$ is an algebraic operation on
credence values, not a claim about the joint credence
$\cred(A \land B)$ of two propositions.
The joint depends on the dependence structure and lies in the
Fr\'echet--Hoeffding interval
$[\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0),\; \min(\cred(A), \cred(B))]$:
independence gives $\cred(A) \cdot \cred(B)$;
maximum positive dependence gives $\min(\cred(A), \cred(B))$;
maximum negative dependence gives
$\max(\cred(A) + \cred(B) - 1, 0)$~\cite{nelsen2006}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Relationship to Probability}

Every probability space satisfies the chain rule and complement
axioms.
Probability adds a sigma-algebra, a countably additive measure,
and sigma-additivity~\cite{jaynes2003,cox1961}.
The product De Morgan triplet drops all of these: it works on
bare values in $[0,1]$ with no measure-theoretic infrastructure.
At evidence zero, probability is undefined;
chain-rule conditioning is unconstrained.
The triplet is thus \emph{weaker} than probability (fewer
constraints) but \emph{compatible} with it (every probability
assignment satisfies the triplet axioms).

\subsection{Conjunction Selection: The Min Copula}
\label{sec:min-copula}

The product $c_1 \conjc c_2 = c_1 \cdot c_2$ is the algebraic
conjunction on credences, but it does \emph{not} in general
determine the joint credence $\cred(A \land B)$ from
marginals.
Which truth-functional joint should we use when computing
conditionals?

We impose four desiderata:
truth-functionality ($j(a,b)$ depends on $a$ and $b$ alone),
Bayes consistency (the joint is symmetric: $j(a,b) = j(b,a)$,
so the joint credence is the same regardless of conditioning
direction),
non-trivial conditioning ($j(a,b)/b \neq a$ in general),
and idempotence ($j(a,a) = a$).
These four are not independent: truth-functionality and Bayes
consistency are subsumed by the copula axioms (any copula is
truth-functional and symmetric by definition), and non-triviality
follows from idempotence ($\min(a,b)/b \neq a$ whenever $a < b$).
The operative conditions are symmetry and idempotence alone.

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the Min Copula]
\label{thm:min-copula-unique}
Among symmetric copulas on $[0,1]$, the minimum
$C(a,b) = \min(a,b)$ is the unique idempotent one.
Idempotence, together with the copula axioms (groundedness, unit
marginals, 2-increasing), forces $\min$.

Lean: \texttt{copula\_idempotent\_unique},
\texttt{min\_copula\_unique}.
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.
\end{theorem}

The product copula $C(a,b) = a \cdot b$ (independence) fails
idempotence and gives trivial conditioning
($a \cdot b / b = a$; Lean: \texttt{prod\_trivial\_conditioning});
the \L ukasiewicz copula $C(a,b) = \max(a+b-1, 0)$ also fails
idempotence (Lean: \texttt{luk\_not\_idempotent}).
A comparison table appears in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}
(Table~\ref{tab:joint-comparison}).

\section{Chain-Rule Conditioning}
\label{sec:conditioning}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet provides the connectives
($\negc$, $\conjc$, $\disjc$).
Conditioning is a separate structure, governed by the chain rule.

\subsection{The Chain Rule}

\begin{definition}[Conditioning Structure]
\label{def:conditioning}
A \emph{conditioning structure} for $(j, e) \in [0,1]^2$
(joint $j$, evidence $e$) consists of $c \in [0,1]$ satisfying
$c \conjc e = j$.
In propositional notation:
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.
\end{definition}

The joint is supplied as input, not computed from marginals.
H\'ajek, Godo, and Esteva~\cite{hajek1995} used the chain-rule
constraint as a technical device within fuzzy logic;
here it is the defining constraint.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed consistent
plausible reasoning must be isomorphic to probability;
chain-rule conditioning relaxes Cox's requirement that the
conditional be a function of the arguments.

In the running example, with the min-copula joint
$\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet}) = \min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$:
$\condc{\text{rain}}{\text{wet}} \cdot 0.9 = 0.8$,
so $\condc{\text{rain}}{\text{wet}} = 8/9 \approx 0.889$.
Rain is slightly more credible given wet streets than it was a priori.

\subsection{Existence and Uniqueness}

\begin{proposition}[Existence and Uniqueness of Conditioning]
\label{prop:conditioning}
For $e > 0$: a conditioning structure exists iff
$j \leq e$, and is then unique: $c = j / e$.
For $e = 0$: the constraint forces
$j = 0$ but leaves $c$ unconstrained
--- every $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies $c \cdot 0 = 0$.

Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_mk},
\texttt{conditioning\_unique},
\texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Arithmetic on $[0,1]$: division when the denominator is positive;
the annihilator property $c \cdot 0 = 0$ when it is not.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Path Dependence at Evidence Zero}

The conditional $\min(a,b)/b$ for $b > 0$ is well-defined, but
its limit as $b \to 0$ depends on \emph{how} $a$ and $b$ approach
zero together.

\begin{proposition}[Path Dependence]
\label{prop:path-dep}
Under the min-copula joint, $\lim_{b \to 0} \min(a,b)/b$ is
path-dependent:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Fixed $a > 0$, $b \to 0$: limit $= 1$.
\item $a = \lambda b$, $\lambda \in (0,1]$: limit $= \lambda$.
\item $a = b^\alpha$, $\alpha > 1$: limit $= 0$.
\end{itemize}
Every value in $[0,1]$ is achieved.
The singularity at the origin is essential, not removable.

Lean: \texttt{path\_dep\_fixed\_a},
\texttt{path\_dep\_proportional},
\texttt{path\_dep\_square},
\texttt{path\_any\_positive\_value}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Bayesian Update}

\begin{definition}[Bayesian Update]
\label{def:bayesian}
Given a joint credence $\cred(A \land B)$ and evidence
$\cred(B) > 0$, the Bayesian posterior is
$\cred'(A) = \cred(A \land B) / \cred(B)$.
This satisfies the chain rule by construction:
$\cred'(A) \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$.

Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule},
\texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique}.
\end{definition}

In the running example, the Bayesian posterior under the min copula:
$\cred'(\text{rain}) = \min(0.8, 0.9) / 0.9 = 8/9 \approx 0.889$.
Rain was already likely; conditioning on wet streets (which are
maximally positively dependent) makes it slightly more likely.
Jeffrey conditionalization~\cite{jeffrey1965}, which handles
uncertain evidence via a mixture, is deferred to
Section~\ref{sec:update} where it is applied.

\begin{proposition}[Zero-Evidence Underdetermination]
\label{prop:zero-underdet}
When evidence has credence $0$, any conditional
$c \in [0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule.
The set of consistent posteriors is the entire interval $[0,1]$
--- the maximal imprecise
posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.

Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}.
\end{proposition}

%=============================================================================
\section{The Kleene Collapse}
\label{sec:collapse}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet has three algebraically distinguished
values: the conjunction-idempotent pair $\{0, 1\}$ and the negation
fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
These are not postulated, as in finite-valued logics, but derived
from the continuous algebra on $[0,1]$.
This section shows that the three values define a surjective
homomorphism onto the Kleene lattice, classifies all finite
congruences of the algebra, and explains why no other collapse is
possible.

\subsection{Algebraic Fixed Points}
\label{sec:fixed-points}

Self-conjunction strictly decreases interior credences:
for $c \in (0,1)$,
\begin{equation}
  c \conjc c = c^2 < c.
  \label{eq:self-conj}
\end{equation}
Non-idempotence is a feature, not a defect.
Repeated uncertain evidence \emph{should} decrease joint credence:
if you are $80\%$ sure it will rain and $80\%$ sure (independently)
it will rain again, your joint credence in both is $64\%$, not $80\%$.
Only impossibility ($0$) and certainty ($1$) satisfy $c^2 = c$
(Lean: \texttt{conj\_idempotent\_iff}).

Dually, self-disjunction strictly increases interior credences:
$c \disjc c > c$ for $c \in (0,1)$
(Lean: \texttt{self\_disj\_gt}).
The power sequence $c^{n+1} < c^n$ is strictly decreasing for any
interior $c$, with $c^n \to 0$: unrenewed evidence decays
(Lean: \texttt{pow\_strictly\_decreasing}).

The negation fixed point is unique: $\negc c = c$ if and only if
$c = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Since $\negc c = 1 - c$, the equation $1 - c = c$ has the sole
solution $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Lean: \texttt{liar\_fixed\_point},
\texttt{neg\_fixed\_point\_unique}).

\begin{proposition}[Algebraic Fixed Points]
\label{prop:equilibria}
A credence $c \in [0,1]$ belongs to $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ if and
only if $c^2 = c$ or $\negc c = c$.
Lean: \texttt{equilibria\_characterization}.
\end{proposition}

The three values are \emph{derived} from the algebra: $0$ and $1$
are the conjunction-idempotent fixed points, and $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is
the negation fixed point.
Finite-valued logics (K$_3$, LP, RM3) take $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
as primitive; the continuous framework derives them.

The following table compares the three basic continuous t-norms on
$[0,1]$.
Each t-norm shares the negation $\negc c = 1 - c$ and its unique
fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$, but the conjunction behavior differs
sharply.

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccccc}
\toprule
\textbf{T-norm} & \textbf{Self-conj} & \textbf{Non-idemp?}
  & \textbf{$\negc$-fixed pt?} & $T(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2})$
  & \textbf{Exactly 3?} \\
\midrule
Product & $c^2 < c$ & Yes & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $\tfrac{1}{4}$
  & Yes \\
G\"odel & $\min(c,c) = c$ & No & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $\tfrac{1}{2}$
  & No$^*$ \\
\L ukasiewicz & $\max(2c{-}1,0)$ & Yes & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & $0$
  & No \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}
\smallskip
\noindent
{\footnotesize $^*$G\"odel: every value in $[0,1]$ is
conjunction-idempotent, so infinitely many fixed points exist.
\L ukasiewicz:
$T_{\text{\L}}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 0 \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$,
which destroys the negation fixed point under conjunction
(Lean: \texttt{luk\_conj\_half\_half}).}

\begin{remark}[Algebraic vs.\ Dynamical Fixed Points]
\label{rem:algebraic-vs-dynamical}
The results above are algebraic facts about the product De Morgan
triplet, not claims about Bayesian convergence.
Whether iterated Bayesian update drives $\cred_n(A) \to 0$,
$\tfrac{1}{2}$, or $1$ depends on the stochastic model for the
evidence sequence --- a question orthogonal to the algebraic structure.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Collapse Homomorphism}
\label{sec:collapse-hom}

Define the \emph{collapse}:
\[
\mathrm{collapse}(c) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } c = 0, \\
\tfrac{1}{2} & \text{if } 0 < c < 1, \\
1 & \text{if } c = 1.
\end{cases}
\]

\begin{theorem}[Collapse Homomorphism]
\label{thm:collapse}
The collapse is a surjective homomorphism from
$([0,1], \negc, \conjc, \disjc)$ to
$(\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}, \mathrm{neg}, \min, \max)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Case analysis on the boundary/interior status of the arguments.
Negation: $\mathrm{collapse}(0) = 0 \mapsto 1$, and if
$0 < c < 1$ then $0 < 1 - c < 1$, so interior maps to interior.
Conjunction: $0$ absorbs, $1$ is identity; if $0 < c_1, c_2 < 1$
then $0 < c_1 c_2 < 1$.
Disjunction follows by De Morgan duality.
Lean: \texttt{collapse\_neg}, \texttt{collapse\_conj},
\texttt{collapse\_disj}, \texttt{collapse\_surjective}.
\end{proof}

The target is the \emph{Kleene lattice}~\cite{kleene1952}, the
shared truth-table structure underlying
K$_3$~\cite{kleene1952}, LP~\cite{asenjo1966,priest1979}, and
RM3~\cite{anderson1975,meyer1972}.
In the bilattice framework of Belnap~\cite{belnap1977} and
Dunn~\cite{dunn1976}, this is the knowledge lattice of the three
``told'' values; Fitting~\cite{fitting1991,fitting2006} developed
bilattice semantics for logic programming in this setting.

\begin{remark}[Connection to Possibility Theory]
\label{rem:possibility}
The Kleene quotient's $\min/\max$ operations are the
conjunction/disjunction of possibility
theory~\cite{dubois1988}: the collapse maps the product
De Morgan triplet to the possibility/necessity structure.
This is not a coincidence: possibility theory's operations are
precisely what remains when continuous credences are coarsened to
the boundary/interior/boundary trichotomy.
The analogy extends partially to conditioning:
Dubois and Prade~\cite{dubois1988} define possibility-theoretic
conditioning as $\Pi(A \mid B) = \Pi(A \land B)$ when
$\Pi(A \land B) < \Pi(B)$, and $1$ otherwise --- which matches the
chain-rule conditional under the min copula at boundaries
(Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary}, case~(iv): $a \geq b$ gives
conditional $= 1$).
At interior values, however, the analogy breaks down for the same
reason the conditional bridge fails: possibility-theoretic
conditioning, like the G\"odel conditional, is truth-functional on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ and therefore cannot capture the ratio
information that the chain rule preserves on $[0,1]$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Uniqueness and Impossibility}
\label{sec:impossibility}

The Kleene lattice is the \emph{only} three-element quotient.
No quotient to classical logic, G\"odel logic, or \L ukasiewicz
logic is possible.

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to Classical Logic]
\label{prop:no-classical}
No $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, 1\}$ preserves negation.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ has no solution
in $\{0, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{no\_boolean\_neg\_retraction}).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No Collapse to G\"odel or \L ukasiewicz]
\label{prop:no-godel-luk}
No surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ preserves
negation and G\"odel conjunction (which lacks a negation fixed point),
nor negation and \L ukasiewicz conjunction (which satisfies
$T_{\text{\L}}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 0 \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$).
Lean: \texttt{no\_godel\_collapse}, \texttt{no\_luk\_collapse}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness of the Three-Element Quotient]
\label{thm:quotient-unique}
Any surjective $\varphi : [0,1] \to S$ with $|S| = 3$ that
preserves negation and conjunction yields the Kleene lattice (up to
relabeling).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The negation fixed point $\tfrac{1}{2}$ maps to the unique
negation fixed point of $S$.
The remaining elements $\varphi(0)$, $\varphi(1)$ form the
$2$-cycle.
The conjunction table is forced:
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2} \conjc \tfrac{1}{2})
= \varphi(\tfrac{1}{4}) = \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ since
$\tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$ maps to the interior class.
This is Kleene $\min$
(Lean: \texttt{three\_element\_quotient\_unique}).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Congruence Classification}
\label{sec:congruence}

The collapse is the unique three-element quotient
(Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}).
A full congruence classification appears in
Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} (Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}):
under $[0,1]$-multiplication the Kleene lattice is the unique
nontrivial three-element quotient and boundary classes $\{0\}$,
$\{1\}$ are singletons, while under $\Real$-multiplication no
nontrivial finite quotient exists at all.
Whether a unit congruence with $4$ or more classes exists under
$[0,1]$-multiplication alone is \textbf{open}.

%=============================================================================
\section{The Propositional Bridge}
\label{sec:bridge}
%=============================================================================

The collapse homomorphism maps the continuous algebra to the Kleene
lattice.
This section shows that the mapping lifts from connectives to
formulas, and that consequence relations on the two structures
correspond exactly: LP consequence on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is
positivity consequence on $[0,1]$, and K$_3$ consequence is
certainty consequence.

\subsection{Propositional Formulas and Evaluation}
\label{sec:formulas}

Propositional formulas over atoms $\alpha$ are built from variables,
negation, conjunction, and disjunction.
Given an assignment $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$, \emph{credence evaluation}
is recursive:
\[
\llbracket \negc\varphi \rrbracket_v
= \negc \llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v, \qquad
\llbracket \varphi \conjc \psi \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v \conjc
  \llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v, \qquad
\llbracket \varphi \disjc \psi \rrbracket_v
= \llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v \disjc
  \llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v.
\]
Three-valued evaluation uses Kleene connectives analogously:
negation swaps $0$ and $1$ and fixes $\tfrac{1}{2}$; conjunction is
$\min$; disjunction is $\max$
(Lean: \texttt{Formula}, \texttt{evalCred}, \texttt{evalThreeVal}).

\subsection{Collapse Commutes with Evaluation}
\label{sec:collapse-eval}

Because the collapse is a homomorphism on each connective, it
commutes with any formula built from those connectives.

\begin{proposition}[Collapse--Evaluation Commutativity]
\label{prop:collapse-eval}
For any assignment $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ and formula $\varphi$:
\[
\mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v)
  = \llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_{\mathrm{collapse} \circ\, v}.
\]
Conversely, for any $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$:
\[
\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_w
  = \mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_{\mathrm{lift}
    \circ\, w}),
\]
where $\mathrm{lift}$ maps $0 \mapsto 0$,
$\tfrac{1}{2} \mapsto \tfrac{1}{2}$, $1 \mapsto 1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Structural induction on $\varphi$.
The base case (atoms) is the definition of function composition.
Each inductive case applies the corresponding homomorphism property
($\mathrm{collapse} \circ \negc = \mathrm{neg} \circ
\mathrm{collapse}$, etc.).
The converse follows from $\mathrm{collapse} \circ \mathrm{lift}
= \mathrm{id}$ on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{collapse\_eval\_eq}, \texttt{lift\_eval\_eq},
\texttt{lift\_collapse\_id}).
\end{proof}

The commutativity reduces all formula-level questions to the
atom-level collapse: one direction projects, the other lifts.

\subsection{Consequence Relations}
\label{sec:consequence}

We define four consequence relations.
On the continuous side:

\begin{definition}[Positivity and Certainty Consequence]
\label{def:pos-cert}
\emph{Positivity consequence}
$\Gamma \models^+ \varphi$: for every $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$,
if $\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v > 0$ for all $\psi \in \Gamma$,
then $\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v > 0$.

\emph{Certainty consequence}
$\Gamma \models^1 \varphi$: for every $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$,
if $\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v = 1$ for all $\psi \in \Gamma$,
then $\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v = 1$.
\end{definition}

On the three-valued side:

\begin{definition}[Three-Valued Consequence]
\label{def:three-val-cons}
For a designated set $D \subseteq \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$,
$\Gamma \models_D \varphi$: for every $w : \alpha \to \{0,
\tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, if $\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_w \in D$ for all
$\psi \in \Gamma$, then $\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_w \in D$.
LP designates $D = \{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$; K$_3$ designates
$D = \{1\}$.
\end{definition}

All four relations quantify over \emph{all} assignments, not just
homomorphisms or probability measures.

\begin{proposition}[Structural Rules]
\label{prop:structural}
All four consequence relations satisfy reflexivity, monotonicity, and
cut.
Lean: \texttt{formulaLP\_reflexivity/monotonicity/cut},
\texttt{formulaK3\_*}, \texttt{formulaPositivity\_*},
\texttt{formulaCertainty\_*}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Reflexivity: the conclusion is among the premises.
Monotonicity: adding premises restricts the set of admissible
valuations.
Cut: if $\Gamma \models \varphi$ and $\Gamma, \varphi \models \psi$,
then every valuation satisfying $\Gamma$ also satisfies $\varphi$
(by the first entailment) and hence $\psi$ (by the second).
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Bridge Theorems}
\label{sec:bridge-thms}

The collapse connects the four consequence relations in two
equivalences.

\begin{theorem}[LP Bridge]
\label{thm:lp-bridge}
LP consequence is equivalent to positivity consequence:
\[
\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{LP}} \varphi
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \models^+ \varphi.
\]
Lean: \texttt{lp\_formula\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$
Let $v : \alpha \to [0,1]$ with $\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v > 0$
for all $\psi \in \Gamma$.
Set $w = \mathrm{collapse} \circ v$.
By Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval},
$\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_w = \mathrm{collapse}(
\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v)$.
A credence $c$ is positive if and only if $\mathrm{collapse}(c)
\in \{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$ --- the LP designated set
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_designated\_iff\_pos}).
So each premise is LP-designated under $w$.
By LP consequence, $\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_w$ is
LP-designated, hence $\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v > 0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$
Let $w : \alpha \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ with each premise
LP-designated.
Set $v = \mathrm{lift} \circ w$.
By the converse of Proposition~\ref{prop:collapse-eval},
$\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_w = \mathrm{collapse}(
\llbracket\psi\rrbracket_v)$.
LP-designation under $w$ gives positivity under $v$
(same bridge lemma).
By positivity consequence, $\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v > 0$.
Collapsing: $\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_w =
\mathrm{collapse}(\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket_v)$ is LP-designated.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[K$_3$ Bridge]
\label{thm:k3-bridge}
K$_3$ consequence is equivalent to certainty consequence:
\[
\Gamma \models_{\mathrm{K}_3} \varphi
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\Gamma \models^1 \varphi.
\]
Lean: \texttt{k3\_formula\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Identical structure to the LP bridge, replacing ``positive'' with
``equal to $1$'' and the LP designated set with $\{1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_designated\_iff\_one}).
\end{proof}

The proofs are symmetric in a precise sense: one direction collapses,
the other lifts.
The collapse does not merely project --- it \emph{reflects}:
every three-valued counterexample lifts to a continuous one, and
every continuous counterexample collapses to a three-valued one.
An LP logician working with $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is implicitly
performing positivity reasoning on $[0,1]$; a K$_3$ logician is
implicitly performing certainty reasoning.

\subsection{Consequences of the Bridge}
\label{sec:bridge-consequences}

\begin{corollary}[Finite Model Property]
\label{cor:finite-model}
Positivity and certainty consequence on $[0,1]$ are decidable:
non-consequence reduces to a counterexample in
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}^{|\alpha|}$.
LP and K$_3$ consequence are
coNP-complete~\cite{priest2008,avron1991}; the bridge transfers
this complexity to positivity and certainty consequence.
\end{corollary}

The bridge also transfers the paraconsistency and paracompleteness
properties that distinguish LP and K$_3$.

\begin{example}[Explosion Failure]
\label{ex:explosion}
Set $\cred(\text{rain}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and
$\cred(\text{snow}) = 0$.
Both $\text{rain}$ and $\negc\text{rain}$ are positive
($\tfrac{1}{2} > 0$ and $\negc\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2} > 0$),
but $\cred(\text{snow}) = 0$ --- contradictory evidence about rain
does not force belief in snow.
Via the bridge, the LP counterexample
$w(\text{rain}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $w(\text{snow}) = 0$ refutes
$\text{rain}, \negc\text{rain} \models_{\mathrm{LP}} \text{snow}$
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[No Tautologies under K$_3$]
\label{ex:no-tautologies}
K$_3$ has no tautologies: the constant-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ valuation
makes every formula non-designated (since $\tfrac{1}{2} \notin
\{1\}$).
Via the bridge, certainty consequence on $[0,1]$ has no tautologies
either
(Lean: \texttt{k3\_no\_tautology}).
\end{example}

\subsection{Transition}
\label{sec:transition}

The bridge covers negation, conjunction, and disjunction --- the
propositional connectives of the product De Morgan triplet.
Does it extend to conditioning?

%=============================================================================
\section{The Conditional Bridge}
\label{sec:cond-bridge}
%=============================================================================

The propositional bridge transfers consequence between $[0,1]$ and
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ for formulas built from connectives.
This section asks whether the same transfer works for conditioning.
The answer is no: no truth-functional conditional bridge exists.
We characterize exactly when the bridge holds (at boundaries) and
when it fails (everywhere else), and connect the failure to the
role of dependence information lost by the collapse.

\subsection{Min-Copula Conditioning on $[0,1]$ vs.\
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$}
\label{sec:min-copula-cond}

Under the min copula, the conditional for $b > 0$ is
$\min(a,b)/b$.
On the Kleene lattice, the natural candidate for a three-valued
conditional is the G\"odel implication, which coincides with the
product-residuated implication on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
(Lean: \texttt{godel\_impl\_eq\_prod\_resid}).
The three conditioning tables (Table~\ref{tab:three-conditionals})
show the key difference: the chain rule leaves the zero-evidence row
unconstrained, while both residuation and Kleene material fill it
with~$1$.

\begin{example}[Bridge Failure]
\label{ex:bridge-failure}
$\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8$, $\cred(\text{wet}) = 0.9$.
Min-copula conditional:
$\cred(\text{rain} \mid \text{wet})
= \min(0.8, 0.9)/0.9 = 8/9 \approx 0.889$.
Collapsing: $\mathrm{collapse}(8/9) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (interior value).
G\"odel on collapsed values:
$\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\mathrm{collapse}(0.9),
\mathrm{collapse}(0.8))
= \mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 1$.
The two paths disagree: $\tfrac{1}{2} \neq 1$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Impossibility Theorem}
\label{sec:impossibility-thm}

\begin{theorem}[No Truth-Functional Conditional Bridge]
\label{thm:no-cond-bridge}
There is no function
$f : \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}^2 \to \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
satisfying
\[
\mathrm{collapse}\!\left(\frac{\min(a,b)}{b}\right)
= f(\mathrm{collapse}(b),\, \mathrm{collapse}(a))
\]
for all $a, b \in [0,1]$ with $b > 0$.
Lean: \texttt{no\_truthfunctional\_cond\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Two witnesses force contradictory values for
$f(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})$.

\emph{Witness~1:}
$a = 0.3$, $b = 0.7$.
Both are interior, so
$\mathrm{collapse}(a) = \mathrm{collapse}(b) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
The conditional is $\min(0.3, 0.7)/0.7 = 3/7 \in (0,1)$,
so $\mathrm{collapse}(3/7) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
This forces $f(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

\emph{Witness~2:}
$a = 0.7$, $b = 0.3$.
Again $\mathrm{collapse}(a) = \mathrm{collapse}(b) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
The conditional is $\min(0.7, 0.3)/0.3 = 0.3/0.3 = 1$,
so $\mathrm{collapse}(1) = 1$.
This forces $f(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 1$.

Contradiction: $\tfrac{1}{2} \neq 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Comparison with Lewis Triviality]
\label{rem:lewis}
The structural setting differs from Lewis~\cite{lewis1976}.
Lewis works \emph{within} probability theory and shows that
identifying $P(A \mid B)$ with the probability of a conditional
proposition forces the measure to be trivial --- a contradiction
internal to a single framework.
Theorem~\ref{thm:no-cond-bridge} works \emph{between} two
frameworks and shows that the collapse homomorphism, which faithfully
preserves connectives, cannot extend to conditioning.
The obstacle is that collapsed values lose the ratio
information needed to determine the conditional:
two pairs $(a, b)$ with the same collapsed image can produce
different conditional values because $\min(a,b)/b$ depends on the
ratio, not the collapsed class.
Lewis requires measure-theoretic machinery; our result is finitary
(two explicit witnesses).
See Halpern~\cite{halpern2003} for Lewis's conditions;
Adams~\cite{adams1975} for a probabilistic conditional logic that
avoids Lewis's assumptions differently.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Boundary Characterization}
\label{sec:boundary}

The conditional bridge fails in general but holds at the boundaries.

\begin{theorem}[Boundary Characterization]
\label{thm:boundary}
For $a, b \in [0,1]$ with $b > 0$:
\[
\mathrm{collapse}\!\left(\frac{\min(a,b)}{b}\right)
= \mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\mathrm{collapse}(b),\,
\mathrm{collapse}(a))
\]
whenever at least one of the following holds:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item $a = 0$ (conclusion impossible),
\item $a = 1$ (conclusion certain),
\item $b = 1$ (evidence certain),
\item $a \geq b$ (conclusion at least as credible as evidence).
\end{enumerate}
In all four cases, the min-copula conditional is either $0$ or $1$.
Lean: \texttt{cond\_bridge\_boundary},
\texttt{cond\_bridge\_at\_a\_zero},
\texttt{cond\_bridge\_at\_a\_one},
\texttt{cond\_bridge\_at\_b\_one},
\texttt{cond\_bridge\_at\_a\_ge\_b}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(i)~$a = 0$: $\min(0,b)/b = 0$;
$\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\mathrm{collapse}(b), 0) = 0$ for $b > 0$.
(ii)~$a = 1$: $\min(1,b)/b = b/b = 1$;
$\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\mathrm{collapse}(b), 1) = 1$.
(iii)~$b = 1$: $\min(a,1)/1 = a$; collapsing commutes trivially.
(iv)~$a \geq b$: $\min(a,b)/b = b/b = 1$; and
$\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\mathrm{collapse}(b),
\mathrm{collapse}(a)) = 1$ when $a \geq b > 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[When the Bridge Works]
\label{ex:bridge-works}
If $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.95 > 0.9 = \cred(\text{wet})$
(case~(iv): $a \geq b$), then
$\min(0.95, 0.9)/0.9 = 0.9/0.9 = 1$.
Collapsing: $\mathrm{collapse}(1) = 1$.
G\"odel: $\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 1$.
Both paths agree.
The boundary condition forces a boundary conditional, and at
boundaries the collapse preserves everything.
\end{example}

\subsection{Interior Failure}
\label{sec:interior-fail}

The bridge fails precisely when the conditional is interior ---
that is, when both $a$ and $b$ are interior and $a < b$.

\begin{theorem}[Interior Bridge Failure]
\label{thm:interior-fail}
For any $a, b \in (0,1)$ with $a < b$, the conditional bridge fails:
$a/b \in (0,1)$, so $\mathrm{collapse}(a/b) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, but
$\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 1$.
Lean: \texttt{cond\_bridge\_fails\_interior},
\texttt{minCopulaCond\_interior}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
When $0 < a < b < 1$: $\min(a,b)/b = a/b$.
Since $0 < a < b$, we have $0 < a/b < 1$, so
$\mathrm{collapse}(a/b) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Since $0 < a < 1$ and $0 < b < 1$, both collapse to $\tfrac{1}{2}$,
and $\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})
= 1 \neq \tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{proof}

The failure is systematic, not accidental.
Whenever both the conclusion and evidence are uncertain (interior)
and the conclusion is less credible than the evidence, the
min-copula conditional is interior; but the G\"odel conditional on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ maps $(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})$ to
$1$ (certainty), losing the distinction between ``partially
supports'' and ``fully supports.''

\begin{corollary}[Complete Characterization]
\label{cor:complete-boundary}
For $a, b \in [0,1]$ with $b > 0$, the conditional bridge holds
(i.e., $\mathrm{collapse}(\min(a,b)/b) =
\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\mathrm{collapse}(b),\,
\mathrm{collapse}(a))$)
if and only if at least one of conditions
\textup{(i)--(iv)} of Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary} holds.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Sufficiency: Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary}.
Necessity: when none of (i)--(iv) hold, both $a$ and $b$ are interior
with $a < b$, and Theorem~\ref{thm:interior-fail} gives failure.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Role of Dependence}
\label{sec:dependence}

The interior failure connects to path dependence
(Proposition~\ref{prop:path-dep}, Figure~\ref{fig:path-dependence}):
the conditional $\min(a,b)/b$ has an essential singularity at the
origin, so the collapse cannot capture the directional information
that determines the conditional value.

The min copula represents maximum positive dependence.
What happens under other dependence structures?

Under the \emph{independence copula}
($\cred(A \land B) = a \cdot b$), the conditional is
$a \cdot b / b = a$ --- it does not depend on the evidence at all.
The bridge trivially holds (collapsing $a$ gives the same answer
either way), but the conditional is uninformative: learning $B$
does not change your credence in $A$.

For $\mathrm{collapse}(a) = \mathrm{collapse}(b) = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(both interior), the set of min-copula conditionals is $(0, 1]$ ---
maximally underdetermined by the collapsed values
(Lean: \texttt{interior\_cond\_range}).
This is the precise sense in which the collapse loses too much
information: interior credences are unified into a single class, but
their ratios span almost the entire unit interval.
The collapse preserves the qualitative status (possible, impossible,
certain) but discards the quantitative relationships that
conditioning requires.

\section{Update Through the Bridge}
\label{sec:update}
%=============================================================================

The conditional bridge fails in general but holds at boundaries.
This section shows that Bayesian update inherits the bridge under
the same boundary conditions, defines qualitative update on
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, and connects zero-evidence behavior
across the probabilistic and logical perspectives.

\subsection{Bayesian Update on $[0,1]$}
\label{sec:bayesian}

As defined in Section~\ref{sec:conditioning}, the chain rule
$\condc{A}{B} \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
determines the conditional uniquely when $\cred(B) > 0$.
The Bayesian posterior is this unique value:
$\cred'(A) = \cred(A \land B) / \cred(B)$.
It satisfies the chain rule by construction:
$\cred'(A) \conjc \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
(Lean: \texttt{bayesianUpdate\_chain\_rule},
\texttt{bayesianUpdate\_unique}).

\begin{example}[Running Example: Bayesian Update]
\label{ex:bayesian}
Under the min copula:
$\cred(\text{rain} \land \text{wet}) = \min(0.8, 0.9) = 0.8$.
The Bayesian posterior:
$\cred'(\text{rain}) = 0.8/0.9 = 8/9 \approx 0.889$.
Rain was already likely; conditioning on wet streets (which are
correlated) makes it slightly more likely, but not certain.
\end{example}

\subsection{Qualitative Update on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$}

\begin{definition}[Qualitative Update]
\label{def:qual-update}
For $e, p \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, the \emph{qualitative
update} is $\mathrm{qual}(e, p) = \mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(e, p)$:
the G\"odel implication applied to evidence and prior.
Lean: \texttt{qualitativeUpdate}.
\end{definition}

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{r|ccc}
\multicolumn{4}{c}{\textbf{Qualitative update table}:
$\mathrm{qual}(e, p) = \mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(e, p)$} \\
$e \!\downarrow\;\; p$ & 0 & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1 \\
\hline
0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 0 & 1 & 1 \\
1 & 0 & $\tfrac{1}{2}$ & 1
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}
\smallskip
\noindent
Lean: \texttt{qualUpdate\_zero\_zero} through
\texttt{qualUpdate\_one\_one}.

The table has a natural reading.
Certain evidence ($e = 1$) preserves the prior: what you believed
stays as it was.
Intermediate evidence ($e = \tfrac{1}{2}$) maps uncertain
conclusions to certainty --- the G\"odel conditional's ``benefit of
the doubt'' for compatible evidence --- while mapping impossibility
($p = 0$) to impossibility.
Zero evidence ($e = 0$) maps everything to $1$: this is the ex falso
row of the G\"odel conditional, which the chain rule avoids by
leaving the conditional unconstrained.

\subsection{Update Bridge Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Update Bridge]
\label{thm:update-bridge}
Under boundary conditions (Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary}), collapsing
after min-copula conditioning equals qualitative update after
collapsing:
\[
\mathrm{collapse}\!\left(\frac{\min(a,b)}{b}\right)
= \mathrm{qual}(\mathrm{collapse}(b),\, \mathrm{collapse}(a)).
\]
That is: if $a = 0$, $a = 1$, $b = 1$, or $a \geq b$ (with
$b > 0$), then conditioning on $[0,1]$ followed by collapsing
gives the same result as collapsing followed by qualitative update.

\smallskip\noindent
Lean: \texttt{update\_bridge}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary}: the qualitative update
is defined as $\mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(e, p)$, which is what the
boundary characterization gives.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Running Example: Update Bridge]
\label{ex:update-bridge}
Case (iv) from Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary}: suppose
$\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.95 > 0.9 = \cred(\text{wet})$.
Then $\min(0.95, 0.9)/0.9 = 1$.
On the left:
$\mathrm{collapse}(1) = 1$.
On the right:
$\mathrm{qual}(\mathrm{collapse}(0.9), \mathrm{collapse}(0.95))
= \mathrm{qual}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})
= \mathrm{g\ddot{o}del}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}) = 1$.
Both paths agree.

But with the original values $\cred(\text{rain}) = 0.8 <
0.9 = \cred(\text{wet})$ (interior, $a < b$), the bridge fails:
$\mathrm{collapse}(8/9) = \tfrac{1}{2} \neq 1 =
\mathrm{qual}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Jeffrey Update}

\begin{definition}[Jeffrey Update]
\label{def:jeffrey}
Jeffrey conditionalization~\cite{jeffrey1965} handles uncertain
evidence via a mixture:
$\cred'(A) = \condc{A}{B} \cdot q + \condc{A}{\negc B} \cdot (1-q)$,
where $q$ is the new credence for $B$.
At $q = 1$ this reduces to Bayesian update; at $q = 0$ it conditions
on $\negc B$
(Lean: \texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_certain},
\texttt{jeffreyUpdate\_zero}).
\end{definition}

For the Jeffrey update bridge to hold, \emph{both} conditional arms
must satisfy the boundary conditions of Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary}:
$\condc{A}{B}$ must be at a boundary, and so must
$\condc{A}{\negc B}$.
This is restrictive.
In the running example, unless $\cred(\text{rain})$ is $0$, $1$,
or at least as large as both $\cred(\text{wet})$ and
$\cred(\negc\text{wet})$, at least one arm is interior and the
Jeffrey bridge fails.

\subsection{Zero-Evidence Trichotomy}
\label{sec:zero-trichotomy}

The algebraic fact $c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c \in [0,1]$ is the
common root of three phenomena.

\begin{theorem}[Zero-Evidence Trichotomy]
\label{thm:zero-trichotomy}
The following three statements are simultaneously consequences of
the identity $c \conjc 0 = 0$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},nosep]
\item \textbf{Underdetermined posteriors:}
  When evidence has credence $0$, any conditional
  $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies the chain rule.
  The set of consistent posteriors is $[0,1]$ --- the maximal
  imprecise posterior~\cite{walley1991,levi1980}.
\item \textbf{LP explosion failure:}
  The value $\tfrac{1}{2}$ makes both $A$ and $\negc A$
  LP-designated (since both $\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\negc\tfrac{1}{2}
  = \tfrac{1}{2}$ are in $\{1, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$) without forcing
  any particular value for $B$.
  This is the witness that defeats explosion.
\item \textbf{Conditional bridge failure:}
  At evidence $0$, the chain rule is satisfied by every conditional
  value.
  No truth-functional $f$ can capture this:
  $f(\mathrm{collapse}(0), \mathrm{collapse}(a))$ must return a
  single value, but any value is equally valid.
\end{enumerate}

\smallskip\noindent
Lean: \texttt{zero\_evidence\_duality}
(packaging \texttt{zero\_evidence\_underdetermined},
\texttt{zero\_evidence\_paraconsistency},
\texttt{zero\_evidence\_bridge\_failure}).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(i)~$c \cdot 0 = 0$ for all $c$
(Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
(ii)~$v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $v(B) = 0$ satisfies
$v(A) > 0$ and $\negc v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2} > 0$ but $v(B) = 0$
(Lean: \texttt{lp\_no\_explosion}).
(iii)~Any truth-functional
$f(\mathrm{collapse}(0), \mathrm{collapse}(a))$ must return a
single value in $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$, but all values in $[0,1]$
are equally valid conditionals at evidence $0$ (by part~(i)), so no
single output can be correct for all $a$
(Lean: \texttt{conditioning\_zero\_any}).
\end{proof}

The trichotomy provides a unified perspective on the paper's results.
The propositional bridge (Section~\ref{sec:bridge}) operates through
the connectives, which are homomorphic under collapse.
The conditional bridge (Section~\ref{sec:cond-bridge}) fails because
conditioning depends on \emph{ratios}, and the collapse loses ratio
information by mapping all of $(0,1)$ to $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
The zero-evidence case is the extreme: the ratio $0/0$ is maximally
underdetermined, which manifests simultaneously as paraconsistency
(the $\tfrac{1}{2}$ witness), imprecise probability (the vacuous
posterior), and bridge failure (no truth-functional conditional can
handle the underdetermination).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet combines well-studied components:
the product t-norm~\cite{klement2000,goguen1969},
the standard complement~\cite{bergmann2008},
and the probabilistic sum (De Morgan dual).
The Kleene lattice and its logics are
classical~\cite{kleene1952,asenjo1966,priest1979,priest2008,avron1991}.
The collapse homomorphism to three-valued logic is observed in
Ferm\"uller's game semantics~\cite{fermuller2014}.

\textbf{Primitive conditioning.}
Primitive conditioning has precedents in
R\'enyi~\cite{renyi1955}, Popper~\cite{popper1959},
de Finetti~\cite{definetti1936,definetti1937},
and \'Egr\'e--Rossi--Sprenger~\cite{egre2021}.
Adams~\cite{adams1975} developed a probabilistic logic of
conditionals; van Fraassen~\cite{vanfraassen1976,vanfraassen1983}
studied the interplay of probability and conditionals.
Lewis~\cite{lewis1976} showed that identifying conditional
probability with the probability of a conditional proposition
forces triviality (Remark~\ref{rem:lewis} compares our
impossibility theorem).

\textbf{Algebraic approaches.}
Algebraic approaches to probability appear in
Calabrese~\cite{calabrese1987} and
Coletti--Scozzafava~\cite{coletti2002};
conditional events in
Gilio--Sanfilippo~\cite{gilio2013,gilio2014}.
Copulas are surveyed in Nelsen~\cite{nelsen2006}; comonotonicity
in Dhaene et~al.~\cite{dhaene2002};
imprecise probability in Walley~\cite{walley1991} and
Levi~\cite{levi1980}.
Cox~\cite{cox1961} and Jaynes~\cite{jaynes2003} showed that
consistent plausible reasoning must be isomorphic to probability;
Gaines~\cite{gaines1978} unified fuzzy and probabilistic
uncertainty.

\textbf{Many-valued logic and consequence.}
Pavelka~\cite{pavelka1979i} introduced graded inference
in many-valued logic;
B\v{e}hounek and Cintula~\cite{behounek2006} developed
degree-preserving consequence.
Suszko~\cite{suszko1977} argued that every Tarskian consequence
relation is two-valued at the level of consequence (though not
necessarily at the level of truth values);
Malinowski~\cite{malinowski1993} and
Font--Jansana--Pigozzi~\cite{font2003} developed this theme in
abstract algebraic logic.
The bridge theorems make Suszko's reduction constructive: the
collapse homomorphism explicitly maps $[0,1]$-valuations to
three-valued valuations, and the lift provides the inverse
direction.
Mundici's~\cite{mundici2011} theory of states on MV-algebras
provides a different bridge between many-valued logic and
probability, using \L ukasiewicz connectives;
Flaminio and Montagna~\cite{flaminio2009} extended this to a
probabilistic logic.
The product bridge differs in using the product t-norm (which
preserves the interior) rather than \L ukasiewicz (which can
collapse interior values to zero).
Belnap~\cite{belnap1977} and Dunn~\cite{dunn1976} developed FDE;
Fitting~\cite{fitting1991,fitting2006} connected bilattices to
logic programming.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

The product De Morgan triplet on $[0,1]$ --- product conjunction,
probabilistic-sum disjunction, standard complement --- admits a
natural collapse to the Kleene lattice
$\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ via a surjective homomorphism.
The paper develops two main threads.

\textbf{Thread 1: Properties of the triplet and its collapse}
(independent of conditioning).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item The bridge theorem: LP consequence on
  $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ is equivalent to positivity consequence
  on $[0,1]$; K$_3$ consequence is equivalent to certainty
  consequence
  (Theorems~\ref{thm:lp-bridge}
  and~\ref{thm:k3-bridge}).
\item The algebraic fixed points $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ are
  derived from the algebra: $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is the unique
  negation fixed point; $0$ and $1$ are the unique conjunction
  absorber and identity.
\item The Kleene collapse is the unique three-element quotient
  preserving negation and conjunction.
\item The congruence classification: under $[0,1]$-multiplication,
  the Kleene lattice exists and is the unique three-element
  quotient; under $\Real$-multiplication, no non-trivial finite
  quotient exists at all (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Thread 2: Conditioning} (the paper's main contributions).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,start=5]
\item The conditional bridge impossibility: no truth-functional
  function on $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$ can serve as a conditional
  bridge (Theorem~\ref{thm:no-cond-bridge}).
  This is a constructive Lewis triviality for the product De Morgan
  triplet, witnessed by two explicit pairs.
\item The boundary characterization: the bridge holds if and only if
  evidence is certain, conclusion is impossible or certain, or
  conclusion is at least as credible as evidence
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary},
  Corollary~\ref{cor:complete-boundary}).
\item The update bridge: Bayesian update inherits the conditional
  bridge under boundary conditions
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:update-bridge}).
\item Zero-evidence trichotomy: the algebraic fact $c \cdot 0 = 0$
  simultaneously forces underdetermined posteriors,
  LP explosion failure, and conditional bridge failure
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:zero-trichotomy}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{The common root is $c \cdot 0 = 0$.}
The identity that makes zero an annihilator for the product
simultaneously provides the paraconsistency witness
($v(A) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ satisfies both $v(A) > 0$ and
$\negc v(A) > 0$ without constraining $v(B)$),
the vacuous posterior (any $c \in [0,1]$ satisfies
$c \cdot 0 = 0$), and the bridge failure (collapse maps
all of $(0,1)$ to $\tfrac{1}{2}$, discarding the ratio
information that determines the conditional).

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Four or more classes under $[0,1]$-multiplication.}
  The Kleene lattice is the unique three-element quotient, but
  whether four or more classes are possible under
  $[0,1]$-multiplication remains open.
  Any such quotient faces strong constraints: boundary classes
  $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ must be singletons, and each interior class
  must be dense near $0$ (since multiplication can shrink any
  element arbitrarily close to $0$).
\item \textbf{Non-truth-functional conditional bridge.}
  A conditional bridge with an explicit dependence indicator
  (e.g., a function
  $g : \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}^2 \times D \to
  \{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
  for some dependence type $D$) might recover the bridge at the
  cost of an additional parameter.
  What is the minimal such $D$?
\item \textbf{Four-valued FDE extension.}
  K$_3$ and LP are both fragments of Belnap--Dunn four-valued logic
  (FDE)~\cite{belnap1977,dunn1976}.
  Does the bridge theorem extend to FDE, and what is the
  corresponding consequence relation on $[0,1]$?
\item \textbf{Proof theory for chain-rule conditioning.}
  Is there a sound and complete calculus for chain-rule conditioning
  on $[0,1]$?
  H\'ajek's completeness theorem~\cite{hajek1998} answers this for
  product logic with residuation; replacing residuation with the
  chain rule changes the answer.
\item \textbf{Iterated update convergence.}
  Under what stochastic model for the evidence sequence does
  iterated Bayesian update drive $\cred_n(A) \to 1$, $0$, or
  $\tfrac{1}{2}$?
\item \textbf{Graded consequence.}
  Generalizing the discrete consequence relations to a continuous
  threshold parameter on $[0,1]$ yields sharp no-explosion and
  excluded-middle thresholds; a systematic study is left for
  future work.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{app:prelim}
%=============================================================================

This appendix collects standard definitions used throughout the paper.

\textbf{T-norms.}
A \emph{triangular norm} (t-norm) is a binary operation
$T : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ that is commutative, associative,
monotone, and satisfies $T(a, 1) = a$ for all
$a$~\cite{klement2000}.
The three basic continuous t-norms are:
product ($a \cdot b$),
G\"odel ($\min(a,b)$), and
\L ukasiewicz ($\max(a+b-1, 0)$).

\textbf{Copulas.}
A \emph{copula} is $C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ satisfying:
grounded ($C(a,0) = C(0,b) = 0$), unit marginals
($C(a,1) = a$, $C(1,b) = b$), and the 2-increasing
property~\cite{nelsen2006}.
The Fr\'echet-Hoeffding bounds apply:
$\max(a+b-1, 0) \leq C(a,b) \leq \min(a,b)$.
Comonotonicity corresponds to the upper bound $C(a,b) = \min(a,b)$;
independence to the product $C(a,b) = a \cdot b$.

\textbf{Coherence.}
De Finetti~\cite{definetti1937} introduced coherence: an assignment
is coherent if no finite combination of bets yields a sure loss.
The chain rule
$\cred(A \mid B) \cdot \cred(B) = \cred(A \land B)$
is necessary for coherence~\cite{coletti2002,gilio2014}.

\textbf{Residuation.}
Given a left-continuous t-norm $T$, the \emph{residuum} is
$a \to_T b = \sup\{c : T(a,c) \leq b\}$~\cite{hajek1998,galatos2007}.
For the product: $a \to_P b = \min(b/a, 1)$ when $a > 0$, and
$0 \to_P b = 1$ (ex falso).

The impossibility theorem
(\texttt{no\_truthfunctional\_cond\_bridge}) uses

\subsection{Min Copula Uniqueness}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:min-copula-unique}]
Let $C$ be a symmetric, idempotent copula.

\emph{Upper bound.}
The 2-increasing property on $[0,a] \times [b,1]$ with unit
marginal gives
$C(a,1) - C(a,b) - C(0,1) + C(0,b) \geq 0$, hence
$a - C(a,b) \geq 0$, so $C(a,b) \leq a$.
By symmetry $C(a,b) \leq b$, giving $C(a,b) \leq \min(a,b)$.

\emph{Lower bound.}
Assume $a \leq b$.
Apply 2-increasing to $[0,a] \times [a,b]$:
$C(a,b) - C(a,a) - C(0,b) + C(0,a) \geq 0$.
By idempotence $C(a,a) = a$; by groundedness $C(0,b) = 0$
and $C(0,a) = 0$; hence $C(a,b) \geq a = \min(a,b)$.

Together: $C(a,b) = \min(a,b)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The Min Copula as the Comonotonicity Copula]
\label{rem:comonotonicity}
The min copula is not an ad hoc construction.
In probability theory, it is the
\emph{comonotonicity copula}~\cite{dhaene2002,nelsen2006}:
the extremal case of maximal positive dependence, where all
random variables are increasing functions of a single latent
variable.
It is the \emph{upper} Fr\'echet--Hoeffding bound:
$\min(a,b)$ is the largest possible joint for given marginals.
The copula axioms (including 2-increasing) ensure that
$\min$ is a valid joint distribution consistent with probability
theory, not merely a logical convenience~\cite{nelsen2006}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Congruence Classification}

The collapse is a congruence --- an equivalence relation compatible
with the algebraic operations.
How many such congruences exist?
The answer depends on whether multiplication compatibility is
required only for $[0,1]$-valued inputs or for all of $\Real$.

A \emph{unit congruence} is an equivalence relation $\sim$ on
$[0,1]$ satisfying: (i) $a \sim b$ implies $(1-a) \sim (1-b)$
(negation compatibility), and (ii) $a \sim b$ and $c \sim d$, with
$a,b,c,d \in [0,1]$, imply $ac \sim bd$ ($[0,1]$-multiplication
compatibility).
A \emph{real congruence} strengthens (ii) to unrestricted
$\Real$-multiplication: $a \sim b$ and $c \sim d$ (for any
$a,b,c,d \in \Real$) imply $ac \sim bd$.
That is, the equivalence classes are subsets of $[0,1]$, but the
compatibility condition permits multipliers from all of $\Real$,
not just $[0,1]$.

\begin{theorem}[Congruence Classification]
\label{thm:congruence}
Let $\sim$ be a congruence on $([0,1], \negc, \conjc)$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item \textbf{Singleton boundaries} (unit congruence):
  if $0 \sim \varepsilon$ for any $\varepsilon > 0$, then $\sim$
  is trivial; likewise for $\{1\}$ by negation duality.
  Lean: \texttt{UnitCongruence.singleton\_zero},
  \texttt{singleton\_one}.
\item \textbf{No two-element quotient:}
  no surjective homomorphism $[0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ preserves
  negation (Proposition~\ref{prop:no-classical}).
\item \textbf{Uniqueness at three elements:}
  if the quotient has exactly $3$ classes, it is the Kleene lattice
  $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1\}$
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient-unique}).
  The Kleene partition is itself a verified unit congruence
  (Lean: \texttt{kleeneCongruence},
  \texttt{kleene\_nonTrivial}).
\item \textbf{$\Real$-rigidity:}
  under $\Real$-multiplication, no non-trivial finite quotient
  exists.
  Lean: \texttt{RealCongruence.no\_nontrivial\_finite\_quotient}.
\end{enumerate}
Whether a unit congruence with $4$ or more classes exists under
$[0,1]$-multiplication alone is \textbf{open}.
\end{theorem}

The proof of (1) iterates the contraction $x \mapsto x(1-\varepsilon)$
to drive any $x$ below $\varepsilon$, then uses
$[0,1]$-multiplication to factor through $0 \sim \varepsilon$.
The proof of (4) uses the pigeonhole principle on powers of
$\tfrac{1}{2}$ to find two powers in the same class, then multiplies
by the real inverse (which lies outside $[0,1]$) to derive
$1 \sim \tfrac{1}{2}^p$, contradicting singleton boundaries.

The $\Real$-rigidity proof relies on multiplicative inverses
outside $[0,1]$.
Under $[0,1]$-multiplication alone, the scaling trick fails because
$[0,1]$ has no multiplicative inverses for elements in $(0,1)$:
$a/x$ may leave the interval.
This isolates \emph{$[0,1]$-cancellation} as the precise obstacle
to resolving the four-or-more-class question.

\noindent
The product De Morgan triplet is a \emph{reduct} of the product
BL-algebra: it drops the residuated implication and replaces it with
the involutive complement.
Dropping residuation removes the constraint $0 \to b = 1$ (ex falso)
that forces simplicity in the BL
case~\cite{agliano2003,galatos2007}, leaving room for the Kleene
partition.
In MV-algebras~\cite{cignoli2000,mundici2011}, Chang's completeness
theorem gives a rich congruence lattice.
The De Morgan reduct sits between: at least one non-trivial finite
congruence exists (Kleene), but whether it is the only one remains
unknown.

The Lean formalization is in \texttt{Cred/Congruence.lean}.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:congruence}
(Unit Congruence Classification)]

\emph{(1) Singleton boundaries.}
Suppose $0 \sim \varepsilon$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$.
Negation compatibility gives $1 \sim 1 - \varepsilon$.
For any $x \in [0,1]$, multiplication compatibility applied to the
pairs $(x, x)$ and $(1, 1 - \varepsilon)$ (all in $[0,1]$) gives
\[
x = x \cdot 1 \;\sim\; x(1 - \varepsilon).
\]
Iterating: $x \sim x(1-\varepsilon)^n$ for all $n \in \Nat$.
Since $0 < 1 - \varepsilon < 1$, the sequence
$(1-\varepsilon)^n \to 0$, so for sufficiently large $n$ we have
$x(1-\varepsilon)^n \leq \varepsilon$.
Write $x(1-\varepsilon)^n = \varepsilon \cdot q$ where
$q = x(1-\varepsilon)^n / \varepsilon \in [0,1]$.
From $0 \sim \varepsilon$, multiplication compatibility with
$(q, q)$ gives
$0 = 0 \cdot q \;\sim\; \varepsilon \cdot q = x(1-\varepsilon)^n$.
Transitivity then gives $x \sim 0$ for all $x \in [0,1]$:
the congruence is trivial, contradicting non-triviality.

For the class $\{1\}$: if $1 \sim y$ with $y < 1$, then
negation gives $0 \sim 1 - y$ with $1 - y > 0$, reducing to
the previous case.

\emph{(2) No two-element quotient.}
Any negation-preserving map $\varphi : [0,1] \to \{0,1\}$ must
satisfy $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 1 - \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$, which
has no solution in $\{0,1\}$.

\emph{(3) Uniqueness at three elements.}
Let $\varphi : [0,1] \to S$ with $|S| = 3$ be a surjective
homomorphism preserving negation and conjunction.
The element $\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$ must be the unique negation
fixed point of $S$.
The remaining elements $\varphi(0)$ and $\varphi(1)$ form the
2-cycle under negation.
The conjunction table is then forced:
$\varphi(0)$ absorbs ($0 \conjc c = 0$), $\varphi(1)$ is the
identity ($1 \conjc c = c$), and
$\varphi(\tfrac{1}{2}) \conjc \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})
= \varphi(\tfrac{1}{4}) = \varphi(\tfrac{1}{2})$
since $\tfrac{1}{4} \in (0,1)$ maps to the interior class.
This forces Kleene $\min$.

\emph{(4) Existence.}
The partition $\{0\}, (0,1), \{1\}$ is verified as a unit
congruence (Lean: \texttt{kleeneCongruence}).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of the $\Real$-Multiplication Impossibility]
Suppose a non-trivial real congruence $\sim$ admits a finite quotient
with $n \geq 2$ classes on $[0,1]$.
(The case $n = 2$ is ruled out by the no-two-element argument above.)

For $n \geq 3$: set $a = \tfrac{1}{2}$.
Among the $n + 1$ values $a^0 = 1, a^1, \ldots, a^n \in [0,1]$,
pigeonhole on $n$ classes gives $0 \leq i < j \leq n$ with
$a^i \sim a^j$.
Write $a^j = a^i \cdot a^p$ where $p = j - i \geq 1$.
Using $\Real$-multiplication compatibility, multiply both sides
of $a^i \sim a^i \cdot a^p$ by $(a^i)^{-1}$:
since $(a^i)^{-1} = 2^i > 1$ is a valid $\Real$-valued
multiplier, we obtain
\[
1 = (a^i)^{-1} \cdot a^i \;\sim\; (a^i)^{-1} \cdot a^i \cdot a^p
= a^p.
\]
Negation gives $0 \sim 1 - a^p$.
Since $0 < a = \tfrac{1}{2} < 1$ and $p \geq 1$,
we have $0 < a^p < 1$, hence $1 - a^p > 0$.
This contradicts singleton boundaries (part~(1) above).
\end{proof}
